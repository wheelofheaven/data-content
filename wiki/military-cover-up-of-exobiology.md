**The military cover-up of exobiology** — increasingly called the
**government UAP cover-up** — is the contested claim that governments,
principally that of the United States, have concealed the investigation,
evidence, and alleged recovery of **unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP)**,
known for most of the twentieth century as **unidentified flying objects
(UFOs)**, and of a non-human technological presence on Earth. The subject
spans a documented record and a much larger body of unverified allegation,
and any careful treatment has to keep the two apart.

A documented core is not in serious dispute. The CIA's 1953 Robertson Panel
recommended a program of public debunking; Project Blue Book closed more than
seven hundred cases as unidentified before it was shut down in 1969; and
classified Pentagon programs ran through decades in which the government's
public posture was that it studied nothing. What remains contested — and in
its most dramatic form, unverified — is the further claim that agencies hold
recovered craft and non-human remains and have reverse-engineered alien
technology across decades. Between 2017 and 2024 a wave of official
disclosures moved the whole question from the fringe into congressional
hearings and Pentagon press releases without settling the larger allegation
either way.

The Wheel of Heaven framework reads the question against its own account of
contact. On the canon the [Elohim](../elohim/) are a real, advanced human
civilization, and contact on their terms is meant to be open, official, and
mutually recognized — the [Embassy](../embassy/) of
*[Message from the Designers](../message-from-the-designers/)*, not a leaked
video. A terrestrial cover-up, if it is real, is therefore a human institution
withholding what the canon holds humanity is entitled to know.

## A note on terminology

The vocabulary has shifted with the politics. "Flying saucer" gave way to
"UFO" — coined within the Air Force by Edward Ruppelt around 1953 — which in
turn gave way after 2017 to "UAP," first *Unidentified Aerial Phenomena* and,
since 2022, *Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena*. The newer term is preferred in
policy contexts precisely because it drops the implication that the objects
are "flying" or that they are "craft." The 2023 hearings added "non-human
intelligence" (NHI) and Grusch's phrase "non-human biologics." The drift is
away from "UFO" and "extraterrestrial" and toward language that commits to
less.

## The documented record, 1947–2017

Organized U.S. study of the phenomenon begins with Kenneth Arnold's June 1947
sighting and the Roswell recovery a few weeks later. The Air Force's first
study, **Project Sign** (1947–1949), reportedly produced an "Estimate of the
Situation" favoring an extraterrestrial explanation that the Chief of Staff
rejected; the document itself has never been declassified, and the account
rests on Ruppelt's 1956 memoir. Its skeptical successor, **Project Grudge**
(1949–1952), set the debunking tone that would dominate for two decades.

That tone was formalized by the **Robertson Panel** of January 1953, a
CIA-convened scientific board chaired by the physicist H. P. Robertson. Its
report — declassified through Freedom of Information Act requests in the 1970s,
and so a matter of record rather than rumor — recommended that mass media be
used to "strip the UFOs of the special status they have been given," and that
reporting be discouraged. This documented recommendation of an official
debunking program is the cover-up question in its most defensible form.

**Project Blue Book** (1952–1969) carried that framework for seventeen years,
investigating 12,618 reports and closing 701 of them as unidentified before
the **Condon Report** — a University of Colorado study under the physicist
Edward Condon — concluded in 1968 that further study was unwarranted. The
Condon study is itself contested: an internal memo by its coordinator, Robert
Low, proposed that the project "appear a totally objective study" while
conveying to scientists "the image of a group of nonbelievers." Blue Book
closed in December 1969.

For nearly five decades afterward the government maintained no publicly
acknowledged UAP program. Two developments complicate that official silence.
First, the declassified paper trail kept growing — the CIA, FBI, NSA, and Air
Force records released under FOIA and gathered by John Greenewald's **Black
Vault** archive (founded 1996) run to millions of pages, and show more
attention to the subject than the public posture admitted. Second, classified
work continued: the Defense Intelligence Agency's **AAWSAP** (2008–2010),
funded through appropriations championed by Senator Harry Reid, and the
Pentagon's **AATIP** (2007–2012), later directed by Luis Elizondo,
investigated UAP through years in which officials denied doing so. The
existence of these programs, revealed in 2017, is the clearest documented
proof that the work continued behind the disengaged façade.

## The 2017–2024 disclosure wave

The modern shift began on **16 December 2017**, when Helene Cooper, Ralph
Blumenthal, and Leslie Kean reported AATIP's existence in *The New York Times*
and published two Navy sensor videos — the 2004 Nimitz "tic-tac" encounter and
a 2015 case — alongside Commander David Fravor's first-person account. On
**27 April 2020** the Department of Defense officially released three videos
(FLIR1, Gimbal, GoFast), confirming that they were authentic and unexplained.
On **25 June 2021** the Office of the Director of National Intelligence
assessed 144 encounters logged by U.S. sources between 2004 and 2021, of which
only one could be identified with confidence — a deflating balloon.

In July 2022 the Pentagon stood up the **All-Domain Anomaly Resolution Office
(AARO)** as its standing investigative body. The wave crested on
**26 July 2023**, when the House Oversight Committee's National Security
Subcommittee heard three witnesses under oath: Navy pilot Ryan Graves and
Commander Fravor described encounters and a reporting stigma, while **David
Grusch**, a former Air Force and National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency
officer, alleged that the government holds "non-human craft" and "non-human
biologics" and has run reverse-engineering programs outside congressional
oversight — and that people had been harmed to keep it secret. Grusch spoke
with Intelligence Community Inspector General whistleblower protection,
following an IG finding that his complaint was "urgent and credible." He
testified to what others had told him; he presented no physical evidence, and
his central claims remain unverified.

A NASA independent panel, chaired by the astrophysicist David Spergel,
reported in September 2023 that UAP deserved systematic scientific study
rather than dismissal, while noting that existing data quality was poor.
Follow-up hearings in November 2024 extended the process without a comparable
revelation. Cutting the other way, AARO's own 2024 Historical Record Report
concluded that the rumored reverse-engineering programs traced to misidentified
conventional projects rather than to recovered non-human technology — a finding
Grusch and others reject, and which stands as the government's formal counter
to the cover-up thesis even as other arms of the government confirm that the
phenomena are real.

## Whistleblowers, contested documents, and the nuclear pattern

Grusch sits at the credible end of a whistleblower tradition that runs back
decades and varies widely in evidential weight. **Bob Lazar** has claimed
since 1989 to have reverse-engineered alien propulsion at a site called S-4,
powered by a stable form of element 115; when element 115 (moscovium) was
synthesized in 2003 it proved to have a half-life of milliseconds, and neither
university he cites has any record of his attendance. His account is best
treated as unverified. **Robert Salas**, by contrast, is a first-hand witness
to a documented event: the March 1967 shutdown of ten Minuteman missiles at
Malmstrom Air Force Base during reports of an object over the site,
corroborated by other personnel and preserved in declassified records.
Researcher Robert Hastings has assembled roughly 150 such nuclear-facility
cases in *UFOs and Nukes* and organized a 2010 National Press Club briefing of
former missile officers — a genuinely documented pattern, whatever its cause.
Figures like Steven Greer, whose Disclosure Project staged an earlier witness
briefing in 2001, and the late Phil Schneider, whose "Dulce base" claims have
no independent support, sit further from verification.

The contested-document tradition is weaker still. The **Majestic 12 papers**,
which surfaced in 1984 and purport to describe a secret Truman-era committee,
were judged fabricated by the FBI in 1988 on forensic grounds, and the
2017–2024 disclosures have not rehabilitated them. The intersection with
documented deception is real but limited: some Cold War "UFO" sightings were
classified aircraft — the U-2 and SR-71 among them — and the CIA's MK-ULTRA
program shows that the agencies were capable of concealment, but neither fact
establishes a recovered-craft program.

Serious historical work on the question exists. Richard Dolan's two-volume
*UFOs and the National Security State* organizes the declassified record
chronologically; Leslie Kean's *UFOs: Generals, Pilots, and Government
Officials Go on the Record* (2010) brought military testimony into the
mainstream press; and Luis Elizondo's *Imminent* (2024) is a former program
director's account. Internationally the contrast is instructive: France's
**GEIPAN**, run within the national space agency since 1977, investigates UAP
openly and publishes its case files on a four-level scale — a demonstration
that a government can study the phenomenon without any cover-up at all.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework treats the documented secrecy as real and the recovered-craft
allegation as unproven, and reads both against the canon's own logic of
contact.

On the canon the [Elohim](../elohim/) are not hiding. They are an advanced
civilization that has stated its terms plainly: contact is to be open,
official, and mutually recognized, formalized in an [Embassy](../embassy/)
built with the diplomatic standing of a state.
*[Message from the Designers](../message-from-the-designers/)* states the terms
and leaves the next move to humanity —

> "The Elohim are now waiting for the State of Israel to grant such
> extra-territorial status for the new embassy — the third temple — but so far
> there has been no positive response to the seven requests that have been
> made."

Read this way, a terrestrial cover-up is not the Elohim concealing themselves;
it is a human institution withholding what the canon holds humanity is
entitled to know. That entitlement is itself a canon theme. The corpus reads
the long arc of the alliance's dealings with Earth as bending, over ages,
toward open disclosure — and that was precisely the position of the exiled
[Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction, the creators who argued that humanity should be
given full knowledge of its origins rather than kept in managed ignorance. On
the framework's reading Lucifer's advocacy has been gradually vindicated by
that trajectory. It is worth being exact about the canon here, because popular
tradition collapses the distinction: the disclosure-and-education faction is
Lucifer's, the faction of the [Serpent](../serpent/); the opposed faction, led
by [Satan](../satan/) on the home world, argued not for secrecy but for ending
the human project altogether. Secrecy versus disclosure is a human argument in
the canon's terms, not the Elohim's.

The framework therefore locates the resolution of the disclosure question not
in a leaked video or a Pentagon admission but in that formal, mutual
recognition. The 2017–2024 wave is read as movement in the right direction —
the subject brought into the open — but not as the resolution itself, which
awaits the Embassy. This last connection, tying a contemporary policy story to
the canon's diplomatic framework, is interpretive synthesis rather than
anything stated in a single source, and is offered as such.

## Skeptical and critical perspectives

The mainstream skeptical position deserves a fair hearing, because on the
evidence it holds much of the ground. Investigators such as Philip Klass and
Joe Nickell argued for decades that the residue of genuinely unexplained cases
shrinks under scrutiny: most reports resolve into aircraft, balloons,
astronomical objects, sensor artifacts, and misperception, and the burden of
proof for an extraordinary claim has not been met. The strongest documented
facts — that hearings were held, that the Navy videos are real, that the
Robertson Panel urged debunking — establish institutional secrecy and
unexplained sensor data, not visitors. The most extraordinary claims are the
least supported: Grusch's testimony is secondhand and unaccompanied by
physical evidence; the MJ-12 papers are fabrications; Lazar's account fails on
its own details; and AARO's 2024 review found no evidence of any
reverse-engineering program. A responsible reading grants the documented core,
withholds judgment on the rest, and does not mistake a real record of
government secrecy for proof of what the secrecy concealed.
