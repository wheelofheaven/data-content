**Four Levels** is the name the Wheel of Heaven corpus gives to a rule stated by
[Yahweh](../yahweh/) in the Raëlian source texts: that any situation should be
weighed against four frames of reference at once — infinity, the
[Elohim](../elohim/) who created us, human society, and the individual self. The
rule first appears in the "Humanity" section of "The Keys," in
*Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet* (1975):

> In every situation, we must always consider things in regard to four levels: in
> relation to infinity; in relation to the Elohim, our parents and creators; in
> relation to human society; and finally, in relation to the individual.

The same passage fixes the framework's two organising principles. One level
carries more weight than the others — "the most important level is that relating
to infinity, for it is in relation to this level that all things must be judged" —
yet the judgement is never made on that level alone. It is made "with one constant
factor: love." The Four Levels are therefore not a ladder on which the higher rung
overrules the lower, but four angles held together, with love running through all
of them. Where the broader Raëlian cosmology — [infinity](../infinity/),
[fractal cosmology](../fractal-cosmology/), the [living Earth](../living-earth/) —
describes what the universe is, the Four Levels describe how a person is meant to
act inside it.

## The four levels

**Level one: the Infinite.** Measured against infinity, a human life is nothing.
The FAQ passage in *Message from the Designers* puts it without softening: "Our
life means nothing when compared with the Infinite. If we die, if all of humanity
disappears, it will not change anything in the Infinity of time or space."
Nothing we do registers on the cosmic scale. The framework reads this as a release
rather than a defeat: because the cosmos does not depend on us, we are freed from
the impossible weight of cosmic responsibility and can act at our own scale. The
posture at this level is harmony with the infinitely large and the infinitely
small, not a pretence of cosmic importance.

**Level two: the Elohim.** Against infinity we are nothing; against our makers we
are not. "In relation to the Elohim, our life is very important, because we are
their children." The [Elohim](../elohim/) are the specific, finite beings who
created humanity, and our actions matter to them the way a creation matters to
those who made it and are watching what it becomes. This is the level of
accountability — of taking the creators' guidance into account and acting so that
human society hears it — and of the pride the source attaches to being "created in
their image": conscious of infinity, and one day able to create life in turn. That
last clause ties the level to the [cosmic chain](../cosmic-chain/), the corpus's
picture of creators who were themselves created by earlier creators.

**Level three: human society.** A life is also part of a collective longer than
itself. The source calls each person a cell "of this huge being that is Humanity,"
heir to "a long list of survivors who have escaped the epidemics and wars." At
this level a life matters because it contributes to a shared trajectory — the
"cells of Humanity" image reaching straight into the
[living Earth](../living-earth/) framework, where humanity is itself an
organism-like whole. The reading of Level three is critical rather than
conformist: society "must be taken into account" but "should not be followed," and
is instead to be helped "to emerge from its primitive straitjacket by regular
questioning of all its habits and traditions." Belonging, here, carries the duty
to improve what one belongs to.

**Level four: the individual self.** Finally, "in relation to our own self, our
life has only the importance that we give it." This is the level of personal
meaning: the significance of a life is not handed down from the cosmos but chosen
by the one living it. The source treats fulfilment as a precondition rather than a
reward — "it is impossible to harmonize yourself with infinity and become a new
man if you are not fulfilled." Without a fulfilled individual at Level four, the
other three levels have no one to act through.

The order in which the source lists the levels runs from the largest frame inward
— cosmos, creators, community, self — but the last level loops back to the first.
Fulfilment at Level four is the very thing that makes harmony with infinity at
Level one possible, so the framework is a circle rather than a descending scale:
the cosmic perspective that opens the list is reached, in the end, only through the
individual life that closes it. The list is also meant as complete rather than
illustrative — the four levels are offered as the whole set of frames a decision
has to answer to, not a sample of a longer one.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The corpus takes the four-level list as stated and reads it as a single working
instrument. This systematic reading — as opposed to the bare list — is where the
corpus develops the source rather than merely quoting it.

Its central claim is that the four levels correct one another. Each, taken alone,
fails in a predictable direction, and each is the antidote to a neighbour's
failure. The Infinite alone collapses into nihilism — if nothing matters
cosmically, nothing matters at all — so the Elohim level answers it with a
concrete addressee for whom our actions do matter. The Elohim level alone could
harden into deference, so the individual level insists that meaning is finally
self-assigned. The individual level alone slides into solipsism, which the society
level checks by placing the self inside a collective project. And the society
level alone invites conformity or collective tyranny, which the Infinite level
checks by reminding every human institution of its own smallness. Held together,
the four keep one another honest.

What holds them together is love — not one of the levels but the constant across
them, changing shape at each. At the Infinite level it is the recognition that "we
must live in harmony with others, because they are a part of infinity as well":
cosmic harmony reached through people rather than around them. At the Elohim level
it is the wish "to transmit your message of love to the Elohim." At the level of
society it is engagement with the human project rather than mere obligation to it;
at the level of the self it is care for one's own development. The source's
meditative instruction gathers all four into one practice: "Place yourself in
harmony with the infinitely large and with the infinitely small by radiating love
towards what is above and towards what is below, and by being conscious that you
yourself are part of infinity" — then turning that same love toward the Elohim and
toward the world around you.

This is why the framework insists the levels are held at once rather than ranked
and resolved. Infinity is the most important reference point — it is where "all
things must be judged" — but its importance is orienting, not overriding. A
decision that satisfied only the cosmic frame while ignoring the creators, the
community, or the self would, on this reading, be a failure of the framework
rather than an application of it. The Four Levels are the practical face of the
corpus's cosmology: the [infinity](../infinity/) and [living Earth](../living-earth/)
entries describe a universe of nested scales and living wholes, and the Four Levels
are the orientation a person adopts to live well among them.

## Comparative frameworks

Multi-level ethical schemes recur across many traditions, and the resemblances are
worth noting so long as the differences are kept in view — the parallels are
structural, and each tradition means something of its own.

The Hindu *puruṣārthas* — dharma, artha, kama, and moksha — name four legitimate
aims of a human life and ask that they be pursued in balance. The likeness to the
Four Levels is real at the level of four-fold integration, but the purusharthas
are goals one pursues where the Four Levels are angles from which one judges, and
they carry a doctrine of liberation from rebirth that the Raëlian framework does
not share. The Stoic circles of concern, articulated by Hierocles, arrange ethical
regard in rings widening from the self outward to all rational beings; this is an
expanding scope rather than a set of simultaneous frames, and its cosmic term is
the providential *logos* rather than embodied creators. The Buddhist
*brahmavihāras* — loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity —
offer the closest parallel to the love-as-constant principle, since they too place
a form of love at the centre of practice, though they are cultivated states rather
than levels of judgement. The Christian tradition's four loves (storge, philia,
eros, agape) and the Islamic sequence of *islam*, *iman*, and *ihsan* likewise
grade ethical and spiritual life, and Sufi accounts of the *maqāmāt* stage it as a
path of stations.

The framework's own contribution within this landscape is easy to state. Its
levels are grounded in a specific cosmology — the nested scales of the
[infinity](../infinity/) and [fractal cosmology](../fractal-cosmology/) entries —
rather than asserted on traditional authority; they are meant to be held at once
rather than climbed; love runs through all of them rather than crowning one; and
the second level has no counterpart in the secular schemes, because it answers to
the Elohim as finite makers rather than to an abstract divine principle. Set
against a bleaker reading of the same cosmic scale — the Lovecraftian view in which
human insignificance yields only dread — the Four Levels put the identical fact to
opposite use: the vastness that dwarfs us is, at the first level, precisely what
frees us to act.
