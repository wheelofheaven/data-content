**Jean Sendy** (1910–1978) was a Russian-born French author and
translator who published, between 1963 and 1975, the first sustained
statement of the reading this project is built on: that the Hebrew
Bible, taken at the letter, relates the sojourn on Earth of a
plurality of physical, mortal beings called the
[Elohim](../elohim/). He published it five years before Erich von
Däniken made the general idea a global commonplace, eleven years
before the Raëlian canon of 1974, and roughly half a century before
Mauro Biglino and [Paul Wallis](../paul-wallis/) re-derived the
reading from the Hebrew text in Italy and Australia. Within the
lineage the corpus files under [neo-euhemerism](../neo-euhemerism/),
Sendy is the earliest finished voice — and, in one respect, still the
most disciplined: he is the only author in the tradition's history to
have staked his entire reading on a falsifiable prediction, in print,
with a deadline, and to have accepted in advance what its failure
would mean.

Because Sendy wrote a decade before the canon, his position in the
corpus's reference apparatus differs from that of the later
fellow-travelers. With Wallis, independence runs forward: an author
who never read the Raëlian material converged on its load-bearing
joints from the text alone, which is what makes the convergence
evidential. With Sendy the same logic runs in reverse, and the corpus
states it openly rather than around it: his system was on the shelves
of France for eleven years before the 1974 message, the young Claude
Vorilhon had in all likelihood read him, and the question of what the
canon owes his books is a real one, treated below without polemic.
What the record supports on either reading of that question is that
Sendy worked his system out from the Hebrew text and the exact
sciences, with no claimed help from anyone — a parallel witness, not
a rough draft. The full-length study is the Explainer [*The Man Who
Bet the Bible on the
Moon*](/articles/the-man-who-bet-the-bible-on-the-moon/); this entry
gives the biographical and bibliographical record.

## Life

Sendy was born Dimitri Abelson in Petrograd on 16 November 1910, to a
Jewish family from Kaunas. The revolution carried the family through
Finland and Norway to New York harbor — the Ellis Island registers of
May 1919 land the eight-year-old Dimitri off a liner from Kristiania —
and finally to Paris. The 1930s photographs by Wols show a young man
of the avant-garde with slicked-back hair, a patterned necktie, and a
monocle; the pseudonym he later adopted may nod to the Polish
alchemist Michael Sendivogius. He was naturalized French in 1938 as a
"journalist," and after the war he surfaces filing from London for
the Resistance-founded *Parisien libéré*.

The career that followed ran through the engine room of French
publishing: translator of Raymond Chandler, James Hadley Chase, and
Peter Cheyney for the crime imprints, of Fredric Brown's science
fiction, of documents on the Katyn massacre and the German-Soviet
pact; subtitler of *The Graduate*. A friend who visited his flat
found it nearly empty except for a professional woodworking machine
on which Sendy was building his own dining table. He described
himself as an *honnête homme* in the seventeenth-century sense — the
cultivated gentleman whose ambition, in his own formulation, "is not
to know everything about everything, but to reduce the number of his
wrong ideas to a minimum." The sentence is on his gravestone. He died
at Coulommiers on 24 April 1978, three years before the first Space
Shuttle flew.

## The books and the thesis

The system arrives essentially complete in *Les cahiers de cours de
Moïse* — "Moses' Course Notebooks" — published by Julliard in
February 1963 from a manuscript dated 1962. The title states the
thesis: the Torah is not divine dictation but a *notebook*, the
course notes of a teaching that predates Moses, who recovered and
codified it. The teachers are "Theosites" from a planet Theos in
1963, *les Célestes* and *les Venus-du-ciel* — "the
Come-from-the-sky" — from 1968, *Galaxiens* by 1970; under every
coinage sits the Hebrew plural Elohim. The sequence that followed —
*Les dieux nous sont nés* (1966), *La lune, clé de la Bible* (1968),
*Ces dieux qui firent le ciel et la terre* (1969), *Nous autres, gens
du Moyen Âge* (1969), *L'Ère du Verseau* (1970), and the late *Les
temps messianiques* (1975) — develops one hypothesis "from book to
book," as he put it. Three titles reached English through Berkley in
New York, all translated by Lowell Bair: *Those Gods Who Made Heaven
and Earth* (1972), *The Coming of the Gods* (1973), and *The Moon:
Outpost of the Gods* (1975).

On Sendy's reading, [Genesis](../genesis/) is the log of a
recolonization. Around twenty-one millennia before our era, with
Earth wrapped in the cloud cover of the last glacial maximum — the
*tohu-bohu* of Genesis 1:2 — a small expedition of cosmonauts arrived
at a drowned and lightless world and began a six-phase public-works
program that he reads off the days of the creation week: dispersal of
the clouds, management of the waters, drainage and revegetation, the
re-mapping of the sky, the restocking of seas and land, and the
taking in hand of the planet's surviving bipeds. Each "day"
corresponds to a precessional epoch — one twelfth of the
[Great Year](../great-year/), 2,160 years per sign — which makes the
biblical chronology an astronomical one and the
[precession of the equinoxes](../precession/) the clock of the whole
account. [Eden](../eden/) is the walled biological installation of
the sixth phase, where staff with "all the time in the world" breed a
managerial elite from indigenous stock and enforce an access policy
on knowledge; the serpent is the leak. The
[Flood](../great-flood/) is the decommissioning of the installations
at departure, with Noah's *tebah* — a word that names a sealed
container, never a ship — as the capsule of "seeds of life" that
rides it out. The [tower of Babel](../tower-of-babel/) is a
post-departure heresy: initiates' descendants attempting to reach the
departed teachers prematurely, with launch infrastructure — for, as
Sendy argued, nobody who has not seen an industrial launch site
conceives the *necessity* of a tower "whose head reaches the
heavens."

Two disciplines set Sendy apart from every telling that came after.
The first is his claim hygiene. In block capitals in *Ces dieux*: he
has NEVER affirmed that cosmonauts arrived; he AFFIRMS that the
biblical text, taken at the letter, relates that; he THINKS that
Genesis reflects a historical truth. Affirmed, argued, believed —
three registers kept separate on the page, an ancestor of the
claim-type discipline this corpus practices by rule. He told readers
to check him against nothing but the Hebrew or the
[Dhorme translation](../dhorme-bible-translation/), the one French
Bible that leaves *Iahvé Elohim* standing. The second is the reading
principle stated in 1969: read the Bible *"comme Schliemann lisait
Homère"* — as Schliemann read Homer, a document that means what it
says, all of what it says, and nothing more than what it says. The
corpus catalogs that principle, and its later independent
re-derivation, under the [Biglino Method](../biglino-method/).

Beneath the space-age surface ran two theses that were entirely his
own. The first is the medieval postulate, the argument of *Nous
autres, gens du Moyen Âge*: that on the one question that matters,
pre-Renaissance exegesis stood closer to the text's real meaning than
Renaissance and Enlightenment humanism ever did — that humanity is
not owner of the planet but *concessionaire*, trustee of a grant from
grantors who left and promised to be findable again. He dedicated
*L'Ère du Verseau* to the memory of Giordano Bruno, burned in 1600
"because these medieval ideas... displeased the humanists of the
Renaissance." The second is the Kabbalah, engaged not through
occultist compilations but through *La Cabale* of Alexandre Safran,
Chief Rabbi of Geneva, whose testimony that the Tradition "surpasses
the Sinai revelation in antiquity" — and whose sentence "man will
renew the acts related at the beginning of Genesis" — Sendy read as
the custodians' own confirmation that the teaching is older than its
codifier, and as a program now underway. Safran agreed to chair
Sendy's 1969 Geneva lecture; each man borrowed the other's facts and
declined the other's frame. The corpus's entry on
[the Tradition](../the-tradition/) descends directly from this seam,
and its [Kabbalah](../kabbalah/) entry works the same material from
the canon's side.

## The Moon wager

What makes Sendy unique in this literature — unique, as far as the
corpus can determine, in the history of religious reinterpretation —
is the move he made in 1968. If the Celestials were physically "in
our image," their seven-millennium operation on Earth needed a
staging base, and celestial mechanics dictates the Moon. Its subsoil
should therefore still hold traces — *"un Orly cosmonautique,"* a
cosmonautical Orly. In *La lune, clé de la Bible* he published the
positive case — "a single pottery shard would suffice... to validate
the totality of the biblical text that allowed me to predict, by
theory, the discovery" — and then wrote the negative case with equal
care: if no trace of cosmonauts is found on the Moon, "the overall
coherence I propose will be refuted." In *Nous autres*, signed to
press in December 1968, he formalized the protocol and set the
deadline: *"En 1970, si rien de tel n'est retrouvé, mon hypothèse
sera à tenir pour fausse"* — in 1970, if nothing of the kind is
found, my hypothesis is to be held false. A hypothesis that leaves
its author branches to grab when refuted, he added, "is not a
hypothesis but a charlatan's enterprise." In *L'Ère du Verseau* he
went further and printed coordinates, seizing on a provisional
negative gravity anomaly in the early JPL mascon data: 56°N 75°W.

Apollo settled the wager. Six landings returned basalt and breccia
and no shard; the lunar anomalies he had leaned on dissolved, over
the following decades, into tidal locking, the giant-impact origin,
and better gravimetry. By the letter of his own charlatan-clause the
lunar ark, as formulated, stands refuted — the plain reading of the
record, and the one he would have demanded be written. His own
conduct afterward sits between concession and relapse: the 1975 book
quietly relocates the target to a subsurface cavity awaiting the
Space Shuttle, which his own protocol must count as a branch grabbed;
but he never denied the miss, never re-dated the prophecy, founded
nothing, monetized nothing, and went on publicly refuting
UFO-contactee claims at the height of the von Däniken tide. He had
also printed his critics inside the wager book itself — the errata of
*La lune* thank Leroi-Gourhan for correcting his Lascaux dating,
Schatzman for redoing his energy calculations, and quote the
geneticist Cuny's verdict that his interbreeding scenario had "no
more than a 0.01% chance of being right." It was Schatzman, a
militant rationalist, who wrote the introductions for Sendy's 1970
American tour, where he put his questions to Freeman Dyson at
Princeton and Carl Sagan at Cornell. The corpus reads the wager, won
or lost, as the tradition's conscience: a standing challenge to stake
any part of the framework on a checkable consequence.

## The conditions of coherence

Sendy's methodological legacy is codified in the corpus under
[Sendy's Conditions of Coherence](../sendys-conditions-of-coherence/)
— the five preconditions he listed in chapter 18 of *L'Ère du
Verseau* for the biblical text to appear "with the relief and
coherence of a hologram": familiarity with the Bible itself;
familiarity with astronautics within the framework of contemporary
physics; elementary but recent knowledge of biology, prehistory,
history, and evolution; refusal of any "explanation by the
Inexplicable"; and the implicit fifth, acceptance of a universe
intelligible to human reason. That entry treats the framework in
full, including its pairing with the Biglino Method — Sendy supplies
what the reader must *have*, Biglino what the reader must *do* — and
the medievalist-rationalist positioning of the fourth condition,
which rejects the supernatural not as Enlightenment naturalism but as
the recovery of a pre-Plotinian reading in which the Tradition is
transmitted knowledge from physical visitors. This entry does not
duplicate that treatment; the biographical point is only that the
conditions were the late formalization of habits Sendy had practiced
from the first book onward.

## Relation to the Wheel of Heaven framework

The convergences between Sendy's system and the canon are extensive
and specific. The plural Elohim read as concrete physical beings —
his *Venus-du-ciel* and the canon's gloss "those come from the sky"
are nearly the same French words. Eden as a managed biological
installation, with the forbidden tree as access policy rather than
morality, and nothing falling — a protocol breached. The Flood as a
deliberate reset with genetic stock preserved in a sealed container;
Babel as an interrupted reach for the creators' sky. History
structured by the precessional [world ages](../world-age/), with the
decisive threshold at the turn into Aquarius — Sendy's 1950, by the
vernal point, sits five years from the canon's 1945, dated by
Hiroshima: two entirely different roads to almost the same door. And
deepest, the shared eschatology of graduation: Sendy's Aquarius is
the age in which men "equal themselves to the gods" and in time seed
other worlds as a new race of Elohim — the relay the corpus files
under [the cosmic chain](../cosmic-chain/).

The divergences are equally structural, and they are what make Sendy
a parallel witness rather than a rough draft. First, creation. The
canon's Elohim create life on Earth — all of it, from alga to man —
in their laboratories; Sendy's Celestials create nothing of the
kind. His bipeds are indigenous, evolved on Earth by the standard
account; his six days are a restoration of a ruined biosphere, and
his Eden accelerates and selects existing stock. On this axis Sendy
stands farther from the canon than Biglino or Wallis, both of whom
keep the fabrication of the Adam. Second, Yahweh. The canon seats
[Yahweh](../yahweh/) as president of the
[Council of the Eternals](../council-of-the-eternals/); Sendy removes
him from the roster of persons altogether — his Iahvé is the
unpronounceable *Principle*, "as immaterial as a mathematical
principle," the Law of laws that the Elohim themselves obey — and he
flagged the area himself: "Yahweh is the weak point of my thesis."
Third, the return. Sendy expected none: "the cosmonauts, in all
likelihood, will not come back," leaving humanity an inheritance to
excavate and, beyond it, contact between equals. The canon's entire
practical program is the opposite — the
[Great Return](../great-return/) as diplomacy, with an
[embassy](../embassy/) to build and hosts rather than excavators.

Then there is the question of 1974. Sendy read, or heard enough of,
the young Claude Vorilhon's first book, and left one sentence about
it in *Les temps messianiques*: the "amiable humbug of the good young
man who, having read my books, decided to call 'the Elohim' those
extraterrestrials to whom he attributes the platitudes he delivers in
lectures." The plagiarism dossier built on that resemblance —
catalogued motif by motif by ex-Raëlians and at book length by Maryse
Péloquin, answered point by point from the Raëlian side, recorded
without adjudication by academic observers from Palmer to Bigliardi —
is summarized in the Explainer. The corpus reads the Raëlian texts as
canon, so its own position is stated with complete transparency, and
it can be, because the logic is symmetrical. If the 1973 encounter
did not happen, the message is a condensation of Sendy — his
hypothesis with the falsifiability clause amputated and a testimony
grafted on. If it did happen, then the Elohim chose a France in which
Sendy had spent eleven years teaching the reading public how to parse
the words "we are men like you," and left in print an independent
corroboration arrived at by scholarship alone — which is how French
Raëlians in fact used his books. On either branch Sendy's stature
rises: he is either the true author of the century's most
consequential reinterpretation of Genesis, or the man who worked out
from the text, unaided, most of what the canon says a witness was
later told. The one verdict the record cannot support is the
dismissive one — *scooped precursor*, *mere source* — that both camps
have filed him under.

## Legacy

In the lineage the corpus calls neo-euhemerist, Sendy's priority is
simply chronological fact: the *Cahiers* precede *Chariots of the
Gods?* by five years, Sitchin's *The 12th Planet* by thirteen, and
the entire text-led branch of the tradition descends from — or, more
precisely, re-derives — positions he stated first. Biglino, who
reached the same Schliemann principle through seventeen years of
professional Hebrew translation, has denied having read Sendy; Wallis
cites him nowhere; the corpus treats both re-derivations as
methodological convergence rather than influence, and notes that the
convergence itself is evidence — two later readers, working the same
text independently, arriving at the structure Sendy published in
1963–1969 suggests the text produces the method when engaged closely.
His contemporary reception was thin and largely adversarial: the
rationalist ufologists reviewed him skeptically in his lifetime,
mainstream scholarship ignored him, and the one substantial academic
study — Stefano Bigliardi's "A Gentleman's Joyous Esotericism"
(2017) — arrived four decades after his death. Only three of the
seven books were ever translated, which left his two most original
contributions, the medieval thesis and the Safran engagement,
invisible to Anglophone readers.

Within this corpus his standing is different in kind from any other
author's. The conditions of coherence and the Schliemann principle
anchor the methodology entries; the medieval postulate charters the
corpus's stance that the supernatural is a late corruption of a
concrete record; the precessional spine — Genesis days as world
ages, the pre-Hipparchan antiquity of precessional knowledge, the
alliance with *Hamlet's Mill* — is structural in the
[Wheel of Heaven](../wheel-of-heaven/) framing itself, which takes
its name from the object Sendy spent his life pointing at. And the
register is his: *"je n'apporte pas un évangile, je propose une
hypothèse"* — I bring no gospel, I propose a hypothesis. A project
that reads apocalypse as unveiling and holds every exit unlocked is
writing in Sendy's voice, and knows it.
