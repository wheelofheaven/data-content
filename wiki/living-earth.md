**Living Earth** is the Wheel of Heaven corpus's name for a claim the Raëlian
source texts state plainly: that **the Earth — and every other planet, and the
larger cosmic structures above them — is a literal living being**. Not a metaphor
for a self-regulating system, but an organism in the same sense that we are
organisms, at a scale and on a timescale too vast for us to perceive from within.
Living Earth is the biological strand of the corpus's cosmology. It sits alongside
[Infinity](../infinity/) (the cosmos without edge, beginning, or smallest scale),
[Fractal Cosmology](../fractal-cosmology/) (the nested, self-similar structure of
those scales), and [Mass Effect](../mass-effect/) (the way time runs faster or
slower at different scales), and reads the structure those frameworks describe as,
quite literally, alive.

The claim rests on a passage [Yahweh](../yahweh/) delivers in *Extra-Terrestrials
Took Me to Their Planet* (1975), in the "Neither God nor Soul" section:

> But everything in the universe is alive and in harmony with the infinitely large
> and the infinitely small. The Earth is alive like all the planets, and for the
> small growth that is humanity, it is difficult to notice this because of the time
> lag due to the enormous difference in mass, which prevents you from perceiving its
> palpitations. Nor could one of our red blood cells, or better still, one of the
> atoms that make up our body, imagine that it forms, with its peers, a living being.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

On the canon's reading the statement is meant at face value. The Earth is not
*like* an organism; it is one. Its oceans, atmosphere, biosphere, and crust are
differentiated subsystems the way a body's organs are; its biogeochemical cycles
are its metabolism; its climate self-regulation is its homeostasis. What makes
this hard to see is not that the life is absent but that it runs at a scale our
senses were never built to register.

The reason is the [Mass Effect](../mass-effect/): "Time is, in fact, inversely
proportional to the mass, or rather to the level of the form of life." A being as
massive as the Earth lives far more slowly than we do — a single "palpitation"
might span centuries — so its life is invisible to us for the same reason a red
blood cell cannot register that it belongs to a person. We stand to the Earth as
the cells of our body stand to us. The absence of any perceptible heartbeat is
exactly what the framework predicts, not evidence against it.

The Earth is one case of a general claim. "Everything in the universe is alive,"
and the same aliveness runs in both directions along the scale-hierarchy that
Infinity and Fractal Cosmology describe. Upward, the stars and planets are the
atoms of a gigantic being who "certainly contemplates other stars with curiosity,"
and in the time it takes a millennium to pass for us, that being takes a single
step. Downward, intelligent life exists at the infinitely small, living "on
particles that are planets and suns to them." Each scale hosts living beings whose
constituents are themselves living beings, without end.

*The Book Which Tells the Truth* (1974) puts the same idea in starker terms in its
chapter "Mankind: A Disease of the Universe": humanity is "a disease inside this
gigantic being, and the planets and stars are its atoms," and "this same gigantic
being is in its turn a parasite on other greater atoms. In both directions, it is
infinite." The register is anti-anthropocentric rather than pessimistic — a
statement about scale, not about worth; the fuller treatment of that framing lives
in the [Infinity](../infinity/) entry.

Living Earth is also where the corpus's cosmology turns into an ethic. Yahweh's
instruction is to "live in harmony with the infinitely large, the infinitely
small, and with our fellow human beings" — the demand that becomes the
[Four Levels](../four-levels/) of consideration. If the Earth is a living being of
which humanity is one constituent among many, then ecological care is not sentiment
but the recognition of a real relationship, of the same kind a cell owes the body
it lives in.

The label "Living Earth" is the corpus's own; the source speaks of everything
being alive rather than naming a doctrine. Placing the claim as the biological
correlate of the Infinity–Fractal-Cosmology–Mass-Effect cluster is likewise corpus
development, anchored in Yahweh's "everything in the universe is alive" but
reaching past what the source states outright. It is offered as a `framework`
claim: explicit in the Raëlian canon, and not one mainstream science endorses.

## The Gaia hypothesis and kindred science

The nearest scientific neighbour is the **Gaia hypothesis** of James Lovelock and
Lynn Margulis. Lovelock's first article, "Gaia as seen through the atmosphere,"
appeared in 1972; the collaborative statement with Margulis followed in 1974, and
the book-length *Gaia: A New Look at Life on Earth* in 1979. Its claim is that
Earth's biosphere behaves as a self-regulating system, holding atmospheric
composition and temperature within the range life needs through coupled biological
and physical feedbacks. The Raëlian statement (1973–75) sits inside this same
window — after Lovelock's first article, before his book — so the two are better
described as contemporaneous and convergent than as one anticipating the other.

The convergence is real but partial, and the difference is the point. Gaia is
usually split into a *weak* form — Earth's biosphere has homeostatic,
organism-*like* properties without being a single organism — and a *strong* form,
in which the planet is literally an organism. Weak Gaia is broadly defensible and
has been absorbed into mainstream Earth-system science; strong Gaia is not the
scientific consensus, and Lovelock himself generally treated the organism language
as a heuristic rather than a literal biological claim. Living Earth is a literal
reading — closest to strong Gaia — and then carries it well past anything Lovelock
argued, to every planet and every scale of the cosmos. The scientific hypothesis
is a systems-level figure for a genuine regulatory pattern; the canon claim is
that the pattern is a life.

Living Earth has older kin as well. James Hutton, the founder of modern geology,
described the Earth as a kind of superorganism in his *Theory of the Earth* (1788).
Vladimir Vernadsky's *The Biosphere* (1926) treated living matter as a
planetary-scale geological force, and later, with Pierre Teilhard de Chardin,
developed the idea of a "noosphere." Contemporary Earth-system science and the
planetary-boundaries framework of Johan Rockström and colleagues (2009) model the
Earth as one integrated system with its own stability limits. None of these
asserts that the planet is literally alive; each treats integration and
self-regulation as system properties, which is the line the canon crosses and they
do not.

A different neighbour is the revival of **panpsychism** in philosophy of mind
(Galen Strawson, Philip Goff, David Chalmers), which argues that consciousness is a
fundamental and pervasive feature of reality. It is structurally parallel to Living
Earth — both extend a property normally confined to our own scale across the whole
cosmos — but the content differs: panpsychism distributes *mind*, Living Earth
distributes *biological life*. They are complementary readings rather than the same
claim, and neither entails the other.

## Earth-as-living-being across traditions

A living or maternal Earth recurs across a wide range of cultures: the Greek Gaia
of Hesiod's *Theogony* (the name Lovelock borrowed); the Vedic Earth-goddess of the
*Bhūmi Sūkta* (Atharvaveda 12.1), with its plea "may we not injure thy vitals or
thy heart"; the Andean Pachamama; Native American Mother Earth traditions; the
living landscape of the Aboriginal Australian Dreaming; the Norse Jörð; the Chinese
Hou Tu; and numerous African Earth-mother figures. Mainstream folklore and
anthropology read this distribution as some mixture of independent invention — the
human tendency to personify the ground that feeds us — diffusion, and shared
cognitive archetype, and are right to be cautious about deriving one buried fact
from many myths.

The corpus offers a narrower and openly interpretive reading: that the recurrence
is at least consistent with the situation Living Earth describes — cultural memory
of a genuinely living planet surviving in devotional dress. That reading is hedged
deliberately, and it does not flatten the traditions into one — the Dreaming's
ancestral landscape, Pachamama's reciprocal offerings, and the *Bhūmi Sūkta*'s
hymnody are not interchangeable. It also keeps a firm line between them and the
canon claim: these traditions are devotional and animist, addressing the Earth as a
person or a mother, whereas Living Earth advances a biological claim about
organism-status. The overlap is in the intuition, not in the argument.

## Scholarly and critical perspectives

Mainstream biology does not extend organism-status to planets, and the objections
are serious, not merely conventional. Planets do not reproduce, lack cellular
organization and discrete organismal boundaries, and operate on timescales unlike
those of any known organism; on the standard criteria, a planet is not an organism.
The strong-Gaia version of the idea drew sharp criticism from neo-Darwinian
biologists, who argued that natural selection acts on competing individuals and
genes, not on a single planet that has no population to be selected among and no
lineage of rival variants. Earth-system science kept the regulatory insight and
dropped the organism.

The framework's own defence — that Earth's life is imperceptible to us because of
the Mass Effect — is, from a critical standpoint, also its exposure: a claim built
so that no observation could count against it is unfalsifiable in the ordinary
scientific sense. The corpus does not dispute the point so much as reclassify it.
Living Earth is not offered as an experimental result but as a reading of the
Raëlian source — a `framework` claim, explicit in the canon and outside mainstream
science — and its warrant is textual and cosmological, not empirical. Where a
scientist treats "the living Earth" as a vivid figure for a regulatory system, the
corpus treats the figure as the literal fact, and says so plainly.
