**Forerunners** are, in the Wheel of Heaven framework, the functional class of
prophets and visionaries whose missions *prepare the way* — figures who ready a
population for a more central figure still to come rather than delivering the
age-defining message themselves. The model is John the Baptist, whom the Gospels
cast as the herald of Jesus in fulfilment of the old preparation prophecies. The
pattern the framework draws from him runs in both directions: backward, through
the Hebrew prophets who cultivated messianic expectation across the Aries age,
and forward, through the nineteenth- and twentieth-century figures whose cultural
groundwork the corpus reads as preparation for the Aquarian-age contact of 1973.
Within the roughly forty-figure [prophet](../prophet/) lineage the framework
tracks, Forerunners are the preparatory sub-class — not the inaugurators of an
age ([Moses](../moses/), [Jesus](../jesus/), [Raël](../rael/)) but the ones who
make an inauguration receivable.

"Forerunner" is inherited vocabulary, not a term of the Raëlian canon. The source
material does not use it; the corpus adopts it from the biblical-theological
tradition that already had a word for the role and reads the pattern it names
onto the prophetic institution. The category is therefore a synthesis — a way of
grouping figures the canon treats one at a time — and this entry labels it as
such rather than presenting it as a settled doctrine.

## Etymology and naming

The English *forerunner* renders a cluster of older terms. The Greek **πρόδρομος**
(*prodromos*, "one who runs ahead") joins πρό (*pro*, "before") to δρόμος
(*dromos*, "running") and was used in classical Greek for a military scout, a
herald announcing a dignitary, or the "first fruit" that precedes the harvest.
The New Testament applies it once, and to Jesus rather than John (Hebrews 6:20),
but later Christian usage fixes it on John the Baptist, and the Eastern Orthodox
tradition still names him *Prodromos* as a standing liturgical title.

The Hebrew equivalent is **מַלְאָךְ** (*malakh*, "messenger, envoy") — the word
behind Malachi 3:1's *malakhi*, "my messenger," and behind the prophet's own name
(Malachi is literally "my messenger"). The same term runs from human courier to
prophet to the "angelic" messenger of English Bibles. The Latin **praecursor**
("one who runs before") carries the sense into the Vulgate and, through it, into
the Romance and Germanic languages: French *précurseur*, Italian *precursore*,
Spanish *precursor*, German *Vorläufer* (a direct calque, *vor* + *Läufer*),
Russian *predtecha*. The English "forerunner" appears around 1300, applied first
to John and later broadened to any preparatory figure.

One usage should be kept apart. The Latter-day Saint tradition sometimes speaks of
"forerunners" for figures of the pre-mortal existence who prepared conditions for
a later prophetic mission; that is a distinct theological idea within Mormon
cosmology and does not map onto the sense used here, which is the older
prophet-who-prepares-the-way.

## The biblical pattern

The idea has a compact scriptural core. **Malachi 3:1** supplies the governing
image:

> "Behold, I will send my messenger (*malakhi*), and he shall prepare the way
> before me: and Yahweh, whom ye seek, shall suddenly come to his temple."

The verb translated "prepare the way" — from *panah*, to turn or clear away
obstacles — and its sequence (messenger first, the awaited coming second) fix the
shape of the role. **Isaiah 40:3** gives the same figure a setting:

> "The voice of him that crieth in the wilderness, Prepare ye the way of Yahweh,
> make straight in the desert a highway for our God."

A third text names a specific forerunner: **Malachi 4:5–6** promises that Elijah
will be sent "before the coming of the great and dreadful day of Yahweh" — a
prophecy that becomes the seed of an enduring Jewish expectation, preserved
liturgically in the Passover seder's cup for Elijah.

The Gospels braid these strands together and tie them to a single figure. **Mark
1:2–3** opens by combining the Malachi messenger with the Isaiah voice and
applying both to [John the Baptist](../john-the-baptist/); Matthew 3:1–3, Luke
3:1–6, and John 1:6–8, 19–28 repeat the identification. Jesus makes the link to
Elijah explicit — "if ye will receive it, this is Elias, which was for to come"
(Matthew 11:14) — and John caps the pattern with the line that states the
forerunner's whole logic: **"He must increase, but I must decrease"** (John 3:30).
The one who prepares the way yields to the one he announces; his mission is
complete precisely when the central figure's begins.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework reads prophecy not as a category of religious experience but as an
institution: roughly forty human interlocutors whom the [Elohim](../elohim/)
alliance selected across history to maintain contact with Earth, each suited to
the cultural moment at which they appeared (the full account lives in the
[Prophet](../prophet/) entry). Set against that institution, the Forerunner
category names a division of labour within it. Some prophets *inaugurate* an age's
mission — Moses opens the Aries age, Jesus the Piscean, Raël the Aquarian — and
deliver its defining content. Others *prepare the ground* for an inauguration
still to come. Forerunners are the second kind.

On this reading John the Baptist is the clearest case: he did not deliver Jesus's
teaching but cultivated the repentance-and-expectation in first-century Judea into
which that teaching could land. The Hebrew prophets, taken together, are read as
the cumulative forerunner of the Aries-to-Pisces transition — centuries of
messianic anticipation that made a Piscean-age mission legible when it came. And
within the Piscean age the pattern recurs in miniature in the **Bab** (Mirza Ali
Muhammad Shirazi, 1819–1850), who declared himself in 1844 the forerunner of "He
whom God shall make manifest," a figure the [Baha'i](../bahaullah/) tradition
identifies with Baha'u'llah. The Bab is the one place where a forerunner is not an
interpretive label applied afterward but a role the figure claimed for himself.

**[Elijah](../elijah/)** occupies a special position. The corpus reads his
translation "by a whirlwind into heaven" in a chariot of fire (2 Kings 2:11) as a
physical removal to the Elohim home world rather than a death, consistent with the
framework's broader account of prophets preserved there; his promised return
(Malachi 4:5–6) is then read as a functional expectation that John the Baptist
fulfils. Jesus's own statement is taken at the level it states: John is Elijah
"functionally," the figure who does what the Elijah prophecy designated, not a
literal reincarnation of a man the framework holds to be alive elsewhere.

### The Aquarian-age preparation

The reading's distinctive move is to carry the forerunner pattern forward to the
opening of the Aquarian age. The corpus treats the late Piscean centuries as a
long preparation phase for the contact Claude Vorilhon (Raël) reports receiving on
13 December 1973, at the crater of Puy de Lassolas near Clermont-Ferrand in the
Auvergne. On this account several nineteenth-century founder-prophets already
recognised in the forty-prophet list did double duty. **Joseph Smith**
(1805–1844), founder of the Latter-day Saint movement; the **Bab** (1819–1850);
and **Baha'u'llah** (1817–1892) each delivered a mission to his own moment and, in
combination, reopened the cultural possibility of new prophecy after the long
assumption — shared by Judaism, Christianity, and Islam alike — that the age of
prophets had closed. The Baha'i emphasis in particular, on progressive revelation
through a succession of messengers appearing across many traditions, prefigures
the pluriform picture the corpus later states more directly.

Around those figures the framework reads a wider cultural matrix, and here its
voice shifts from the direct register it uses for canon-internal claims to a
deliberately cautious one. The **Theosophical** revival — Helena Blavatsky
(1831–1891) and her successors — popularised the notion of advanced beings
guiding human evolution and of many inhabited worlds, ideas that loosely prefigure
the corpus's cosmology. The mid-century **UFO-contactee** tradition — George
Adamski (1891–1965), George Van Tassel (1910–1978), Howard Menger (1922–2009),
Daniel Fry (1908–1992), and others — supplied the narrative template of a human
who meets extraterrestrial visitors and returns with a message. The corpus does
*not* endorse these as genuine alliance contacts. It reads them honestly as a
mixed record — some possibly-genuine perception alongside a great deal of
elaboration, misinterpretation, and, in places, fabrication — whose value was not
in their content but in the cultural conditioning they performed. By 1973 the
broader matrix of science fiction, the space race, and the 1960s counterculture
had produced a generation for whom a reported contact could register as a
recognisable kind of event rather than an unclassifiable anomaly.

The rhythm the framework proposes is therefore consistent across ages:
a **preparation phase**, in which forerunners cultivate receptive conditions, is
followed by a **disclosure phase**, in which an inaugural prophet delivers the
mission. John did not predict the specifics of Jesus's message; he readied the
ground for it. In the same way, the late-Piscean figures are not read as having
predicted Raël's content but as having prepared the matrix in which it could be
received. The 1973 contact is read as the disclosure that all of this preparation
pointed toward, and the [Embassy](../embassy/) the source material requests as the
mission's intended end — the moment the [Apocalypse](../apocalypse/), in the
framework's technical sense of an unveiling, is meant to make possible.

### What a Forerunner is not

Two distinctions keep the category clean. First, "Forerunner" here means a
prophet-who-prepares-the-way; it does not refer to the pre-Elohim creator
civilisation — the older makers who, in the framework, preceded the Elohim
themselves. That is a genuinely distinct subject, and it belongs to the
[Cosmic Chain](../cosmic-chain/) entry, not under this heading. Second, the
category is the project's own synthesis. Because the canon does not use the word,
the cross-age Forerunner pattern — and especially its extension to the Aquarian
preparation — is offered as interpretation rather than as a claim resting on any
single source. The canon-internal pieces it rests on (the prophet institution, the
selection of the named figures) can be stated directly; the pattern assembled from
them is labelled speculative on purpose.

## Comparative and critical perspectives

The framework's reading sits alongside a substantial body of scholarship rather
than in place of it.

- **The historical John the Baptist.** Mainstream New Testament scholarship — John
  P. Meier's *A Marginal Jew* (vol. 2), Joan Taylor's *The Immerser*, Robert
  Webb's *John the Baptizer and Prophet* — treats John as a historical figure of
  Second Temple Judaism and reads the Malachi-and-Elijah identifications as the
  Gospel writers' typological framing rather than as literal fulfilment. The
  framework does not dispute the historical reconstruction; it differs on what the
  preparation motif ultimately records.
- **Jewish and Christian reception.** Jewish tradition preserves the Elijah-return
  expectation in the Talmud and in the Passover cup of Elijah; the Christian
  tradition, and the Eastern Orthodox liturgy in particular, keeps *Prodromos* as
  John's proper title. Both are registered here in their own terms.
- **Islam.** The Quran honours Yahya (John the Baptist) as a prophet in his own
  right (suras 3, 6, 19, 21) rather than as a herald subordinate to Jesus — a real
  difference in emphasis the framework preserves rather than flattens.
- **Baha'i.** The Bab's self-designation as the forerunner of Baha'u'llah is the
  one living, explicit forerunner doctrine among the traditions surveyed, developed
  across Baha'u'llah's own writings and in Shoghi Effendi's *God Passes By*. The
  corpus reads the Bab and Baha'u'llah as genuine late-Piscean prophets, so it
  engages this material as broadly congruent while noting that Baha'i theology
  elevates its Manifestations in a way the framework's biological-prophet reading
  does not.
- **UFO-contactee scholarship.** Academic study of the contactees — Jacques
  Vallée's *Passport to Magonia*, Christopher Partridge's edited *UFO Religions*,
  George Chryssides's reference work on new religious movements — treats them as a
  sociological and folkloric phenomenon. The framework draws on this literature
  descriptively, to characterise the cultural matrix, without adopting the
  contactee narratives as evidence.
- **Cross-cultural preparation figures.** Other traditions carry structurally
  similar figures: the Buddhist **Maitreya**, the buddha-yet-to-come; the
  Zoroastrian **Saoshyant**, a future saviour; various heralds and
  dynasty-announcing figures across cultures. The framework reads the recurrence as
  a possible convergence — several traditions preserving the memory of a real
  preparation practice — while mainstream comparative religion accounts for it
  through common narrative structure, cultural diffusion, and independent
  invention. That comparative claim is the most speculative part of the reading and
  is presented as such.

The honest summary is that mainstream scholarship reads the whole forerunner motif
as a literary and theological pattern — a way traditions frame a central figure by
giving him a herald — while the framework reads it as the trace of an actual
preparation practice within the prophetic institution. The two readings share the
same texts and diverge on what those texts finally record; the Wheel of Heaven
position is candid that its cross-age synthesis extends beyond what any single
source states.
