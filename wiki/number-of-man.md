**The Number of Man** is the value **666** — written in the Greek of Revelation
13:18 as χξϛ — that the seer calls "the number of the beast." In the Wheel of
Heaven framework, following Claude Vorilhon (Raël)'s reading in *Let Us Welcome
the Extra-Terrestrials* (1979), it is taken not as a cipher hiding a name but as
a literal count: the number of human generations that will have lived on Earth
between the creation of the first finalized humans in the [Elohim](../elohim/)'s
laboratories and the moment humanity first turned nuclear energy to destruction.
At roughly twenty years to a generation, 666 generations span some 13,320 years,
and counting back from the atomic threshold of 1945 places that first creation
near the Virgo–Leo precessional boundary, around 11,375 BCE — the date from
which the framework's whole [World Age](../world-age/) chronology descends.

This reading runs against the grain of mainstream scholarship, which treats the
same verse as a puzzle to be decoded and answers it with a name — Nero Caesar.
The two readings are not, on the framework's account, simple rivals; they
address the verse at different levels, and much of the interest of the entry
lies in keeping them apart. What follows sets out the verse and the gematria
tradition that gathered around it, then the framework's generation-count
reading, and finally the comparative and critical landscape in which both sit.

## Revelation 13:18 and the gematria tradition

The verse reads, in the King James rendering: *"Here is wisdom. Let him that
hath understanding count the number of the beast: for it is the number of a man;
and his number is Six hundred threescore and six."* The Greek phrase behind "the
number of a man" is *ἀριθμὸς γὰρ ἀνθρώπου ἐστίν* (*arithmos gar anthrōpou
estin*) — and the number itself is given not in words but as three letters used
as numerals: **χξϛ**, that is chi (600) + xi (60) + stigma (6). Stigma is an
archaic Greek letter kept in the numeral system after it had dropped out of the
alphabet proper; its presence marks χξϛ as a figure, not a word.

Writing a number as letters invited the ancient reader to run the operation in
reverse. In both the Greek **isopsephy** tradition (from *isopsēphia*,
"equal-pebble") and its Hebrew cousin **gematria**, every letter carries a fixed
numerical value, so any name can be summed to a number and any number
back-matched to candidate names. Revelation 13:18 all but instructs the reader
to do exactly this — "count the number" — and interpreters have obliged for two
thousand years.

The dominant modern solution is **Nero Caesar**. Written in Hebrew letters as
נרון קסר (*Nrwn Qsr*), the emperor's name sums to nun (50) + resh (200) + vav
(6) + nun (50) + qof (100) + samekh (60) + resh (200) = **666**. The solution
has the rare merit of also explaining the text's chief variant: a minority of
manuscripts — most importantly **Papyrus 115**, among the earliest witnesses to
Revelation — read **616** rather than 666, and 616 is what the same name yields
when spelled in its Latin form without the final *nun* (*Nrw Qsr*). The
second-century bishop **Irenaeus of Lyon** already weighed the number in *Against
Heresies* (c. 180 CE), canvassing candidate names and noting the 616 reading only
to reject it as a copyist's slip; the specifically Neronian identification
hardened into scholarly consensus much later, in the nineteenth and twentieth
centuries.

The number has drawn attention on purely mathematical grounds as well, which is
part of why it resists being left alone. It is the **36th triangular number** —
the sum of every integer from 1 to 36, since 36 × 37 ÷ 2 = 666. It is the sum of
the squares of the first seven primes (2² + 3² + 5² + 7² + 11² + 13² + 17² =
666). It is a **harshad number**, divisible by its own digit-sum (6 + 6 + 6 =
18, and 666 ÷ 18 = 37), and it is both a repdigit and a palindrome. None of this
bears on the framework's reading, which turns on counting rather than on
number theory; but the accumulated numerical coincidence helps explain the
cultural charge the figure has carried.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework reads 666 through the same direct-reading lens it brings to the
rest of the apocalyptic material: the verse means what it says, and what it says
is a number of humans. The articulation is Vorilhon's, in the section of *Let Us
Welcome the Extra-Terrestrials* titled "The Apocalypse of the Final Nuclear
Cataclysm," where [Yahweh](../yahweh/) glosses the passage directly:

> *"This final test of Humanity, that is, the choice between self-destruction or
> the passage into the Golden Age, is the last opportunity for Satan to prove
> that he was right in saying that humanity was bad. [...] The 'beast', as
> described further on in the text, is simply the use of nuclear energy for
> murderous means: [...] In fact, 666 is the number of human generations which
> will have existed on Earth since the creation of the first men in the original
> laboratories. The very first humans were created some 13,000 years ago, and a
> human generation is estimated at an average of 20 years, so if one multiplies
> 666 by 20, it equals 13,320 years. The generation born at the beginning of the
> Age of Apocalypse in 1945 of the Christian era, was in fact the 666th since
> the creation of the first man in a laboratory by the Elohim. [...] Once again,
> interpretation was not required in order to understand. One had only to read
> what had been written."*

**The beast as nuclear weaponry.** On this reading the *thērion* — the "beast"
the number belongs to — is not a person to be unmasked but a technology: nuclear
energy turned to killing, the capability humanity acquired at Alamogordo,
Hiroshima, and Nagasaki in 1945. The "last opportunity for Satan" is exact
canon: [Satan](../satan/) is the framework's name for the leader of the
home-world abolitionist faction that argued from the beginning that humanity was
a bad creation and should be destroyed. A species that can now destroy itself is
Satan's case made concrete — and its choice not to is the case refuted. (This is
a separate figure from [Lucifer](../lucifer/), the exiled pro-human faction of
creators who stayed with their creation; the two are routinely conflated in the
inherited tradition and kept firmly apart here.)

**666 as a census of generations.** The count runs from the finalized creation
of humanity to that threshold. Taking the source's working figure of twenty
years to a generation, 666 generations come to 13,320 years; subtracting that
span from 1945 CE places the creation at about 11,375 BCE — near the boundary
between the Age of Virgo and the [Age of Leo](../age-of-leo/) on the corpus's
precessional reckoning. The generation born as the atomic age opened, in 1945,
is on this scheme the 666th, and its arrival coincides precisely with the first
destructive use of nuclear energy. (Raël himself, born on 30 September 1946, is
read as the first of that generation to receive the alliance's contact.)

**The finalized-humans refinement.** A later corpus reading, in the
*timeline.epub* material, sharpens what the 11,375 BCE date marks, against a
natural objection from the fossil record:

> *"The 13,320-year figure is the date of the *finalized* humans — the
> production of *Homo sapiens sapiens*, the design endpoint, the version of
> humanity the seven teams agreed was satisfactory. It is not the date of the
> first hominid prototype. The earlier hominid forms in the fossil record
> represent the prior iterations, distributed across the longer span of the
> active design phase."*

So the date does not deny the earlier hominids — *Homo erectus*,
*heidelbergensis*, the Neanderthals, archaic *sapiens* — that paleoanthropology
places across hundreds of thousands of years. It reads them as earlier drafts of
a long design programme and reserves 11,375 BCE for the moment the design was
finalized and the current form produced in parallel by the seven creator teams.

**The generation length is a working figure, not a fixed one.** The corpus is
candid that twenty years is an estimate the source supplies rather than an
independently verified constant; historical generation lengths have varied, and
demographic studies often put the average nearer 25 to 30 years. A plausible
range of eighteen to twenty-five years shifts the creation date by several
centuries either way:

| Generation length | 666 × generations | Creation date | Precessional age |
|---|---|---|---|
| 18 years | 11,988 years | 10,043 BCE | mid Age of Leo |
| 20 years | 13,320 years | 11,375 BCE | Virgo–Leo boundary |
| 22 years | 14,652 years | 12,707 BCE | mid Age of Virgo |
| 25 years | 16,650 years | 14,705 BCE | early Age of Virgo |

The defensible claim is therefore that human finalization occurred *near* the
Virgo–Leo boundary, with the exact year sensitive to the figure chosen — not
that 11,375 BCE is a precise calendar date.

**The precessional geometry.** The 13,320-year span carries a structural
resonance the framework reads as more than accident. Divided by the 2,160-year
[Great Month](../great-month/) — one-twelfth of the 25,920-year Great Year, the
full precessional cycle — it comes to about 6.166 Great Months, close to half a
complete [Great Year](../great-year/) (six Great Months, or 12,960 years,
exactly). The creation-to-Apocalypse trajectory thus runs across roughly half
the precessional wheel, opening near the Virgo–Leo boundary and closing at the
Pisces–Aquarius boundary of the mid-twentieth century — two points nearly
opposite each other on the circle. The framework reads the remaining half-cycle
as humanity's own turn on the [Cosmic Chain](../cosmic-chain/), the passage from
being a creation to becoming a creator. The 360-year excess over the exact half
(13,320 − 12,960) is left as an open question rather than smoothed away: a
slightly shorter generation would erase it, the rounded Great Year is not the
precise astronomical value, and 360 has its own numerical weight (the degrees of
a circle, the days of several ancient calendar years). The corpus holds the
13,320-year figure as canonical while flagging the 360-year remainder honestly.

**The paired number, 144,000.** Revelation supplies a second figure the
framework reads alongside 666: the 144,000 of Revelation 7:4–8 and 14:1, read as
the number of individuals to be reconstituted on the
[Planet of the Eternals](../planet-of-the-eternals/) should the cataclysm come —
"a sufficient number to start a new generation on Earth" once it is habitable
again. Where 666 counts the generations up to the threshold, 144,000 (structured
as 12 × 12 × 1,000) counts the individuals held in reserve past it; together they
frame the dual-path stakes of the [Apocalypse](../apocalypse/) — the
[Golden Age](../golden-age/) on one side, self-destruction on the other.

Read this way, the Number of Man is less a curiosity than a load-bearing part of
the chronology. It fixes the Age of Leo creation date, dates the close of the
old age and the opening of the [Age of Aquarius](../age-of-aquarius/) to the
1945 threshold, and preserves in a single much-read verse a quantitative claim
the framework can check against its own precessional record.

## Comparative and scholarly perspectives

The framework's reading is a minority one, and it does not pretend otherwise.
The mainstream consensus reads Revelation 13:18 as first-century political
cryptography: 666 encodes Nero, the emperor whose persecution shadowed the early
church, and the verse is a warning legible to insiders and opaque to Roman
censors. That reading is developed with care across the standard commentaries —
the New Testament scholars Adela Yarbro Collins, Richard Bauckham, G. K. Beale,
Craig Koester, and David Aune all defend some form of it — and the 616 variant,
far from unsettling it, is usually taken to confirm it, since both figures
resolve to the same name under different spellings. The corpus does not require
this reading to be wrong. It treats the Neronian solution as a sound
historical-contextual reading of what John may have encoded for his own
moment, and reads its own generation-count as a second, deeper register that the
Roman-political reading does not reach. The honest point is that the framework's
reading rests on the authority of the Raëlian source, not on scholarly
consensus, and the two should not be confused.

The verse's later history is a caution in its own right. Once a number can be
matched to a name, almost any adversary can be fitted to it. The Reformation
turned 666 against the papacy — Martin Luther and a long Protestant line read
the beast as Rome — and the modern centuries have in turn named Napoleon, an
assortment of monarchs, Hitler, Soviet leaders, and a rotating cast of sitting
politicians. The pattern of each generation discovering its own villain in the
figure is precisely why mainstream scholars are wary of confident name-matching,
and the framework shares that wariness: its reading deliberately does not name a
person at all.

The verse also sits inside a much wider human habit of reading meaning in
numbers. Alphabetic-numeral systems — Hebrew gematria, Greek isopsephy, and the
Sanskrit *kaṭapayādi* among them — let letters and numbers trade places;
non-alphabetic traditions build their symbolism differently, from the Chinese
five phases and sexagenary cycle to the Mesoamerican Long Count and its 260-day
sacred calendar. Mainstream comparative religion and cognitive science generally
explain this near-universal recurrence through a shared human facility with
number, cultural diffusion, and independent elaboration, and the framework does
not dispute that those factors are real and largely sufficient. What it adds is
narrower and specific to this case: that behind the number 666 lies not only a
cognitive habit but an actual generational count, supplied by the alliance and
recoverable once the chronology is in hand.

Finally, the framework keeps its distance from the conspiracy-theoretic material
that has attached itself to 666 and to the paired
[Mark of the Beast](../mark-of-the-beast/) — the successive identifications with
barcodes, credit cards, RFID chips, vaccines, and digital-identity schemes. The
corpus does not endorse those readings. It notes only that the Revelation
imagery describes an identity-and-commerce infrastructure whose partial
contemporary realization raises a real moral question about tracking and human
freedom, and it engages that question at the civilizational level rather than
the conspiratorial one.
