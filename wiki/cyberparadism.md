**Cyberparadism** is a contemporary aesthetic-cultural movement that pictures a
future in which advanced technology and the natural world are held together
rather than set against one another, turning scarcity into abundance. It was
first formalised on the Aesthetics Wiki{{ footnote(id="1") }} and then set out
systematically in the *Cyberparadism Manifesto* (2023){{ cite(id="cyberparadism-manifesto", text="[1]", title="Cyberparadism Manifesto, zarazinsfuss, 2023") }},
which organises the movement around **eight Laws** — Abundance, Equity, Freedom,
Harmony, Preservation, Recognition, Responsibility, and Simplicity — and **six
Signs**: fusion energy, anti-gravity, synthetic life engineering, terraforming,
full automation, and open-source collaboration.

In the Wheel of Heaven framework, Cyberparadism is read as the aesthetic-cultural
counterpart of [Paradism](../paradism/), the Raëlian doctrine of a post-scarcity,
fully automated, moneyless society modelled on the home world of the
[Elohim](../elohim/). Where Paradism states that content as political economy —
machines perform the necessary work, money falls away, and people are freed to
think and create — Cyberparadism gives it a face: a visual language and a cultural
mood rather than a programme. The two run close enough that the corpus treats the
aesthetic as cultural reception of what the doctrine describes, a picture arrived
at from the culture's side without the framework's commitments.

The digital and AI turn of the early twenty-first century is where the two meet
most sharply. Paradism's mechanism — automation carried to the point where
production stops being the binding constraint — is now being supplied by
industrial robotics and artificial intelligence, the very technologies
Cyberparadist imagery foregrounds. In that sense Cyberparadism is the picture of
Paradism realised through computing and robotics: post-scarcity abundance rendered
as an aesthetic of the machine age it depends on.

## The manifesto and its principles

The Aesthetics Wiki (aesthetics.fandom.com) is a community-edited catalogue of
internet-native aesthetics, and its entry was where Cyberparadism first took
shape{{ cite(id="aesthetics-wiki-cyberparadism", text="[2]", title="Aesthetics Wiki entry on Cyberparadism — the origin venue") }}.
The *Cyberparadism Manifesto* extended that entry into a systematic statement. It
opens with an Alan Kay epigraph — *"The best way to predict the future is to
invent it"* — and is explicit about its register: *"This is not a political or
economical manifesto. It serves the purpose to declare the aesthetical principles
of Cyberparadism and its derivates."* The distinction matters for placing the
movement beside Paradism: Cyberparadism is a picture and an orientation, not a
policy.

Its central claim is compact: *"Cyberparadism is an aesthetic that celebrates the
positive potential of technology and scientific progress to create a better future
for humanity... a future where technology and nature coexist in harmony, where
abundance and prosperity are available to all, and where the human experience is
enhanced by the responsible use of technology."*

The eight Laws divide that claim into complementary commitments. Abundance sets
the movement against scarcity economics; Equity guarantees a universal baseline
while Recognition preserves reward for individual contribution; Freedom defends
thought, privacy, and autonomy against surveillance and coercion; Harmony and
Preservation carry the signature demand that technology be integrated with the
living world rather than imposed on it; Responsibility weighs the ethical cost of
great technological power; and Simplicity treats minimalism as a design principle
rather than a style. The Manifesto insists the Laws are *"not hierarchical in
nature, but rather complementary facets of a whole"*{{ footnote(id="2") }}. The
six Signs then name the concrete technologies through which the Laws would be
realised — fusion energy as the "holy grail of clean and abundant energy,"
anti-gravity for sustainable spaceflight, synthetic life engineering for
agriculture and ecological restoration, terraforming for habitability beyond
Earth, full automation to remove menial labour, and open-source collaboration as
the movement's social principle.

The Manifesto notes its own resemblance to Marc Andreessen's *Techno-Optimist
Manifesto* (16 October 2023){{ cite(id="the-techno-optimist-manifesto", text="[3]", title="Andreessen, The Techno-Optimist Manifesto, 2023") }},
which appeared within months of it — *"very reminiscent of the Cyberparadism
Manifesto. Go check it out."* Both belong to an early-2020s turn toward explicit
techno-optimism against the preceding decade's techno-pessimism{{ footnote(id="3") }}.

## Etymology and naming

The name joins *cyber-* to *paradise*. The prefix descends from the Greek
κυβερνήτης (*kybernētēs*, "steersman"), and entered modern use through Norbert
Wiener's *Cybernetics* (1948){{ cite(id="wiener-cybernetics", text="[4]", title="Wiener, Cybernetics, 1948") }};
from there it spread across the vocabulary of computing — *cyberspace*, coined in
William Gibson's *Neuromancer* (1984){{ cite(id="gibson-neuromancer", text="[5]", title="Gibson, Neuromancer, 1984 — the cyberspace coinage") }},
then *cyborg*, *cybernetic*, *cyberpunk*. *Paradise* runs the other way, from Old
Persian *paridaida* ("walled enclosure") through Greek παράδεισος (*paradeisos*),
the word the Septuagint used to render the garden of [Eden](../eden/) at
{% libref(book="genesis-woh", chapter=2, verse=8) %}Genesis 2:8{% end %}{{ footnote(id="4") }}.
The compound therefore reads as "cybernetic paradise" — the oldest word for the
garden fitted to the machine age. The Manifesto records alternative names —
*Cyber Paradise*, *Cybergenesis*, *Neogenesis* — that stress the spatial,
originary, and renewal senses in turn.

## Aesthetic and lineage

Cyberparadist imagery has a recognisable surface. Architecture reads as an
extension of its setting rather than an imposition on it, favouring domes,
spheres, and hexagons that double as engineering and as natural pattern; palettes
run to aerospace orange, teal and aquamarine, bone white, and deep green; settings
range from forest-embedded settlements and reclaimed coastlines to terraforming
frontiers and orbital habitats. The look draws on a line of real design
traditions — Buckminster Fuller's geodesics and "Spaceship Earth"{{ cite(id="fuller-spaceship-earth", text="[6]", title="Fuller, Operating Manual for Spaceship Earth, 1969") }},
Frei Otto's tensile structures{{ cite(id="otto-hangende-dach", text="[7]", title="Otto, Das Hängende Dach, 1954") }},
Paolo Soleri's arcologies{{ cite(id="soleri-arcology", text="[8]", title="Soleri, Arcology: The City in the Image of Man, 1969") }},
and the biomimetic tradition after Janine Benyus{{ cite(id="benyus-biomimicry", text="[9]", title="Benyus, Biomimicry, 1997") }} — and prizes serenity
and collective purpose over the agitation and scarcity of its dystopian opposite.

That opposite is cyberpunk, the movement's declared foil{{ footnote(id="5") }}:
Cyberparadism keeps the *cyber-* prefix and inverts the content, replacing
corporate dystopia with an integrated abundance. Between the two sit its nearer
relatives. **Cyberprep** supplies the clean, optimistic counter-current to
cyberpunk. **Post-Cyberpunk**, in Neal Stephenson's *The Diamond Age*
(1995){{ cite(id="stephenson-diamond-age", text="[12]", title="Stephenson, The Diamond Age, 1995") }} and its kin, supplies a mature
attention to what advanced technology does to a society without collapsing into
utopia or dystopia. And **Solarpunk** — the closest cousin, carried by anthologies
such as *Solarpunk* (2018){{ cite(id="solarpunk-anthology-2018", text="[23]", title="Solarpunk, World Weaver Press, 2018") }},
*Sunvault* (2017){{ cite(id="sunvault-anthology", text="[24]", title="Sunvault: Stories of Solarpunk and Eco-Speculation, 2017") }}, and
*Multispecies Cities* (2021){{ cite(id="multispecies-cities-anthology", text="[25]", title="Multispecies Cities: Solarpunk Urban Futures, 2021") }} — supplies the
ecological commitment and the vocabulary of integrated nature and technology.
Cyberparadism differs from Solarpunk chiefly in scale and technological register:
where Solarpunk tends local and low-tech, Cyberparadism embraces fusion,
automation, and a cosmic frontier.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework reads Cyberparadism not as speculation about an unbuilt future but
as a memory and an anticipation of something the corpus holds to be real. Three of
its own claims are Cyberparadist in character.

[Eden](../eden/) is the first of them. On the corpus's reading Eden was not a
garden of myth but the laboratory in which the earliest humans lived in
provided-for ease, in harmony with engineered biology and the technology of their
makers — a historical Cyberparadism, complete in miniature. The
[Elohim](../elohim/) who ran it — and in particular the exiled
[Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction, the creators who took humanity's side and were
condemned to remain among them — built the condition the aesthetic now paints as
future. The [Planet of the Eternals](../planet-of-the-eternals/) — the Elohim home
world as [Raël](../rael/) describes it in his contact account{{ cite(id="les-extra-terrestres-m-ont-emmen-sur-leur-plan-te", text="[26]", title="Vorilhon, Les Extra-Terrestres m'ont emmené sur leur planète, 1975") }} — is
the second: a post-scarcity civilisation of integrated biology and technology,
great longevity, and cooperative order, the mature form of every Cyberparadist
principle at planetary scale. The **Aquarian-age threshold** is the third: the
point at which humanity is expected to inherit that technology and cross from
picturing the condition to living it.

Placed in the Raëlian programme, this is why Cyberparadism belongs beside
Paradism. Paradism is one part of the [New Commandments](../new-commandments/), the
Aquarian-age programme that also carries [Geniocracy](../geniocracy/) for
governance and [Humanitarianism](../humanitarianism/) for property; together they
describe the [Golden Age](../golden-age/) the framework expects the age to reach.
Cyberparadism is not itself a commandment. It is the aesthetic in which a culture
with no knowledge of the canon nonetheless recognises the same destination — which
the corpus reads as an instance of the [Doubled Signature](../doubled-signature/),
the pattern by which Aquarian-age culture recovers a memory it cannot
source{{ footnote(id="6") }}. On that reading the Elohim civilisation is itself
Cyberparadist, and contemporary Cyberparadism its distant cultural echo.

## Comparative currents

Cyberparadism's core — automation as liberation, the end of scarcity, technology
reconciled with nature — recurs widely in contemporary thought, and the
resemblances are close enough to name, even though each secular version grounds the
vision differently from the framework and from one another.

The nearest political sibling is **Fully Automated Luxury Communism** (Aaron
Bastani, 2019), which likewise treats extreme automation as the infrastructure of
an abundant society; the discourse of **digital abundance** and universal basic
income converges partway, keeping money and market where Cyberparadism's fuller
vision lets them fall away. **Techno-optimism** and its **accelerationist**
currents — Andreessen's manifesto, the e/acc movement, the abundance writing of
Diamandis and Kotler — share the technological confidence while pressing it toward
growth as such rather than toward ecological integration, which is where
Cyberparadism parts from them. In fiction the vision is older and often fuller: the
moneyless *Star Trek* Federation{{ cite(id="star-trek-tng", text="[14]", title="Star Trek: The Next Generation, 1987–1994") }},
Iain M. Banks's post-scarcity Culture{{ cite(id="banks-culture-series", text="[10]", title="Banks, The Culture series, 1987–2012") }}, and Ursula K. Le
Guin's cooperative Anarres{{ cite(id="le-guin-dispossessed", text="[11]", title="Le Guin, The Dispossessed, 1974") }} each render a mature version of
it, as Thomas More's *Utopia* (1516){{ cite(id="mores-utopia", text="[13]", title="More, Utopia, 1516") }} does at the head of the whole tradition.
Hayao Miyazaki's films — *Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind*{{ cite(id="miyazaki-nausicaa", text="[15]", title="Miyazaki, Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind, 1984") }},
*Castle in the Sky*{{ cite(id="miyazaki-castle-in-the-sky", text="[16]", title="Miyazaki, Castle in the Sky, 1986") }},
*Princess Mononoke*{{ cite(id="miyazaki-princess-mononoke", text="[17]", title="Miyazaki, Princess Mononoke, 1997") }} — and Pixar's
*WALL-E*{{ cite(id="wall-e", text="[18]", title="WALL-E, Pixar, 2008") }} and Brad Bird's
*Tomorrowland*{{ cite(id="tomorrowland-film", text="[19]", title="Tomorrowland, 2015") }} carry the same reconciliation of technology
and restored nature into popular film. The framework reads these as convergent
recognitions of one possibility, arrived at independently; its own distinctive
claim is not the abundance, which it shares widely, but the account of where the
memory of it comes from.

## Critical perspectives

The objections that attach to post-scarcity optimism generally attach to
Cyberparadism too, and it is fairer to state them than to pass over them. The Signs
it rests on are uneven: fusion, anti-gravity, and universal molecular manufacturing
remain hypotheses rather than achieved technologies, and a picture is not a
demonstration that they will arrive. As an aesthetic, Cyberparadism is also
detachable from its own commitments — its surface can be worn without its
substance. Two films make the point against it. *Elysium* (2013){{ cite(id="elysium-film", text="[21]", title="Elysium, dir. Neill Blomkamp, 2013") }} builds a
luxurious orbital paradise for an enclave while the planet below is left in
scarcity, a Cyberparadist look laid over a plain violation of the Law of Equity;
*Jurassic World* (2015){{ cite(id="jurassic-world", text="[20]", title="Jurassic World, dir. Colin Trevorrow, 2015") }} and, in a quieter key,
*Oblivion* (2013){{ cite(id="oblivion-film", text="[22]", title="Oblivion, dir. Joseph Kosinski, 2013") }} attach the harmonious-technology
surface to stories of breakdown and concealment. Read from within the movement,
these are cautionary cases — evidence that the aesthetic without the Laws produces
the dystopia it means to answer.

The framework's own reading carries a further, sharper objection. To treat the fit
between a contemporary aesthetic and the corpus's content as evidence for the
corpus — the Doubled Signature move — risks reading confirmation into any
optimistic culture whatever, since a hopeful age will always produce hopeful art.
The corpus presents the alignment as a framework claim, explicit in its own terms
and not endorsed by mainstream scholarship, rather than as proof; on that footing
Cyberparadism is best understood as a cultural symptom the framework interprets,
not an independent witness that settles anything.
