**Humanitarianism** is, in the Wheel of Heaven framework, the Raëlian doctrine
of property and inheritance — the second of the seven
[New Commandments](../new-commandments/) that [Yahweh](../yahweh/) delivered to
[Claude Vorilhon (Raël)](../rael/) during the December 1973 contact at the
Puy-de-Lassolas crater and that Vorilhon recorded in
[*The Book Which Tells the Truth*](../../library/the-book-which-tells-the-truth/)
(1974). Its proposal is stark: outright ownership is abolished in favour of
forty-nine-year rental tenures, inheritance ends at the family home, and every
person begins adult life on roughly equal financial footing, free thereafter to
be rewarded for their own work rather than their parents'. The forty-nine-year
term is not arbitrary — it is drawn directly from the Jubilee of Leviticus 25,
whose cycle of seven sevens the source names as scriptural precedent.

The doctrine is offered as a third way. It rejects Capitalism, which lets
fortunes compound across generations, and Communism, which the source judges to
have failed for want of any incentive to excel; against both it keeps the
individual reward of the first while ending the intergenerational accumulation of
the second. To keep it distinct from the generic ethic of humanitarian aid, the
corpus writes the doctrine with a capital H: lowercase *humanitarianism* is the
broad commitment to human welfare associated with the Red Cross and the wider
relief tradition, while capital-H **Humanitarianism** is this specific Raëlian
programme for how a planetary economy should hold property. This entry sets out
the doctrine and its proof-texts, its place among the New Commandments, its
parallels in the modern property-reform traditions, and the objections it draws.

## The doctrine

The argument opens on a diagnosis of the two dominant systems of the twentieth
century. "Your world is paralyzed by profit," the source has Yahweh say, "and
Communism fails to provide a carrot big enough to motivate people and encourage
them to make progress." Capitalism is faulted for what it concentrates,
Communism for what it fails to motivate; between the two, the doctrine sets out
to keep merit and abolish inheritance.

Its foundation is equality at the starting line: "You are all born equal; this is
also written in the Bible. Your governments should ensure that people are born
with approximately the same level of financial means." Unequal starting
positions, on this reading, hide genuine merit behind inherited advantage — and
the injustice runs both ways. The source presses the point sharply:

> *"It is unacceptable that children of low intelligence should live in luxury
> thanks to the fortunes amassed by their fathers, while geniuses die of hunger
> and do any menial chore just to eat. This way they forsake occupations where
> they could have made discoveries benefiting the whole of humanity."*

The remedy is to replace ownership with tenancy. "This world is not yours," the
passage continues; "you are only tenants. Thus all goods should be rented for
forty-nine years. This will eliminate the injustice of inheritance." Under the
scheme no one owns land, buildings, businesses, or capital outright; all of it is
held on a forty-nine-year lease from the community and reverts when the term
ends. The naming comes at the close: "This political orientation of humanity is
not Communism; its preoccupation is the future of humanity. If you want to give
it a name, call it 'Humanitarianism'."

The source works the mechanism through concrete examples. A young worker of
twenty-one who wants a home does not buy it but rents it for forty-nine years
from the State that built it — paying, say, a hundred thousand francs in monthly
instalments — so that by seventy he has paid in full and lives there rent-free
until death, after which the house returns to the State, which must then let his
children use it freely. Enterprises follow the same rule: "If someone creates a
business, it is theirs for their entire life, and they can rent it out, but never
for more than forty-nine years," and the same for farmland, which passes back to
the State to be re-let. Merit is preserved throughout — the harder worker rents a
larger house, or two, or builds one to lease out and draws the income — but at
death everything returns to the community except the one standing exception, the
family home: "Inheritance must be completely abolished, except for the family
house. This does not, however, prevent each person from being rewarded
individually for their merits."

The whole design turns on the forty-nine-year figure, which the source ties
explicitly to Leviticus 25 — the passage naming the sabbatical cycle of "seven
times seven years" that yields "forty and nine years," the instruction to price
land by the years remaining to the Jubilee, and the principle that "the land
shall not be sold for ever: for the land is mine; for ye are strangers and
sojourners with me." On the canon's reading, Humanitarianism is less an invention
than a recovery: the Jubilee was given to the Hebrew tradition by the
[Elohim](../elohim/), forgotten over the centuries, and now proposed again for
the incoming age at planetary scale.

## Etymology and naming

The English *humanitarian* descends from the Latin *humanitas* — "human nature,
kindness, refinement," the ideal Cicero cultivated — by way of *humanus* and
*homo*; the *-ism* marks it as a doctrine rather than a disposition. The word
entered wide English use only in the nineteenth century, at first in theological
argument and then as the general name for a commitment to human welfare. The
Raëlian coinage borrows that word and narrows it: capital-H Humanitarianism is a
programme for property and inheritance, not a synonym for charity.

A near-coincidence is worth noting. The Polish oculist L. L. Zamenhof, creator of
Esperanto, gave the name *Homaranismo* — "Humanitarianism" in Esperanto — to an
ethical doctrine of universal human community set out in his *Deklaracio pri
Homaranismo* (1906). Zamenhof's doctrine is ethical and cultural where the
Raëlian one is socio-economic; the shared translation is a parallel of
vocabulary, not of content, and the two stand independently of one another.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

Read in the canon's own voice, Humanitarianism is not Vorilhon's private
economics but the Elohim's stated intention for the age. Because the Raëlian
material is presented as Yahweh's first-person account, the corpus treats the
forty-nine-year scheme as a designer's instruction rather than a human proposal —
the property-and-inheritance dimension of the single Aquarian-age programme the
New Commandments lay out.

That programme is meant to be read whole. [Geniocracy](../geniocracy/) supplies
its governance, [World Government](../world-government/) its coordination,
[Demilitarization](../demilitarization/) its peace,
[Science as Religion](../science-as-religion/) its cultural reorientation,
[Telepathic Cultivation](../telepathy/) and the
[Metaphysical Clarifications](../metaphysical-clarifications/) its cognitive and
doctrinal ground; Humanitarianism supplies its property law. The commandments
presuppose one another, and Humanitarianism in particular leans on two of them.
It leans on Geniocracy, because a reform this long-range needs leaders able to
grasp its logic — the source says so in the same breath: "If geniuses are
admitted to power, they will understand the usefulness of these reforms." And it
leans on World Government, because a forty-nine-year tenure system cannot hold in
one country while capital flees to another: "You must also see to it that all the
nations of the Earth unite to form only one government." The doctrine, in short,
is not implementable piecemeal; it presumes the rest of the programme around it.

It is also a direction rather than a deadline. The corpus is explicit that the
New Commandments describe where the [Age of Aquarius](../timeline/age-of-aquarius/)
— roughly the span from the mid-twentieth century to the fifth millennium on its
chronology — is to travel across centuries, not a change to be legislated
tomorrow:

> *"These seven commandments — geniocracy, humanitarianism, world government,
> demilitarization, science as religion, telepathic cultivation, and the
> metaphysical clarifications — constitute the specific political and religious
> program the alliance has proposed for the Aquarian age. Their full
> implementation is a long-term project, extending across centuries rather than
> years."*

Two neighbouring doctrines sharpen where Humanitarianism sits.
[Paradism](../paradism/) — the Raëlian vision of a fully automated, moneyless
society — describes a further horizon in which money and labour have themselves
become unnecessary; Humanitarianism is read as the nearer-term transition that
still runs on money and work, Paradism as the destination beyond it. The two are
stages, not rivals. And the digital-abundance movement
[Cyberparadism](../cyberparadism/) shares Humanitarianism's commitments to equity
and shared access, pursuing the same end through different means.

## Comparative and adjacent traditions

Humanitarianism's central moves — common ownership of land and capital, finite
tenure in place of freehold, the abolition of inheritance — recur across a long
line of thought, and the corpus reads the doctrine alongside these parallels
rather than as their equal. The resemblances are close enough to be worth naming;
the differences are real, and worth keeping.

The scriptural anchor comes first, and is treated as more than parallel. The
Hebrew [Jubilee](../jubilee/) of Leviticus 25 — the fiftieth-year "liberty
throughout all the land," the return of every family to its holding, the
principle that land belongs to Yahweh and is only leased to its tenants — is the
text the doctrine cites directly. Mainstream biblical scholarship (Roland de
Vaux, Jeffrey Fager, John Bergsma) tends to read the Jubilee as an ideal
prescription with little evidence of consistent practice; the framework reads
that limited implementation not as a strike against the principle but as a
symptom of trying it at less than planetary scale.

The closest secular parallels cluster around land and inheritance. Henry George's
*Progress and Poverty* (1879) held that land value belongs to the community while
improvements belong to the improver, to be captured by a land-value tax — an
overlap with Humanitarianism on land, though George reforms through taxation
where the doctrine reforms through tenure. The left-libertarianism of Hillel
Steiner and Peter Vallentyne argues, on independent philosophical grounds, that
natural resources are owned in common and that private appropriation owes
compensation to the excluded — again convergent on natural resources, though
narrower in reach than the doctrine's hold over all assets. Silvio Gesell's
*Freiwirtschaft* pairs a "free land" of rental tenure with monetary reform; the
Rawlsian ideal of a property-owning democracy, developed by Elizabeth Anderson,
seeks to disperse capital widely and block dynastic concentration, though from
within a reformed capitalism the doctrine means to leave behind; and the Catholic
distributism of Chesterton and Belloc offers yet another third way between
capitalism and socialism, keyed to the wide spread of small property. Thomas
Piketty's work on inheritance and the *r > g* dynamic supplies, five decades
after Vorilhon, a data-driven account of exactly the intergenerational
concentration the doctrine names — while proposing progressive wealth taxation
rather than the abolition of ownership. Universal Basic Income, in the arguments
of Philippe Van Parijs and others, addresses the same floor of economic security
but operates within existing property arrangements rather than replacing them;
the corpus reads it as a possible complement to Humanitarianism rather than a
substitute.

Beyond the modern West, common-ownership and finite-tenure arrangements appear in
nearly every major tradition: the early Christian community of goods (Acts 2 and
4), the common ownership of the Benedictine and Franciscan rules, the Islamic
institutions of *zakat* and *waqf*, the property discipline of the Buddhist
*sangha*, and a wide range of indigenous common-property systems, down to the
modern experiments of the utopian communes and the Israeli kibbutz. The corpus
takes the breadth of this pattern as evidence that private property with
unrestricted inheritance is one arrangement among many rather than a human
default — and reads the recurrence, in its own interpretive voice, as scattered
memory of the arrangement the Elohim first set out. Mainstream economic history
explains the same pattern through pre-modern constraints and religious-cultural
elaboration; the framework does not dispute those factors so much as add its own
reading on top of them.

## Critical perspectives

The doctrine draws the objections that meet any programme of this ambition, and
they deserve stating plainly.

The first is feasibility. A universal forty-nine-year lease requires an
administrative apparatus of a scale and honesty no society has demonstrated —
valuing every asset, tracking every tenure, cycling property back to the
community at each term without corruption or capture. Critics note that the
doctrine assumes rather than shows how this machinery would work, and that the
reversion of businesses and farms at a fixed term could blunt exactly the
long-horizon investment it means to reward.

The second is coordination. By the source's own admission the scheme cannot hold
in one jurisdiction while wealth moves freely to another, which makes it hostage
to [World Government](../world-government/) — itself a distant and contested
proposal. A reform that presupposes planetary political unity inherits every
objection to that unity.

The third is the family-home exception. Letting the family house pass to children
reintroduces, in miniature, the very inheritance the doctrine sets out to
abolish; where the line falls between a protected home and prohibited dynastic
property is a question the source leaves largely to be worked out.

The corpus does not treat these as fatal. Consistent with its reading of the
whole programme, it presents Humanitarianism as a direction for debate across the
long Aquarian age rather than a finished blueprint — a claim about which way an
economy ought to develop, offered for the centuries rather than the legislative
session.
