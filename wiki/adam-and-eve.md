**Adam** (<span class="hebrew">אָדָם</span>, *ʾāḏām*) and **Eve** (<span class="hebrew">חַוָּה</span>, *ḥawwāh*) are the first humans of the Hebrew Bible's Genesis narrative. Adam is formed in Genesis 2:7 by Yahweh Elohim "from the dust of the ground"; Eve is built in Genesis 2:21–22 from a part of his side and named in Genesis 3:20 "the mother of all living" (*ʾēm kol-ḥay*). Together they are the focal pair of Genesis 2–4: placed in the garden of Eden, given the single prohibition concerning the [Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil](../tree-of-the-knowledge-of-good-and-evil/), persuaded by the [serpent](../serpent/) to eat from it, expelled from the garden, and named as the parents of Cain, Abel, and Seth. Their record in the Hebrew Bible closes with Adam's death at 930 years (Genesis 5:5); Eve's death is not reported.

In the reading developed in the Raëlian source material and adopted by the Wheel of Heaven corpus, Adam and Eve are historical figures rather than symbols or the first humans as such. They are the first humans engineered by the **Israel team** of [Elohim](../elohim/) creators at their [Eden](../eden/) installation — one of seven creator-team installations at work across the antediluvian supercontinent — during the late Age of Leo.{{ footnote(id="5") }} They were assembled by [genetic engineering](../genetic-engineering/) from Elohim genetic material and terrestrial substrate, taught inside the Eden enclosure, and there received the withheld scientific knowledge that the dissenting [Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction disclosed to them — the act Genesis 3 records as the serpent's intervention. They were expelled when the home-world Council moved to restrict that knowledge, and their offspring founded the Eden-lineage civilisation whose later history the Hebrew Bible preserves.{{ cite(id="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", text="[1]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *The Book Which Tells the Truth* (1974), ch. 2 — Adam and Eve as the Israel team's first engineered humans") }}

## The Genesis narrative

Genesis carries two creation accounts, and the corpus reads them as covering different scopes rather than telling one story twice.{{ footnote(id="1") }} Genesis 1:26–28 is the summary: "Let us make humankind in our image" (*naʿaśeh ʾāḏām bə-ṣalmēnû*), "male and female he created them." No individuals are named; the plural "let us make" is the Elohim speaking as the plural civilisation they are.

The detailed account follows in Genesis 2. There Yahweh Elohim forms the first human and animates him:

{% library(book="genesis-woh", chapter=2, verse=7) %}
YHWH-Elohim formed the human, dust from the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life; and the human became a living being.
{% end %}

Yahweh Elohim plants the garden "in Eden, in the east," sets in it the Tree of Life and the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil, and gives the one prohibition: of that second tree "you shall not eat." The man names the animals; then, finding "no helper fit for him," Yahweh Elohim casts him into a "deep sleep" (*tardēmâ*), takes one of his ribs (*ṣēlāʿ*), and builds it into a woman, whom the man greets as "bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh." The chapter closes on the pair "naked, and not ashamed."

Genesis 3 turns on the serpent, "more crafty than any beast of the field," who tells the woman she will not die but will become "like Elohim, knowing good and evil." She eats, gives to Adam, and he eats; their eyes open; they cover themselves. Yahweh Elohim confronts them — the man blaming the woman, the woman the serpent — and pronounces sentences on all three, expelling the pair from the garden and stationing the cherubim and "a flaming sword which turned every way" to bar the Tree of Life. Genesis 4–5 then records their children (Cain, Abel, Seth, and unnamed others) and the genealogy running from Adam to Noah.

## Etymology and naming

The names are load-bearing. Hebrew *ʾāḏām* is bound to *ʾădāmâ* ("ground, soil"), both from the root *ʾ-d-m*, "to be red," after the reddish earth of the Levant. Genesis 2:7 makes the pun explicit: the *ʾāḏām* is formed from the *ʾădāmâ*. Across the Hebrew Bible *ʾāḏām* works in three registers — the generic "humanity" (as in Genesis 1:26), the species term set against the animals, and the proper name of the first man, with the definite *hā-ʾāḏām* ("the human") giving way to *ʾāḏām* as a name. The corpus reads the wordplay as the Hebrew preserving, in the vocabulary itself, the claim it develops: the first human was assembled from the materials of this planet, and named for them.

*Ḥawwāh* has its own gloss in Genesis 3:20 — "the mother of all living" — from the root *ḥ-y-h* / *ḥ-w-h*, "to live." She is "the living one," the matriarch of the Eden lineage. Scholars have proposed further links: to Aramaic *ḥewyāʾ* ("snake"), suggested in Genesis Rabbah and elaborated in Kabbalah, and to a Phoenician life-goddess whose name the Hebrew Bible would have demythologised. The corpus takes the explicit Genesis etymology as the primary one and does not adopt the serpent-link; on its reading Eve is not to be identified with the serpent. The names travel outward as Greek *Adam* and *Heua*, Latin *Adam* and *Heva* (later *Eva*), and Arabic *Ādam wa-Ḥawwāʾ* in the Qur'anic tradition.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The corpus reads Genesis 2–3 as a compressed but faithful record of work done at the Eden installation, and reads its details concretely.

**Creation as engineering.** Adam and Eve were made, not born. The "dust of the ground" is read as the raw terrestrial material of a laboratory synthesis, and the "breath of life" as the moment the finished organism was brought to life on an Elohim genetic template.{{ footnote(id="2") }} The name *ʾāḏām* records the substrate; the *bə-ṣelem ʾĕlōhîm* ("in the image of Elohim") records the template — humans carry the close genetic and structural likeness to their makers that the phrase reports. Adam was made first; Eve was built from cells or genetic material taken from him during the *tardēmâ*, the anaesthetised "deep sleep," which is why the *ṣēlāʿ* — "rib," but also "side" or "support" — gives her a close genetic kinship to Adam and grounds his recognition of her as "bone of my bones."

**Eden as laboratory.** The garden is the enclosed, controlled site of the Israel team, where the pair were synthesised, taught, and observed. Their education was real but bounded: the Council's containment policy withheld the creators' scientific knowledge, and the prohibition on the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil is that policy in narrative form — an order to keep the creation from closing the gap with its makers.

**The disclosure.** The serpent of Genesis 3 is neither a snake nor Satan. It is the Lucifer faction in its disclosing role — a minority of the Israel team's own scientists, led by the Eloha the later tradition names Lucifer, who had grown attached to the humans they had made and judged the containment order unjust. Their act was to give Adam and Eve the knowledge they had been denied. On the corpus's reading the serpent does not lie: its two claims — that the humans will not die, and that they will become "like Elohim, knowing good and evil" — both hold. This point calls for care. The disclosing faction (Lucifer, the serpent) wanted humanity enlarged and enlightened; it is the opposite of [Satan](../satan/), who led the separate home-world faction that judged the whole Earth project a mistake and wanted it ended. Later tradition fused serpent, Lucifer, and Satan into a single devil and, in doing so, erased a real division inside the creator civilisation.

**The expulsion as restriction.** The sentences of Genesis 3 are read as the Council's verdict once the disclosure could not be undone. The serpent "cursed to crawl" is the Lucifer faction's exile — stranded on Earth, cut off from the home world — among the humans it had chosen to teach. Adam and Eve are put out of the enclosure to live by their own labour. The cherubim and the "flaming sword which turned every way" are read, following the source material, as armed sentries barring the way back to the Tree of Life. The disclosure was equally the work and the choice of both humans; the corpus rejects the reading that makes Eve uniquely culpable.{{ footnote(id="4") }}

**After Eden.** Adam and Eve's children — Cain, Abel, Seth, and others — founded the antediluvian Eden civilisation, taught onward by the exiled Lucifer faction. The corpus reads Cain and Abel as their ordinary sons and declines the reading that makes Cain a child of the serpent's line. Adam's recorded 930 years are read as the [Tree of Life](../tree-of-life/) longevity the Lucifer faction later obtained for the Eden patriarchs, not as a natural human span. The source material further names Adam the first prophet — the first human to receive direct communication from the creators — and reports both Adam and Eve among the resurrected on the Planet of the Eternals, awaiting the open return at the Aquarian-age embassy.{{ footnote(id="6") }}{{ cite(id="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", text="[2]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet* (1975) — the Planet of the Eternals and the resurrected prophets") }} One point the framework holds firmly: Adam and Eve are the first humans of *one* lineage, not of all humanity.{{ footnote(id="3") }}

## Comparative first-human traditions

First-human stories recur across cultures, and the corpus reads the closest of them as parallel memory rather than coincidence — a comparative claim, offered as interpretation and held more loosely than the canon-internal reading above.

The nearest Mesopotamian neighbour is **Enkidu** of the *Epic of Gilgamesh* — a man fashioned from clay by the goddess Aruru, wild until the woman Shamhat draws him into human awareness. The passage from wildness to knowledge through a woman's mediation runs close to the Eve narrative, and comparative scholarship has long noted it.{{ cite(id="george-epic-of-gilgamesh", text="[3]", title="George (trans.), *The Epic of Gilgamesh* (1999) — the Enkidu narrative") }} The **Adapa** myth is closer still on the point of withheld life: Adapa, the first sage, is warned by his patron Ea not to eat the food he will be offered in heaven — food that turns out to be the bread of life — and so forfeits immortality, an exact inversion of the Eden prohibition.{{ cite(id="before-the-muses", text="[4]", title="Foster, *Before the Muses* (2005) — the Adapa myth") }} Beyond Mesopotamia the pattern widens: **Manu** in the Vedic tradition is the first man and flood-survivor; the Norse **Ask and Embla** are shaped from wood and given breath and mind by the gods; **Nüwa** forms the first humans from yellow earth; and the Maya *Popol Vuh* records the gods trying and failing to make humans of mud and of wood before succeeding with maize.

Mainstream folklore treats this spread as a mix of independent invention, diffusion, and shared human experience, and is right to caution against reading one event behind every version. The framework's claim is narrower: that the tightest cluster of parallels — a first pair, an animating breath, a woman-mediated passage into knowledge, a withheld immortality — is better explained as fragmentary memory of the seven creator teams' parallel work than as coincidence. This is the [Doubled Signature](../doubled-signature/) principle applied to the making of humanity, and it is labelled speculative where it goes beyond what any single source states.

## Scholarly and critical perspectives

The framework's reading sits alongside the scholarship on Genesis 2–3, not in place of it.

**Source criticism.** Mainstream scholarship assigns Genesis 1 to the Priestly source and Genesis 2–3 to the Yahwist, and reads the two as juxtaposed accounts left unharmonised by the redactor — different vocabulary, different order of creation, an anthropomorphic deity who walks in the garden. The commentaries of Skinner, von Rad, Westermann, and Sarna treat the Eden narrative as a theologically serious story of the human condition rather than a historical report.{{ cite(id="skinner-genesis-icc", text="[5]", title="Skinner, *Genesis* (ICC, 1910)") }}{{ cite(id="von-rad-genesis", text="[6]", title="von Rad, *Genesis: A Commentary* (1961)") }}{{ cite(id="westermann-genesis-1-11", text="[7]", title="Westermann, *Genesis 1–11* (1974)") }}{{ cite(id="sarna-genesis-jps", text="[8]", title="Sarna, *Genesis: The JPS Torah Commentary* (1989)") }} The corpus does not dispute the textual analysis; it reads the seam between the two accounts as a memory carried through more than one channel, and reframes their relation as a difference of scope rather than contradiction.

**Christian reception.** The dominant Western reading runs through Paul — "by one man sin entered into the world" (Romans 5:12) — and through Augustine, whose doctrine of inherited original sin made Genesis 3 the hinge of Western anthropology.{{ cite(id="city-of-god", text="[9]", title="Augustine, *The City of God* — the original-sin synthesis") }} The corpus diverges here most sharply: it keeps the sense that the episode is pivotal but rejects the machinery of hereditary guilt, reading a political disclosure with political costs, specific to one lineage, rather than a universal fall. Elaine Pagels's *Adam, Eve, and the Serpent* documents how contested and late the Augustinian synthesis in fact was — a history the corpus reads as consistent with its own diagnosis.{{ cite(id="pagels-adam-eve-serpent", text="[10]", title="Pagels, *Adam, Eve, and the Serpent* (1988)") }}

**Feminist scholarship.** Phyllis Trible's close reading of Genesis 2–3 argues for mutuality in the Hebrew — *ʿēzer kə-negdô* as "a helper corresponding to him," not a subordinate, and the *ṣēlāʿ* as a parallel side rather than a derivative part — while Carol Meyers reads Eve as the labouring woman of Iron Age Israel and the Genesis 3 "curses" as descriptions of hard agricultural life rather than prescriptions of hierarchy.{{ cite(id="trible-rhetoric-sexuality", text="[11]", title="Trible, *God and the Rhetoric of Sexuality* (1978)") }}{{ cite(id="meyers-discovering-eve", text="[12]", title="Meyers, *Discovering Eve* (1988)") }} On the single point that Eve is not uniquely to blame, the corpus agrees; it does not adopt the wider feminist-theological programme, and it notes without endorsing the medieval Lilith tradition, which the Raëlian material nowhere mentions.

**Ancient-astronaut readings.** Mauro Biglino's strict-literal Hebrew work reads the "dust" formation and the *ṣēlāʿ* as plainly biological — synthesis and cloning — which aligns with the corpus; the two part company where Biglino makes Cain a child of the serpent's line, a reading the corpus rejects.{{ cite(id="the-naked-bible", text="[13]", title="Biglino & Cattaneo, *The Naked Bible* (2022)") }} Jean Sendy and Paul Wallis both read Adam and Eve as the first humans of the Israel-team lineage, and are the corpus's principal antecedents here.{{ cite(id="those-gods-who-made-heaven-and-earth", text="[14]", title="Sendy, *Ces dieux qui firent le ciel et la terre* (1969)") }}{{ cite(id="the-eden-conspiracy", text="[15]", title="Wallis, *The Eden Conspiracy* (2024)") }}

**Human origins.** Mainstream paleoanthropology traces *Homo sapiens* to evolutionary processes over roughly 200,000–300,000 years, with no identifiable first pair. The framework does not contest that record for the species at large; its claim concerns one engineered lineage set within that longer history, and it distinguishes what the science establishes from what the framework adds.
