**Abraham** (Hebrew אַבְרָהָם, *Avraham*; Arabic إبراهيم, *Ibrāhīm*) is the founding
patriarch of the biblical tradition — the man called out of Mesopotamia to whom
the covenant promises of land, descendants, and blessing are made, and the shared
ancestor claimed by Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. In the Wheel of Heaven
framework Abraham is read as a recruitment-and-recovery figure: a verified-loyal
human lineage-head enlisted, after the [Sodom](../sodom-and-gomorrah/)
destruction, to reorganise the surviving [Eden](../eden/) lineage around a stable
covenant — the beginning of the [Aries-age](../timeline/age-of-aries/) recovery
program that the corpus tracks forward into the later prophetic traditions.

## Etymology and naming

The patriarch bears two names in Genesis. He is introduced as **Avram**
(אַבְרָם, "exalted father"); at the covenant renewal of Genesis 17:5 the name is
changed to **Avraham** (אַבְרָהָם), glossed there as "father of a multitude of
nations" (*av-hamon goyim*). The etymology is, as often in Genesis, a
theological word-play rather than a strict derivation — the added syllable marks
the covenant, not a change of language.

The name travels with the traditions that claim him: Greek Ἀβραάμ (*Abraám*) in
the Septuagint and New Testament, and Arabic إبراهيم (*Ibrāhīm*), the form under
which he becomes one of the most frequently named figures in the Quran. Later
tradition adds the honorifics — *Avraham Avinu* ("Abraham our father") in Jewish
usage, *al-Khalīl* ("the friend [of God]") in Islam — that register the weight
the figure carries across the three traditions.

## The Genesis narrative

The Abraham cycle runs from Genesis 11:26 to 25:11 and is among the most
sustained biographical treatments in the Hebrew Bible. Its principal episodes:

- **Origins (Genesis 11).** Abram is born in Ur of the Chaldees and migrates with
  his father Terah to Haran.
- **The call — *lekh lekha* (Genesis 12).** "Go forth from your land" — Abram is
  told to leave for a land he will be shown, with the promise "I will make of you
  a great nation." He travels through Canaan into Egypt and back.
- **The covenant (Genesis 15 and 17).** In two articulations — the "covenant
  between the pieces" of Genesis 15 and the circumcision covenant of Genesis 17 —
  the promises of land and innumerable descendants are formalised and sealed.
- **Hagar and Ishmael (Genesis 16 and 21).** Sarah's Egyptian servant Hagar bears
  Abraham a first son, Ishmael; after Isaac's birth Hagar and Ishmael are sent
  away, and a parallel promise is made over Ishmael.
- **Mamre and Sodom (Genesis 18–19).** Three visitors are received at Mamre and
  announce Isaac's coming birth; Abraham then intercedes over the impending
  destruction of [Sodom and Gomorrah](../sodom-and-gomorrah/).
- **The Akedah (Genesis 22).** Abraham is commanded to offer Isaac and is stopped
  at the last moment — the "binding of Isaac" (*Akedat Yitzchak*), the tradition's
  most theologically loaded passage.
- **Final episodes (Genesis 23–25).** Sarah dies and is buried at Machpelah; a
  wife, Rebekah, is secured for Isaac; Abraham dies "old and full of years."

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework reads the Abraham cycle as the record of a deliberate recovery
operation mounted after a period of collapse, and reads its details
operationally.

**Abraham as recruitment.** The *lekh lekha* call is read as a recruitment: after
the destruction of the rebel centre at Sodom, a verified-loyal figure was needed
around whom the surviving Eden lineage could be reorganised. The promise of "a
great nation" is read as the charter of that reorganisation rather than as a
reward for private piety. On this reading Abraham is chosen for reliability, not
for uniqueness of faith.

**The covenant as a political program.** The land-and-descendants covenant is
read as the founding instrument of the [Alliance](../the-alliance/) — a durable
arrangement, in the line of the earlier [Noahic covenant](../noahic-covenant/),
by which a human lineage is cultivated over generations. The circumcision sign of
Genesis 17 is read as the covenant's marker of membership.

**The Mamre visitation.** The three visitors of Genesis 18, who eat, are addressed
as men, and announce a birth before turning toward Sodom, are read as
alliance-officer contact rather than as a theophany — an on-the-ground engagement
consistent with the physical, embodied character the corpus attributes to the
[Elohim](../elohim/).

**The Akedah as loyalty test.** The binding of Isaac is read as exactly what the
text calls it — a test (Genesis 22:1, *nissah*, "tested") — administered to
confirm the reliability of the recruited lineage-head before the covenant was
allowed to pass to the next generation. The staying of the knife is read as the
test's completion, not as a repudiation of the demand.

**The Ishmael line.** The framework reads the parallel promise over Ishmael
(Genesis 21:13) as significant rather than incidental: the recovery program runs
through both of Abraham's sons, and the Ishmael branch produces its own recovery
tradition in the rise of Islam (below). This is an `inferred` reading, consistent
with the sources rather than stated by them.

### The Hanafiyya — Islam as Abrahamic restoration

The corpus reading of Abraham as a recruitment-and-recovery figure finds an
unusually explicit confirmation in the origins of Islam. The earliest Islamic
sources do not present the new religion as an innovation but as the recovery of
*millat Ibrāhīm*, the religion of Abraham — the [Hanafiyya](../hanafiyya/)
(Arabic *al-Ḥanīfiyyah*, "the upright primordial monotheism"). The Quran claims
Abraham as older than the traditions that quarrel over him — *"Abraham was
neither a Jew nor a Christian, but he was an upright one (ḥanīf)"* (Quran 3:67) —
and commands the following of his religion directly (Quran 16:123; 2:135). Ibn
Isḥāq's biography preserves a tradition of four pre-Islamic Qurashī seekers who,
judging that their people had corrupted the religion of their father Abraham,
"went their several ways in the lands, seeking the Hanifiyya, the religion of
Abraham."

On the framework's reading the ["monotheism"](../monotheism/) Abraham turns
toward is not the abstract single God of later theology — the [Elohim](../elohim/)
are a [plurality](../plurality-of-gods/) of embodied creators — but the recovery
of accurate knowledge of those creators against [idolatry](../idolatry/). The
Raëlian canon places this exact failure in Abraham's own period: after the Sodom
destruction men "began stupidly to worship pieces of stone and idols, forgetting
who had created them" ([*The Book Which Tells the
Truth*](../../library/the-book-which-tells-the-truth/)). The religion of Abraham
is the turn back from those dead images to the makers humanity had forgotten; see
[Hanafiyya](../hanafiyya/) and the full Explainer [*Monotheism Is the Wrong
Question*](/articles/monotheism-is-the-wrong-question/).

This restoration ran specifically through the **Ishmael** line — through Hagar
(Genesis 16, 21), the Egyptian handmaid whose son the alliance protects and
invests in alongside the covenant line. A revisionist hypothesis (associated with
Dan Gibson) locates the Abrahamic altar and holy city at [Petra](../petra/), the
Nabataean capital, rather than the Hijazi Mecca; that geographic claim is treated
as `speculative` in the [Petra](../petra/) and [Nabataeans](../nabataeans/)
entries, while the theological claim — Islam's self-understanding as the restored
religion of Abraham — rests on the Quran and Ibn Isḥāq directly. See the Explainer
"[The First Mosques Faced Petra, Not
Mecca](/articles/the-first-mosques-faced-petra-not-mecca/)."

## Comparative and scholarly perspectives

Abraham is unusual among biblical figures in being a live foundational claim for
three living traditions at once, and the framework's reading sits alongside the
scholarship rather than in place of it.

- **The three-faiths founder.** Judaism reads Abraham as the first patriarch and
  the covenant's origin; Christianity (Romans 4; Galatians 3) reads him as the
  father of faith prior to the Law; Islam reads him as a *ḥanīf* and *muslim*
  before either. The framework treats these not as rival claims to be adjudicated
  but as three branches of one recovery program, and preserves their differences
  rather than flattening them.
- **Source criticism and historicity.** Mainstream scholarship reads the Abraham
  cycle through the Documentary Hypothesis (the interwoven J, E, and P sources of
  Gunkel, von Rad, Westermann) and debates the patriarch's historicity, with
  minimalist historians treating the narratives as later literary construction.
  The framework does not require a fixed second-millennium dating and reads the
  composite text as a memory transmitted through more than one channel.
- **Rabbinic, Christian, and Bahá'í engagement.** The rabbinic tradition (Genesis
  Rabbah; the Talmud's Abraham-rejecting-idolatry narratives) develops Abraham as
  the discoverer of the one God; the Bahá'í tradition reads him within its
  progressive-revelation frame as one of a succession of Manifestations. These are
  registered in their own terms.
- **Ancient-astronaut readings.** Earlier interventionist writers (Sitchin) and
  the philological work of Biglino read the patriarchal contacts as involving
  physical, non-divine agents. The framework shares the premise of embodied
  contact but grounds its reading in the Raëlian canon rather than in Sitchin's
  Sumerian reconstruction.
