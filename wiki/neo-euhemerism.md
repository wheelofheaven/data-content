**Neo-Euhemerism** is the interpretive method that reads the gods and divine
beings of myth as garbled memory of real historical figures — and, in its modern
form, identifies those figures as members of a technologically advanced
civilization who were mistaken for gods by the people who recorded them. It
updates **Euhemerism**, the ancient Greek approach named for
**Euhemerus of Messene** (c. 340–260 BCE), who read the Olympian gods as former
kings elevated to divine status after their deaths. The classical method treated
the historical figures behind the gods as exceptional humans; the neo-
extension replaces "exceptional humans" with "advanced visitors" and keeps
everything else. In the [Wheel of Heaven](../wheel-of-heaven/) framework it is
the working method behind reading the [Elohim](../elohim/) as the historical
civilization that the world's scriptures remember as gods.

## Euhemerus and the classical method

Euhemerus of Messene was a Greek writer active at the court of Cassander, king
of Macedon, at the turn of the fourth and third centuries BCE. His lost work, the
*Hiera Anagraphē* (Ἱερὰ Ἀναγραφή, *Sacred Inscription* or *Sacred History*),
survives only in fragments quoted by later authors — principally
**Diodorus Siculus** (first century BCE), who preserved the fullest account, and
the Christian apologist **Lactantius** (c. 250–325 CE), with further notices in
**Eusebius**, **Cicero**, and **Plutarch**. The name *Euhēmeros* means, aptly,
"happy day."

The work is framed as a travel narrative. Euhemerus reports a voyage to a
fictional island, Panchaea, in the Indian Ocean, where he finds a golden pillar
inscribed with the true history of the gods. The inscription reveals that Uranus,
Cronus, Zeus, and the rest of the Greek pantheon had once been mortal kings and
benefactors whose grateful subjects raised them, over generations, to the status
of gods. Two moves define the method that took his name: first, the gods are not
supernatural beings but historical figures preserved in mythologized form;
second, the mythologizing happened through ordinary cultural and religious
elaboration of a human past. Whether Euhemerus meant his island as sober
reconstruction, philosophical allegory, or veiled religious criticism has been
debated since antiquity; the reading that mattered for later history was the
plain one.

Euhemerism became one of the standing options for making sense of pagan myth.
The Church Fathers found it useful: if even a pagan philosopher had concluded
that the gods were only deified men, the case against pagan worship made itself,
and Lactantius, Eusebius, and Augustine all pressed it to that end. The
Renaissance recovered Euhemerus through the printed Diodorus; Giambattista Vico's
*Scienza Nuova* (1725) built a historical reading of myth on cognate ground; and
Enlightenment writers — Fontenelle's *De l'Origine des Fables* (1724), Dupuis's
*Origine de tous les Cultes* (1795) — folded it into their rationalizing accounts
of religion. Nineteenth-century anthropology (Tylor, Frazer, Lang) absorbed
elements of it into broader theories of religious origin. By the twentieth
century, mainstream scholarship treated Euhemerism as one theory of myth among
several, and a limited one — the standard modern reconstruction is Marek
Winiarczyk's *Euhemeros von Messene* (2002). What it is not, in current
scholarship, is a master key.

## The neo- turn

The modern extension keeps Euhemerus's method and changes the identity of the
figures at the bottom of it. Where the classical reading found deified kings,
neo-euhemerism finds members of a technologically advanced civilization. The
mythologizing works the same way, but on different material: observers without
the vocabulary for what they were seeing recorded flight as gods who fly, medicine
and biological engineering as miraculous healing or the making of life, metallurgy
as divine craftsmanship, astronomical knowledge as gods who read the stars, and
advanced weapons as thunderbolts. The supernatural framing is the register in
which a pre-scientific culture stored an encounter it could not otherwise
describe.

The reading became a recognisable position in the mid-twentieth century, though
its practitioners rarely used the word "euhemerism." **Jean Sendy** read the
Hebrew Bible this way from 1963 onward; **Erich von Däniken**'s *Chariots of the
Gods?* (1968) carried a similar thesis to a mass audience; **Zecharia Sitchin**'s
*The 12th Planet* (1976) and the *Earth Chronicles* built the most detailed
single system, identifying the Sumerian Anunnaki with visitors from a putative
planet Nibiru; **Robert Charroux** developed a French popular version; and
**Mauro Biglino** and **Paul Anthony Wallis** carried the reading into close work
on the biblical text. Most of this literature is filed by scholarship under the
[ancient astronaut hypothesis](../ancient-astronaut-hypothesis/) or the
paleocontact hypothesis, and the two labels name much the same content. The value
of "neo-euhemerism" is that it names a *method* — a way of reading myth — rather
than only a *claim* about the past, and so ties the modern position to the long
interpretive tradition it descends from.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework reads myth neo-euhemeristically as a matter of method. Genesis 1's
[Elohim](../elohim/) are read as a plural, physical civilization of creators
rather than a single supernatural God; the theophanies of the
[Hebrew Bible](../hebrew-bible/) — the burning bush, Sinai, Ezekiel's vision — as
records of alliance contact rather than manifestations of the divine; the
[Great Flood](../great-flood/) as a deliberate strike remembered as a supernatural
inundation; and the broader cross-cultural mythology — Mesopotamian, Egyptian,
Greek, Vedic, Mesoamerican, Chinese — as memory of the same historical contact
refracted through many cultures. These are foundational premises of the
framework, not conclusions mainstream scholarship shares, and the corpus states
them as such.

Jean Sendy is the framework's principal antecedent here. Reading the Hebrew Bible
as a plain historical document rather than an allegory, he argued that its
strangeness dissolves once its claims about its own subject matter are taken at
face value:

> *"JE NE SAIS PAS si de tels Galaxiens se sont posés, aux temps protohistoriques,
> devant nos ancêtres primitifs éberlués ; MAIS CE QUE JE SAIS, je le sais de
> science certaine : le Mythe commun à toutes les Premières Civilisations affirme
> que de tels Galaxiens ont vécu sur Terre, aux temps protohistoriques."*
>
> ("I DO NOT KNOW whether such Galaxians landed, in protohistoric times, before
> our astonished primitive ancestors; BUT WHAT I KNOW, I know with certainty: the
> Myth common to all First Civilizations affirms that such Galaxians lived on
> Earth, in protohistoric times.")

Sendy's characteristic move is to treat the choice between the ancient and modern
accounts of human knowledge — "humans owe everything to the teaching of Hermes"
versus "humans discovered everything by themselves" — as a choice between two
assumptions rather than between superstition and science, and then to weigh the
mythological and astronomical evidence on that level playing field. He also noted
that myth describes gods who eat, breathe, and interbreed with humans — beings
made "like us, only more beautiful" — which he read as more consistent with a
biological civilization than with the alien monsters of fiction. The
strict-translational wing makes the same move philologically. Reading the Hebrew
concretely rather than metaphorically, Mauro Biglino argues that the text names
physical beings:

> *"we are interested in knowing how, where and when the Bible speaks of Anaqìm:
> Concrete beings that have come to Earth from other worlds... As we analyze the
> Old Testament, we will identify these concrete beings using their correct Hebrew
> names ('Those who from heaven to Earth came' in Hebrew are 'Anaqiti' instead of
> the more well-known published Sumerian name, 'Anunnaki')."*

Where the framework parts company with the wider field is in what it adds. It is
not simply a contribution to neo-euhemerism in the way Sitchin's Mesopotamian
reconstruction is. Three commitments extend it. First, a **precessional spine**:
following [*Hamlet's Mill*](../hamlets-mill/) (Santillana and von Dechend, 1969),
the corpus organises the history of contact around the precession of the equinoxes
and its twelve ages, and reads the astronomical structure of the myths as
deliberate encoding rather than incidental survival (see
[Doubled Signature](../doubled-signature/) and [World Age](../world-age/)).
Second, an **ongoing alliance**: the Elohim are read as a continuing reality
rather than a closed chapter of antiquity, with the contact resuming through
Vorilhon in 1973 and the contemporary UFO/UAP record read as part of the same
story — a commitment most of the popular field does not share. Third, **specific
source-texts**: Vorilhon's contact accounts, Sendy's earlier work, and the
Hebrew Bible read through the framework's own hermeneutic, which give the reading a
particularity that a general appeal to "ancient astronauts" lacks.

The method also disciplines how the framework reads individual figures. The gods
of a given tradition are treated as memory of specific historical actors, and the
corpus keeps their politics distinct rather than flattening them into a single
divine cast. [Yahweh](../yahweh/) is read as the Council president leading a
moderate, preserve-with-containment line; the exiled [Lucifer](../lucifer/)
faction — the [Serpent](../serpent/) of the Eden disclosure — as the party that
wanted humanity told the truth and empowered; and [Satan](../satan/) as the leader
of the separate abolitionist faction that held the creation a mistake and argued
for its destruction. Lucifer and Satan are opposite positions, not the same fallen
figure; their conflation in later Christian theology is, on the framework's
reading, one of the more consequential errors the method is meant to correct. The
corpus is likewise careful with cross-cultural identity: it holds, as a canon
claim, that the Sumerian [Anunnaki](../anunnaki/) name the same creators as the
Elohim, but treats the relationship between the Elohim and other pantheons —
Egyptian, Greek, Vedic — as a matter for case-by-case comparison rather than the
blanket equivalence common in the popular literature.

## Critical and scholarly perspectives

Two distinct judgments have to be kept apart. Classical Euhemerism is a respected
object of study in the history of religion; the neo-euhemeristic extension is not
accepted by mainstream scholarship, which classes it with the ancient astronaut
field as pseudoscience. The framework engages both fairly.

Against Euhemerism as a general theory of myth, the standard objection is that it
is reductive. Myth is not only encoded history. The dominant twentieth-century
approaches — Mircea Eliade's phenomenology of the sacred, C. G. Jung and Joseph
Campbell's archetypal readings, Claude Lévi-Strauss's structural analysis (see
[Mytheme](../mytheme/)), Georges Dumézil's comparative work — treat mythological
material as symbolic, psychological, social, and cognitive at once, and a reading
that resolves it into a garbled newspaper report loses most of what those
approaches recover. This objection applies to any euhemerism, ancient or modern,
and the framework accepts its force: reading myth as memory of contact does not
require denying that the same stories also do symbolic and social work. The
corpus's position is that the symbolic elaboration is real but is not the whole
story — that it accreted around historical content rather than out of nothing.

Against the neo- extension specifically, the objections are sharper and, in
several cases, sound. Archaeology (Kenneth Feder's *Frauds, Myths, and
Mysteries*) rejects the claim that ancient peoples needed outside help to raise
their monuments or work out their astronomy, and faults the field for
cherry-picking anomalies out of context — a charge with real moral weight when it
carries the assumption that non-European builders could not have produced their
own achievements. Philology rejects the specific reconstructions: Assyriologists
do not accept Sitchin's readings of the cuneiform, and Hebrew Bible scholars do
not accept Biglino's word-for-word method. And the field's claims are often framed
so loosely that they cannot be tested. The framework does not wave these criticisms
away. It grants that indigenous achievement is real and that unfalsifiable claims
are worthless, and it reads contact as occasional cultivation rather than a
substitute for human work. What it holds, against the consensus and openly as a
minority position, is that a disciplined reading — anchored in specific texts,
honest about each inferential step, and labelled by claim type — can still recover
a real history behind *some* of the traditions, and that the genuine insight of the
euhemeristic method should not be discarded along with the field's excesses.
