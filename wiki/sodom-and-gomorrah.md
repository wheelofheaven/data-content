**Sodom and Gomorrah** (Hebrew: סְדֹם וַעֲמֹרָה, *Sedom va-Amorah*) is the
destruction of the Cities of the Plain recorded in [Genesis](../genesis/) 18–19.
In the Wheel of Heaven framework it is read as a targeted strike by the
home-world [Council of the Eternals](../council-of-the-eternals/) against an
organized human vengeance movement: the descendants of the [Babel](../babel/)-era
scientific elite, who had regathered forbidden knowledge at the Cities of the
Plain and were preparing an expedition against the creators who had earlier tried
to destroy them. Structurally the event echoes the [Great Flood](../great-flood/)
but on a tactical scale — a localized strike on a single rebel centre rather than
a planetary reset — with [Abraham](../abraham/) interceding beforehand and his
nephew Lot extracted before the blow fell. On this reading the "two angels" of
Genesis 19 are Council scouts, the "brimstone and fire" an atomic detonation, and
the modern Dead Sea basin the crater it left.

## Etymology and naming

The English pairing **Sodom and Gomorrah** renders the two cities that the
biblical narrative names together throughout the Hebrew Bible (Genesis 13, 14,
18, 19; Deuteronomy 29; Isaiah 1 and 13; Jeremiah 23, 49, 50; Ezekiel 16; Amos 4;
Zephaniah 2) and the New Testament (Matthew 10:15; Romans 9:29; 2 Peter 2:6;
Jude 7).

The Hebrew **סְדֹם** (*Sedom*) is of uncertain derivation. Proposals connect it
to a root *s-d-m* ("to scorch, to burn"), to a Semitic *šdm* ("field, cultivated
land"), or to a lost pre-Hebrew place-name preserved from an earlier Canaanite
stratum. **עֲמֹרָה** (*Amorah*) — Anglicized "Gomorrah" through the Greek
Septuagint's Γόμορρα — is likewise contested, variously tied to roots meaning
"submerged," "ruined," or "heap." The names survive in the tradition with their
post-destruction connotations rather than any pre-destruction civic designation.

The collective **Cities of the Plain** (Hebrew עָרֵי הַכִּכָּר, *arei ha-kikkar*)
names the wider cluster: Sodom and Gomorrah as the two principal cities, with
Admah, Zeboiim, and Zoar — the last the town Lot fled to, and the only one of the
five spared. The event passes into other traditions as Greek Σόδομα καὶ Γόμορρα,
Latin *Sodoma et Gomorrha*, and Arabic قَوْم لُوط (*Qawm Lūṭ*, "the people of
Lot"), the Quran's usual designation.

## The Genesis narrative

The account runs across two chapters. In **Genesis 18**, three figures appear at
Abraham's camp by the oaks of Mamre; he receives them with hospitality, and they
announce the coming destruction of Sodom for its "wickedness" (18:20–21). Abraham
then bargains: will the city be spared for fifty righteous people, forty-five,
forty, thirty, twenty, ten? The visitor agrees each time, down to ten.

In **Genesis 19**, two of the figures — the "two angels" (Hebrew שְׁנֵי
הַמַּלְאָכִים, *shenei ha-malakhim*; *malakh* means "messenger," not a winged
being) — reach Sodom at evening, and Lot takes them in. The men of the city
surround the house and demand the visitors be handed over. The visitors strike
the crowd with "blindness" (19:11) and warn Lot to leave. Lot, his wife, and his
two daughters flee toward Zoar; Yahweh "rains brimstone and fire" on the cities
(19:24–25); Lot's wife looks back and "becomes a pillar of salt" (19:26); and
Abraham, looking down the next morning, sees "the smoke of the country" rising
"as the smoke of a furnace" (19:28). In later biblical and post-biblical usage
the pair become the standing paradigm of a city destroyed by divine judgment.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework takes Genesis 18–19 as a compressed, partly misremembered record of
a real event and reads its details operationally, following the account in *The
Book Which Tells the Truth*. Yahweh's passage there frames the whole episode as an
atomic strike:

> *"Among the humans who had been dispersed on Earth, a few nursed the desire for
> vengeance, so they gathered in the towns of Sodom and Gomorrah and, having
> managed to salvage a few scientific secrets, they prepared an expedition aimed
> at punishing those who had tried to destroy them. Consequently, the creators
> sent two spies to investigate what was going on… Some humans tried to kill
> them, but the spies managed to blind their attackers with a pocket atomic
> weapon… They warned those who were peaceful to leave the town because they were
> going to destroy it with an atomic explosion… And the bomb fell on Sodom and
> Gomorrah… As you now know, burns caused by an atomic explosion kill those who
> are too near and make them look like salt statues."*

**A rebellion, not a moral failure.** On this reading the "wickedness" of the
cities is not sexual or social corruption but the same thing Genesis 6:5 ascribes
to the pre-Flood world — the drive toward scientific autonomy — now sharpened by
grievance. The Cities of the Plain were not depraved; they were the surviving
concentration of the knowledge the Eden lineage had rebuilt since Babel. Their
inhabitants were the descendants of the scientific elite the Council had
scattered, and across the long centuries between Babel and Sodom — as dispersed
communities reconnected through trade and intermarriage and preserved fragments
of the old technical training — enough capability had reaccumulated to support a
project no single community could have mounted alone: an "expedition aimed at
punishing" the creators who had loosed the Flood.

**Distinct from Babel.** The framework separates Sodom sharply from the earlier
[Babel](../babel/) intervention, which conventional readings fold together with
it as "cities punished by God." Babel, on the canon's reading, was a peace
overture that the Council ended through dispersion; Sodom was an armed rebellion
it ended through a strike. The continuity the rebels claimed — inheritance from
the [Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction, whose Serpent they invoked as their own
emblem — was real at the level of cultural transmission but inverted in spirit.
The Lucifer faction had loved the human creation and built to preserve it; the
Sodom rebels had come to hate the Council and organized to destroy. The full
treatment of Babel lives in its own entry.

**The scouts and the strike.** The Council, monitoring the project from a
distance, sent two personnel to confirm the threat directly. These are the
"angels" of Genesis 19 — scouts, not supernatural beings, carrying the personal
hardware a reconnaissance party of an advanced civilization would issue. The
hostile reception confirmed the reading that the cities were operating bases
rather than ordinary towns; the "blindness" inflicted on the mob (19:11) is read
as the flash of a directed-energy weapon discharged at close range. The
instruction to Lot not to look back (19:17) is read as a technical safety warning
of exactly the kind personnel at twentieth-century nuclear tests would later be
given: observing the flash at close range would destroy the retina. The earlier
Mamre bargain sets the protocol — the strike would be withheld if a threshold of
uninvolved people could be found — and the scouts' verification established that
it could not; the cities were committed to the rebellion, and Lot's family alone
was extracted before the blow.

**The Dead Sea signature.** The framework reads the modern Dead Sea basin as the
crater the strike left. Genesis 13:10 remembers the plain, before the
destruction, as "well watered everywhere, even as the garden of Yahweh, like the
land of Egypt" — fertile ground that supported fortified cities and cannot be
reconciled with the sterile basin that exists now. That basin, on this reading,
is what the weapon produced: its depth (about 430 metres below sea level) a sudden
displacement rather than slow subsidence; its hypersalinity, its unusual
magnesium-chloride dominance, its anomalous heavy metals, and its surrounding
sterile salt flats the redeposited signature of an atomic-scale detonation at
ground level roughly five thousand years ago. The reading does not deny the
Jordan Rift Valley, a genuine tectonic feature produced by the separation of the
Arabian and African plates; it holds that the Sodom event did not create the rift
but carved the basin within it.

**The relapse and the Serpent.** The strike's cost, in the source's own terms,
was the loss of the Eden lineage's educated class: *"because of the destruction of
centers of progress such as Sodom and Gomorrah and the elimination of the most
intelligent individuals, human beings had lapsed back into a very primitive state
and had begun, rather stupidly, to adore pieces of stone and idols, forgetting
those who had really created them."* The framework reads this idolatry not as
primordial sin but as the specific condition that follows the loss of a literate
class: the population kept the forms of its religion but lost the framework that
explained them. Abraham himself, on this account, is a herdsman descended from a
civilization that had once built starships.

The event also reversed the meaning of the Serpent. Before Sodom, the Serpent had
been the Eden lineage's positive emblem for the [Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction —
the dissident creators of Genesis 3 who disclosed the forbidden knowledge — and
the same positive symbolism survives in the Egyptian *uraeus*, the healing
serpent of the Near Eastern rod, and the feathered serpent of Mesoamerica.
Because the Sodom rebels had flown that emblem over an armed conspiracy, the
surrounding peoples came to associate the Serpent with the catastrophe that
consumed the cities that bore it. Within the diminished Eden lineage the
distinctions then blurred entirely: the Lucifer faction, the Serpent that stood
for it, [Satan](../satan/) (the home-world faction that had wanted humanity
destroyed), and Yahweh (the Council president who ordered the strike) had been
four distinct roles, and as the framework was lost they progressively merged into
the single adversary of later tradition. The detailed history of that four-way
conflation lives in the [Satan](../satan/) entry.

## Comparative traditions

The destruction of a doomed city by fire from the sky is one of the most widely
distributed of mythic patterns, and the framework's comparative claim is offered
as interpretation rather than proof. The Greek tradition preserves the scorching
of the earth in the Phaethon myth and a range of Olympian punishments by
thunderbolt. Hindu tradition preserves a closer parallel in the destruction of
**Tripura**, the "three cities" burned from the sky by Shiva in the *Mahabharata*
and the *Puranas*; the same epic's descriptions of the *Brahmastra* and related
*astras* — heat that consumes armies, bodies reduced to ash, hair and nails
falling out, food turned poisonous — are read by interventionist writers as a
memory of radiation effects. The Greek Niobe, turned to weeping stone, offers a
distant analogue to the salt-pillar motif.

Mainstream folklore and comparative religion explain such convergences through
some mixture of independent invention, cultural diffusion, and shared cognitive
tendencies — the near-universal impulse to read a catastrophe as a judgment. The
framework does not so much reject that account as add to it: it proposes that
beneath the tightest of these parallels lies a memory of real events of the kind
it describes, each tradition preserving that memory in its own idiom. This is the
[Doubled Signature](../doubled-signature/) principle applied to Sodom, and it is
labelled speculative where it runs beyond what any single source states.

## Scholarly and critical perspectives

**Mainstream Genesis scholarship.** The Sodom narrative has been closely studied
in the commentary tradition — Hermann Gunkel's form-critical *Genesis* (1910),
the mid-century commentaries of Gerhard von Rad and Claus Westermann, E. A.
Speiser's Anchor Bible *Genesis* (1964), and later work by Robert Alter and Bill
Arnold. This scholarship reads the account as a theologically shaped narrative
attached to the striking Dead Sea landscape, and the framework aligns with it at
the textual and philological level while parting company on what the narrative
ultimately records.

**The "sin of Sodom."** The traditional Christian reading took the cities' sin to
be sexual — reading the mob's demand to "know" the visitors (19:5) in its sexual
sense — and built the later category of "sodomy" on it. Much contemporary Hebrew
Bible scholarship has moved away from that reading toward one of inhospitality and
social violence, noting that Ezekiel 16:49 names Sodom's sin as "pride, fullness
of bread, and abundance of idleness… neither did she strengthen the hand of the
poor and needy," with no mention of sex (Boswell; Trible; Helminiak; Jordan on
the medieval invention of the category). The framework's political-military
reading sits outside both, though it shares with the revisionist scholarship the
judgment that the later sexual reading is not what the earliest text is about.

**Was it a real event?** Whether any historical destruction lies behind the
narrative is contested. The most-discussed recent claim concerns Tall el-Hammam,
a Middle Bronze Age city in the southern Jordan Valley whose excavation directors
(Bunch et al., *Scientific Reports*, 2021) argue was destroyed around 1650 BCE by
a Tunguska-class cosmic airburst — citing melted pottery and mudbrick, shocked
quartz, and metallic microspherules, with an energy release they estimate at many
times the Hiroshima bomb. The claim is genuinely disputed: Mark Boslough and
others have challenged the sampling and the airburst interpretation, and the
~1650 BCE date falls several centuries after the corpus's nominal Sodom. The
framework therefore does not identify Tall el-Hammam as Sodom itself; it reads the
site, at most, as evidence that destructions of this order did occur in the region
during the Bronze Age. Steven Collins, who directs the excavation, argues on
separate geographic grounds that Tall el-Hammam *is* the biblical Sodom — a
minority position in the field.

**Dead Sea geology.** Mainstream geology attributes the basin to the Jordan Rift
Valley and dates the modern hypersaline lake's emergence from the larger, fresher
late-Pleistocene Lake Lisan to gradual climatic and tectonic change (Frumkin;
Niemi and colleagues). The framework accepts the rift setting but reads the
basin's sharper anomalies as the imprint of a single catastrophic event
superimposed on it — a reading it presents as an alternative to, not a refutation
of, the standard account.

**Alternative-history readings.** Zecharia Sitchin (*The Wars of Gods and Men*,
1985) read Sodom as a nuclear event within his Anunnaki reconstruction; Paul
Anthony Wallis and Mauro Biglino reach broadly interventionist readings from close
translation of the Hebrew. The framework converges with these at the level of an
underlying historical event involving advanced technology, while grounding its own
reading in the Raëlian source material rather than in Sitchin's Sumerian scheme.
