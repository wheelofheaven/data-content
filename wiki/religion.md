**Religion** (Latin *religio*, most plausibly from *religare*, "to bind, to tie,
to link") is, in ordinary usage, the domain of belief, ritual, and institution
through which human communities relate to what they hold sacred. In the
[Wheel of Heaven](../wheel-of-heaven/) framework it is read more specifically —
and more literally — as *that which binds* humanity to the [Elohim](../elohim/),
the advanced civilization that produced terrestrial life and humanity through
deliberate scientific work. On this reading religion is not, at root, a symbolic
or psychological phenomenon but a cultivated relationship with real creators: a
bond first established through direct contact and since maintained, across
generations, through ritual, instruction, textual preservation, and the
orientation of sacred architecture. The world's religions are read as the
distributed record of that contact — stored in the pre-scientific vocabularies of
the cultures that received it, and so garbled to varying degrees, but decodable by
the [euhemerist method](../neo-euhemerism/) the framework works from.

## Etymology

The English *religion* comes from Latin *religio* — "religious obligation,
scrupulous observance, sanctity" — whose own derivation has been debated since
antiquity. Two candidates dominate. Cicero (*De Natura Deorum* II.72) derived it
from *relegere*, "to go over again, to re-read," making religion a matter of
careful, repeated observance. Lactantius (*Divinae Institutiones* IV.28) derived
it instead from *religare*, "to bind fast": *"hoc vinculo pietatis obstricti Deo
et religati sumus"* — "by this bond of piety we are tied and bound to God, whence
religion itself takes its name." The *religare* etymology, favored across medieval
and early modern Christian thought, is the one the framework works from — both
because it aligns with the framework's own reading and because it is the
derivation that shaped the Western sense of what religion is.

*Religare* sits in a wide Latin family descended from *ligare*, "to bind":
*ligament*, *ligature*, *liaison*, *alliance* (from *alligare*, "to bind to"),
*oblige* (from *obligare*), and *rely* itself. The semantic field is consistently
one of active binding — a relationship maintained by cultivated connection rather
than a passive state of belief. Cognate concepts recur across traditions without
being identical to it: Sanskrit *dharma*, Chinese *dao*, Arabic *dīn*, and Greek
*eusebeia* and *threskeia* each name a religious-ethical order that overlaps with
*religio* while carrying its own irreducible content. The framework works
principally with the Latin term while engaging these through comparison.

## Defining religion

Defining religion has been one of the standing problems of the academic study of
religion, and no definition commands consensus. E. B. Tylor (*Primitive Culture*,
1871) offered the minimal "belief in spiritual beings." Émile Durkheim (*The
Elementary Forms of Religious Life*, 1912) proposed a social-functional account:
"a unified system of beliefs and practices relative to sacred things ... which
unite into one single moral community ... all those who adhere to them." Clifford
Geertz ("Religion as a Cultural System," 1966) read religion as a system of
symbols that establishes pervasive moods and motivations; Mircea Eliade organised
it around the distinction between sacred and profane; Ninian Smart mapped it
across seven dimensions (doctrinal, mythological, ethical, ritual, experiential,
institutional, material); and Talal Asad (*Genealogies of Religion*, 1993) argued
that "religion" as a universal category is itself a modern Western construct,
cautioning against treating any single definition as neutral. These accounts
differ over whether religion is essentially belief, practice, feeling, symbol, or
social function — a disagreement the framework reads as a symptom of asking what
religion resembles rather than what, historically, it is about.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework reads religion as the cultivated bond between humanity and its
makers. Where the biblical *elohim* are taken not as a supernatural singular God
but as a plurality of embodied creators (see [Elohim](../elohim/) and
[Plurality of Gods](../plurality-of-gods/)), religion becomes the institution that
maintains the link to them. Vorilhon states the etymological reading plainly:

> *"Religion from the Latin religio means 'that which links' or 'the tie', which
> unites the creators to their creation."*

Read this way, the world's scriptures, rituals, and priesthoods are the preserved
record of contact with a real civilization — stored in the mythological and
supernatural vocabularies available to the cultures that received it, and so
distorted in transmission, but recoverable by a reader who recognises the
historical reality behind the theological framing. This is the
[euhemerist method](../neo-euhemerism/) applied to religion as a whole: the gods
of myth are read as garbled memory of a technologically advanced civilization
mistaken for the divine. The framework holds a majority of established traditions
to preserve, in varying states of clarity and corruption, authentic testimony of
such contact — a claim worked out tradition by tradition in the
[list of prophets and religions](../list-of-prophets-and-religions/). These are
foundational premises of the framework, not conclusions mainstream scholarship
shares, and the corpus states them as such.

The reading does not require dismissing the naturalistic accounts of religion.
Religion plainly has social, symbolic, and experiential dimensions, and has been
put to ideological use. What the framework adds is a referent: something specific
the traditions are *about*. The academic definitions describe how religion
operates; the framework's claim concerns what, on its reading, religion originally
cultivated a link to.

**Religion as an "atheist religion."** The clearest modern statement of the
framework's position is the [Raëlian](../raelism/) self-description as an "atheist
religion" — religion in the *religare* sense of a cultivated bond between creators
and created, atheist in the sense of denying any supernatural divinity, since the
Elohim are biological beings rather than gods:

> *"the Raelian Movement is a religion; it ties the creators of Humanity with
> their creation, even if it is in fact an atheist religion, in the sense that it
> does not believe in the existence of a God — atheist from the Greek atheos which
> means 'denying the existence of any form of divinity'."*

The formulation sits deliberately athwart the standard religious-studies typology,
occupying the religious and the atheist categories at once.

**Sendy and the priesthood as link-keepers.** The framework's *religare* reading
was anticipated by [Jean Sendy](../jean-sendy/), whose *L'ère du Verseau* (1970)
treats the etymology as recovering the original purpose of religion against its
later ideological reputation:

> *"« Religion » vient du latin religare, qui veut dire « relier ». Or depuis que
> Lénine a dit que la religion est l'opium du peuple, l'usage s'est établi
> d'oublier que l'étymologie définit parfaitement l'objet initial des religions :
> maintenir le lien entre « les cieux » et la Terre."*
>
> ("'Religion' comes from the Latin *religare*, 'to link.' Yet since Lenin said
> that religion is the opium of the people, it has become customary to forget that
> the etymology perfectly defines the original object of religions: to maintain the
> link between 'the heavens' and the Earth.")

Sendy's supporting observation is that the priesthoods of the earliest
civilizations were not merely instruments of social control but working
mathematicians, astronomers, and architects who spent their lives preserving
precise astronomical and calendrical knowledge — labour, he argues, that a mere
"spiritual opium" would never have required. In a characteristic inversion he
casts modern scientific research as the functional heir of that ancient priestly
work: both are disciplined efforts to maintain the link between Earth and the
wider cosmos. The framework reads the cross-cultural recurrence of
priesthood-architecture-ritual complexes — Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Vedic,
Greco-Roman, Chinese, Mesoamerican, and the shamanic traditions — as the same
link-keeping function surfacing under many cultural framings, carried (on the
framework's reading) by the astronomical inheritance of the pre-flood
[Eden](../eden/) lineage rather than arising independently in each place.

**The first religious community.** The corpus places the origin of
religion-as-institution earlier than the temple cults of the first cities. On its
reading of the flood, [Noah](../noah/)'s pre-flood community — the small group who
knew a catastrophe was coming and worked to bring life through it — was the first
religious community in the strict *religare* sense: bound to the exiled
[Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction, the [Serpent](../serpent/) of the Eden disclosure
and the creators who had sided with their creation, by shared work on a
preservation project. That faction is distinct from the abolitionist party led by
[Satan](../satan/), which held the human creation a mistake and argued for its
destruction; the two are opposite positions, not the same fallen figure. The
religious meaning later tradition attached to Noah is, on this reading, the
cultural memory of that operational alliance.

**Religion and revelation.** The framework distinguishes religion — the ongoing
cultivated relationship — from revelation, the contact-events that supply its
content. That content is delivered through the [prophetic](../prophet/) institution
— roughly forty messengers across human history, from Adam through Noah, Abraham,
Moses, Buddha, Zoroaster, Jesus, and Muhammad, concluding in transformed form with
[Raël](../rael/) — while religion is the institutional practice by which each
revelation is preserved and transmitted across the generations. A recurring
corollary concerns the categories *monotheism* and *polytheism*: the framework
reads the ancient contests remembered as "one God against many" less as arithmetic
than as struggles over memory and allegiance, with a confession of "one" true as a
matter of one authorised command and one direction of loyalty without being a
discovery that the makers were only ever one (see
[Monotheism Is the Wrong Question](/articles/monotheism-is-the-wrong-question/)).

## Comparative and critical perspectives

The academic study of religion offers a range of naturalistic accounts of what
religion is and why it recurs, and the framework's reading sits alongside them as a
minority position rather than in place of them.

- **Sociology of religion.** From Max Weber and Durkheim through Peter Berger,
  Robert Bellah, and José Casanova, the sociological tradition treats religion
  through its social functions and historical development, including the long
  secularization debate over religion's changing place in modern societies. It
  documents, in detail, the social work religion does; the framework takes that
  description as real without treating it as the whole story.
- **Cognitive science of religion.** Pascal Boyer, Scott Atran, Justin Barrett,
  and Harvey Whitehouse explain religious belief as a by-product of ordinary
  cognitive machinery — hyperactive agency detection, theory of mind, and the
  memory dynamics of ritual — on which account religion needs no external referent
  to arise. The framework grants the mechanisms while holding that they describe
  how contact-memory would be received and transmitted, not that there was nothing
  to receive.
- **Religion as ideology.** The Marx–Lenin tradition reads religion as "the opium
  of the people," a consolation that masks and reproduces social domination. Sendy
  engages this tradition directly as the foil for his *religare* reconstruction:
  religion can be turned to ideological use, he grants, without that exhausting what
  it originally was.
- **The New Atheist critique.** Richard Dawkins, Christopher Hitchens, Sam Harris,
  and Daniel Dennett treat religion principally as cognitive error and social harm.
  The framework shares their rejection of supernatural divinity — hence "atheist
  religion" — while parting company on whether the traditions preserve anything
  real beneath the theology.
- **Perennialism.** René Guénon, Frithjof Schuon, and Huston Smith read the
  religions as many expressions of a single transcendent truth. The framework's
  disagreement is specific: its unifying referent is not a transcendent Absolute
  but a particular embodied civilization, and it preserves the irreducible
  differences between traditions rather than resolving them into one.

Two judgments should be kept apart, as with the euhemerist method generally. The
academic description of religion — its social forms, its cognitive substrate, its
histories — is largely compatible with the framework and is drawn on throughout the
corpus. The framework's interpretive claim — that a real civilization stands behind
the traditions — is not endorsed by mainstream scholarship, which classes the wider
ancient-astronaut reading as pseudoscience. The corpus holds that claim openly as a
minority position, anchored in specific texts and labelled by claim type, and
treats the naturalistic accounts as fair descriptions of the phenomenon rather than
as adversaries to be dismissed.
