**New Jerusalem** is, in the Wheel of Heaven framework, one of three names — alongside
[Third Temple](../third-temple/) and [Embassy](../embassy/) — for the single structure
the [Elohim](../elohim/) have asked humanity to build to receive their formal return.
The three names describe the same building from three angles. *New Jerusalem* is its
Christian-apocalyptic name, taken from the holy city that descends from heaven in
Revelation 21. *Third Temple* is its Jewish-historical name, successor to Solomon's
First Temple (destroyed by the Babylonians in 586 BCE) and the Second Temple (destroyed
by the Romans in 70 CE). *Embassy* is its operational name — the reception residence
with extraterritorial status whose architecture the source material lays out in detail.
The corpus reaches for whichever name suits the passage at hand, but all three point to
one physical thing: the seat of open Elohim–human contact in the
[Age of Aquarius](../timeline/age-of-aquarius/).

The phrase is scriptural, and [Yahweh](../yahweh/) uses it directly. In Claude
Vorilhon's *Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet* (1975), in the passage addressed
"To the People of Israel," the request is made explicit:

> *"The State of Israel must give some territory located near Jerusalem to the Guide of
> Guides so that he may build there the residence, the embassy of the Elohim. The time
> has come, people of Israel, to build the New Jerusalem as it was foreseen. Claude Rael
> is the one who was foretold. Reread your writings and open your eyes."*

Three moves are folded together here: a request for land near Jerusalem with
extraterritorial status; the identification of that building with the New Jerusalem of
Revelation 21; and the claim that the request arrives through a prophet whose coming had
been foretold.

## The Revelation 21 vision

Revelation 21 opens the New Testament's closing vision. John of Patmos, writing in the
late first century CE, describes what he is shown:

> *"And I saw a new heaven and a new earth: for the first heaven and the first earth were
> passed away; and there was no more sea. And I John saw the holy city, new Jerusalem,
> coming down from God out of heaven, prepared as a bride adorned for her husband. And I
> heard a great voice out of heaven saying, Behold, the tabernacle of God is with men,
> and he will dwell with them, and they shall be his people, and God himself shall be
> with them, and be their God."*
>
> — Revelation 21:1–3 (KJV)

The chapter goes on to measure the city: twelve foundations bearing the names of the
twelve apostles, a perfect cube twelve thousand furlongs on a side, a wall of jasper,
foundations of precious stones, gates of pearl, streets of pure gold (Revelation
21:9–21). In the mainstream Christian reading this is the crowning image of the
*eschaton* — the perfected City of God arriving at the end of history, when heaven and
earth are made new.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework reads the descending city as a building, not a miracle. What John saw, and
recorded in the only vocabulary first-century apocalyptic gave him, is the Embassy: the
residence the Elohim asked humanity to prepare, and their return to occupy it. The line
that follows the descent names exactly what the Embassy is for — *"the tabernacle of God
is with men, and he will dwell with them."* That is the whole purpose of the structure:
a place where the Elohim land openly and take up settled, continuous contact with the
humanity they created. On this reading the precious-stone architecture is John's attempt
to render an advanced building he had no words for; the descent "out of heaven" is the
arrival of the alliance's craft; and the "no more sea" is the transition between worlds
seen from a departing vantage. The [Apocalypse](../apocalypse/), understood this way,
culminates not in destruction but in construction — a city built, a residence occupied,
the makers dwelling with their creation as a lasting arrangement rather than a passing
theophany.

Two further passages point the corpus at the same event. **Ezekiel 47:1–2** shows water
issuing from under the threshold of the temple and running eastward, deepening into a
river that heals the Dead Sea and makes everything it touches live. The framework reads
this as the image of the New Jerusalem as a source — the point from which the Aquarian
age's knowledge and renewal flow outward to the world, the Ezekiel river made
institutional. **Acts 15:16** — *"After this I will return, and will build again the
tabernacle of David, which is fallen down"* — carries the same two movements the
framework tracks throughout: a return after departure, and a rebuilding of what had
fallen. On the corpus reading the "tabernacle of David" is the whole Elohim–Israel
arrangement of the earlier ages, and its rebuilding is the Embassy's completion.

### The site: near Jerusalem, then anywhere

Yahweh's stated preference was for Israel, near Jerusalem — the ground of the original
[Eden](../eden/) operation and the long Eden-lineage project that followed it. The
passage to the people of Israel frames the grant as a last chance, warning that a refusal
would send the Embassy elsewhere "as happened 2,000 years ago" — Vorilhon's reading of
the rejection narrative repeating itself.

The Raëlian Movement made its first formal request in November 1991, to the Chief Rabbi
in Jerusalem, with further requests to the prime minister and other officials. An Israeli
government commission in 1993 found the movement peaceful and no threat to Israel's
security; two rabbis on it reportedly advised that "it would be better not to do anything
against Rael in case he really is the awaited Messiah." Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin
declined the request, and no later administration reopened it.

On 13 December 1997 — the twenty-fourth anniversary of the first 1973 contact — a message
attributed to Yahweh released the project from Israel:

> *"We must from now on ask for the necessary permission and extra-territorial status from
> all nations on Earth for the erection of our Embassy."*

Israel keeps a final option — *"for the last time, a short period of reflection"* — but the
Embassy may now be built in any country that grants the required status. The same message
also reidentifies the chosen people: the "real Jews on Earth," it says, are no longer the
people of Israel by descent but those who recognize the Elohim as their creators — the
Raëlians. The corpus records this as the source's own claim, a shift from lineage to
recognition; mainstream Judaism does not accept it, and the entry marks it as canon
rather than consensus.

### What the New Jerusalem is for

Beyond the reception itself, the source casts the site as "the spiritual and intellectual
center of the world for millennia to come." From it the Aquarian age's knowledge,
technology, and reorganization are to radiate: pilgrimage from every nation, research and
educational institutions clustering around it, and a public replica open to visitors
alongside the working Embassy so ordinary people can see the site without disturbing its
operations. The building's specifications belong to the [Embassy](../embassy/) entry, but
their scale fits the role — an extraterritorial site of roughly four square kilometers
(land or water, with navigation barred in any water portion), a conference room and
dining room, seven guest rooms, a swimming pool, a landing platform for the alliance
craft, and a surrounding wall no more than two stories high.

## Comparative traditions

The New Jerusalem stands where several older sacred-city expectations meet. The framework
reads them in dialogue with the canon, preserving each tradition's own terms rather than
collapsing them into one.

**The Jewish Third Temple.** Jewish tradition anticipates a Third Temple succeeding the
First and Second, its most detailed blueprint the nine-chapter vision of Ezekiel 40–48.
The contemporary Third Temple movement — the Temple Institute (founded 1987), the Temple
Mount Faithful, and related organizations — actively prepares vessels, priestly training,
and architectural plans, though the historic site is occupied by the Dome of the Rock and
the al-Aqsa Mosque, the third-holiest site in Islam. The corpus's position differs from
both the rabbinic mainstream and the activist movement: it does not require the Temple
Mount specifically, since the source's preference for Israel-near-Jerusalem is not a
requirement for that exact platform, and its New Jerusalem is the Aquarian-age Embassy
with its own specifications.

**The Christian heavenly Jerusalem.** Beyond Revelation 21–22, the New Testament already
speaks of "the heavenly Jerusalem" as a present reality for the community (Hebrews 12:22),
and the theme runs through Augustine's *City of God* into the later theological tradition.
Christian eschatology divides over what the descending city means — premillennialists
read it as a literal future city, amillennialists as a symbol of the Church or the
redeemed, postmillennialists variously — but in each case it is a theological image. The
framework reads the same tradition as a Christian rendering of the New Jerusalem it
identifies with the Embassy.

**The Mormon New Jerusalem.** The Latter-day Saint tradition names a specific site:
Joseph Smith's 1831 revelation (Doctrine and Covenants 57) locates the New Jerusalem at
Independence, Missouri, to be built in preparation for Christ's return. The parallel to
the corpus's reading is structural — a prophet designating a particular divinely appointed
place — while the content is entirely distinct. The two are noted side by side, not merged.

**Islamic al-Quds and the wider pattern.** Islam holds Jerusalem — *al-Quds*, "the Holy" —
as the site of Muhammad's Night Journey (Quran 17:1) and of the Dome of the Rock
(691–692 CE) and al-Aqsa Mosque. Beyond the Abrahamic sphere, sacred-center traditions
recur across the world — Varanasi and the Hindu Sapta Puri, Bodhgaya and the Buddhist
pilgrimage sites, Teotihuacan and Tenochtitlan in Mesoamerica, and many more.
Comparative-religion scholarship explains the recurrence through shared human cognition,
cultural diffusion, and independent elaboration of common concerns, and is right to
caution against reading one event behind every version. The framework does not reject that
account; it adds, as interpretation, that the recurrence may also preserve cultural memory
of real sites of Elohim presence, of which the New Jerusalem would be the Aquarian-age
recovery. That further reading is `speculative`, offered transparently as such.

## Critical perspectives

The framework's reading is not the scholarly one, and the entry marks the difference
plainly. Academic study of Revelation — Bauckham, Beale, Koester, Aune — reads chapter 21
as first-century apocalyptic literature written into the pressures of Roman imperial rule.
Its descending city is understood as a symbol: of the Church, of the redeemed community,
or of a literal future act of God, but in every case a theological image rather than the
description of a building to be constructed. Mainstream Judaism, for its part, does not
accept the identification of a Raëlian Embassy with Ezekiel's Third Temple, which it holds
to belong to the messianic age rather than to a diplomatic residence in an unnamed
country.

The corpus does not dispute that these are the consensus positions. It presents the
immanent, physical reading — the descending city as the Embassy, the Elohim's return as
the event Revelation 21 preserves — as the framework's own: a claim explicit in the
Raëlian canon and foreign to the scholarship. The two readings are left standing beside
each other. The corpus's reading does not require dismissing the mainstream symbolic or
eschatological interpretation; it adds a reading the mainstream does not make, and is
honest that the addition rests on the canon rather than on academic agreement.
