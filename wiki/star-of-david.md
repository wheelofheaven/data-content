**The Star of David** (Hebrew מָגֵן דָּוִד, *Magen David*, "Shield of David") is a
six-pointed star formed by two interlocked equilateral triangles — a
**hexagram**. It is today the best-known symbol of Judaism and the central charge
on the flag of Israel, but that identification is comparatively recent: the
figure became the principal Jewish symbol only in the early-modern and modern
periods, and the hexagram itself is far older and appears across many unrelated
traditions. In the Wheel of Heaven framework the Star of David is the outer
component of the [Raëlian Symbol of Infinity](../raelian-symbol-of-infinity/),
where the two interlocked triangles carry the meaning [Yahweh](../yahweh/)
assigns them — "as above, so below," the correspondence of the very large and the
very small that the [Infinity](../infinity/) framework calls its spatial
dimension. To that reading [Jean Sendy](../jean-sendy/) adds a second: that the
same figure, the seal of Solomon, marks the alliance between the creators and
their human creation, the upward triangle standing for "the heavens" and the
downward for "men."

## Etymology and naming

The Hebrew *Magen David* means literally "Shield of David" — *magen*, "shield" or
"defender," and *David*, the biblical king from whom Jewish royal tradition
descends. The name carries medieval kabbalistic and protective associations,
tying the hexagram to David's guardianship of his people. Through the medieval
period the same figure was more often called the **Seal of Solomon** (Hebrew
*Chotam Shlomo*, Arabic *Khatam Sulaymān*), a name it bore in Jewish, Christian,
and Islamic magical and protective use alike, from the legend of Solomon's
mastery over spirits. As a neutral geometric term it is the **hexagram** (Greek
*hexagrammon*, "six-line figure"); in Hindu tradition the same shape is the
**Shatkona** (Sanskrit षट्कोण, "six-cornered"). "Star of David" — *Étoile de
David*, *Davidstern*, and their equivalents — is the standard modern name in
European languages and dates, as the ordinary designation, from roughly the
nineteenth century.

## History of the symbol

The hexagram is a simple, striking figure, and cultures far removed from one
another arrived at it independently. It appears in Mesopotamian and ancient
Mediterranean material — Greek, Etruscan, and Roman — in decorative and cult
contexts, and some scholars read hexagram-like figures in Indus Valley artifacts,
though those identifications are contested. Through the medieval period the shape
turns up in Jewish synagogue architecture, manuscript illumination, and ritual
objects, but as one motif among many, largely within the Seal of Solomon
tradition rather than as an exclusive marker of Jewish identity; the older Jewish
emblems were the seven-branched menorah and the lion of Judah.

Its consolidation as *the* Jewish symbol was gradual and then sudden. The Karaite
community made early use of it; the Jewish community of Prague adopted it
prominently from the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries — Charles IV granted the
community a flag bearing the hexagram in 1357 — and Prague usage carried it across
Central Europe. In 1648 the Vienna community received the right to a flag with the
same figure. The decisive step was political and modern: the Zionist movement,
and Theodor Herzl in particular, took up the hexagram as the movement's emblem,
and the First Zionist Congress at Basel in 1897 formally adopted it. From that
point the Star of David stood, as no earlier symbol had, for the Jewish people as
a whole.

The twentieth century gave the symbol its gravest and its proudest associations.
Under the Nazi regime the six-pointed star was imposed as a compulsory identifying
badge — yellow, often lettered *Jude* — first in occupied Poland from 1939 and
then across occupied Europe under the decree of September 1941, an instrument of
segregation that preceded mass murder. In 1948 the new State of Israel placed a
blue Magen David at the center of its flag, between two stripes recalling the
prayer shawl. The symbol's modern history thus runs from persecution to statehood
within a single decade, and both meanings remain carried in it.

The foundational scholarly account of this history is Gershom Scholem's ("Magen
David: Toldoteha shel Semel," 1948; in English "The Star of David: History of a
Symbol," 1971). Scholem established that the hexagram's identification as a Jewish
symbol is recent — crystallizing in the seventeenth century and after — while the
figure itself long predates any Jewish use; later work by Joseph Gutmann and
Steven Fine has extended his account. This is the mainstream consensus.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework reads the Star of David not as a Jewish invention but as one of the
[Elohim](../elohim/)'s own emblems, preserved across many cultures because the
creators used it. In [*The Book Which Tells the
Truth*](../../library/the-book-which-tells-the-truth/) [Yahweh](../yahweh/) points
to it as engraved on the craft and on his suit:

> "The emblem you see engraved on this machine and on my suit represents the
> truth. It is also the emblem of the Jewish people, the Star of David, which
> means: 'That which is above is like that which is below', and in its center is
> the swastika, which means that everything is cyclic, the top becoming the
> bottom, and the bottom in turn becoming the top. The origins and destiny of the
> creators and human beings are similar and linked."

The two interlocked triangles carry the Hermetic principle of correspondence
across scales: the upward triangle for "what is above" — the planetary, stellar,
and galactic scales — and the downward triangle for "what is below" — the
cellular, molecular, and atomic. The same patterns of organization recur at every
scale, which is what the [Infinity](../infinity/) framework means by its spatial
dimension, developed under [Fractal Cosmology](../fractal-cosmology/). Yahweh
restates it in *Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet*: "The Star of David,
which is composed of two intertwined triangles, means 'as above, so below,'" the
whole emblem — star and swastika together — "contain[ing] all the wisdom in the
world."

[Jean Sendy](../jean-sendy/), in *L'ère du Verseau* (1970), reads a second meaning
into the same figure. The seal of Solomon, he argues, records the
[alliance](../the-alliance/) between the creators and humanity:

> "This identity between 'that which is above' and 'that which is below' is
> recalled by the seal of Solomon: two identical triangles, that of 'the heavens'
> pointing up, and that of 'men' pointing down, intertwined to recall the promised
> 'alliance.'"

On Sendy's reading the upward triangle is the creators, the downward is humanity,
and their inseparable interlocking is the covenant that binds them — a bond that,
like the figure itself, cannot be pulled apart without destroying it. The two
readings are complementary rather than rival: the mirror symmetry states the
cosmic correspondence, the interlocking states the covenant.

## The Raëlian emblem and the 1990 change

The Star of David does not stand alone in Raëlian use. In the emblem Yahweh
delivered to Raël at the 1973 contact — the [Raëlian Symbol of
Infinity](../raelian-symbol-of-infinity/) — the hexagram is the outer figure and a
[swastika](../swastika/) sits at its center, the swastika carrying the temporal,
cyclic half of the meaning as the star carries the spatial. The swastika in that
emblem is the ancient auspicious sign found across Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain
practice and in many older cultures, not the Nazi form; but in the West the two
are not easily held apart.

In 1990 Raël proposed, and states that the Elohim agreed, to change the emblem for
use by the movement's Western branches, replacing the central swastika with a
galaxy-shaped spiral. Raël gives two reasons: to assist the movement's efforts to
negotiate a site in Israel for the projected [Embassy](../embassy/), and — in his
own words — out of respect "for the sensitivities of the victims who suffered and
died under the Nazi swastika during the Second World War." The original emblem,
with the swastika, remains in use in the movement's Asian branches, where, as Raël
notes, "the swastika can be found in most Buddhist temples" and carries no such
association. The change was presented as a matter of iconographic tact rather than
of doctrine: the spiral and the swastika are held to mean the same thing —
infinity in time — so the framework's content is unaffected. The full history of
the emblem and its two forms is treated in the [Raëlian Symbol of
Infinity](../raelian-symbol-of-infinity/) entry.

## Comparative and critical perspectives

The hexagram's spread invites the framework's comparative claim and also its main
challenge. In Hindu tradition the **Shatkona** joins an upward triangle for Shiva,
pure consciousness, to a downward triangle for Shakti, active energy — the union
of male and female principles — and it structures the great **Sri Yantra** of
Tantric practice. The Hermetic-alchemical tradition read the same figure as the
marriage of opposites, its two triangles the elements fire (pointing up) and water
(pointing down), and articulated the "as above, so below" of the *Tabula
Smaragdina* — "That which is below is like that which is above" — that the Raëlian
source echoes almost verbatim. As the Seal of Solomon the hexagram served
protective and magical ends across medieval Jewish, Christian, and Islamic
practice. The Raëlian source itself points to the *Bardo Thodol*, the Tibetan Book
of the Dead, as a place where star and swastika appear together, "and in many
other writings as well."

The framework reads this recurrence as memory: the same emblem surfacing in many
traditions because the creators used it, each culture keeping it in its own idiom.
Mainstream scholarship reads the recurrence more economically — the hexagram is a
simple figure that many cultures could and did devise independently, elaborated
further by limited diffusion and shared visual instinct — and it is right that a
shape this basic needs no single origin to explain its spread. On the specifically
Jewish history the framework and the scholarship agree: with Scholem, both hold the
Jewish-symbol identification to be modern and the figure itself far older. Where
they part is Sendy's covenant reading, which is an interpretation internal to the
framework, not one proposed by or found in mainstream Jewish or art-historical
scholarship; the corpus presents it as its own reading rather than as established
history.
