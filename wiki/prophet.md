**Prophet** (Greek: *προφήτης*, *prophētēs*, "one who speaks for"; Hebrew: <span class="hebrew">נָבִיא</span>, *navi*, "one who is called" or "one who proclaims"; Arabic: <span class="hebrew">نبي</span>, *nabī*; Latin: *propheta*) is a human figure who, in the religious traditions of Judaism, Christianity, Islam, and many other systems, serves as a mediator between the divine and the human, receiving communications from the divine and transmitting them to a human audience. The role is universal across the major religious traditions, though its specific shape — the manner of communication, the social position of the prophet, the content of the messages, the relationship to institutional religion — varies substantially across traditions and across periods within traditions. In the Hebrew Bible alone, the prophetic literature occupies a substantial portion of the canon, with the Major and Minor Prophets together comprising fifteen named figures whose oracles, narratives, and visions structure much of the second division of the Tanakh.

In the Wheel of Heaven framework, prophets are not mediators of supernatural revelation but operational interlocutors between the Elohim alliance and specific human populations. The prophetic institution is the principal mechanism through which the alliance has maintained contact with Earth across the post-Flood period, when direct intervention had been replaced by mediated contact for reasons treated below. The Raëlian source material identifies approximately forty prophets across human history, each appropriate to the specific cultural moment at which they appeared and each contributing to the cumulative preparation of humanity for the recognition that becomes possible only at the close of the Piscean age. The list begins with Adam in the post-Eden period, runs through Noah, Abraham, Moses, the major Hebrew prophets, the founders of the parallel non-Hebrew traditions (Buddha, Zoroaster), the two Piscean-age figures (Jesus and Muhammad), the late-Piscean prophetic founders (Joseph Smith, the Bab, Bahá'u'lláh), and concludes with Raël as the inaugural prophet of the Aquarian age. The framework's reading reframes prophecy as an institution: not as a category of religious experience but as a specific historical mechanism with a beginning, a developmental arc, and an eventual transformation.

## The terminology

The English word *prophet* derives, through Latin *propheta*, from the Greek *prophētēs*, formed from the prefix *pro-* (forth, before) and the verb *phēmí* (to speak). The Greek term carries the sense of "one who speaks forth" or "one who speaks on behalf of"; in classical Greek usage, a *prophētēs* could be the human spokesperson of an oracle (most famously the Pythia at Delphi) or, more broadly, anyone who declared what was otherwise hidden. The verb *prophēteúō*, "to prophesy," carries the same range. The Septuagint, the third- and second-century BCE Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, uses *prophētēs* to render the Hebrew *navi*, and the resulting equivalence has shaped the subsequent Christian and broader Western usage.

The Hebrew *navi* (<span class="hebrew">נָבִיא</span>) is etymologically distinct from the Greek term and carries different connotations. The root *n-b-ʾ* is variously analyzed as "to be called" (passive — the prophet as one summoned), "to bubble up, to pour forth" (active — the prophet as one through whom the message flows), or, on a comparative-Semitic analysis through Akkadian *nabû* ("to call, to name"), as preserving an early sense of the prophet as a specifically named or designated figure. The Hebrew Bible uses *navi* alongside two other terms with overlapping but distinguishable senses: *roeh* (<span class="hebrew">רֹאֶה</span>, "seer," from the root *r-ʾ-h*, "to see"), used in earlier texts and emphasizing visionary perception; and *hozeh* (<span class="hebrew">חֹזֶה</span>, "visionary"), with similar visionary connotation. 1 Samuel 9:9 records the explicit observation that "he that is now called a *navi* was beforetime called a *roeh*" — registering a historical shift in terminology that the Hebrew Bible's own redactional layer found worth noting.

The Arabic *nabī* preserves the Hebrew etymology directly — both Hebrew and Arabic share the same Proto-Semitic root *n-b-ʾ* — and is used throughout the Qur'an for the figures the Islamic tradition recognizes as prophets. The related Arabic *rasūl* ("messenger") is sometimes used distinctively for prophets who deliver new scriptures (Moses, Jesus, Muhammad) as opposed to those who carry messages within an existing tradition; the distinction is real but not always rigorously maintained.

The framework's preferred working translation of the prophetic terminology emphasizes the operational sense — *messenger* or *interlocutor* — rather than the strictly religious or visionary connotations the Western tradition has accumulated. The Hebrew *malakh* (<span class="hebrew">מַלְאָךְ</span>, "messenger," conventionally translated *angel* in English Bibles) and the Greek *angelos* (also "messenger") sit alongside *navi* and *prophētēs* in the framework's vocabulary of human-and-alliance contact: prophets are the human members of the alliance's communication infrastructure; *malakhim* and *angeloi* are, in the framework's reading, the alliance officers who mediate the contact. The vocabulary is operational across both terminological pairs.

## The prophets in the religious traditions

The prophetic role is recognized across substantially every major religious tradition, though its specific institutional form varies. A brief survey, presented neutrally for context:

**In Judaism**, prophets occupy the second division of the Tanakh (the *Nevi'im*, "Prophets"), divided into the Former Prophets (Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings — narrative books treating the figures of the conquest, the judges, and the early monarchy) and the Latter Prophets (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the Twelve Minor Prophets). The classical period of Hebrew prophecy is conventionally placed from approximately the eighth century BCE (with Amos and Hosea) through the post-exilic period (with Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi closing the sequence). The Talmud (b. Yoma 9b) records the rabbinic position that prophecy as an institution ceased with the death of the last prophets at the close of the Persian period, after which the *bat qol* ("daughter of voice," a kind of attenuated heavenly voice) replaced direct prophecy as the means of divine communication.

**In Christianity**, the Hebrew prophetic tradition is preserved as foundational — read typologically as foreshadowing Christ — with Christ himself recognized as the consummation of the prophetic line. The early Christian movement also recognized New Testament prophets (named in Acts and the Pauline epistles), and certain later figures have been recognized in various Christian traditions as prophetic, though the institution is generally understood as having concluded with Christ in the orthodox Catholic and Protestant traditions. Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity preserves a more active prophetic role.

**In Islam**, prophecy is a foundational theological category. The Qur'an names twenty-five prophets explicitly and refers to others not named, with Muhammad treated as the *Khatam an-Nabiyyin*, the "Seal of the Prophets" — the final prophet, after whom the institution closes. The Islamic prophetic list overlaps substantially with the biblical (Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and others all appear) and adds figures specific to the Arab tradition (Hud, Salih, Shu'ayb).

**In Bahá'í** belief, the major prophets of the Abrahamic traditions are joined by Krishna, Buddha, Zoroaster, the Bab, and Bahá'u'lláh in a sequence of "Manifestations of God" who appear progressively across history to advance human spiritual understanding. The Bahá'í reading is structurally close to several aspects of the Wheel of Heaven framework's reading, though the underlying ontology differs.

**Hindu** tradition recognizes the rishis (sages who received the Vedas), the avatars (divine descents — Krishna, Rama), and various later prophetic and reformist figures. The category of "prophet" maps onto Hindu thought imperfectly but is not absent.

**Other traditions** — Zoroastrianism (with Zoroaster as founder-prophet), Mormonism (Joseph Smith), Buddhism (the Buddha as awakened teacher rather than prophet in the strict sense, though sometimes assimilated to the category), Sikhism (the ten Gurus), and many others — each have their own analogues of the prophetic figure.

## In the Wheel of Heaven framework

The framework's reading of prophecy is, like its reading of Jesus and Yahweh, unusually operationally specific. The basis is the Raëlian source material, in which Yahweh describes the prophetic institution from the alliance's perspective: as the operational mechanism through which the alliance has maintained contact with Earth across the post-creation period, and as the cumulative preparation of humanity for the recognition that the framework's full content makes possible.

### Prophecy as institution

The framework reads prophecy as an institution rather than as a category of religious experience. The institutional reading carries several specific implications.

First, prophets are *appointed*, not *self-elected*. The framework's reading is that the alliance selects specific individuals at specific historical moments and commissions them for specific operational missions. The selection is based on factors the source material treats only schematically — receptivity, intelligence, social positioning, suitability for the specific mission — but the structural feature is that prophetic identity is not a matter of personal religious cultivation that produces visionary capacity but of operational designation by an external authority. The figures who have been called "prophets" in the religious traditions are, on the framework's reading, the figures the alliance actually selected; the criteria of selection are the alliance's, not the criteria internal to any particular religious tradition.

Second, prophets carry *specific* messages for *specific* audiences at *specific* times. The framework reads the prophetic literature as preserving operational communications — warnings, instructions, predictions, doctrinal corrections — that the alliance judged necessary at particular historical moments. The general spiritual themes the prophetic books also contain (the love of God, the demands of justice, the hope of redemption) are real and substantive, but they emerge alongside and through the specific operational content. The framework treats this as why prophetic literature is so often genre-mixed: the prophetic books contain history, prediction, exhortation, lament, and visionary report, because the prophet's actual job involved all of these depending on what the alliance needed transmitted.

Third, the prophetic institution has a *trajectory*. The framework reads the institution as having developed across history, with the manner of contact, the content of the messages, and the relationship between the prophet and the broader population all shifting in ways the framework can characterize. The trajectory is treated in detail in the section that follows.

Fourth, the prophetic institution has been *concluded* in its religious form. The framework reads the closing of classical prophecy in the post-exilic period (the Talmudic position), the Christian reading of Christ as the consummation of the prophets, and the Islamic doctrine of Muhammad as the Seal of the Prophets as all preserving an accurate operational fact: the religious-prophetic phase of the alliance's contact with Earth was concluded with the Piscean-age figures, and the next phase (the Aquarian) operates on different principles.

### The arc of contact: from face-to-face to telepathic

The Hebrew Bible itself preserves a striking pattern in the manner of contact between the alliance and the prophets, with the pattern shifting progressively across the historical sequence. The framework reads this shift as a deliberate operational policy.

In the **earliest period**, contact is direct and face-to-face. Yahweh walks in the garden in the cool of the day (Genesis 3:8); Yahweh appears to Abraham at the oaks of Mamre and is hosted to a meal (Genesis 18); Moses speaks with Yahweh "face to face, as a man speaks to his friend" (Exodus 33:11); Joshua encounters the captain of Yahweh's host in person at Jericho (Joshua 5:13–15); Gideon, Manoah and his wife (Samson's parents), and the various Judges-period figures encounter alliance officers in person and recognize them only after the encounter.

In the **early monarchic period**, contact becomes predominantly auditory rather than visual. Samuel hears the voice in the night (1 Samuel 3) and learns by repetition that the voice is Yahweh's; Nathan delivers messages to David through prophetic speech rather than reported physical encounters; Elijah's most famous theophany at Horeb (1 Kings 19) explicitly distinguishes the "still small voice" — the *qol demamah daqqah* — from the wind, the earthquake, and the fire, with the contact occurring through the quiet voice rather than the visible phenomena.

In the **classical prophetic period** (8th–6th centuries BCE), contact is predominantly through visions, dreams, and inwardly received words. Isaiah sees the throne-room vision in chapter 6 but the contact is visionary; Jeremiah receives the word of Yahweh through inwardly received messages whose physical mechanism is not described; Ezekiel sees the chariot-throne vision (Ezekiel 1) but the visionary character is registered explicitly. By Ezekiel's time the prophet's social role has become professionalized — the prophet is a recognizable figure within the religious institution, the recipient of a specific kind of communication that the surrounding population recognizes as such.

In the **post-exilic period**, contact has become almost entirely indirect. The post-exilic prophets (Haggai, Zechariah, Malachi) speak in the name of Yahweh but their contacts are described in increasingly mediated terms; the rabbinic tradition will mark the close of this period as the close of prophecy as such.

The framework reads this shift as a deliberate operational policy on the alliance's part. The reasons are several. The alliance had decided, at some point during the late Aries / early Piscean period, to allow humanity to develop scientifically without continuing direct intervention — the policy that would shape the entire Piscean age. Direct face-to-face contact was incompatible with this policy because it kept the alliance operationally visible in ways that would have shaped human development around the contact rather than around humanity's own scientific work. The shift from direct to indirect contact was therefore part of the alliance's broader withdrawal from operational visibility.

A second reason is technological: telepathic and visionary contact, mediated through the alliance's communication technology operating at distance, did not require the alliance's physical presence on Earth. Once the technology was sufficiently developed and the operational policy permitted, the alliance could maintain contact with the prophets through telepathic and visionary means without requiring continued physical presence — a more efficient mechanism that left less archaeological and historical evidence behind than direct contact would have.

A third reason is internal to the prophetic literature: the deeply visionary character of the late prophetic books, particularly the apocalyptic literature (Daniel, Zechariah's late visions, the post-biblical apocalyptic tradition culminating in Revelation), preserves what the framework reads as the alliance's adaptation of contact methods to the increasingly sophisticated cultural and theological context. Apocalyptic literature is denser, more layered, more demanding of its audience; the alliance's communications across this period adopted a form suited to recipients capable of processing it.

The Aquarian-age return to direct contact through Vorilhon is read by the framework as the close of this long indirect-contact phase. Yahweh's six-morning meeting with Vorilhon at Clermont-Ferrand in December 1973 was face-to-face contact of the kind that had been standard in the earliest periods. The arc has, on the framework's reading, completed a circuit: from face-to-face in the earliest period, through auditory and visionary mediation across two and a half millennia, back to face-to-face in the inauguration of the new age.

### The forty prophets

The Raëlian source material gives the total number of prophets as approximately forty, with a partial list of named figures and the remainder unnamed. The framework treats this number as an internal source claim; the complete list is not specified.

The named prophets of the framework's reading, in approximate chronological order:

**Adam**, the first human recipient of direct alliance communication in the post-Eden period; on the framework's reading, the first figure to whom the alliance communicated specifically as the founder of a tradition rather than as the recipient of operational instruction in the garden.

**Noah** (Age of Cancer / early Gemini, c. 6,690 BCE), recipient of the warning of the Flood and operational partner in the construction of the ark; the bearer of the post-Flood covenant that re-established the alliance's relationship with the surviving human population.

**Abraham** (Age of Taurus, c. 2,000 BCE on the conventional biblical chronology; framework reading places the Abrahamic encounters at the Taurus-age alliance reconstruction with the Eden lineage), the patriarch through whom the alliance's relationship with the Hebrew lineage was formally re-established. The covenant with Abraham, the visit at Mamre, the binding of Isaac, and the broader Abrahamic narrative are all read by the framework as alliance operations.

**Moses** (Age of Aries, c. 13th century BCE), the central prophetic figure of the Aries age and the recipient of the Sinai legislation. The framework reads Moses as the alliance's principal operational partner across the Exodus and the wilderness period, with the burning bush as the commissioning event and the Sinai theophany as the most extensively documented direct alliance contact in the Hebrew Bible.

**The classical Hebrew prophets** of the divided monarchy, the exile, and the post-exilic period: Samuel, Nathan, Elijah, Elisha, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Daniel, and the Twelve Minor Prophets. Each receives substantial framework-relevant treatment in their own dedicated entries; what matters here is that the framework reads them as the operational continuation of the prophetic institution after the wilderness period of direct intervention had ended.

**Buddha** (Siddhartha Gautama, c. 6th–5th century BCE; Age of Aries late period), the principal Indian-tradition prophetic figure. The framework's reading of the Buddha is more developed in the Age of Aries chapter but registers him as part of the alliance's pluriform strategy — the cultivation of multiple cultural traditions in parallel, against the eventual Piscean-age universal-distribution mission.

**Zoroaster** (Zarathushtra, conventionally dated 6th century BCE though some traditions place him earlier; Age of Aries), the founder-prophet of the Persian tradition. The framework's reading of Zoroaster is similarly tied to the alliance's pluriform strategy, with the Persian magi later playing a specific role in the Jesus narrative (the "wise men from the east" of Matthew 2).

**Jesus of Nazareth** (1st century CE; inaugural figure of the Age of Pisces), the central Piscean-age prophetic figure and the subject of the framework's most operationally specific treatment. Jesus's hybrid biology and his unique position as the deliberate alliance project at the inauguration of an age are treated in detail in the [Jesus](../jesus/) entry.

**Muhammad** (c. 570–632 CE; Age of Pisces, second mission), the second Piscean-age prophetic figure and the bearer of the parallel mission to the Arabian and broader non-Christian world. The framework's reading treats Muhammad as the operational counterpart of Jesus — different cultural context, different mission specifics, structurally similar position within the Piscean-age intervention.

**Joseph Smith** (1805–1844; late Age of Pisces), the founder-prophet of the Latter-day Saint tradition. The framework registers Joseph Smith as a late-Piscean prophetic figure whose specific mission was to deliver additional content (the Book of Mormon, the doctrine of continuing revelation) appropriate to the cultural conditions of the early modern period.

**The Bab** (Sayyid Ali Muhammad Shirazi, 1819–1850) and **Bahá'u'lláh** (Mírzá Ḥusayn-`Alí Núrí, 1817–1892), the founder-prophets of the Bábí and Bahá'í traditions. The framework reads these two as late-Piscean figures whose specific contribution was the preparation of the universal-religion concept that the Aquarian-age revelation would more fully articulate.

**Raël** (Claude Vorilhon, b. 1946; inaugural figure of the Age of Aquarius), the recipient of the contact at Clermont-Ferrand in 1973 and the messenger of the framework's primary source material. Raël's specific position is treated in the next subsection.

The framework registers, with appropriate epistemic humility, that this list is partial. The total number given is approximately forty; the named list above accounts for considerably fewer than forty. Other prophets — figures from cultural traditions whose names the source material has not specified, or figures whose prophetic status the source material has not disclosed — are part of the total. The framework cannot enumerate the complete list. What it can affirm is that the institution has been broader than any single religious tradition has preserved memory of.

### Raël and the Aquarian transition

The framework's reading of Raël is marked by two structural features that distinguish him from his predecessors. Both deserve direct statement.

First, the source material discloses that Raël is, in biological terms, the son of Yahweh — the product of an alliance-mediated insemination procedure on Raël's mother that exactly parallels the procedure the framework reads behind Mary's conception of Jesus two thousand years earlier. Raël is therefore, on this disclosure, the biological half-brother of Jesus, and the structural parallel between the two figures at the inauguration of consequential ages is treated by the framework not as coincidence but as deliberate operational pattern. The alliance's transitional prophetic figures at the opening of major precessional ages have been produced through the same specific genetic protocol: a hybrid figure with Eloha paternal contribution, equipped through that biology with capabilities ordinary humans do not possess, conceived for a specific mission appropriate to the age being inaugurated.

The disclosure was made during the second contact in 1975, and the source material records Yahweh's request that Raël not reveal the parental tie for three years afterward. Raël's own handling of the material has been consistently modest. The source material itself preserves his framing of the matter: *"It is not the messenger who is important, but the message itself. … Do not look at my finger, but rather in the direction in which it's pointed."* The framework adopts this framing. The disclosure is part of the framework's understanding of the structural pattern of prophetic conception at consequential transitions; it is not a basis for elevating Raël personally above the message he was commissioned to deliver.

Second, Raël's mission is structurally distinct from those of the earlier prophets. Earlier prophets — Moses, Jesus, Muhammad, the others — delivered their content in religious vocabulary because that was the vocabulary their audiences could process. The Sinai legislation, the Sermon on the Mount, the Qur'anic recitations: each was framed in the religious-cultural idiom appropriate to its time. The framework reads this as a feature of the audience rather than of the content. The content the alliance was conveying across these missions was, at its core, the same set of facts the framework now articulates — about the actual origin of life on Earth, about the alliance's role in that origin, about the cosmic-competition framework, about humanity's situation. The earlier prophets could not deliver this content in scientific vocabulary because their audiences could not have processed it; the religious vocabulary was the carrier the audience could receive.

Raël's mission, on the framework's reading, is the first that operates on different principles. By 1973 humanity had developed the scientific vocabulary, the technological self-understanding, and the cultural conditions that permitted the same content to be delivered directly in scientific terms. Raël is therefore, in the source material's striking phrase, *"the first prophet who can be understood only by scientifically evolved beings."* The content that Moses encoded in legislation and Jesus encoded in parables, Raël delivers in the form of a direct technical account: the Elohim are an extraterrestrial civilization, life on Earth was synthesized by their scientists, the religious traditions preserve compressed and partly distorted memories of these events, the time has come for accurate information to replace religious framing. The reception this asks for is intellectual evaluation rather than religious faith.

This shift — from religion-as-vocabulary to science-as-vocabulary — is the framework's reading of why prophecy as a religious institution has concluded and why the Aquarian-age phase operates differently. The institution has not ceased; it has been transformed.

### The current status of the prophets

The Raëlian source material reports that the prophets of the past are currently resurrected on the Planet of the Eternals — the Elohim home world, where the cell-transfer continuity technology has been applied to the major prophetic figures in the same way it has been applied to the senior members of the Council of Eternals. Vorilhon, during the second contact in 1975, met the resurrected prophets in person: Moses, Elijah, Buddha, Jesus, Muhammad, and others, all alive in renewed bodies on the home world. The prophets are described as living in conditions of practical immortality, awaiting the alliance's planned open return to Earth at the embassy whose construction the source material requests.

The framework treats this disclosure with the same care it treats the broader cell-transfer continuity material in the [Yahweh](../yahweh/) entry. The internal mechanism of the technology is not specified in detail; the broad framework — that the major prophetic figures continue to exist in renewed bodies on the home world — is what the source material affirms. The framework does not claim that this can be independently verified.

### What the framework does not claim

What the framework does not claim about the prophetic institution is worth stating directly.

It does not claim that every figure recognized as a prophet by some religious tradition has been an alliance-commissioned prophet in the framework's specific sense. The framework's claim is that the alliance has commissioned approximately forty prophets across human history, with the named partial list above representing the figures the source material has specifically identified. Other figures recognized in their respective traditions as prophets may or may not be among the forty; the framework cannot specify case by case beyond the named list.

It does not claim that the messages of the various prophets are identical in content. Each prophet was commissioned for a specific mission appropriate to a specific cultural moment, and the messages reflect those specific contexts. The cumulative content across the prophetic tradition reflects, on the framework's reading, the alliance's pluriform strategy: different cultures, different vocabularies, different specific emphases, contributing to the cumulative preparation of humanity for the recognition that becomes possible only at the close of Pisces.

It does not endorse the claims of pseudoprophetic and cultic figures who have, throughout history and continuing into the present, claimed prophetic authority without operational basis. The framework reads the prophetic institution as a real and limited operational mechanism, not as a category open to anyone who claims to receive communications from the divine. The criteria of authentic prophetic commissioning are the alliance's, not internal to the would-be prophet's self-understanding.

It does not claim that the framework's reading of any specific prophet supersedes the religious tradition's reading of that prophet. The Hebrew tradition's reading of Moses, the Christian tradition's reading of Christ, the Islamic tradition's reading of Muhammad — each preserves substantive content that the framework respects and incorporates. The framework's reading reframes the underlying ontology (the prophets as alliance interlocutors rather than as supernatural mediators) but does not deauthorize the religious traditions that have preserved the prophetic content across the long centuries.

## Open questions

- The complete list of the approximately forty prophets is not specified in the available source material. The framework knows that there are unnamed prophets — figures from cultural traditions whose names have not been disclosed or whose prophetic status the source has not specified — but cannot enumerate them.
- The selection criteria by which the alliance identifies and commissions prophets are described only schematically in the source material. The framework cannot specify what makes a particular individual the right candidate at a particular historical moment.
- The relationship between the Hebrew prophetic tradition and the parallel non-Hebrew traditions (Buddhist, Zoroastrian, the various indigenous traditions whose prophetic figures may be among the unnamed) is treated by the framework only in broad terms. The pluriform-strategy reading is structurally clear; the specific operational coordination across the parallel missions is not.
- The mechanism by which the alliance currently maintains contact with humanity, in the period between the close of the religious-prophetic phase and the open return at the embassy, is not specified in the source material. Raël's role is the principal known channel; whether other channels operate alongside is open.
- The extent to which the framework's reading of pre-Hebrew and non-Hebrew prophetic figures (Adam, Noah, Buddha, Zoroaster) is supported by the textual records of those traditions, versus the extent to which the identifications are a reconstruction the framework has performed, varies across the figures and is treated with appropriate epistemic care in their dedicated entries.
- The framework's reading of Raël as biological son of Yahweh is internal to the source material and is not independently verifiable. The framework treats the disclosure as the source's own statement and notes the structural parallel to the Jesus narrative without making independent verification claims.
