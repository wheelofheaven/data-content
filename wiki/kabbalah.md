**Kabbalah** (Hebrew: קַבָּלָה, *Qabbalah*, "received tradition") is the
principal Jewish mystical and esoteric tradition — a body of teaching on
cosmology, the nature of the divine, and spiritual practice, developed within
Judaism across roughly two millennia. Its central image is a structured divine
order: the infinite, unknowable source (*Ein Sof*) unfolds through ten
emanations (*sefirot*) into the created world. In the Wheel of Heaven framework
the Kabbalah holds a singular place. It is the tradition that [Yahweh](../yahweh/),
in *The Book Which Tells the Truth*, singles out as the richest surviving store
of "traces of the truth" — and the corpus reads its structured plurality of
emanations as a preserved memory of the plural creators, the [Elohim](../elohim/),
themselves.

## Etymology and spellings

The name comes from the Hebrew root **קבל** (*q-b-l*, "to receive"). *Qabbalah*
is the verbal noun — "reception," "that which is received" — so the tradition
names itself not by its content but by its mode of transmission: teaching handed
from master to student down a chain, rather than reasoned out anew. The same
root yields *shalshelet ha-Qabbalah*, "the chain of tradition," and the
Friday-evening liturgy *kabbalat Shabbat*, "receiving the Sabbath." The
self-designation matters. Where the medieval Jewish philosophers — Maimonides
above all — worked by rational argument, the Kabbalists claimed a *received*
deposit, older than any one thinker.

Three spellings track three receptions of the same material:

- **Kabbalah** — the Jewish tradition and the standard modern scholarly form.
- **Cabala** (Latinate; French *Cabale*) — the older European spelling, carried
  by the Renaissance Christian Kabbalah of Pico della Mirandola and Johannes
  Reuchlin, and used by Jean Sendy and his source Alexandre Safran.
- **Qabalah** — the Hermetic and Western-esoteric appropriation of the late
  nineteenth and twentieth centuries (the Golden Dawn, Mathers, Crowley).

Within Judaism the material also travels under *torat ha-sod* ("the secret
teaching"), *chokhmat ha-nistar* ("the hidden wisdom"), and the older names for
its two great themes — *ma'aseh bereshit* ("the work of creation") and
*ma'aseh merkavah* ("the work of the chariot," the Ezekiel throne-vision).

## The tradition

Mainstream scholarship traces the Kabbalah through a sequence of textual phases
rather than to a single founding.

**The foundational period.** The oldest layer is the *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of
Formation), a short, dense cosmological text composed sometime between the second
and sixth centuries CE. It describes creation through "thirty-two paths of
wisdom" — the ten *sefirot* (the word's first attested use) and the twenty-two
letters of the Hebrew alphabet. It sorts those letters into three "mothers,"
seven "doubles," and twelve "simples," and assigns the twelve simple letters to
the twelve constellations of the zodiac, the twelve months, and the twelve
organs of the body — the wheel of heaven stamped at once into language,
calendar, and flesh. That astral inheritance is preserved with visible
ambivalence: the Talmud's debate at *Shabbat* 156a–b sets one authority who
holds there is *mazal* — a constellation, hence a fortune — for Israel against
another who answers *ein mazal le-Yisrael*, "there is no constellation for
Israel." The redactors side with the exemption rather than the denial: the stars
are real, but the covenant overrides them. Alongside the *Sefer Yetzirah* stands
the late-antique *Hekhalot* literature of heavenly ascent, which prefigures the
later tradition.

**The medieval emergence.** The Kabbalah as an identifiable movement appears in
Provence and northern Spain in the late twelfth and thirteenth centuries — the
*Sefer ha-Bahir* (late 12th c.), the Provençal circle of Isaac the Blind, and
the Geronese school of Azriel of Gerona and Nahmanides. Its central text is the
***Zohar*** (Book of Splendor), a vast Aramaic commentary on the Torah presented
as the teaching of the second-century sage Shimon bar Yochai. Modern
scholarship, following Gershom Scholem, attributes it instead to Moses de León in
late-thirteenth-century Castile.

**The Lurianic synthesis.** In sixteenth-century Safed, Isaac Luria (1534–1572,
known as the *ARI*) recast the whole system. He wrote almost nothing himself; his
teaching survives principally through his disciple Hayyim Vital's *Etz Chaim*.
Lurianic Kabbalah added its three great doctrines: *tzimtzum*, the
self-contraction of the *Ein Sof* to make room for creation; *shevirat
ha-kelim*, the "breaking of the vessels" that could not hold the divine light and
shattered, scattering sparks through the lower worlds; and *tikkun olam*, the
"repair of the world," the human task of gathering those sparks. This became the
dominant Kabbalistic framework, and through the eighteenth-century Hasidic
movement — the Baal Shem Tov and his successors — it reached a mass following.

**The system.** At the center of the tradition is the map of emanation. The
*Ein Sof* — "without end," the transcendent source beyond all attribute —
expresses itself through the ten *sefirot*: **Keter** (Crown), **Chokhmah**
(Wisdom), **Binah** (Understanding), **Chesed** (Mercy), **Gevurah** (Severity),
**Tiferet** (Beauty), **Netzach** (Eternity), **Hod** (Splendor), **Yesod**
(Foundation), and **Malkhut** (Kingdom). These operate across four worlds
(*Atziluth*, *Beriah*, *Yetzirah*, *Asiyah*) and are arranged in the *Etz
Chaim*, the [Tree of Life](../tree-of-life/) diagram, on three pillars joined by
twenty-two paths — one for each Hebrew letter. The *sefirot* are not separate
gods: they are distinct emanations of a single source, each with its own
attribute, name, and role.

That structure was carried outward into two further traditions, each preserving
the material under its own frame. The **Christian Kabbalah** of the Renaissance
(Pico's *Conclusiones*, 1486; Reuchlin's *De Arte Cabalistica*, 1517) read the
*sefirot* toward Christian theology. The **Hermetic Qabalah** of the modern
Western-esoteric revival (Mathers, Dion Fortune, Crowley) took the Tree of Life
into a magical and correspondential system far from its Jewish source. Both are
genuine receptions, but derivative: the structure preserved, the interpretive
frame replaced.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework's estimate of the Kabbalah is unusually high, and it rests on an
explicit source. In *The Book Which Tells the Truth*, in the section "At the
Roots of All Religions," Yahweh tells Raël that traces of the truth survive
across many religions, and singles one out:

> *"The Kabala especially is one of the richest in testimonies, but it would not
> have been easy for you to get hold of one. If one day you can find a copy, then
> you will be able to see that there are a great number of allusions to us."*

Two features of the tradition carry that reading, and the corpus states them
directly as canon while marking the interpretive step in each.

**The sefirot as preserved plurality.** The Kabbalah describes the "divine" as
neither a single deity nor a strict unity but a structured order of distinct
emanations, each with its own function and relations. On the framework's reading
this preserves the memory of what the creators actually were: the
[Elohim](../elohim/), a [plurality](../plurality-of-gods/) of individual
beings — a civilization — that constituted "the divine" from the human vantage
while remaining distinct persons within their own. The *sefirot*, so read, are a
diagram of an alliance mistaken for a metaphysics. This is an interpretive
mapping rather than a claim the Kabbalists made, and the corpus offers it as an
`inferred` reading; it does not press one-to-one identifications of individual
*sefirot* with individual Elohim beyond the general correspondence (the
association of *Tiferet* with the divine name *Yahweh* is noted, not built upon).

**The chain of tradition as deep memory.** The Kabbalah's account of itself — a
teaching received in an unbroken line — the framework reads as largely
accurate. The Mishnah (Pirkei Avot 1:1) traces the line to Moses at Sinai, using
the same root: "Moses *received* [*kibbel*] the Torah at Sinai and transmitted it
to Joshua." The Kabbalists pressed it further back. Alexandre Safran
(1910–2006), Chief Rabbi of Geneva, whose *La Cabale* (1960) supplied Jean
Sendy's reading of the tradition, put it plainly: *"The Kabbalah exceeds, in
antiquity, the Revelation of Sinai; it dates back to prehistoric times. Moses
only introduced it into the history of Israel."* The framework reads that not as
pious inflation but as a memory of transmission reaching back through the
patriarchs to the creators' earlier contacts. Sendy adds the two-worlds
correspondence — the celestial and the earthly, unified by human action — and
Safran's line that "man will renew the acts recounted at the beginning of
Genesis," which the corpus aligns with its own account of humanity's eventual
turn to becoming creators in its own right.

The framework also credits the tradition's esotericism with a protective
function. Transmitted only to prepared students within closed circles, its
content resisted the flattening that widely distributed traditions suffered under
the pressure of strict monotheism. What survived intact was, on this reading, the
very plurality the surrounding tradition had otherwise pared away.

**The text behind "the closest book."** Later corpus material sharpens the
source's praise into a specific claim — that the Kabbalah is "the closest book to
the truth" — and it can be traced to a particular text. In the same passage
Yahweh cites two figures, the "height of the creator" at 236,000 *parasangs* and
the "height of his heels" at 30 million, attributing them to "the Song of Songs
(V)." Those numbers come not from the Zohar or the *sefirot* material at all, but
from the *Shi'ur Qomah* ("the Measure of the Stature"), a text of the
pre-Kabbalistic *Hekhalot* literature in which the angel Metatron dictates the
dimensions of the divine body. The figure 236 is generated by *gematria* from
Psalm 147:5, and the text was carried into print inside the *Sefer Raziel
ha-Mal'akh* (Amsterdam, 1701) — which is why an esoteric Hebrew corpus could be
described as a "book" one might obtain a copy of. On the framework's reading the
*parasang* is redefined as the distance light travels in one second, turning the
two figures into the [Elohim home planet](../elohim-home-planet/)'s distance from
its star and from Earth: a garbled record of two real distances, faithfully
recited by a tradition that had stopped understanding it. The full reading —
including the three-way dispute over the text's date and the trail from Scholem
through Henri Sérouya's 1957 *La Kabbale* to the exact wording the canon cites —
is set out in the Explainer [*The Book Closest to the
Truth*](/articles/the-book-closest-to-the-truth/) and the [*Shi'ur Qomah* —
Wheel of Heaven Translation](/library/shiur-qomah-woh/).

## Scholarly and critical perspectives

The framework's reading sits alongside a mature body of scholarship, not in place
of it.

- **Historical-critical study.** Gershom Scholem (1897–1982) founded the academic
  study of the Kabbalah, dated the Zohar to Moses de León, and reconstructed the
  tradition's phases; Moshe Idel, Yehuda Liebes, and Elliot Wolfson have since
  revised and extended his picture, and Daniel Matt's Pritzker edition
  (2003–2017) is the standard scholarly Zohar. This work reads the *sefirot* as
  the achievement of Jewish religious imagination, shaped by Neoplatonic and
  Gnostic currents. The framework accepts its textual and historical findings and
  parts company only at the interpretive level — where scholarship sees invention,
  the corpus reads preserved memory.
- **The plurality objection.** Mainstream Jewish theology holds the *sefirot*
  firmly within monotheism: distinct emanations of one God, not many beings. This
  is the point on which the framework's reading is a real departure, and it is
  labelled as such rather than smuggled in as consensus.
- **Emanationist parallels.** The *sefirot* have close structural cousins —
  Plotinus's Neoplatonic hierarchy, the Gnostic *aeons* and the Sophia myth, Ibn
  Arabi's divine names — which mainstream scholarship explains through diffusion
  and a shared philosophical inheritance across the Mediterranean world. The
  framework's broader suggestion that these parallels preserve a common *memory*
  rather than a common philosophy goes beyond what any single source states, and
  is offered as `speculative`.
- **Popular Kabbalah.** The modern popularizations (the Kabbalah Centre and
  others) detach the material from the pedagogical setting that historically
  governed it. The framework, like most scholars, distinguishes these from the
  traditional line they draw on.
