**Paul Anthony Wallis** is an Australian author and former senior churchman
whose six-book *Eden Series* (2020–2025) argues that the Hebrew Bible,
read from the root meanings of its key words, preserves memories of
contact between early humanity and a plurality of technological visitors —
the *elohim*, in his rendering "the Powerful Ones." He arrived at the
position from inside the institution most invested in the conventional
reading: thirty-three years in Anglican ministry, fifteen of them training
pastors in hermeneutics, ending as an archdeacon in the Anglican Church in
Australia. Within the contemporary reinterpretive landscape the corpus
places him beside {% wiki(slug="biglino-method") %}Mauro Biglino{% end %}
as one of the two most consequential living voices of
{% wiki(slug="neo-euhemerism") %}neo-euhemerism{% end %}, and registers
him across its entries as "the principal Anglophone popularizer of the
Biglino-adjacent approach."

Wallis's independence is part of his evidential value to this project. A
full-text search of the six Eden books finds no mention of
{% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %}, of the Raëlian source material, or —
more surprisingly for the genre — of Zecharia Sitchin. His convergences
with the framework were reached from the text alone, which is precisely
what makes them worth mapping. The full-length comparison lives in the
Explainer [*The Archdeacon and the
Dragon*](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/); this entry gives the
biographical and bibliographical record.

## Biography

Wallis was born in Britain and grew up in Buckinghamshire. By his own
account his parents introduced him, at age eleven, to Erich von Däniken's
*Chariots of the Gods?* and to the comparative mythology of Immanuel
Velikovsky — "both writers invited me to look more deeply at the stories
of our past, to notice the anomalies and the unexplained in the mainstream
theories." As a schoolboy he wielded ancient-astronaut arguments against
his born-again classmates until, at seventeen, reading the New Testament
to sharpen his attacks, he converted: "The Jesus of the Gospels appeared
to be about liberating people from hierarchies and from living in fear.
That is how my journey as a Christian began."

The church career that followed ran thirty-three years: parish ministry
and church-planting, "Church Doctor" assignments as an intentional interim
minister sent to struggling congregations, fifteen years as a theological
educator, professional-standards work during Australia's Royal Commission
into institutional child sexual abuse, and the senior rank of archdeacon.
He published books on Celtic spirituality and Eastern Orthodox mysticism.
Nothing in the public record of that career anticipates the turn it took.

The turn came through an injury. Playing Ultimate Frisbee with his church
youth group, Wallis wrecked his lower right leg badly enough to spend
weeks in a traction device, convalescing in a converted shipping crate at
the end of his driveway while preparing to preach through Genesis. Slowed
down, working from an interlinear Bible with the Hebrew on one side and
the Greek of the Septuagint on the other, he found the text he had
preached for three decades would no longer cooperate: "Each time I sat
down to read through the book of Genesis, the same anomalous verses kept
getting in my face and flagging me down, as if to say, 'Paul! Stop! Don't
read any further. You've got the story wrong!'"

*Escaping from Eden* was published in 2020 and ended his ministry — he
describes releasing his congregation "from their obligation to me" and
becoming "a heretic on my own dollar." His retrospective five years later
is the nearest thing the series has to a credo: "All my professional
credibility as senior churchman of thirty-three years' standing … were
laid on the line because of the conclusions to which these narrative
connections inevitably led me. As inconvenient as it was to my livelihood
and reputation, where the data led, I had to follow." He now writes and
broadcasts full-time, principally through the YouTube channel The 5th Kind
TV, where his interlocutors have included von Däniken and Biglino —
Biglino's standing blurb on his books reads, "Though far apart
geographically we are spiritually close! We are a good team."

## Method

Wallis's method is continuous with the
{% wiki(slug="biglino-method") %}Biglino Method{% end %} while carrying
his own emphases. Its elements:

- **Anomalies as portals.** "Anomalies are supposed to be our friends.
  They're the little clues that our metanarrative is off… Give them enough
  attention and you'll realize they are the portals to another world"
  (*Escaping from Eden*). The starting anomalies are the ones theology
  never metabolized: the mineral survey of Genesis 2, the plural "let us
  make," the moral incoherence of punishing beings who lacked moral
  knowledge, the Nephilim's survival of a total flood.
- **Root meanings of key words.** *Elohim* read as the plural "Powerful
  Ones" on the strength of ordinary *-im* morphology ("a kruv is a cherub.
  Kruvim are many cherubs, not the various superlative qualities of
  cherub-kind"); technical terms — *kavod*, *ruach*, *ap*, *bab-el* —
  returned to their concrete senses.
- **Mainstream criticism as platform.** Wallis accepts the documentary
  hypothesis, the exilic redaction, and "a very broad scholarly consensus"
  on the Bible's composite character before departing from the consensus
  on what the recovered stories describe. He engages hostile experts by
  name — most substantively Michael Heiser, whose divine-council
  scholarship assembles the same plurality data against all paleocontact
  readings.
- **Comparative triangulation.** "The moment you begin to compare notes
  with cultures around the world, that's when you see the repeats and
  overlaps. That's when you start cracking the code" (*The Eden Enigma*).
  The comparanda run from the Mesopotamian cuneiform corpus through the
  Popol Vuh, West African Mami Wata traditions, Hawaiian Mo'o narratives,
  Aboriginal and Cherokee Pleiades traditions, and the Urartian reliefs of
  the Van highlands.
- **Narrative candor.** The books are memoir-travelogues as much as
  treatises, and Wallis discloses the genre's license: "I have conflated
  various visits, characters and occasions with just enough creative
  license to protect the privacy of those who have entrusted me with their
  conversations." Quotations of his dialogues should be treated
  accordingly.

## The Eden Series

**Escaping from Eden (2020)** does the philology. Its thesis: restore the
plural to *elohim* and Genesis performs a "volte-face" — from polemic
against the Mesopotamian stories into their summary. Its proof-text is
Joshua 24, "the smoking gun," where Israel is told to put away the
*elohim* their ancestors served and serve Yahweh alone: one Powerful One
demanding exclusivity against the others.

**The Scars of Eden (2021)** takes the result on a world tour of contact
traditions and asks the subtitle's question — *has humanity confused the
idea of God with memories of ET contact?* Its central analogy is the
Spanish missionaries' equation of the Incan Viracocha with the Christian
God: "This is why confusing GOD with other entities, whether Viracocha,
the Elohim, the Sky People or the Engineers, is such a critical error."
The world's origin stories, he argues, "read like the flashbacks of a
patient with amnesia."

**Echoes of Eden (2022)** asks what human capacities were buried along
with the memory of contact, reading the Popol Vuh's account of a
deliberate cognitive downgrade alongside Babel and the Efik story of
Abassi and Atai. It also debuts the Akhekh argument (below).

**The Eden Conspiracy (2023)** reconstructs the redaction: Hezekiah's
destruction of the bronze serpent as "a deliberate and radical rebranding
of YHWH himself," the reforms under the boy-king Josiah as "a silent
coup," the sixth-century redactors whose "grammatical glitches were the
scar tissue left by the surgery that changed the Bible from a library of
paleocontact into a book about God." Asked directly whether the Bible is
a conspiracy, Wallis answers "Short answer: 'Yes!'" — and immediately
adds, "I don't think it is fair of us to demonize these redactors… to
their own thinking there was an integrity to what they were doing."

**The Invasion of Eden (2024)** asks the darkest form of the question:
whether some of the remembered visitors were exploiters, whether the
biblical *Seba Hassamayim* ("Sky Armies") names an occupation, and whether
the arrangement ever ended. His verdict is deliberately non-binary — of
the visitations in his own proof-texts he counts roughly two-thirds
benevolent, and warns that "it is not that we humans are the goodies and
the ETs are the baddies."

**The Eden Enigma (2025)** goes to ground in the Van highlands of eastern
Türkiye, reading the Urartian reliefs of winged Dingir tending trees of
life as the memory of civilization's post-Younger-Dryas reboot — down to a
hand-pollination reading of the pinecone-and-bucket motif. Its summary
line: "Our ancestors were the rememberers. We are the ones who have
misunderstood."

## Signature readings

Several of Wallis's word-studies parallel readings the corpus catalogs in
the {% wiki(slug="list-of-etymological-readings") %}list of etymological
readings{% end %}; others are distinctively his own.

- **elohim = "the Powerful Ones."** The foundation of the series. The
  canon's own gloss is functional — "those come from the sky" — while
  Wallis's is root-based; they pick out the same referent.
- **YHWH as loan word and "memory of a sound."** In *The Eden Conspiracy*
  Wallis argues the name enters the record at Midian as a foreign word
  with no Hebrew etymology, to be treated "as the memory of a sound,
  rather than a memory of meaning."
- **The Akhekh identification.** His most arresting single image: a
  worldwide family of dragon-names sharing a *k-k* percussion (Kukulkan,
  Ququmatz, Quetzalcoatl, Kucedra, Kholkhis, Akhekh), connected to YHWH
  through an affrication argument — "Akhekh is the Egyptian dragon.
  Yakhwekh is the Hebrew name" — and staged at Joshua's Shechem assembly
  as a choice between "ACH ECH of Egypt or yACHwECH of Israel." The
  corpus's audit (in [*The Archdeacon and the
  Dragon*](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/)) finds the phonology
  fails — YHWH is spelled with *he*, while the velar sound the argument
  needs has its Hebrew reflex in *ḥet* — while the structural claim
  underneath, Yahweh as one regional Powerful One among jurisdictional
  peers (the El of Ekron, the "prince of Persia," the allotments of
  Deuteronomy 32:8–9), stands on independent evidence.
- **kavod = "a heavy thing."** Ezekiel's *kavod* read as machinery: "It
  should leave us in no doubt that the kavod of YHWH … was not what we
  think of as the 'glory of God.' It was a machine."
- **bab-el = "gateway of the Powerful Ones,"** cross-read with the Enuma
  Elish's three hundred observers stationed in the heavens; the Babel
  event as "the obliteration of a technological, spacefaring civilization."
- **Ur of the Chaldees relocated.** Reading *Xaldaion* through the Urartian
  god-name Haldi, *The Invasion of Eden* and *The Eden Enigma* move
  Abraham's origin from Woolley's southern Ur to the high country of
  Ararat/Urartu.

## Theology

Wallis did not exit into atheism. His constructive position separates
YHWH-as-character — "a Powerful One … often punitive and brutal" — from
"GOD – the harmonious Source of all things," glimpsed in Amos, the
Johannine prologue, and Paul at Athens. His Jesus stands against the
Yahweh narratives ("Jesus never used the name Yahweh as a name for God.
It's that simple.") in a reading he acknowledges as the vindication of
Marcion's distinction, though unlike Marcion he keeps the Hebrew Bible as
indispensable evidence. He describes himself as "a fan and follower of
Jesus for forty-two years" on the model of the pre-Constantinian
communities of Thomas and Q, and his pastoral practice with alarmed
believers is documented at length in the books: "ninety percent of my
detractors are willing and able to make their way into a friendlier
discourse once we actually get talking."

## Relation to the Wheel of Heaven framework

The convergences are specific and load-bearing: the plural Elohim; the
{% wiki(slug="eden") %}Eden{% end %} enclosure as a managed installation
near exploitable resources; the prohibition on the tree as policy rather
than morality; the {% wiki(slug="serpent") %}Serpent{% end %} as a
dissenting member of the making team, exiled to Earth for upgrading the
humans — an arc Wallis also finds in the Chinese four-dragons story, where
rank-breaking helpers of humanity are banished under the mountains of the
Earth; the {% wiki(slug="great-flood") %}Flood{% end %} as a council
decision with a dissenting benefactor forewarning Noah; Babel as the
destruction of a spacefaring capability; a council of factions (his
*El-Ba'adat*, "the Council of Power") divided over "how intelligent the
humans should be, how numerous and how long-lived"; and a monotheizing
edit that concealed all of it. The corpus's Satan entry additionally
credits *The Eden Conspiracy* with "substantial Satan–Lucifer
disentanglement content" — Wallis, like the corpus, refuses the late
conflation of the Eden serpent with the Job prosecutor.

The divergences are equally structural. Wallis pins the Bible's
dragon-imagery on Yahweh himself ("CH-CH the Dragon"); the corpus keeps
Yahweh a man — president of the
{% wiki(slug="council-of-eternals") %}Council of the Eternals{% end %} —
and assigns the serpent-and-dragon language to the exiled
{% wiki(slug="lucifer") %}Lucifer{% end %} faction, reading the same
Isaiah 27:1 that Wallis cites as Yahweh's government pursuing the exiles'
undersea bases. Wallis reads Jesus against Yahweh; the corpus reads Jesus
as Yahweh's son and envoy. Wallis retains a Source-God above the Powerful
Ones; the corpus's ontology has no such being — above the Elohim it finds
only [the infinite in both
directions](/articles/the-infinite-in-both-directions/). And where the
later Eden Series weighs the evidence for exploitation and invasion, the
corpus reads a project conducted, on the whole, in love, with a
{% wiki(slug="great-return") %}return{% end %} to be welcomed rather than
an occupation to be resisted. The full argument for both lists, with the
philological audit of the Akhekh reading, is in [*The Archdeacon and the
Dragon*](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/).

## Reception

Within the paleocontact tradition Wallis's standing is documented on his
own covers: von Däniken ("Paul Wallis is one of my best colleagues. I have
full respect for him") and Biglino blurb the later books, and George Noory
called *Escaping from Eden* "this generation's *Chariots of the Gods*."
From his former world the reception ran from quiet distancing —
colleagues telling him "let's stay friends, but I won't be reading your
book" — to the hate mail he reprints verbatim and answers with pastoral
patience. Mainstream biblical scholarship has not engaged the Eden Series
in the academic literature; the nearest engagement is adversarial by
proxy, since Michael Heiser's divine-council corpus covers the same
plurality data while rejecting paleocontact readings categorically, and
Wallis answers Heiser directly in *The Eden Enigma*. The corpus's own
positioning is on record across a dozen entries and is best summarized by
its source-catalog verdict on *The Eden Conspiracy*: "broadly compatible
with the corpus's reading; the principal accessible recent treatment."
