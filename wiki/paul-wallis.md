**Paul Anthony Wallis** is an Australian author and former senior churchman
whose six-book *Eden Series* (2020–2025) argues that the Hebrew Bible,
read from the root meanings of its key words, preserves memories of
contact between early humanity and a plurality of technological visitors —
the *elohim*, in his rendering "the Powerful Ones." He arrived at the
position from inside the institution most invested in the conventional
reading: thirty-three years in Anglican ministry, fifteen of them training
pastors in hermeneutics, ending as an archdeacon in the Anglican Church in
Australia. Within the contemporary reinterpretive landscape the corpus
places him beside {% wiki(slug="biglino-method") %}Mauro Biglino{% end %}
as one of the two most consequential living voices of
{% wiki(slug="neo-euhemerism") %}neo-euhemerism{% end %}, and registers
him across its entries as "the principal Anglophone popularizer of the
Biglino-adjacent approach."

Wallis's independence is part of his evidential value to this project. A
full-text search of the six Eden books finds no mention of
{% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %}, of the Raëlian source material, or —
more surprisingly for the genre — of Zecharia Sitchin. His convergences
with the framework were reached from the text alone, which is precisely
what makes them worth mapping. The full-length comparison lives in the
Explainer [*The Archdeacon and the
Dragon*](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/); this entry gives the
biographical and bibliographical record.

## Biography

Wallis was born in Britain and grew up in Buckinghamshire. By his own
account his parents introduced him, at age eleven, to Erich von Däniken's
*Chariots of the Gods?* and to the comparative mythology of Immanuel
Velikovsky — "both writers invited me to look more deeply at the stories
of our past, to notice the anomalies and the unexplained in the mainstream
theories." As a schoolboy he wielded ancient-astronaut arguments against
his born-again classmates until, at seventeen, reading the New Testament
to sharpen his attacks, he converted: "The Jesus of the Gospels appeared
to be about liberating people from hierarchies and from living in fear.
That is how my journey as a Christian began."

The church career that followed ran thirty-three years: parish ministry
and church-planting, "Church Doctor" assignments as an intentional interim
minister sent to struggling congregations, fifteen years as a theological
educator, professional-standards work during Australia's Royal Commission
into institutional child sexual abuse, and the senior rank of archdeacon.
He published books on Celtic spirituality and Eastern Orthodox mysticism.
Nothing in the public record of that career anticipates the turn it took.

The turn came through an injury. Playing Ultimate Frisbee with his church
youth group, Wallis wrecked his lower right leg badly enough to spend
weeks in a traction device, convalescing in a converted shipping crate at
the end of his driveway while preparing to preach through Genesis. Slowed
down, working from an interlinear Bible with the Hebrew on one side and
the Greek of the Septuagint on the other, he found the text he had
preached for three decades would no longer cooperate: "Each time I sat
down to read through the book of Genesis, the same anomalous verses kept
getting in my face and flagging me down, as if to say, 'Paul! Stop! Don't
read any further. You've got the story wrong!'"

*Escaping from Eden* was published in 2020 and ended his ministry — he
describes releasing his congregation "from their obligation to me" and
becoming "a heretic on my own dollar." His retrospective five years later
is the nearest thing the series has to a credo: "All my professional
credibility as senior churchman of thirty-three years' standing … were
laid on the line because of the conclusions to which these narrative
connections inevitably led me. As inconvenient as it was to my livelihood
and reputation, where the data led, I had to follow." He now writes and
broadcasts full-time, principally through the YouTube channel The 5th Kind
TV, where his interlocutors have included von Däniken and Biglino —
Biglino's standing blurb on his books reads, "Though far apart
geographically we are spiritually close! We are a good team."

## Method

Wallis's method is continuous with the
{% wiki(slug="biglino-method") %}Biglino Method{% end %} while carrying
his own emphases. Its elements:

- **Anomalies as portals.** "Anomalies are supposed to be our friends.
  They're the little clues that our metanarrative is off… Give them enough
  attention and you'll realize they are the portals to another world"
  (*Escaping from Eden*). The starting anomalies are the ones theology
  never metabolized: the mineral survey of Genesis 2, the plural "let us
  make," the moral incoherence of punishing beings who lacked moral
  knowledge, the Nephilim's survival of a total flood.
- **Root meanings of key words.** *Elohim* read as the plural "Powerful
  Ones" on the strength of ordinary *-im* morphology ("a kruv is a cherub.
  Kruvim are many cherubs, not the various superlative qualities of
  cherub-kind"); technical terms — *kavod*, *ruach*, *ap*, *bab-el* —
  returned to their concrete senses.
- **Mainstream criticism as platform.** Wallis accepts the documentary
  hypothesis, the exilic redaction, and "a very broad scholarly consensus"
  on the Bible's composite character before departing from the consensus
  on what the recovered stories describe. He engages hostile experts by
  name — most substantively Michael Heiser, whose divine-council
  scholarship assembles the same plurality data against all paleocontact
  readings.
- **Comparative triangulation.** "The moment you begin to compare notes
  with cultures around the world, that's when you see the repeats and
  overlaps. That's when you start cracking the code" (*The Eden Enigma*).
  The comparanda run from the Mesopotamian cuneiform corpus through the
  Popol Vuh, West African Mami Wata traditions, Hawaiian Mo'o narratives,
  Aboriginal and Cherokee Pleiades traditions, and the Urartian reliefs of
  the Van highlands.
- **Narrative candor.** The books are memoir-travelogues as much as
  treatises, and Wallis discloses the genre's license: "I have conflated
  various visits, characters and occasions with just enough creative
  license to protect the privacy of those who have entrusted me with their
  conversations." Quotations of his dialogues should be treated
  accordingly.

## The Eden Series

**Escaping from Eden (2020)** does the philology and states the method.
Its thesis: restore the plural to *elohim* and Genesis performs a
"volte-face" — from polemic against the Mesopotamian stories into their
summary. Its proof-text is Joshua 24, "the smoking gun," where Israel is
told to put away the *elohim* their ancestors served and serve Yahweh
alone: one Powerful One demanding exclusivity against the others. Two
further moves make the book more than a translation exercise. Wallis
reads Genesis 1 not as a creation account but as a *recovery* account —
the *tohu wa bohu*, the "chaotic, empty wasteland" of verse 2, as the
ash-canopy and meltwater of a real post-cataclysm Earth (he ties it to
the Younger Dryas extinction), and the six days as a terraforming and
re-seeding by a superior species. And he counts the cast precisely:
pre-Flood Genesis holds "at least four kinds of entity" — two ET species
(*elohim* and *benei elohim*), humans, and the hybrid Nephilim — governed
by a body he simply calls "the Council," because, he notes, "in Genesis 1
elohim doesn't have a heavenly council, elohim *is* the heavenly council."
The darker corollary is a "slave-wiring" thesis: humanity was engineered
as a workforce, and the reflex to defer, tithe, and sacrifice is the
vestige of that original design.

**The Scars of Eden (2021)** takes the result on a world tour of contact
traditions and asks the subtitle's question — *has humanity confused the
idea of God with memories of ET contact?* Its central analogy is the
Spanish missionaries' equation of the Incan Viracocha with the Christian
God: "This is why confusing GOD with other entities, whether Viracocha,
the Elohim, the Sky People or the Engineers, is such a critical error."
The world's origin stories, he argues, "read like the flashbacks of a
patient with amnesia." The book assembles a star-origin catalogue —
Sirius (the Dogon), Orion (the Egyptian and Mayan books of the dead),
and above all the Pleiades (Cherokee, Aboriginal Australian) — and reads
Job 38's roll-call of the Pleiades, Orion, and "Sirius with her
satellites" as a surviving "Sky Council" text. It names the mechanism of
forgetting as a seven-stage suppression running from the Ten Commandments
through the sixth-century redaction, Theodosius, the Nag Hammadi burial,
and the conquistadors to the 1947 National Security Act — and, at its
boldest, turns the telescope around: in the "Hugo is my cat" parable
Wallis casts humanity as, to some visitors, "like pets… like cattle…
like vermin."

**Echoes of Eden (2022)** asks what human capacities were buried along
with the memory of contact. Its answer is a *cognitive-downgrade* thesis:
reading the Popol Vuh's account of a "noxious vapour" sprayed over early
humanity to "limit their power of vision," Wallis proposes that our
makers deliberately dialled down a species they had made too
intelligent, switching off latent faculties — "telepathy, precognition,
and remote viewing" — to leave a manageable workforce. He grounds the
claim in Acquired Savant Syndrome (Darold Treffert's catalogue of
injury-released genius) as evidence that the faculties remain inhibited
rather than absent. The book also debuts the Akhekh argument and its
wider *dragons-and-teachers* duality (below): the serpent-dragon is kept
deliberately "light and shade," and its benevolent pole — the feathered
serpent as the *engineer* of humanity — is the seed of the reading the
corpus most values. Its thesis line: at all costs "we mere humans must
forget who we really are: powerful cosmic beings with … advanced
cognitive abilities lying latent and waiting to be activated."

**The Eden Conspiracy (2023)** reconstructs the redaction on a
divine-council foundation. Its presiding figure is **El Elyon**, "above
the others," and Yahweh, Wallis concludes, "is in fact the junior of El
Elyon, one of a number of subordinate elohim," each allotted a share of
the planet (see *The Council of Power*, below). Onto that he lays two
distinct upheavals a reader should keep apart: the **"silent coup"** — the
Josiah-era textual centralization and sixth-century redaction whose
"grammatical glitches were the scar tissue left by the surgery that
changed the Bible from a library of paleocontact into a book about God" —
and the **"Great Coup"** of 1 Samuel 8, where the people themselves
replace Yahweh's direct rule with a human king. He details the editorial
machinery: the priest Hilkiah's conveniently discovered scroll, the
"blame the foreign wife" device for excusing pro-Asherah kings, and
Hezekiah's destruction of the bronze serpent Nehushtan as "a deliberate
and radical rebranding of YHWH himself." Asked directly whether the Bible
is a conspiracy, Wallis answers "Short answer: 'Yes!'" — then adds at
once, "I don't think it is fair of us to demonize these redactors… to
their own thinking there was an integrity to what they were doing." Its
moral thesis: "worshipping a monster makes monsters out of us."

**The Invasion of Eden (2024)** asks the darkest form of the question —
whether some of the remembered visitors were exploiters — and keeps the
answer deliberately non-binary. The "invasion" he argues for is *cultural
annexation, not bombardment*: he stresses that in the whole filed record
of modern encounters "there is not even a single incidence of 'non-human'
aggression," and locates the signature of conquest in soft controls —
loss of land-rights, debt-money, proxy war, governance by threat — read
off the Hawaiian Mo'olele traditions and the biblical *Seba Hassamayim*
("Sky Armies"). Of the visitations in his own proof-texts he counts
roughly two-thirds benevolent, and warns that "it is not that we humans
are the goodies and the ETs are the baddies": world mythology is "a
kaleidoscope of colours," and the task is to "move beyond a 'friend or
foe' mentality." The book closes forward-looking, on a note the corpus
reads with interest — that when humanity "finally master[s] spacetime and
become[s] a truly space-faring civilization" the relationship will change,
and "we will be in each other's midst, not as retrievals, abductees or
resources for research, but as neighbours."

**The Eden Enigma (2025)** goes to ground in the Van highlands of eastern
Türkiye. Reading the Urartian reliefs of winged **Dingir** ("Advanced
Beings") tending trees of life — pinecone (*mullilu*) and bucket
(*banduddu*) in hand — Wallis takes the hand-pollination motif literally:
"when the royal artists at Ayanis and Erebuni show the benevolent Dingir
hand-pollinating the trees of life, they are showing us the dawn of
civilization as we know it." He argues the geography of Eden is here:
Ezekiel's *gan elohim* ("garden of the powerful ones") is *behar elohim*
("in the mountain country of the powerful ones"), Genesis sets the reboot
*al hare Ararat*, and "Ararat is the Masoretic Hebrew way of writing
Urartu. It is the same word." Above the city of Van, the "Light Gate" of
Meher Kapı — whose doorway names forty-seven Dingir who "materialized …
in the deep past" — carries a local promise that Haldi "will return one
day on a beam of light." And the book takes on the biblical scholar
Michael Heiser directly: crediting him for admitting the Bible's
Mesopotamian dependency, Wallis rejects his "inversion" thesis point by
point and reads the First-Temple-era **Yeho'ezer seal**, engraved with a
winged figure "indistinguishable from an Urartian Dingir," as evidence a
senior Judean official still signed his name under the old imagery. Its
summary line: "Our ancestors were the rememberers. We are the ones who
have misunderstood."

## Signature readings

Wallis's argument rests on a small, fixed lexicon of Hebrew and Greek
words returned to their concrete root senses; the same retranslations
recur book to book. Several parallel readings the corpus catalogs in the
{% wiki(slug="list-of-etymological-readings") %}list of etymological
readings{% end %}; others are distinctively his own.

**The plural and the Council**

- **elohim = "the Powerful Ones."** The foundation of the series, read as
  an ordinary masculine plural (the *-im* of *kruv/kruvim*), not a plural
  of majesty. The canon's own gloss is functional — "those who came from
  the sky" — while Wallis's is root-based; they pick out the same referent.
- **benei elohim = "the God-like ones."** Not "sons of God" but, on his
  reading, a distinct species standing to the *elohim* as "humans" stand
  to "humankind" — the Watchers of Genesis 6.
- **El Elyon = "above the others."** Not the abstract "Most High" but a
  relative rank — "higher than the rest," a position of command — which
  Wallis makes the president of the Council (below).
- **El Shaddai = "the Powerful One, the Destroyer"** (root *shadad*, "to
  act with violence, to devastate"), against the traditional "God
  Almighty," a sense the standard lexica themselves call uncertain.
- **seba hassamayim = "the Sky Armies."** The airborne "armies of the
  sky," not the "heavenly host" of the hymnbooks.

**The craft in the text**

Wallis's boldest cluster reads a set of technical terms as the vocabulary
of machinery rather than glory:

- **kavod = "a heavy thing."** Ezekiel's *kavod* as hardware: "not what we
  think of as the 'glory of God.' It was a machine."
- **ruach = a hovering, wind-making craft** (a Sumerian loan, he argues,
  surviving in the Amharic *roha*), not "spirit."
- **tub = "the goods,"** heavy equipment rather than divine "goodness";
  **hayyah** (conventionally "living creature") = the "life-form" piloting
  the *kavod*, which addresses Ezekiel as "Earthling."
- **keli mashetow / keli mappasow = "the destroying thing" and "the
  shattering thing,"** the weapons of the executioners in Ezekiel 9.
- **bab-el = "the gateway of the Powerful Ones,"** cross-read with the
  Sumerian dispatch of "three hundred observers to their stations in the
  stars"; the Babel event as the destruction of "a technological,
  spacefaring civilization."

**The reframed theology**

- **YHWH as a loan word and "the memory of a sound."** The name enters the
  record at Midian with no Hebrew etymology, to be treated "as the memory
  of a sound, rather than a memory of meaning" — the hinge of the dragon
  reading below.
- **olam ≠ "eternal."** Following the lexicographer Daniel Garrone, Wallis
  reads *olam* as "the unknown," "the Great Beyond," even "plural worlds
  or universes" — draining the word of the timeless-eternity theology
  built on it.
- **Yeshua = "salvation *from* Yahweh."** A deliberate re-pointing of the
  name usually read "Yahweh is salvation," turning Jesus's mission against
  the Powerful One rather than toward him.

**Places and names**

- **adam = "the earthling"** (from *adamah*, the soil) and **Eve = "the
  Living"** — "the stories of Adam" and "of Eve" read as stories of the
  earthling and the living rather than of two individuals.
- **Ur of the Chaldees relocated.** Reading *Xaldaion* through the
  Urartian god-name **Haldi** ("taken" or "conquered"), *The Invasion of
  Eden* and *The Eden Enigma* move Abraham's origin from Woolley's
  southern Ur to the high country of **Ararat / Urartu** — "the same
  word," on his reading, as Genesis's *al hare Ararat*.

The one reading the corpus formally audits — the **Akhekh
identification** — is treated in full below.

## The Council of Power

The spine of *The Eden Conspiracy* is a divine-council reading that runs
close to the corpus's own. Wallis calls the body the **El-Ba'adat**, "the
Council of Power," and identifies it with the *seba hassamayim*: "project
Earth," he writes, "has been governed over by a federation of beings as
diverse as the crowd in the famous Star Wars bar scene." At its head is
**El Elyon**, "above the others"; beneath
him sit a number of subordinate *elohim*, each allotted a territory and
its people. Wallis reads Deuteronomy 32:8–9 as the literal record of that
allotment — Elyon "parceling the humans out among the elohim," with
"Yahweh's portion" being Jacob — and reads the Hebrew Bible's rival
deities as fellow council-members holding neighbouring jurisdictions:
Akhekh of Egypt, the El of Ekron (with whom Yahweh trades insults as one
*elohim* to another), El Shaddai the Destroyer, the "prince of Persia" of
Daniel 10. The decisive move is his placement of Yahweh *within*, not
above, this body: Yahweh "is in fact the junior of El Elyon, one of a
number of subordinate elohim." He cites the textual scholar Emanuel Tov
for the reading that "Elyon, El and Yahweh correspond to three different
individuals," and Mauro Biglino's tally of some twenty distinct beings in
the text.

This is, structurally, the
{% wiki(slug="council-of-eternals") %}Council of the Eternals{% end %} of
the Raëlian canon — a governing body of the Elohim, deliberating over the
human project and presided over by one of their number. The convergence
is striking given Wallis's independence from the source material. The
sharp divergence is one of rank: Wallis subordinates Yahweh *beneath* El
Elyon as a regional junior, whereas the canon makes Yahweh himself the
Council's president. The corpus reads the "Most High" language not as a
superior standing over Yahweh but as one of the Council's own titles;
Wallis reads it as the trace of the senior whom the redactors later fused
with Yahweh to manufacture a single God.

## The dragon, the serpent, and the teacher

The reading that most caught this project's attention is Wallis's
treatment of the dragon. Across *Echoes of Eden* and *The Eden
Conspiracy* he assembles a worldwide family of serpent- and dragon-names
that share a percussive *k-k / k-ch / ch-ch* sound — Kukulkan, Ququmatz,
Quetzalcoatl (Mesoamerica), Kucedra and the Stoor Worm (Celtic), Kholkhis
(Georgia), Coca (Iberia), the Nagas (India), Teng (China), and, in Egypt,
**Akhekh** — and argues, through a sound-softening ("affrication") chain,
that the name behind YHWH belongs to the same family: "Akhekh is the
Egyptian dragon. **Yakhwekh** is the Hebrew name." On this reading the
Bible's own dragon-language is Yahweh's — the fiery *ap* (nostrils), the
leathery skin and flight-feathers, the tribute of "gold, beef, lamb and
virgin girls," the Leviathan of Isaiah 27:1 — and Joshua's Shechem
assembly becomes a choice between "ACH ECH of Egypt or yACHwECH of
Israel." (The corpus's philological audit, in [*The Archdeacon and the
Dragon*](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/), finds the specific
phonology fails — YHWH is written with *he*, not the guttural *ḥet* the
argument needs — while judging the structural claim beneath it, Yahweh as
one regional Powerful One among peers, well founded on other grounds.)

What makes the reading matter to the canon is that Wallis keeps the
dragon **dual** — "light and shade," in his phrase. Drawing on Julien
d'Huy's morphological work, he distinguishes an ancient stratum of
dragon-stories — benevolent rain-, river-, and agriculture-giving
serpents "taking pity" on humanity — from a later stratum of predatory
overlords. His emblem of the benevolent pole is the Chinese tale of the
four dragons who "break rank" to bring water to starving humans against
the sky-emperor's will and are punished for it, buried each beneath a
mountain. It is the same shape he reads in Genesis 3: the
{% wiki(slug="serpent") %}Serpent{% end %} is a Council member who
performs an "unauthorized upgrade" on humanity and is exiled to Earth for
it — a dissenting benefactor, not a tempter.

Here Wallis and the canon read the same figure from opposite ends. In the
Raëlian frame the serpent-and-dragon imagery belongs to
{% wiki(slug="lucifer") %}Lucifer{% end %}'s faction — the Elohim who
broke the Council's discipline to give humanity the knowledge of its own
making, were punished for it, and in part remained on Earth to help the
people they had raised. Wallis's benevolent dragon — the one who breaks
rank, upgrades humanity, and is exiled beneath the Earth — is, structurally,
that faction reconstructed from the outside, from Chinese, Mesoamerican,
and Hebrew sources at once. The two frameworks part only on where they pin
the *hostile* dragon: Wallis makes it Yahweh himself, the overlord to be
escaped; the canon keeps Yahweh a man, president of the Council, and reads
the pursuing dragon-language of Isaiah 27:1 as Yahweh's government hunting
the exiled Lucifer faction. The picture that survives the disagreement is
the one Wallis's dragon-material makes vivid: the serpent as a teacher who
paid for the gift, not a monster who tempted.

## Theology

Wallis did not exit into atheism. His constructive position separates
YHWH-as-character — "a Powerful One … often punitive and brutal" — from
"GOD – the harmonious Source of all things," glimpsed in Amos, the
Johannine prologue, and Paul at Athens. His Jesus stands against the
Yahweh narratives ("Jesus never used the name Yahweh as a name for God.
It's that simple.") in a reading he acknowledges as the vindication of
Marcion's distinction, though unlike Marcion he keeps the Hebrew Bible as
indispensable evidence. He describes himself as "a fan and follower of
Jesus for forty-two years" on the model of the pre-Constantinian
communities of Thomas and Q, and his pastoral practice with alarmed
believers is documented at length in the books: "ninety percent of my
detractors are willing and able to make their way into a friendlier
discourse once we actually get talking."

## Relation to the Wheel of Heaven framework

Beyond the Council polity and the dragon treated above, the convergences
are specific and load-bearing: the plural Elohim; the
{% wiki(slug="eden") %}Eden{% end %} enclosure as a managed installation
near exploitable resources, with the prohibition on the tree a matter of
policy rather than morality; the {% wiki(slug="serpent") %}Serpent{% end %}
as a dissenting member of the making team, exiled to Earth for upgrading
the humans; the {% wiki(slug="great-flood") %}Flood{% end %} as a council
decision with a dissenting benefactor forewarning Noah; Babel as the
destruction of a spacefaring capability; and a monotheizing edit that
concealed all of it. The corpus's Satan entry additionally credits *The
Eden Conspiracy* with "substantial Satan–Lucifer disentanglement
content" — Wallis, like the corpus, refuses the late conflation of the
Eden serpent with the Job prosecutor.

The divergences are equally structural, and honestly held. Wallis
subordinates Yahweh beneath El Elyon and pins the Bible's dragon-imagery
on him; the corpus keeps Yahweh a man and president of the
{% wiki(slug="council-of-eternals") %}Council of the Eternals{% end %}
(both set out above). Wallis reads Jesus *against* Yahweh; the corpus
reads Jesus as Yahweh's son and envoy. Wallis retains a Source-God above
the Powerful Ones; the corpus's ontology has no such being — above the
Elohim it finds only [the infinite in both
directions](/articles/the-infinite-in-both-directions/). And where the
later Eden Series weighs the evidence for exploitation and invasion, the
corpus reads a project conducted, on the whole, in love, with a
{% wiki(slug="great-return") %}return{% end %} to be welcomed rather than
an occupation to be resisted — a difference that narrows, notably, in
Wallis's own closing image of contact-as-neighbours. The full argument
for both lists, with the philological audit of the Akhekh reading, is in
[*The Archdeacon and the
Dragon*](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/).

## Reception

Within the paleocontact tradition Wallis's standing is documented on his
own covers: von Däniken ("Paul Wallis is one of my best colleagues. I have
full respect for him") and Biglino blurb the later books, and George Noory
called *Escaping from Eden* "this generation's *Chariots of the Gods*."
From his former world the reception ran from quiet distancing —
colleagues telling him "let's stay friends, but I won't be reading your
book" — to the hate mail he reprints verbatim and answers with pastoral
patience. Mainstream biblical scholarship has not engaged the Eden Series
in the academic literature; the nearest engagement is adversarial by
proxy, since Michael Heiser's divine-council corpus covers the same
plurality data while rejecting paleocontact readings categorically, and
Wallis answers Heiser directly in *The Eden Enigma*. The corpus's own
positioning is on record across a dozen entries and is best summarized by
its source-catalog verdict on *The Eden Conspiracy*: "broadly compatible
with the corpus's reading; the principal accessible recent treatment."
