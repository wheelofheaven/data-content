**The ancient astronaut hypothesis** — also called the paleocontact hypothesis,
and, in its popular television form, "ancient aliens" — is the interpretive
tradition proposing that intelligent beings from beyond Earth visited the planet
in prehistory or antiquity and shaped human religion, technology, and culture. It
treats a recurring set of phenomena — anomalous megalithic monuments,
cross-cultural myths of sky-descending beings, precocious astronomical knowledge
in pre-modern cultures, and the "gods" of the world's scriptures — as candidate
records of real contact with a non-human civilisation rather than as products of
autonomous human development.

The [Wheel of Heaven](../wheel-of-heaven/) framework belongs to this wider family.
But where much of the field proceeds by speculative interpretation of ancient
evidence, the corpus grounds its reading in the [Raëlian](../raelism/) canon — the
contact account [Claude Vorilhon (Raël)](../rael/) reported in 1973 — and in the
earlier, more disciplined work of [Jean Sendy](../jean-sendy/); and it is careful
to distinguish its own position from the better-known reconstructions of Erich von
Däniken and Zecharia Sitchin. It should be said plainly at the outset that the
field as a whole is rejected by mainstream archaeology and the academic study of
religion, which regard it as pseudoscience. The corpus shares many of those
disciplines' specific objections while parting company on the underlying question
of whether any real contact lies behind the traditions.

## The field and its name

The term pairs "astronaut" — from Greek *astron* ("star") and *nautēs* ("sailor,
navigator") — with "ancient," casting the sky-beings of old traditions in the
vocabulary of modern spaceflight. The combination is a deliberate anachronism: it
asserts that figures the old texts describe as gods or sky-visitors are better
understood as travellers with a technology than as supernatural deities. The field
travels under several names. "Paleocontact hypothesis" and
"extraterrestrial-intervention hypothesis" are the more academic labels; "ancient
aliens" is the popular usage, fixed by the long-running History Channel series of
that name; German writers speak of *Prä-Astronautik* or *Paläo-SETI*, French of
the *hypothèse des anciens astronautes*. Wheel of Heaven prefers "ancient
astronaut theory" and generally avoids "ancient aliens," whose pop-cultural,
sometimes mocking connotation tends to obscure the more serious textual work the
corpus is interested in.

The word *hypothesis* is worth keeping in view. It marks the field's claims as
provisional and offered for examination, not as settled doctrine — a distinction
its more disciplined practitioners insist on and its popular presentations often
blur.

## A short history of the field

**Antecedents.** Before the field had a name, several nineteenth- and
early-twentieth-century currents prepared its ground. The Theosophy of Helena
Blavatsky (*The Secret Doctrine*, 1888) described advanced "Masters" guiding human
evolution; Charles Fort (*The Book of the Damned*, 1919) catalogued the anomalies
that later writers would mine; and H. P. Lovecraft's fiction populated Earth's deep
past with "Great Old Ones" from the stars. None of these was yet the hypothesis —
Theosophy worked within an esoteric cosmology and Lovecraft wrote avowed fiction —
but together they seeded the imagery on which the later field would draw.

**The mid-century wave.** The modern field took shape in the 1950s and 1960s
alongside the post-war UFO phenomenon (treated in the [Forerunners](../forerunners/)
entry). Morris Jessup's *The Case for the UFO* (1955), the Soviet mathematician
Matest Agrest's 1959 essays reading the terraces of Baalbek and the destruction of
Sodom in technological terms, and Jacques Bergier and Louis Pauwels's *Le Matin des
magiciens* (1960) circulated the idea widely; in France, Robert Charroux built a
popular alternative history on it.

**The 1968–1970 pivot.** Three works published within two years fixed the modern
shape of the field. Jean Sendy's *Les dieux nous sont nés* (1968) and *Ces dieux
qui firent le ciel et la terre* (1969) read the Hebrew *Elohim* as a physical,
plural presence rather than as a transcendent single God. Erich von Däniken's
*Chariots of the Gods?* (1968) carried a similar thesis to a mass audience and
became one of the best-selling non-fiction books of the century. And Giorgio de
Santillana and Hertha von Dechend's *Hamlet's Mill* (1969) argued — without any
appeal to extraterrestrials — that a vast body of world myth encodes the slow
astronomical cycle of the precession of the equinoxes. Sendy and *Hamlet's Mill*
both matter disproportionately to Wheel of Heaven, for reasons given below.

**Sitchin and the elaboration of the system.** Zecharia Sitchin's *The 12th
Planet* (1976) and the twelve-volume *Earth Chronicles* that followed gave the
field its most detailed single reconstruction: a planet "Nibiru" on a 3,600-year
orbit, and the Sumerian Anunnaki as its inhabitants, who — on Sitchin's reading —
engineered humanity as a labour force to mine gold. In the same year Robert
Temple's *The Sirius Mystery* argued that the Dogon of Mali preserved knowledge of
the companion star Sirius B that they could not have obtained unaided.

**Television and the widening audience.** From 2009 the History Channel series
*Ancient Aliens*, hosted by Giorgio Tsoukalos and featuring von Däniken among
others, carried the material to a global television audience and made its imagery
ubiquitous — while drawing sustained criticism from scientists and historians for
presenting contested claims as established fact.

**Contemporary textual currents.** A more recent strand works closely with the
biblical text itself. [Mauro Biglino](../mauro-biglino/), for seventeen years a
translator of Hebrew for the Vatican's Edizioni San Paolo, argues that a strictly
literal reading of the Hebrew Bible describes the Elohim as physical beings rather
than a single transcendent deity; the Australian writer Paul Anthony Wallis
(*Escaping from Eden*, 2020) develops a comparable comparative reading across the
Hebrew and Mesopotamian material. This textual-philological work sits closer to the
corpus's own method than the monument-cataloguing of the earlier popularisers.

## Key proponents and their claims

**Erich von Däniken** (b. 1935) is the field's principal populariser. His method
is to catalogue apparent anomalies — the engineering of the Egyptian pyramids, the
Nazca lines, the Easter Island *moai*, the vision of Ezekiel — and to read them as
traces of visiting technology. The reach of *Chariots of the Gods?* is hard to
overstate; its method, however, has been widely faulted, including by other writers
within the field, for selective use of evidence and for the assumption that ancient
peoples could not have built their own monuments.

**Zecharia Sitchin** (1920–2010) built the field's most elaborate reconstruction
from Sumerian and Akkadian sources. On his account the Anunnaki — whose name he
glossed as "those who from heaven to earth came" — were visitors from Nibiru who
created humanity through genetic engineering. His readings of the cuneiform are
rejected by professional Sumerologists, who note that he was not a trained reader
of the languages and that the astronomical claim for Nibiru has no observational
support. Sitchin nonetheless remains one of the most widely read authors in the
tradition, and his identification of the Anunnaki with the biblical [Elohim](../elohim/)
is a point the corpus takes seriously even as it rejects the framework he built
around it.

**Jean Sendy** (1910–1978) is, for Wheel of Heaven, the most consequential figure
in the field's history. A Russian-French translator and writer, Sendy read
[Genesis](../genesis/) as a compressed historical-cosmological record, mapped its
"days" onto the precessional ages, and adopted the *Hamlet's Mill* framework early.
He published *L'Ère du Verseau* ("The Age of Aquarius") in 1970, three years before
Vorilhon's reported contact. The corpus treats Sendy as its principal pre-Raëlian
source; his detailed treatment lives in the [Jean Sendy](../jean-sendy/) entry.

**Mauro Biglino** and **Paul Anthony Wallis** represent the contemporary textual
wing described above — the closest of the field's currents, in method, to the
corpus's own reliance on close reading of the [Hebrew Bible](../hebrew-bible/).
Alongside them a broader population of authors and broadcasters — **Robert Temple**
(the Dogon–Sirius argument), **David Hatcher Childress**, **Giorgio Tsoukalos**
(host of *Ancient Aliens*), and the late **Lloyd Pye** — carry the popular field,
with methods and reliability that vary widely from one to the next.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The corpus is a self-aware member of the ancient astronaut family, but it differs
from the rest of the field in both method and substance.

**Grounded in a contact account, not in inference.** The popular field reasons
from ancient evidence *toward* a hypothesis of contact. The corpus reverses the
order: it begins from the Raëlian account of a direct encounter, reported by
Vorilhon (see [Message from the Designers](../message-from-the-designers/)), and
reads the ancient evidence in that light. This is a foundational premise of the
framework — not a conclusion mainstream scholarship shares — and the entry marks it
as such rather than presenting it as demonstrated.

**A synthesis of Sendy and the Raëlian canon.** The corpus's distinctive move is
to integrate Sendy's systematic interpretation of the biblical and comparative
material with the Raëlian contact narrative. The two converge on several points —
the [Elohim](../elohim/) as a physical [plurality](../plurality-of-gods/) rather
than a transcendent God, Genesis as a garbled record of a real cosmological
history, the present as the opening of an Aquarian age — and the corpus reads the
Raëlian account as confirming and extending the framework Sendy had already
sketched.

**The precessional spine.** Following *Hamlet's Mill*, the corpus organises the
long history of contact around the precession of the equinoxes, tracking the
alliance's work across the roughly twenty-six-thousand-year cycle. Santillana and
von Dechend proposed no extraterrestrials whatever; the corpus borrows their
structural argument about myth and precession, not their conclusions, and says so.

**Epistemic discipline.** The corpus labels the status of each of its claims —
distinguishing what a source states directly, what a comparison suggests, what the
framework infers, and what is frank speculation. That discipline is what the claim
badge on this page reflects, and it is meant precisely to answer the standard charge
against the field: that it presents speculation as fact. The related interpretive
method — reading old gods as real figures later mythologised — is treated under
[Neo-Euhemerism](../neo-euhemerism/).

**Respect for the human record.** Much of the field assumes that ancient peoples
could not have raised their own monuments or worked out their own astronomy. The
corpus does not. It reads contact as occasional cultivation rather than as a
substitute for human achievement — a stance nearer to mainstream archaeology's on
the competence of ancient builders than to von Däniken's.

### How the corpus differs from Sitchin

Because Sitchin's is the reconstruction most often confused with the Wheel of
Heaven reading, the differences are worth stating directly. In the corpus:

- the Elohim are a multi-faction civilisation riven by internal politics — the
  conflict the corpus calls the [Theomachy](../theomachy/) — not Sitchin's single,
  unified Anunnaki group;
- their home world lies outside the solar system, not on a hidden planet ("Nibiru")
  within it;
- there is no gold-mining motive: humanity is created deliberately, as an end,
  rather than as mining labour;
- the primary text is the Hebrew Bible, with the Sumerian material read as a
  parallel witness, where Sitchin gives the Sumerian sources priority.

On one point the corpus does stand with the field generally: it holds, as a canon
claim, that the Sumerian Anunnaki and the biblical Elohim name the same creators.
What it rejects is Sitchin's particular reconstruction of who those creators were
and why they came. The disagreement is over interpretation, not over identity.

## Mainstream and critical reception

The ancient astronaut hypothesis is not accepted by mainstream archaeology, the
history of religion, or the wider scholarly community, which classify it as
pseudoscience. Its objections deserve to be stated fairly, because several of them
are sound.

The standard critique — set out at length in Kenneth Feder's *Frauds, Myths, and
Mysteries: Science and Pseudoscience in Archaeology* (first edition 1990, now in
its tenth) and in Jason Colavito's *The Cult of Alien Gods* (2005) and ongoing
commentary — runs along several lines. It charges that the field cherry-picks
supportive details and ignores their context; that it rests on the condescending
assumption that non-European peoples could not have produced their own monuments
and astronomy, an objection with real moral and evidentiary force; that its claims
are often framed so as to be unfalsifiable; and that specific reconstructions —
Sitchin's Sumerian, Temple's Dogon — collapse under expert scrutiny. Carl Sagan,
who as a young astronomer had granted the abstract possibility of ancient contact
in *Intelligent Life in the Universe* (1966, with I. S. Shklovsky), later became
one of the tradition's most effective critics.

The corpus's response is not to wave these criticisms away but to accept most of
them as valid against the field's excesses. It agrees that cataloguing monuments
without context proves little; that the dismissal of indigenous achievement is both
mistaken and offensive; and that unfalsifiable claims are worthless. Where it parts
company is on a narrower question: it holds that a disciplined reading, anchored in
specific texts and honest about each of its inferential steps, can still recover a
real history of contact behind *some* of the traditions — and that the field's
genuine insight should not be discarded along with its bad method. That is a
minority position, held against the scholarly consensus, and the corpus presents it
as exactly that rather than as an established result.
