The **tauroctony** (from Greek *tauroktonos*, "bull-slayer") is the central devotional image of the Roman **cult of Mithras**, a mystery religion that spread across the Roman Empire — especially among soldiers and merchants — from roughly the first to the fourth century CE. Every Mithraic temple (a *mithraeum*, built to resemble a cave) placed at its focus the same scene, repeated with remarkable consistency across the empire: the youthful god Mithras, in a Phrygian cap, kneeling on a bull and driving a dagger into its shoulder, while a **dog** and a **snake** leap toward the wound, a **scorpion** grips the bull's genitals, a **raven** perches nearby, and two torch-bearers (Cautes and Cautopates) flank the scene, one torch raised and one lowered. The uniformity of the image, and the near-total silence of the cult's texts, make the tauroctony the primary evidence for what Mithraism *meant* — and the object of a long argument about how to read it.

## The astronomical reading

The decisive modern interpretation is **David Ulansey's**, in *The Origins of the Mithraic Mysteries* (1989). Ulansey observed that the fixed cast of the tauroctony maps, figure for figure, onto a specific band of **constellations**: the Bull is Taurus; the dog, Canis Minor; the snake, Hydra; the raven, Corvus; the scorpion, Scorpius; the cup (in some versions), Crater; and the ears of wheat sprouting from the bull's tail, the star Spica in Virgo. These are precisely the constellations that lay along the **celestial equator** in the epoch when the spring equinox stood in Taurus — roughly 4000–2000 BCE. The tauroctony, on this reading, is a picture of the sky of the **Age of Taurus**.

Mithras himself, positioned above the bull and killing it, Ulansey identifies with the constellation **Perseus**, which sits directly above Taurus in the sky. And the killing has a precise meaning. When the Greek astronomer **Hipparchus** discovered the {% wiki(slug="precession") %}precession{% end %} of the equinoxes around 128 BCE (the discovery reported in Ptolemy's *Almagest*), he revealed that the entire sphere of the fixed stars slowly moves — that the frame of the cosmos itself is not fixed. Ulansey argues that the Stoic intellectuals of Tarsus, in Cilicia, responded by imagining a god powerful enough to *cause* that motion: a deity who could grip the cosmic sphere and turn it, and who had, in doing so, "killed" the Age of Taurus by dragging the equinox out of the Bull and into the Ram. Mithras killing the bull is the god ending an age. On Ulansey's dating the cult is therefore an intellectual aftershock of Hipparchus's discovery — invented, not inherited.

## The scholarly debate

Ulansey's reading overturned the classic account of **Franz Cumont** (1903), who had derived Roman Mithraism directly from Persian Zoroastrian Mithra-worship; the near-absence of the tauroctony from the Iranian material was one of the weaknesses Ulansey's astronomical thesis exploited. But his specific reconstruction is not the consensus. Other scholars of the cult — notably **Roger Beck** — accept that the tauroctony has a genuine astronomical dimension while differing from Ulansey on the details (Beck reads the scene through a "star-talk" of planetary and zodiacal symbolism rather than Ulansey's single Perseus-and-precession key). Still others resist the astronomical reading altogether, or hold that the cult's meaning is now largely unrecoverable. The corpus registers the debate honestly: that the tauroctony is *astral* in some sense is widely granted; that it specifically encodes precession and the death of the Age of Taurus is Ulansey's contested thesis, not a settled result.

## In the Wheel of Heaven framework

The tauroctony matters to the framework as its **best-documented case** of the pattern the whole precessional argument turns on: an age remembered as ending when its constellation is killed. Where the {% wiki(slug="hamlets-mill") %}Hamlet's Mill{% end %} thesis reconstructs age-transitions from myth of contested antiquity, the tauroctony is a hard artifact — thousands of surviving examples, securely dated to the Roman period — whose most influential scholarly reading says exactly what the corpus's precessional framework says: killing the bull *is* ending the {% wiki(slug="zodiac") %}Age of Taurus{% end %}. The bull's opposite number is present in the image too — the **scorpion**, Scorpio being the sign diametrically across the zodiac from Taurus — which the corpus reads as the {% wiki(slug="doubled-signature") %}doubled signature{% end %} of the age.

The case carries a documented irony the corpus keeps in view. Ulansey himself files *Hamlet's Mill* in a footnote as "a recent attempt to revive something like the Pan-Babylonian theory" — the discredited school of astral-mythology diffusionism — and keeps his distance from it. Yet his own reconstruction vindicates the parent thesis's central *mechanism* in one fully evidenced instance: the discovery of precession was, in his words, "absolutely shattering," and a religion grew up to personify the power it revealed. The realization Ulansey posits behind Mithraism is precisely the one Santillana and von Dechend posited behind the wrecked-mill mythology, transposed to a period where the documentary evidence can bear it. This is registered as `inferred`: the icon and the cult are historical fact, and the astral dimension is broadly granted; the specific precessional reading, and the corpus's use of it as an age-transition marker, are argued rather than asserted.
