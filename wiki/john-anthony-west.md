**John Anthony West** (1932–2018) was an American author, lecturer, and independent Egyptologist who spent his career advancing, in English, the ideas of the French esotericist **René Adolphe Schwaller de Lubicz** (1887–1961). West is one of the two figures — with {% wiki(slug="graham-hancock") %}Graham Hancock{% end %} — through whom the case for an ancient Egypt far older and wiser than the textbooks allow reached a mass audience; and he is the one whose work bears most directly on the corpus's reading of the {% wiki(slug="great-sphinx") %}Great Sphinx{% end %}.

## Schwaller de Lubicz and the symbolist thesis

Schwaller de Lubicz spent fifteen years (1937–1952) studying the Temple of Luxor and concluded, in *Le Temple de l'Homme* (1957), that its proportions and iconography encode a deliberate and sophisticated **sacred science** — a "symbolist" Egypt that understood mathematics, proportion, and cosmology at a level mainstream Egyptology does not credit. This is the *symbolist school*: the reading of Egyptian architecture and art as a consciously constructed symbolic language rather than decoration or naïve religion. It has always sat outside the academic mainstream, which regards it as unfalsifiable and unsupported by the textual record.

Among Schwaller's observations was a remark that would prove the most consequential of all: that the weathering on the Great Sphinx looked to him like the work of **water**, not wind and sand.

## Serpent in the Sky and the precessional claim

West carried Schwaller's difficult, mystical corpus into accessible English in ***Serpent in the Sky: The High Wisdom of Ancient Egypt*** (1979). Beyond the general symbolist thesis, West pressed a specific claim the corpus makes direct use of: that Egyptian religion **changed its symbolism on the precessional schedule**. On this reading the dual symbols and twin capitals of the earliest dynasties answer to the Age of Gemini; the bull-cults of Apis, Mnevis, and Mentu dominate under the Age of Taurus; and the ram-headed Amon rises "on schedule" as the equinox passes into Aries — the pharaohs' names shifting from Mentuhotep to Amenhotep as the age turns. West put the strong version on record: that "from the earliest dynasties, the precession through the zodiac was what guided the course of Egyptian artistic and architectural policy."

## The Sphinx redating

West's most public campaign turned Schwaller's water-weathering remark into a testable geological claim. In the early 1990s he recruited the Boston University geologist **Robert Schoch**, whose analysis of the Sphinx enclosure argued that the deep, rounded, vertical weathering is characteristic of prolonged rainfall — implying the monument was carved before the region became desert, and so is millennia older than its conventional Fourth-Dynasty (c. 2500 BCE) attribution. West and Schoch presented the case at the Geological Society of America and in a 1992 debate with Egyptologists at the American Association for the Advancement of Science; West's documentary *The Mystery of the Sphinx* (1993) brought it to television. Mainstream Egyptology and much of academic geology rejected the redating — Mark Lehner, Zahi Hawass, and the geologist James Harrell among the most prominent critics — and the {% wiki(slug="great-sphinx") %}Great Sphinx{% end %} entry weighs the water-erosion argument and its rebuttals in full.

## In the Wheel of Heaven framework

The corpus draws two distinct threads from West and Schwaller, and labels both honestly. The first is the **Sphinx redating**, which feeds the corpus's reading of the Sphinx as a pre-Flood monument commemorating the [Age of Leo](/timeline/age-of-leo/) — a reading the corpus finds cumulatively persuasive and mainstream Egyptology rejects. The second is the **precession-tracking claim** — that Egypt deliberately changed its animal iconography as the equinox moved from Gemini to Taurus to Aries — which the corpus uses in its [Age of Taurus](/timeline/age-of-taurus/) and [Age of Aries](/timeline/age-of-aries/) chapters, and which pairs with the {% wiki(slug="doubled-signature") %}doubled-signature{% end %} reading (Pharaoh's bull answered by the queen's scorpion).

Both are registered as `inferred`, framework-adjacent readings: West and Schwaller are real figures whose work is real and verifiable, but their symbolist thesis and precession-tracking claim are contested interpretations the mainstream does not accept, adopted by the corpus as argued readings rather than established facts. West sits beside {% wiki(slug="graham-hancock") %}Hancock{% end %} as a populariser of the older-Egypt case whose *specific checkable claims* the corpus engages while declining the surrounding certainties — the fuller treatment is in the {% wiki(slug="great-sphinx") %}Great Sphinx{% end %} entry and the Explainer [The Mill That Grinds the Ages](/articles/the-mill-that-grinds-the-ages/).
