The **Hebrew Bible** (Hebrew: תָּנָ״ךְ, *Tanakh*) is the twenty-four-book
Hebrew-language scriptural corpus, organized into three sections — the **Torah**
(Instruction), the **Nevi'im** (Prophets), and the **Ketuvim** (Writings) — whose
initial letters give the acronym *Tanakh*.{{ footnote(id="1") }} It is the
canonical scripture of Judaism, the source of the Christian Old Testament, and a
text Islam holds in respect while reading through the Quran. Composed across
roughly twelve centuries and transmitted with unusual fidelity through the
Masoretic tradition, it is among the most closely studied bodies of writing in
human history — engaged by textual, archaeological, theological, and literary
scholarship alike.

In the Wheel of Heaven framework the Hebrew Bible is the primary source the
corpus reads, the text at the centre of its synthesis. The framework treats it
not as a book of religious myth requiring belief or allegory, but as a historical
record — imperfect in its pre-scientific vocabulary, yet substantially accurate in
what it reports about the [Elohim](../elohim/) and their interventions across the
ages. The corpus states the position in [Yahweh](../yahweh/)'s voice:

> *"The Hebrew Bible is not a book of religious myths that require either literal
> belief or allegorical reinterpretation; it is a historical record, imperfect in
> its pre-scientific vocabulary but substantially accurate in its content, that
> can be read directly once the hermeneutic key is available."*

Nearly every other entry in the corpus rests on some passage of it.

## Structure, languages, and text

### The three sections

The **Torah** — Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy, the five
books of Moses — carries the creation and primeval narratives, the patriarchal
history, and the Sinai legislation. The **Nevi'im** divide into the Former
Prophets (Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings), which narrate the conquest and the
monarchy through the Babylonian exile, and the Latter Prophets (Isaiah, Jeremiah,
Ezekiel, and the Twelve). The **Ketuvim** gather the remainder: the poetry and
wisdom of Psalms, Proverbs, and Job; the five scrolls (Song of Songs, Ruth,
Lamentations, Ecclesiastes, Esther); and Daniel, Ezra-Nehemiah, and Chronicles.
In the Jewish count these total twenty-four. The Protestant Old Testament reaches
thirty-nine by splitting Samuel, Kings, Chronicles, Ezra-Nehemiah, and the Twelve
into their component books; the Catholic and Orthodox canons add deuterocanonical
books absent from both.

### Language

The text is written principally in **Biblical Hebrew**, whose forms shift across
the long span of its composition. A handful of passages are in **Biblical
Aramaic** — the imperial *lingua franca* of the Persian period — including Daniel
2:4b–7:28, Ezra 4:8–6:18 and 7:12–26, a single verse at Jeremiah 10:11, and a
two-word place-name at Genesis 31:47.

### Composition and canon

Mainstream scholarship dates the corpus across roughly twelve centuries, from the
late Bronze Age (c. 12th century BCE) through the Persian and Hellenistic periods,
with the latest material — Daniel among it — reaching the 2nd century BCE;
traditional Jewish dating places several books earlier. The canon closed in
stages: the Torah first, then the Prophets, then the Writings, with the three-part
structure largely fixed by the late Second Temple period.

### Textual witnesses

The received Hebrew text is the **Masoretic Text**, the consonantal text supplied
with vowels and cantillation marks by the Masoretes of Tiberias between roughly
the 6th and 10th centuries CE; its principal manuscripts are the Aleppo Codex
(c. 930 CE) and the complete Leningrad Codex (1008 CE). Three further witnesses
anchor the text's history. The **Dead Sea Scrolls** (c. 2nd century BCE – 1st
century CE) pushed the manuscript evidence back a full millennium. The
**Septuagint** (LXX), the 3rd–2nd-century-BCE Greek translation made in
Alexandria, sometimes preserves a pre-Masoretic Hebrew reading. The **Samaritan
Pentateuch** transmits an independent recension of the Torah. The witnesses vary
in orthography, order, and occasional verses, but agree closely on the substance:
the Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, c. 125 BCE) matches the Masoretic Isaiah across
more than a thousand years of copying.{{ footnote(id="6") }} Emanuel Tov's
*Textual Criticism of the Hebrew Bible* remains the standard synthesis of this
material.{{ footnote(id="5") }}

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

### Why the Hebrew text is primary

The corpus treats the Hebrew Bible as the most accurate of the surviving ancient
records of the Elohim's work — but on genealogical, not theological, grounds. In
the Raëlian source material the Hebrews are the direct descendants of the humans
synthesized by the Israel team that ran the [Eden](../eden/) operation, and the
text is the literature of that lineage. Its accuracy follows from continuity of
contact: the line that produced it stayed in the closest and longest relationship
with the alliance whose work it records.{{ footnote(id="3") }} The corpus states
the reading directly:

> *"At the structural center of the synthesis is the Hebrew tradition. The Hebrew
> Bible is, on the corpus's reading, the most direct and least mediated record of
> the alliance's communication with Earth. The text was produced by a specific
> lineage — the Eden lineage, the descendants of the original alliance-cultivated
> population in the Eden geographic location — across the long arc from the Taurus
> age's reconstruction through the Aries age's prophetic tradition."*

This primacy is real but not exclusive. The other creator-teams' humans kept
their own origin traditions, many of which survive in fragmentary form in the
comparative-mythology material — the Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Vedic, Greek, and
other corpora. As the source puts it, the Hebrew Bible "is one tradition among
several, not a uniquely privileged source. It is, however, the most complete and
the most explicitly developed, which is why this corpus reads it most closely."
The parallel Near Eastern texts — *Atra-ḫasīs*, *Gilgamesh*, the Sumerian
flood-and-creation material — are read as neighbouring memories of the same
events, transmitted through lineages whose record decayed faster than the Hebrew
one.

### The decoding hermeneutic

Read as a record rather than a myth, the text yields a consistent set of
operational meanings. The corpus decodes Genesis 1 as an account of genetic
engineering rather than supernatural creation; the [Nephilim](../nephilim/) as
alliance–human hybrids; the [Great Flood](../great-flood/) as a deliberate
cataclysm rather than weather; Sinai as a formal alliance audience rather than a
theophany; the parted waters as directed-beam operations; and the
[Elohim](../elohim/) as plural rather than singular. The decoding assumes a
scientifically mature reader — one who possesses the vocabulary the biblical
authors lacked — which is why the framework treats the present age as the moment
the text can finally be read for what it always said.

### Reading the original Hebrew

The framework's method is to read the Hebrew that lies behind the translations.
The most consequential move it reverses is the rendering of the plural *Elohim*
as a singular "God." As *[Message from the Designers](../message-from-the-designers/)*
puts it: *"It replaced the term 'Elohim', which refers to the creators, with a
singular term 'God', whereas in fact Elohim in Hebrew is the plural of Eloha. In
this way, the Church transformed the creators into a single incomprehensible
God."* The full case is developed in
[Plurality of Gods](../plurality-of-gods/); the plural verbs and pronouns of
Genesis — "let *us* make," "in *our* image," "as one of *us*" — are read at face
value.

Several other translation moves flatten the concrete Hebrew into theological
abstraction:{{ footnote(id="4") }}

- **taninim** (Genesis 1:21), the "great reptiles" or dragons of the creation
  account, softened to "whales" or "great sea creatures";
- **malakhim**, "messengers" or envoys, overlaid with the supernatural sense of
  "angels";
- **nachash**, read as the mere "serpent" of the garden, obscuring its role as the
  operational designation of the [Serpent](../serpent/) — the agent of the exiled
  [Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction;
- **benei ha-Elohim**, "sons of the Elohim," whose plural reference is repeatedly
  blurred in translation.

A point of canon precision belongs here, because the tradition invites exactly the
confusion. The Serpent of Eden is the Lucifer faction — the creators who sided
with their own creation and pressed to tell the humans the truth about their
makers. It is *not* [Satan](../satan/). Satan led the separate and opposite
faction: the abolitionists who judged the human project a mistake and pressed for
its destruction. The medieval fusion of Lucifer and Satan into a single figure of
cosmic evil is, on the corpus's reading, one of the costliest confusions in the
history of interpretation, and the framework keeps the two firmly apart.

### Imperfection, and the Church's part

None of this treats the text as flawless. The framework grants that the human
authors did not always understand what they recorded, that centuries of redaction
laid cultural framing over the original content, and that the canonization process
kept some texts and dropped others — the [Book of Enoch](../watchers/) among the
excluded. These are decodable layers, not fatal corruptions; the record survives
them. The corpus extends the same measured judgement to the tradition that carried
the text into the world. Even as it names the translation and theological errors,
it credits the transmission: *"do not be too hard on it, for thanks to the Church,
the word of the Bible, which is a witness to the truth, has spread throughout the
world."* The Hebrew Bible is, on this reading, both the text most distorted in
translation and the text most widely distributed because of it.

### The astral vocabulary

The Hebrew Bible keeps its astronomy sparse, which makes its two explicit
constellation-references stand out. Out of the whirlwind, Job 38:32 asks whether
Job can "bring forth **Mazzaroth** (מַזָּרוֹת) in his season" — a *hapax
legomenon* the ancient versions could no longer translate. Its near-twin,
**mazzalot** (מַזָּלוֹת), appears at 2 Kings 23:5 among the objects of King
Josiah's purge of those who burned incense "to the sun, and to the moon, and to
the mazzalot, and to all the host of heaven." Both descend, by the standard
etymology, from Akkadian *manzaltu*, "the station of a star," and the corpus reads
them as the Hebrew line's own witness to the zodiacal tradition it carried. The
dedicated treatment lives in [Mazzaroth](../mazzaroth/).

## Scholarly and critical perspectives

The framework's reading sits deliberately alongside mainstream scholarship rather
than against it, and shares much of its factual base.

**Source criticism.** The Documentary Hypothesis — given classical form by Julius
Wellhausen in *Prolegomena zur Geschichte Israels* (1878) and made accessible in
Richard Friedman's *Who Wrote the Bible?* (1987) — reads the Pentateuch as woven
from four sources (J, E, D, P), a reconstruction the alternation of the divine
names *YHWH* and *Elohim* first suggested.{{ footnote(id="2") }} The framework
does not dispute the compositeness of the text. It reads the surviving content as
a record of real events regardless of which source-layer preserved a given
passage, and treats the seams as the marks of a memory transmitted through more
than one channel.

**Textual and archaeological scholarship.** On transmission the corpus aligns with
textual criticism (Tov) and with the Dead Sea Scrolls' confirmation of the text's
stability. On archaeology it distinguishes what the evidence establishes from what
it speculates: the minimalist reading of Israel Finkelstein and Neil Silberman
(*The Bible Unearthed*, 2001) is engaged rather than dismissed, as is the earlier
Albrightian tradition it reacts against. On the history of Israelite religion,
the framework reads the divine-council and Canaanite-continuity scholarship of
Frank Moore Cross and Mark S. Smith as recovering, at the philological level, the
plurality the corpus reads at the historical one. Robert Alter's literary
translation and commentary, and the continental commentary tradition (Westermann,
Cassuto, Sarna, Kugel), are read for what they recover of the text's craft and
sense.

**Alternative-history readings.** The framework shares its interventionist premise
with a wider reinterpretive literature — Jean Sendy's philological reading, Mauro
Biglino's strict word-for-word method, Paul Anthony Wallis's comparative work,
and, earlier, Zecharia Sitchin's Anunnaki reconstruction. It adopts Biglino's
discipline of reading the Hebrew literally while grounding its own interpretation
in the Raëlian canon rather than in Sitchin's Sumerian scheme. Where Sitchin
treats Genesis as derivative of the Sumerian sources, the corpus reads it as an
independent and — on the genealogical argument above — more accurate record of the
same events.
