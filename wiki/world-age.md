A **World Age** — also called a **Precessional Age**, **Astrological Age**,
**Zodiacal Age**, or **{% wiki(slug="great-month") %}Great Month{% end %}** — is
one of twelve intervals of roughly 2,160 years that together make up the **Great
Year**, the roughly 25,920-year cycle produced by the
[precession of the equinoxes](../precession/) as it carries the spring-equinox
point backward through the twelve constellations of the zodiac. In the Wheel of
Heaven framework the twelve World Ages are the chronological backbone of the
whole corpus: each age names a phase of the [Elohim](../elohim/) project on
Earth, and the sequence from the Age of Capricorn to the present Age of Aquarius
traces one full turn of the sky against which every major event of that project
is dated.

The astronomy of the cycle is treated in the [Precession](../precession/) entry,
the constellational framework in the [Zodiac](../zodiac/), and the full turn in
the [Great Year](../great-year/); this entry covers the age itself — the
~2,160-year unit — and the use the framework makes of it to structure history.

## The precessional ages

The mechanism is ordinary astronomy. Earth's rotational axis is tilted about
23.4° from the plane of its orbit, and it is not fixed in space: it traces a slow
conical wobble, like a spinning top, completing one cone roughly every 25,920
years. The wobble comes mostly from the gravitational pull of the sun and moon on
Earth's equatorial bulge, and it moves the axis at about 50 arcseconds a year —
too slow to notice from one year to the next, but enough that, over centuries, it
shifts the apparent position of the stars.

Its most useful effect is on the **vernal equinox**, the point in the sky where
the sun stands at the first moment of spring. Against the fixed stars, that point
drifts slowly westward, so the constellation behind the equinox sun changes from
epoch to epoch. Each of the twelve zodiac constellations occupies about
one-twelfth of the circle, so the equinox lingers in each for roughly 2,160
years before crossing into the next. That interval is the World Age. Because the
equinox moves against the sun's annual motion, the ages run backward through the
familiar order of the signs — Capricorn, then Sagittarius, then Scorpio, and so
on around to Aquarius and back to Capricorn — which is a persistent source of
confusion in popular writing on the subject. Two thousand years ago the equinox
sun rose in Pisces; it is now crossing into Aquarius, which is why the present is
described as the transition from the Age of Pisces to the Age of Aquarius.

The phenomenon has a well-documented history. **Hipparchus of Nicaea** identified
precession around 129 BCE by comparing his own star positions with earlier Greek
measurements, and **Isaac Newton** gave it its gravitational explanation in the
*Principia* (1687). The age boundaries themselves are soft rather than sharp: the
zodiac constellations are unequal in size (Virgo is the largest, Cancer among the
smallest), and the criteria for marking a transition differ from tradition to
tradition, so the corpus treats the round 2,160-year figure as a working
idealization and its boundary dates as approximate.

The framework calls the unit a **World Age** in preference to *Astrological Age*
because the latter is bound up with mainstream astrology's **tropical zodiac**,
in which the signs are pinned to the seasons — 0° Aries is simply the spring
equinox, whatever constellation lies behind it — and so decouple from precession
entirely. The framework works instead in the **sidereal** sense, tracking the
actual constellations, where the question "which age is it?" is answered by
looking at where the equinox sun really rises. *Precessional Age* is the most
exact label; *Great Month* is the old Western one, on the analogy that a Great
Month is to the Great Year as an ordinary month is to an ordinary year.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

**The zodiac as an engineering calendar.** The framework reads the precessional
age not as mystical symbolism laid over the sky but as a natural unit of time the
Elohim measured and put to work — the long-duration calendar an extended project
needs to keep its bearings across thousands of years. The day comes from Earth's
rotation, the year from its orbit, the age from the precession of its axis; all
three are there to be measured, and the corpus holds that the Elohim measured
them. As the Libra chapter of *timeline.epub* puts it, "None of it was
mythological for the Elohim... For the project itself, the zodiac was an
engineering instrument." The mythology came later, when human cultures received
fragments of the framework and wove them into their sacred
traditions.{{ cite(id="intelligent-design-message-from-the-designers", text="[4]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *Intelligent Design: Message from the Designers* — the collected Raëlian source material") }}

**Twelve ages, twelve phases.** On this reading the twelve ages are the twelve
chapters of the project, and the corpus's own twelve main chapters walk through
them in order. The Age of Capricorn opens the work around 21,810 BCE with
planetary survey and the beginnings of terraforming; the ages of Sagittarius,
Scorpio, Libra, and Virgo carry the preparation of the world and its life; the
Age of Leo is the age of human creation; Cancer holds [Eden](../eden/); Gemini
holds the [Great Flood](../great-flood/) and [Noah](../noah/); and the later ages
carry the post-flood recovery, the prophetic missions, and the spread of the
message across the world.

**The doubled signature.** Each age, on the framework's reading, leaves its mark
in doubled form — pointing both to its own sign and to the opposite sign 180°
across the zodiac. Taurus is paired with Scorpio, Aries with Libra, Pisces with
Virgo, and the present Aquarius with Leo. The pairing is what lets the
astronomical content survive transmission through religions whose carriers no
longer know what they are carrying. The full argument, and its status as a
[speculative](../doubled-signature/) extension the corpus makes on its own
account rather than a claim any single source states, lives in the
[Doubled Signature](../doubled-signature/) entry.

**Taught, not discovered.** The framework's cross-cultural reading rests on
Giorgio de Santillana and Hertha von Dechend's *Hamlet's Mill* (1969), which
argues that ancient cultures across the world tracked precession and encoded it
in myth.{{ cite(id="hamlets-mill", text="[1]", title="de Santillana & von Dechend, *Hamlet's Mill: An Essay on Myth and the Frame of Time* (Gambit, 1969)") }}
The corpus adopts that thesis openly, as an inferred reading rather than settled
fact, and adds one canon claim of its own about how the knowledge arrived.
Precession is far too slow to notice within a single lifetime, so whoever
demonstrably knew it either kept an observing archive intact across dozens of
generations or was told. The framework holds that the early cultures were taught
— by the exiled [Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction, the creators who sided with their
own creation and stayed on Earth as its teachers, and not by the home-world
Council or by [Satan](../satan/)'s faction, which had argued for humanity's
abolition and had no reason to instruct it. Stated plainly, this is a claim
internal to the canon; the corpus does not present it as mainstream science.

**The current age and the second Great Year.** The present World Age is the **Age
of Aquarius**. The corpus dates its opening to 1950 CE as a round boundary, with
the source-anchored alternative of 1946 — the year of Vorilhon's birth — marking
the operational threshold.{{ cite(id="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", text="[2]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *The Book Which Tells the Truth* (1974) — the Aquarian-opening identification and the 1946 anchor") }}
The age runs to about 4110 CE, when the equinox returns to Capricorn and a second
Great Year begins. The corpus reads that turn as the closure of the first Great
Year, the cycle across which the Elohim's creation project has unfolded, and the
opening of a second in which humanity itself becomes the creating civilization,
carrying the work outward to other worlds. The forty prophets the Raëlian
material describes belong to this architecture, each mission set in the age
appropriate to it and the whole sequence pointing toward the Aquarian
recognition.{{ cite(id="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", text="[3]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *Extraterrestrials Took Me to Their Planet* (1976) — the forty-prophet pattern and the Aquarian transition") }}
The larger nested-scales cosmology this belongs to is treated in the
[Cosmic Chain](../cosmic-chain/) entry.

**Sendy's parallel reading.** Jean Sendy, in *L'ère du Verseau* (1970), develops
a complementary account of the Aquarian opening as the moment of a major
civilizational turn, read through the same precessional lens the corpus
uses.{{ cite(id="the-coming-of-the-gods", text="[5]", title="Sendy, *L'ère du Verseau* (1970), translated as *The Coming of the Gods* — the principal Sendy treatment of the Aquarian age") }}
Sendy's 1946 boundary and the corpus's smoother 1950 boundary do not displace
each other; the first records the source's specific dating, the second the
corpus's round-numbered chronology.

## Comparative cycles

The precessional ages sit within a much wider family of cyclical cosmologies, and
the framework reads them alongside that family without collapsing the differences
between them.

The most elaborate parallel is the Hindu **yuga** system. Its four ages — *Krita*,
*Treta*, *Dvapara*, and the present *Kali* — run in a 4:3:2:1 ratio to a total
*mahayuga* of 4,320,000 years, with the *Kali Yuga* alone reckoned at 432,000
years, far longer than a 2,160-year precessional age.{{ cite(id="bhagavad-gita", text="[7]", title="*Bhagavad Gita* — the principal Hindu text for the *yuga* cycle") }}
A minority tradition, following Sri Yukteswar's *The Holy Science* (1894), reads
the original *yuga* cycle as a shorter, precession-scaled one later inflated by
elaboration, and points to a 24,000-year figure close to the Great Year; that
reading is contested in mainstream Hindu-studies scholarship, which treats the
large Puranic numbers as the standard reckoning. Hesiod's five **ages of man** —
golden, silver, bronze, heroic, iron — give Greece a comparable pattern of
decline, though one with no precessional anchoring: Hesiod predates Hipparchus by
some five centuries.{{ cite(id="theogony-and-works-and-days", text="[6]", title="Hesiod, *Works and Days* (lines 109–201) — the five ages of man") }}
The Mesoamerican **Long Count** turns on a Great Cycle of thirteen *bak'tuns*,
about 5,125 years, close to one-fifth of the Great Year — a numerical resemblance
some writers read as precessional encoding and others as coincidence.{{ cite(id="popol-vuh", text="[9]", title="*Popol Vuh* — the Mayan creation narrative behind the Long Count") }}
The Zoroastrian tradition frames world history as a 12,000-year cycle of four
3,000-year periods ending in the *Frashokereti*, the renovation of the
world.{{ cite(id="avesta", text="[8]", title="*Avesta* — the Zoroastrian 12,000-year world-history cycle") }}

The modern "Age of Aquarius" is a fourth kind of parallel. It reached wide
audiences through the 1967 musical *Hair* and the Theosophical and New Age writing
around it, and it shares the corpus's vocabulary without sharing its warrant: the
framework does not derive its chronology from Theosophical sources, and it dates
the Aquarian opening on its own terms. Mainstream comparative scholarship
explains the recurrence of cyclical cosmologies through some mix of independent
sky-watching, a shared human tendency to imagine time as recurring, and cultural
diffusion — and much of the record is well accounted for that way. The framework
makes a narrower comparative claim: that the tightest of these convergences
preserve a common memory of the real precessional cycle the Elohim used, refracted
through each culture's own framing. It is offered as interpretation, and the
irreducible differences between the traditions — the vast Hindu numbers, the
Greek moralized decline, the Mesoamerican day-count — are kept in view rather than
flattened into one story.

## Scholarly and critical perspectives

The framework's reading of the World Ages layers together claims of very different
epistemic weight, and it is worth keeping them apart.

The astronomy is uncontroversial. Precession is real, well measured, and
physically explained; Hipparchus's discovery around 129 BCE is genuine and
correctly dated, and the modern rate is fixed by the IAU 2006 precession model.
Nothing in the framework contests any of this.

The cross-cultural reading is where the contest lies. *Hamlet's Mill* has had a
mixed reception: influential in the archaeoastronomy that grew up in the 1970s and
1980s, but treated skeptically by many classicists and historians of astronomy,
who object that the method lacks a rigorous way to tell genuine precessional
content from patterns that arise independently, and that the claim of a Neolithic
discovery of precession outruns the evidence. A related objection targets the
famous recurring numbers — 72, 432, 25,920 and their multiples: because 12, 30,
60, and 360 are humanity's favorite round divisors, products like 432,000 can
appear in cosmological texts with no precessional referent at all. The corpus
takes these objections seriously and does not lean on the weakest links in the
alternative-history literature; where writers such as Graham Hancock and Robert
Bauval press deep-time precessional claims, the framework notes the shared
interest in ancient sky-knowledge while grounding its own account in the Raëlian
canon rather than in a lost human civilization.{{ cite(id="fingerprints-of-the-gods", text="[10]", title="Hancock, *Fingerprints of the Gods* (1995) — the precessional-age idea in the 'lost civilization' tradition") }}

The "astrological age" idea draws its own criticism, much of it deserved: popular
treatments routinely confuse the tropical and sidereal frameworks, and the
boundary date for the Aquarian opening ranges across traditions from roughly the
fifteenth century CE to the twenty-seventh, depending on the constellation
boundaries and reference points chosen. The framework's response is to be explicit
about what it is doing — working in the sidereal sense, treating the boundaries as
conventions rather than sharp astronomical lines, and labeling its own distinctive
claim, that the precessional knowledge was taught, as a canon claim rather than a
finding of the sciences it borrows from. David Ulansey's account of the Mithraic
*tauroctony* as a star-map of the Taurus-to-Aries transition (see
[Archaeoastronomy](../archaeoastronomy/)) stands as the best-documented worked
example of precessional content preserved in a religion's imagery — the kind of
case the framework reads as characteristic, offered without overstating what it
proves.
