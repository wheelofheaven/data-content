**Saṃsāra** (Sanskrit संसार, "wandering through, flowing on") is the cycle of
birth, death, and rebirth in which — across the Hindu, Buddhist, Jain, and Sikh
traditions — sentient beings remain bound until they win release through some
form of liberation. The traditions differ over what cycles, what drives the
cycling, and how release is won, but they share the underlying picture of
existence as a wheel that turns until it is broken. In the Wheel of Heaven
framework, Saṃsāra names something the corpus reads beneath that picture: the
cyclical rise and fall of civilizations. On this reading the Indian traditions
preserved, in the vocabulary of the individual soul, a pattern that operates at
the scale of whole species — humanity rising to scientific maturity and then
either escaping the cycle into a [Golden Age](../golden-age/) or falling back
into it, restarting from a primitive state.

The reading is set out by [Yahweh](../yahweh/) in Claude Vorilhon's
*Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet* (1975), where the Buddhist account
of saṃsāra is reread not as the fate of a single soul but as the developmental
choice facing humanity as a whole. This is the entry's central claim, and it is
a claim internal to the Raëlian canon rather than one mainstream scholarship
endorses.

## Etymology and naming

The Sanskrit *saṃsāra* joins the verbal root √sṛ ("to flow, to run, to go") to
the prefix *saṃ-* ("together, completely"), yielding "wandering through, flowing
on, circling." The sense is of continuous movement through successive states
rather than a sequence of discrete events — the cycle as an ongoing flow, not a
chain of separate births. The Pali *saṃsāra* carries the same meaning in the
early Buddhist texts, and the term passes with minor variation across the Indian
languages: Sanskrit संसार in Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain usage, Punjabi ਸੰਸਾਰ
(*saṃsār*) in Sikh usage, and Tibetan འཁོར་བ (*'khor ba*, "cycling") in Tibetan
Buddhism. English scholarship writes it *saṃsāra* with diacritics or *samsara*
without; the corpus keeps the diacritical form for the framework concept.

## Saṃsāra in the Indian traditions

Saṃsāra emerges in the Vedic period (c. 1500–500 BCE) and is systematized in the
Upanishads (from c. 800 BCE), where it is bound to *karma* — the principle that
action carries moral consequence forward into future lives — and to *mokṣa*,
release from the cycle. Each tradition then develops the shared picture in its
own terms, and the differences are not incidental.

In **Hinduism**, the cycling self is the *ātman*, and liberation (*mokṣa*) is
variously understood: the Advaita Vedanta of Śaṅkara reads it as the realization
that ātman and the absolute *brahman* are one, while the Viśiṣṭādvaita of
Rāmānuja and the Dvaita of Madhva preserve a real relation between the soul and
God. Across these schools the cycle is escaped through knowledge, devotion, or
disciplined action.

In **Buddhism**, there is no permanent self to transmigrate; what continues is a
causal stream, and liberation is *nirvāṇa* (Pali *nibbāna*), the extinguishing
of the craving that fuels rebirth. The tradition's fullest map of the cycle is
the **Bhavacakra**, the "wheel of becoming," an iconographic wheel usually shown
gripped by Yama, the lord of death. Its hub holds the three poisons — ignorance,
attachment, and aversion, drawn as a pig, a cock, and a snake — that drive the
cycling; its outer rim sets out the twelve *nidānas* of dependent origination,
the causal chain running from ignorance through craving and becoming to birth,
aging, and death; and its middle band shows the six realms into which beings are
reborn (gods, demigods, humans, animals, hungry ghosts, and hell-beings), with
the human realm alone offering the opportunity for awakening.

In **Jainism**, karma is understood with unusual literalness — as a subtle
material substance that accumulates on the soul (*jīva*) and weighs it down.
Liberation comes through the systematic shedding of that burden by ascetic
discipline and non-violence, the path taught by Mahavira (c. 599–527 BCE). In
**Sikhism**, founded by Guru Nanak (1469–1539 CE), the cycle is escaped not by
withdrawal but by *nām simraṇ*, remembrance of the divine name, and ethical life
within the world; the *Guru Granth Sahib* frames release (*mukti*) in a
monotheistic register absent from the older traditions. The traditions converge
on the wheel and diverge sharply on what turns it and how it stops — a divergence
worth preserving rather than flattening.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The corpus's reading of Saṃsāra is stated in the "Buddhism" section of "The
Second Encounter," the chapter recording Vorilhon's 1975 follow-up contact,
eighteen months after the original December 1973 encounter. [Yahweh](../yahweh/)
takes the Buddhist account as an accurate description that has been applied to
the wrong scale:

> *"Buddhism explains that at the time of death, the soul of the dead person
> must be vigilant enough to escape numerous devils, otherwise it will be
> reincarnated and fall back into the cycle... In fact, this is a very good
> description, which applies not to the individual but to humanity as a whole. It
> must resist those devils, which can make it fall back into the cycle each time
> it is in a position to choose. Those 'devils' are aggressiveness directed
> against our fellow human beings or against nature, and the 'state of bliss
> through awakening' is a golden age of civilization in which science serves the
> people..."*

The reread makes three moves. The "devils" a soul must escape become the two
forms of aggressiveness — toward fellow humans and toward nature — that a
civilization must master. The "awakening" that liberates the soul becomes the
[Golden Age](../golden-age/) in which science is turned to the people's benefit.
And the fall "back into the cycle of reincarnation" becomes civilizational
collapse: a species that fails to master its aggressiveness "must start all over
again from a primitive state and advance progressively towards a more evolved
society in a hostile world, with all the suffering which that entails."

Vorilhon's source extends this into a selection principle operating across the
cosmos:

> *"In a way, this is natural selection at the cosmic level for species that are
> capable of leaving their planet. Only those who perfectly control their
> aggressiveness can reach this stage. The others self-destruct as soon as their
> scientific and technological levels permit them to invent weapons powerful
> enough to do so."*

On the canon's reading the cycle is not metaphor but mechanism. Every developing
species reaches a threshold at which its science yields both the power to leave
its solar system and the power to destroy itself — the "progress-destruction
cycle." Species that have mastered their aggressiveness pass the threshold and
reach the stars; those that have not annihilate themselves and, where anything
survives, begin the long climb again from the beginning. This is why, the source
states, civilizations capable of interstellar travel are invariably peaceful:
sustained violence and starflight cannot coexist for long in the same species.

For humanity today the threshold is dated to 1945. The acquisition of nuclear
weapons places the present [Age of Aquarius](../age-of-aquarius/) at the
selection point the corpus calls the [Apocalypse](../apocalypse/) — not an
ending but a moment of decision, its outcome contingent on how humanity navigates
it rather than predetermined. The 1975 contact frames this within a correction
Yahweh issues at the same meeting: an earlier passage implying that the creators
might intervene to destroy humanity is withdrawn — *"we will not intervene... its
future depends only on itself."* The selection is something humanity undergoes on
its own; the [Elohim](../elohim/) do not impose the result.

The corpus reads the swastika, in the pre-Nazi sense the Raëlian symbol
preserves, as the ancient sign of exactly this cycle — *"the choice between
paradise, which the peaceful use of science makes possible, and the hell of
returning to the primitive stage."* The full treatment lives in the
[Swastika](../swastika/) entry.

### The Flood as a paradigmatic restart

The clearest instance of the cycle within the corpus's own history of Earth is
the [Great Flood](../great-flood/). On the canon's reading the antediluvian
[Eden](../eden/) civilization reached an advanced technological state, and the
home-world [Council of the Eternals](../council-of-eternals/) judged that it had
passed the point the original settlement could sustain and ordered its
destruction. A remnant was preserved not by the faction that pressed for
humanity's end but by the exiled [Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction — the creators
who had sided with their creation, whom the canon identifies with the Serpent of
Eden — who mounted the ark operation that carried a small human crew and the
genetic material of the biosphere through the catastrophe. It was
[Satan](../satan/), leading the separate abolitionist faction, who had argued for
terminating the human experiment outright. The Flood is thus the cycle in
miniature: an advanced civilization thrown back to a primitive starting point,
its survivors left to redevelop across millennia in a devastated world —
precisely the "fall back into the cycle" the Buddhist passage describes, enacted
once in the deep past and held up as the pattern humanity is now positioned to
break.

### Position within the framework

Within the wider corpus, Saṃsāra names the failure mode against which the other
cosmic concepts are defined. The [Golden Age](../golden-age/) is the life that
successful escape produces; the [Cosmic Chain](../cosmic-chain/) is the succession
of created-and-creating civilizations that a species enters if it escapes; the
[Cosmic Competition](../cosmic-competition/) is the specific evaluation — across
several humanities facing the Aquarian threshold at once — that decides who
qualifies. In the language of the [Four Levels](../four-levels/), the reread
relocates saṃsāra from the individual to the collective level: it is humanity as
a whole, not the single soul, that cycles. The corpus does not thereby dismiss the
traditional individual reading; it holds that the individual reading is narrower
than the pattern it preserves, not false.

## Comparative and critical perspectives

The corpus's reading sits alongside a large body of scholarship on saṃsāra rather
than in place of it, and the differences between the traditions it draws on
should not be flattened.

Mainstream Buddhist studies (Rupert Gethin, Richard Gombrich, Walpola Rahula,
Bhikkhu Bodhi) read saṃsāra at the individual and doctrinal level the traditions
themselves foreground, and Mahayana scholarship (Paul Williams, Donald Lopez)
adds the bodhisattva's deliberate remaining-within the cycle to help all beings
toward release. Hindu scholarship (Patrick Olivelle on the Upanishads, Wendy
Doniger on the broader tradition) traces the concept from its Vedic roots through
the Vedantic schools; Jain scholarship (Padmanabh Jaini, Paul Dundas) preserves
the distinctive materialist account of karma; Sikh scholarship (W. H. McLeod)
marks the monotheistic reframing. A secular-Buddhist strand (Stephen Batchelor)
reads saṃsāra naturalistically, as a psychological and existential pattern rather
than literal multi-life rebirth — a reinterpretive move parallel in form, though
not in content, to the corpus's own.

The comparative-religion tradition places saṃsāra within a wider human tendency
toward cyclical cosmology. Mircea Eliade's *The Myth of the Eternal Return*
treats cyclical, "sacred" time as a recurring religious structure, and the
pattern recurs across cultures: in the Greek-Stoic *ekpyrosis* and *palingenesia*
(Heraclitus, Chrysippus), in Nietzsche's eternal return, in the Mayan Long
Count's succession of world-ages, in the Norse Ragnarök and the regenerated earth
that follows it, in the Chinese dynastic cycle and its Mandate of Heaven, and in
indigenous frameworks such as the Hopi succession of worlds. Mainstream
scholarship explains this distribution through a common human experience of
natural cycles, cultural diffusion, and independent invention — and is right to
caution against reading a single event behind every version.

The framework's comparative claim is narrower, and is offered as interpretation.
It reads the convergence of these cyclical cosmologies as consistent with the
memory of a real cosmic-civilizational pattern — species rising, meeting the
weapons threshold, and passing or failing — transmitted through many cultural
refractions. That claim is `speculative` where it goes beyond what any single
source states, and the corpus does not require it in order to make the narrower
point: that the closest and most detailed of these traditions, the Indian
saṃsāra, lends itself unusually well to the collective reading Vorilhon's source
draws from it. The cognitive, diffusionist, and independent-invention
explanations are not rejected; the framework adds to them the possibility that
something real is being remembered.
