**The U.S. Space Force** (USSF) is the sixth and youngest branch of the United
States Armed Forces, established on December 20, 2019, when President Donald
Trump signed the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2020. It sits
under the Department of the Air Force in the same way the Marine Corps sits under
the Department of the Navy, and it is charged with military operations in the
space domain — satellite operations, missile warning, space situational
awareness, GPS, and the defence of U.S. assets in orbit.

In the Wheel of Heaven framework the Space Force is a deliberately modest entry.
It is a documented piece of mainstream military policy, not a pillar of the
canon, and the Raëlian source texts — written in the 1970s — say nothing about
it. What earns it a place here is timing: the branch was stood up in the middle
of the 2017–2024 wave of U.S. government
[UAP disclosure](../military-cover-up-of-exobiology/), and the corpus notes that
coincidence for context while taking care not to inflate it.

## Establishment and mission

The Space Force did not create a new military capability so much as elevate an
existing one. The United States had operated in space for decades — through the
Air Force Ballistic Missile Division in the 1950s, through Air Force Systems
Command, and above all through **Air Force Space Command**, established in 1982
at Peterson Air Force Base, Colorado. For some thirty-seven years Air Force Space
Command ran the country's military satellite constellations, missile-warning
systems, and space surveillance. In 2019 that command was folded into the new
service, so the Space Force is best understood as its promotion to an independent
branch rather than as a capability built from nothing.

The legislative path was short. Trump floated the idea publicly in a March 2018
speech, directed the Defense Department to begin planning that June, and signed
Space Policy Directive 4 in February 2019. The branch was formally created that
December inside the annual defence-authorization bill, which passed both chambers
with bipartisan majorities. General John "Jay" Raymond was sworn in as the first
Chief of Space Operations in January 2020; the first recruits enlisted directly
into the service that September; and in December 2020 the branch adopted
**"Guardian"** as the name for its members, alongside the Army's Soldiers, the
Navy's Sailors, the Air Force's Airmen, and the Marines. A distinct service
dress uniform, unveiled in 2021, and a slowly forming service culture followed —
early markers of an identity still only a few years old.

The work is organized under three commands: **Space Operations Command (SpOC)**,
which runs day-to-day operations; **Space Systems Command (SSC)**, which handles
acquisition and development; and **Space Training and Readiness Command
(STARCOM)**, which trains Guardians and writes doctrine. The principal missions
are:

- **Satellite operations** — running constellations such as GPS (roughly 31
  operational satellites), the Space-Based Infrared System for missile warning,
  and military communications satellites.
- **Missile warning** — detecting ballistic-missile launches and feeding the
  wider missile-defence system; this is the branch's clearest contribution to
  U.S. strategic deterrence.
- **Space situational awareness** — tracking the tens of thousands of catalogued
  objects in Earth orbit and warning operators of possible collisions.
- **GPS** — maintaining the constellation that supplies positioning and timing to
  military and civilian users worldwide.
- **Offensive and defensive space operations** — protecting U.S. satellites
  against anti-satellite weapons and, in a largely classified mission set,
  contesting an adversary's use of space.

The Space Force is by a wide margin the smallest U.S. service — on the order of
8,400 uniformed Guardians in 2024, supported by a comparable number of
civilians — because its missions are technical and operational rather than
centred on projecting large forces. Its budget nonetheless grew quickly, from
about $15.4 billion at its 2020 establishment to roughly $30 billion by fiscal
year 2024. That growth spanned the Trump, Biden, and second Trump
administrations, and the continuity across those transitions is the clearest sign
that the branch reflects settled national-security policy rather than the
initiative of any single president.

## An international pattern

The United States was not acting alone. A cluster of national space commands
appeared within a few years of one another: Russia consolidated its space troops
into the **VKS** Aerospace Forces in 2015; China created the **PLA Strategic
Support Force** the same year, reorganizing it into the **PLA Aerospace Force**
in April 2024; France established its **Commandement de l'espace** in 2019; and
the United Kingdom stood up **Space Command** in 2021, with Germany, Italy,
Japan, Australia, and India developing comparable organizations. The near-
simultaneous timing is itself telling — anti-satellite weapons tests such as
China's 2007 Fengyun-1C strike, which scattered roughly 3,000 trackable pieces of
debris, and Russia's 2021 test made plain that orbit had become a contested
domain, and every major power responded by giving space its own military
command.

This pattern matters for reading the Space Force. It places the branch inside a
broad international trend driven by growing dependence on satellites and by
great-power competition in orbit, rather than treating it as a uniquely
American — or uniquely mysterious — development.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework's engagement with the Space Force is light, and honest about being
light.

**It is mainstream military reality first.** The 2019 establishment, the command
structure, the congressional appropriations, and the parallel foreign services
are all ordinary, well-documented facts of defence policy, and the corpus reads
them as such. The mainstream account of why the branch exists — satellite
dependence, orbital competition, the elevation of space to a war-fighting
domain — is well supported, and the wave of foreign space commands created on the
same schedule is strong evidence that the founding reflects a general trajectory
rather than any hidden cause.

**The timing sits inside the disclosure wave.** What connects the branch to the
corpus at all is chronology. The Space Force was established on December 20,
2019 — roughly two years after the December 2017 *New York Times* article that
disclosed the Pentagon's AATIP program, and about four months before the April
2020 official release of Navy UAP videos. That places its founding in the middle
of the 2017–2024 transformation traced in the
[military cover-up of exobiology](../military-cover-up-of-exobiology/) entry. The
framework treats this as a genuine coincidence of dates worth noting, not as a
causal link: nothing in the documentary record shows the branch was created
because of the UAP question.

**There is a real mission overlap.** The Space Force's space-domain-awareness
mission — watching everything that moves in near-Earth space — does technically
intersect with the detection of unidentified anomalous phenomena, and Guardians
and the affiliated All-Domain Anomaly Resolution Office have engaged the UAP
question in official settings. The overlap is mundane rather than revelatory: an
organization built to track objects in orbit will inevitably record objects it
cannot immediately identify.

**Speculative readings are labelled as such.** A separate
[ufological](../ufology/) literature goes much further, casting the Space Force
as cover for a "secret space program," as preparation for an official disclosure
event, or as proof of recovered non-human technology — claims associated with
figures such as the hacker Gary McKinnon. These readings are speculative. They
rest on inference and assertion rather than on documented programs, and the 2024
AARO Historical Record Report found no evidence for the reverse-engineering
programs they presuppose. The corpus records that this literature exists and
engages it descriptively, but does not adopt it, and keeps it firmly distinct
from the sourced facts above.

**The canon's own answer lies elsewhere.** For the Wheel of Heaven framework the
resolution of the disclosure question is not a military branch at all but the
[Embassy](../embassy/) — the ground that *Message from the Designers* asks
humanity to prepare for open, diplomatic contact with the
[Elohim](../elohim/). Against that, a satellite-operations service is peripheral.
The Space Force belongs to the ordinary machinery of a spacefaring
[Age of Aquarius](../age-of-aquarius/); it is context for the disclosure story,
not a chapter of it.

## Mainstream and critical perspectives

Nothing about the Space Force is contested as a matter of record. It is an openly
legislated, openly funded reorganization of capabilities the U.S. military
already held, and it operates within the **Outer Space Treaty** of 1967, which
bars weapons of mass destruction from orbit and reserves celestial bodies for
peaceful use. The Space Force's missions — satellite operations, warning,
surveillance, navigation, and conventional counter-space work — fall within those
limits.

Skeptics of the ufological readings make the straightforward point that a
documented military bureaucracy needs no exotic explanation, and that the burden
of proof for any "secret space program" claim has not been met. The framework
agrees on the essentials: it distinguishes what is established — a mundane
military branch, founded for stated and sufficient reasons — from what is
speculative — any covert UAP mission — and it declines to let the second borrow
credibility from the first. That discipline, reporting the documented and
flagging the speculative as speculative, is the whole of the corpus's interest in
the Space Force.
