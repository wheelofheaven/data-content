**Plurality of Gods** is the Wheel of Heaven reading that the
[Hebrew Bible](../hebrew-bible/) — and, behind it, most of the world's religious
and mythological traditions — describes *many* divine beings rather than one
supreme deity. The reading rests on a philological fact the standard English
translation hides: the word rendered "God" through most of
[Genesis](../genesis/) is *Elohim* (Hebrew אֱלֹהִים), and *Elohim* is
grammatically plural — the plural of the singular *Eloah*. Wheel of Heaven takes
that plural at face value: the beings the text names *Elohim* are the actual,
embodied [Elohim](../elohim/), a physical creator civilization, not one
incomprehensible abstraction.

Read this way, the plurality is not a residue to be explained away but the
plainest datum in the text. The framework's specific contribution is to say what
the plural refers to: not a pantheon of imagined gods, and not a poetic flourish
of majesty, but the memory of the real makers — the plural teams that carried out
the creation project the Genesis account compresses. The later Christian and
Jewish habit of collapsing *Elohim* into a singular "God" is, on this reading,
the point at which the record of a plurality of creators hardened into the
doctrine of one supreme being.

## A grammatically plural name

Hebrew builds masculine plurals with the ending *-im*, and *Elohim* carries it.
It stands in the same morphological family as *malakhim* ("messengers"),
*sarim* ("princes"), and *adonim* ("lords"). The singular forms exist and are
used — *Eloah* (אֱלוֹהַּ) and the shorter *El* (אֵל) — but across the Hebrew
Bible the plural *Elohim* is by far the more common word for the divine,
appearing roughly 2,600 times. Grammatically, nothing marks it as a special
case; it is an ordinary plural noun.

What makes the plurality more than a fossil is that the surrounding grammar
frequently agrees with it. At the creation of humanity the verb is first-person
plural: *na'aseh adam* (נַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם), "let us make man" (Genesis 1:26), with
the plural possessives *be-tzalmenu* ("in our image") and *ki-dmutenu* ("after
our likeness"). After Eden the speaker says of the human, *ke-achad mimenu*
(כְּאַחַד מִמֶּנּוּ), "as one of us" (Genesis 3:22). At [Babel](../babel/) the
plan is *havah nerdah* (הָבָה נֵרְדָה), "let us go down, and there confound their
language" (Genesis 11:7). Isaiah hears the same first-person plural from the
throne: "Whom shall I send, and who will go for us?" (Isaiah 6:8). These are the
passages a strict singular reading has to work hardest to absorb.

Beyond the pronouns, the text names a class of plural beings outright: the
*benei ha-Elohim* (בְּנֵי־הָאֱלֹהִים), "the sons of the Elohim." They appear
before the flood taking human wives (Genesis 6:1–4), in the heavenly scenes of
Job (1:6; 2:1; 38:7), and in the Psalms (29:1; 89:6), with an Aramaic cognate at
Daniel 3:25. In each case the phrase denotes discrete individuals with their own
agency, not attributes of a single deity — a category of beings, not a facet of
one being.

## The divine council

The plural name has a plural setting. Across the Hebrew Bible the divine is
repeatedly shown deliberating as a body — an assembly the scholarship calls the
divine council. In 1 Kings 22:19–22 the prophet Micaiah sees Yahweh enthroned
"with all the host of heaven standing beside him," and the council debates how to
entrap Ahab. In Job 1–2 the *benei ha-Elohim* "present themselves before Yahweh,"
and one member — *ha-satan*, "the adversary" — argues a case against Job. Isaiah's
commissioning (Isaiah 6) sets the prophet before an enthroned Yahweh and the
seraphim, who ask, "Whom shall I send, and who will go for us?"

The sharpest text is Psalm 82. It opens: "Elohim stands in the assembly of El;
among the elohim he judges" (אֱלֹהִים נִצָּב בַּעֲדַת־אֵל בְּקֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים
יִשְׁפֹּט) — a single verse that uses *elohim* both for the one who presides and
for the many who are judged, a usage no plural-of-majesty can carry. It goes on:
"I said, you are elohim, sons of the Most High, all of you" (82:6). Centuries
later the Gospel of John has Jesus quote exactly this line — "Is it not written
in your law, I said, ye are gods?" (John 10:34) — and treat it as authoritative
scripture rather than as an embarrassment to be explained away.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

On the framework's reading the plurals are exactly what they appear to be. The
Elohim who made humanity were many because the teams were many: the creation
project was staffed by seven teams drawn from the seven provinces of the Elohim
home world, and the "let us make man" of Genesis 1:26 is the ordinary grammar of
a collaborative operation, not a theological riddle. The [Elohim](../elohim/) are
not deities, not personified natural forces, and not a polytheistic residue that
later editors failed to scrub out. They are a physical civilization of biological
beings, and the Hebrew text names them in the plural because there were, in fact,
many of them.

The consequential change came later, in translation. Rendering *Elohim* as a
singular "God" is, in the Raëlian source, the Church's founding error. In
Yahweh's own articulation:

> "It replaced the term Elohim, which refers to the creators, with a singular
> term God, whereas in fact Elohim in Hebrew is the plural of Eloha. In this way,
> the Church transformed the creators into a single incomprehensible God."

The mechanism was gradual and cumulative. The Septuagint rendered *Elohim* with
the Greek *Theos*, the Vulgate with the Latin *Deus*, and the vernacular Bibles
that followed with "God," "Dieu," "Gott," "Dios." Each step was defensible on its
own terms; together they effaced a company of named, embodied makers into one
abstract absolute — and, with it, turned the recovery of who the creators
actually were into the [idolatry](../idolatry/) of an image the tradition could
no longer identify. On this reading the corrective is not the rejection of
[monotheism](../monotheism/) so much as the recovery of its referent.

The plurality is political as well as numerical. The Elohim govern themselves
through the [Council of Eternals](../council-of-eternals/), and the Council
contains standing factions that disagreed sharply over the human creation.
[Yahweh](../yahweh/) presides as its moderate president. [Satan](../satan/) led
the abolitionist faction, which held the making of beings in the creators' own
image to have been a mistake and pressed, at each crisis, for humanity's
destruction. [Lucifer](../lucifer/) led the opposite faction — pro-human, and
exiled to Earth for it — which wanted humanity told the truth about its origins
and taught what its makers knew; the [Serpent](../serpent/) of Eden is this same
faction in its disclosure role. The medieval fusion of Lucifer and Satan into a
single figure of cosmic evil is, on the framework's reading, a costly confusion:
it collapses two opposed political positions — one that wanted humanity
destroyed, one that wanted it enlightened — into one, and erases the very
plurality the divine-council texts preserve.

Finally, the plurality points forward as well as back. Through the
[Cosmic Chain](../cosmic-chain/) the framework reads the divine-council texts as
describing not a fixed hierarchy but an open process: the created are meant to
become creators in their turn. "This means," the source glosses of the reversal
in Matthew 19:30, "that the created shall become creators just as the creators
were created." Psalm 82's "you are elohim, sons of the Most High," and Jesus's
citation of it, are read in the same key — not as blasphemy but as the claim that
humanity stands in the same category as its makers, an early link in a chain with
no visible first term. Plurality of Gods, in this sense, describes not only how
many divine beings exist but what humanity may yet join.

## Comparative divine pluralities

A plural, deliberating company of divine beings is one of the most widely
distributed patterns in the history of religion, and the framework reads its
recurrence as meaningful without flattening the traditions into one another.

The Mesopotamian record is the closest neighbour: the high triad of Anu, Enlil,
and Ea presides over the broader assembly of the Anunnaki, and the councils of
the *Enuma Elish* and *Atrahasis* deliberate over creation and flood much as the
Hebrew council deliberates over Ahab or Sodom. Egypt orders its gods into the
Heliopolitan Ennead and the Hermopolitan Ogdoad; Greece into the Olympian twelve,
who overthrow an older Titan generation; the Norse into the Aesir and the Vanir,
two divine kindreds who war and then reconcile. The Vedic *devas*, the later
Hindu *Trimurti*, the Chinese celestial bureaucracy under the Jade Emperor, and
the Shinto *yaoyorozu no kami* — the "eight million *kami*" — each organise the
divine as a populous, structured plurality rather than as a solitary sovereign.

The framework's reading of this distribution is offered as interpretation, not as
proof: that the tightest of these parallels — councils that deliberate, factions
that divide, makers who are many — preserve, in their own cultural idioms, the
memory of the one creator civilization the corpus calls the Elohim. Mainstream
comparative religion accounts for the same distribution through independent
invention, shared cognitive tendencies, and cultural diffusion, and the framework
does not claim those explanations are wrong so much as incomplete. It adds a
historical referent beneath the structural commonality; it does not require that
every pantheon descend from a single source, only that the closest cluster of
parallels is better explained by shared memory than by coincidence.

## Scholarly and critical perspectives

At the textual level the claim that the Hebrew Bible preserves a suppressed
plurality is closer to mainstream scholarship than the framework's conclusions
are. A generation of critical work has documented the polytheistic background of
Israelite religion. Mark S. Smith (*The Early History of God*; *The Origins of
Biblical Monotheism*) traces the emergence of biblical monotheism out of a West
Semitic world in which El and Yahweh were originally distinct; Frank Moore Cross
(*Canaanite Myth and Hebrew Epic*) established the Canaanite-council background,
corroborated since 1928 by the Ugaritic tablets from Ras Shamra. Margaret Barker
(*The Great Angel*) reconstructs a pre-exilic "two powers" theology later edited
toward strict unity, and Michael Heiser (*The Unseen Realm*) reads the divine
council as a structuring feature of the whole corpus, with *elohim* functioning
as a category-term for the residents of the spiritual realm. Alan Segal's *Two
Powers in Heaven* shows the rabbis still polemicizing, centuries on, against Jews
who read two divine figures in the text. The Documentary Hypothesis's very
division of sources rests in part on the alternation between *Yahweh* and
*Elohim*. The framework accepts this scholarship at the philological level and
parts from it only at the interpretive one: where the academy reads a literary
and religious development, the framework reads the memory of an actual
civilization.

Against the plural reading the mainstream marshals a set of long-standing
objections, and they deserve to be stated fairly. The most common is the *plural
of majesty* (*pluralis majestatis*): the claim that *Elohim*, like a royal "we,"
is plural in form but singular in sense, and that the verbs governing it are
usually singular in any case. Close relatives are the *plural of intensity* (the
plural form heightening a single divine fullness, as *chayim*, "life," is plural
in form), the Christian reading of the plural as a *Trinitarian foreshadowing*,
the *heavenly-court* reading (the plural gestures at God's attendant angels
rather than at multiple gods), and the source-critical view that the plural is an
*archaic survival* of an earlier polytheism within a text that is monotheistic in
its final form. These are serious positions, and the majestic-plural reading in
particular is the working default of most translators. The framework's rejoinders
are that Biblical Hebrew does not otherwise use a majestic plural for God; that
an intensive plural cannot account for the plural *verbs and pronouns* rather
than the noun alone; that a Trinitarian reading is anachronistic to the Hebrew
text; and that the "archaic survival" view already concedes the plurality and
differs only on whether it records anything real. None of these rejoinders is
decisive, and the framework does not pretend the question is closed. What it
claims is narrower: that the plainest reading of the grammar is the plural one.

Two living theological traditions have independently built doctrine on the same
plural. Mormon (Latter-day Saints) theology reads *Elohim* as a genuine plurality
and teaches, in Joseph Smith's King Follett discourse (1844) and Lorenzo Snow's
couplet — "as man now is, God once was; as God now is, man may be" — a doctrine
of exaltation in which humans may themselves become gods. It is the nearest
mainstream-religious parallel to both the plurality reading and the Cosmic Chain.
The Eastern Orthodox doctrine of *theosis* — "the Son of God became man that we
might become God," in Athanasius's formula, elaborated by Gregory Palamas and the
*Philokalia* and grounded in Psalm 82:6 and 2 Peter 1:4 — converges with the
framework's dynamic dimension while remaining within a Christian metaphysics the
framework does not share. Within the alternative-history literature, Jean Sendy,
Mauro Biglino, and Paul Anthony Wallis each reach a plural-creators reading of
*Elohim* by their own routes. These convergences are structural, not identities:
each tradition keeps commitments the framework rejects, and the framework
preserves the differences rather than absorbing them.
