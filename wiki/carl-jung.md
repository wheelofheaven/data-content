**Carl Gustav Jung** (1875–1961) was a Swiss psychiatrist and the founder of **analytical psychology**, the school built on the concepts of the collective unconscious, the archetypes, the process of individuation, the *Self*, and *synchronicity* — meaningful coincidence not explained by cause and effect. Once Freud's designated successor, he broke with psychoanalysis before the First World War and spent the rest of his life on a body of work that ranged far outside clinical psychology, into alchemy, comparative religion, mythology, and astrology. For the Wheel of Heaven corpus, one late work is decisive.

## *Aion* and the Pisces aeon

*Aion: Researches into the Phenomenology of the Self* (1951, Collected Works vol. 9ii) is, among other things, a book-length reading of the two thousand years of Christian history as the astrological **Age of Pisces**. Jung noticed what the {% wiki(slug="hamlets-mill") %}Hamlet's Mill{% end %} tradition would later formalize — that the {% wiki(slug="zodiac") %}zodiacal{% end %} age and the religious era coincide — and stated the correspondence with a bluntness that, by his own account, unsettled his editors: "to the extent that Christ was regarded as the new aeon, it would be clear to anyone acquainted with astrology that he was born as the first fish of the Pisces era, and was doomed to die as the last ram of the declining Aries era."

From there Jung read the whole aeon off the constellation. Pisces contains two fishes, drawn in the star-atlases swimming across each other — one roughly vertical, one horizontal. Jung mapped them onto Christian history: the first fish is the millennium of ascendant faith; an *enantiodromia* (a reversal into the opposite) falls near the year 1000; the meridian of the second fish corresponds to the sixteenth century and the Reformation; and the second fish's tail runs out in what Jung called the "Luciferian development of science and technology" — the modern age. He computed the entry into the next aeon, Aquarius, at various points "in the course of the third millennium," most often cited as **1997 or 2154** depending on the starting star. He hedged the *mechanism* throughout — the counter-swimming fishes are attested mainly in Christian-era sources, which "leads one to suspect a certain tendentiousness" — but never the correspondence itself: "the course of our religious history as well as an essential part of our psychic development could have been predicted more or less accurately, both as regards time and content, from the precession of the equinoxes through the constellation of Pisces."

## The archetypal reading of myth

Jung's wider legacy for the corpus is the **archetypal** approach to myth, which he and (in a different key) Joseph Campbell established as one of the twentieth century's dominant frameworks for reading religious and mythological material. On this reading recurring mythic figures and patterns are expressions of universal structures of the psyche. The corpus's {% wiki(slug="comparative-mythology") %}Comparative Mythology{% end %} entry treats this school at length and distinguishes it from the corpus's own {% wiki(slug="neo-euhemerism") %}neo-euhemerist{% end %} method, which reads myth as compressed memory of real events rather than as projection of inner archetypes.

## In the Wheel of Heaven framework

The corpus reads Jung, on the precessional question, as **reception history** — a witness that the pattern keeps being seen. He belongs beside Frances Rolleston's *Mazzaroth* and the Theosophical writers: serious readers who independently arrived at the recognition that religious history tracks the precessional ages, and whose work the corpus files alongside the stronger evidence rather than on top of it. What Jung adds to that company is stature and specificity — a major twentieth-century intellect, working from inside the Western scientific tradition, committing to the correspondence in print and following it century by century.

Where the corpus parts from him is the **mechanism**. Jung read the age-transitions as *synchronistic* — an acausal, archetypal parallelism between the sky and the collective psyche, "a synchronistic concomitant" rather than a causal influence. The canon reads the same correspondence as an engineered **calendar**: the precessional ages are the {% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim's{% end %} own clock, and the religious eras track them because the {% wiki(slug="the-tradition") %}Tradition{% end %} was taught to track them, not because an archetype surfaces on schedule. The correspondence Jung documented is, on the corpus's reading, real and inferred; his explanation of it is his own. His datings of the Aquarian dawn (1997/2154) join the range of estimates — Sendy's 1950, Campion's 1890, the sidereal AD 2597 — that the corpus lays out in the [Age of Aquarius](/timeline/age-of-aquarius/) chapter and the Explainer [The Mill That Grinds the Ages](/articles/the-mill-that-grinds-the-ages/). This entry describes Jung and his work as fact; the corpus's adoption of the correspondence, and its divergence on the mechanism, are flagged as such.
