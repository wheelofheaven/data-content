**The Tradition** (French *la Tradition*; Hebrew שַׁלְשֶׁלֶת הַקַּבָּלָה,
*shalshelet ha-Kabbalah*, "chain of Tradition") is, in the Wheel of Heaven
framework, the cross-cultural body of preserved knowledge — cosmic,
astronomical, religious, and technical — handed down across millennia within a
set of esoteric teaching lineages and, on the framework's reading, traceable to
the instruction the [Elohim](../elohim/) gave humanity in the early post-flood
ages. The concept is principally the work of [Jean Sendy](../jean-sendy/)
(1910–1978), the French author who argued across his books from 1963 to 1972
that the ancient and medieval traditions of the West — Egyptian priestly, Hebrew
prophetic, Greek philosophical, Hermetic, Christian-scholastic — preserve
accurate memory of contact with "sky-people," carried forward through specific
priestly and pedagogical chains. The corpus inherits Sendy's Tradition and
extends it, joining it to the later Raëlian material (Vorilhon's 1973–1975
contact accounts), to the precessional reconstruction of *Hamlet's Mill*, and to
comparative traditions Sendy engaged less systematically.

The capital *T* is deliberate. **The Tradition** is not tradition in the ordinary
sociological sense — the customs and practices a culture passes down — but a
claim about a specific content: a preserved knowledge with datable origins (the
post-flood ages, particularly [Taurus](../timeline/age-of-taurus/) and
[Aries](../timeline/age-of-aries/) on the corpus's chronology), identifiable
transmission lineages, and recoverable substance (precessional astronomy, the
[plurality](../plurality-of-gods/) of the creators, the wider
cosmic-civilizational picture). Sendy took the capitalization from the same
convention the Hebrew sources use when they speak of *the* received tradition,
and the corpus keeps it.

## Etymology and naming

The English *tradition* descends from Latin *traditio*, from *tradere* —
*trans-* ("across") plus *dare* ("to give"), "to hand over." The word carries a
spread of senses: a handing-over, a legal surrender, the body of teaching
transmitted across generations, and, in late Latin, betrayal (the handing-over
of someone to enemies). The Tradition works in the third sense — teaching handed
across time — and marks it with the capital letter.

The most developed vocabulary for this handing-over is Hebrew. *Kabbalah*
(קַבָּלָה) means simply "reception," from the root ק־ב־ל, "to receive": that
which is received from teacher to student. The phrase *shalshelet ha-Kabbalah*,
"chain of Tradition," names the lineage itself, and its classic statement opens
Pirkei Avot (Mishnah, c. 200 CE): "Moses received the Torah at Sinai and
transmitted it to Joshua; Joshua to the Elders; the Elders to the Prophets; and
the Prophets to the Men of the Great Assembly." The detailed treatment of the
Jewish esoteric stream lives in the [Kabbalah](../kabbalah/) entry; here it
stands as one instance of a wider pattern. Nearly every literate tradition names
the same thing — Sanskrit *paramparā* ("succession," teacher to student), Greek
*paradosis* ("handing over," used across Pythagorean philosophy and in Paul's
letters), Arabic *isnād* and Sufi *silsila* ("chain"), Tibetan *rgyud*
("continuum"), Chinese *daotong* ("lineage of the Way") — a convergence the
framework reads as significant rather than incidental.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

On the framework's reading The Tradition is not a diffuse spiritual inheritance
but a definite body of knowledge that survived inside protected teaching
lineages: the mathematics of [precession](../world-age/), the reading of the
creators as a plurality rather than a single God, the picture of Earth as one
site within a wider cosmic order, and a set of technical anticipations —
transmutation, flight, travel to the Moon, life on other worlds — that Sendy
pressed hardest. The knowledge originates, in this reading, in the instruction
the exiled creators gave the human population that survived the
[Flood](../great-flood/): a teaching begun before the Flood by the exiled
[Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction — the creators who had sided with their creation
and remained on Earth — and carried forward through the covenant lineages of the
post-flood ages. This was the preservation-and-disclosure faction, and it is
distinct from the abolitionist party led by [Satan](../satan/), which sought
humanity's end rather than its schooling; the two held opposite positions, not
one.

Sendy located the transmission in specific chains: the Egyptian priesthood, the
Hebrew prophetic-rabbinic-Kabbalistic line, the Pythagorean-Platonic
philosophers, the Hermetic tradition, and the Christian medieval schools. His
central argument for their unity is compact. In *Ces dieux qui firent le ciel et
la terre* (1969) he set out four points: the priests of Egypt, the Jews, and the
Christians all claimed to inherit a Tradition that came "from the sky"; the
unbroken persistence of the Bull–Ram–Fish zodiacal symbolism shows that a single
Tradition is at issue; the sky-knowledge the Egyptians credited to "gods," the
Jews to "Elohim," and the Christians to "angels" names one reality under three
vocabularies; and the concrete existence of those beings is the most rational
explanation for the precise knowledge of the precession of the equinoxes that
the shared symbolism preserves.

Sendy framed the whole enterprise as a wager he called **medievalism**. He
identified as a *médiéviste* against the Renaissance and Enlightenment
"humanists" who had dismissed the medieval imagination — transmuting metals,
building flying machines, reaching the Moon, populating other worlds — as
superstition. The twentieth century, he argued, had confirmed the medievals and
embarrassed the humanists: "Medievalists have always believed in the possibility
of transmuting metals, making flying machines, and even going to the moon. They
were positively ridiculous in the nineteenth century, when the absurdity of
their wild notions was clearly demonstrated. Today, of course, being a
medievalist, I feel more at ease than a humanist." His statement of the method
is a single sentence: "This book is a reading of that Tradition in the light of
today's scientific knowledge, and it will be either confirmed or invalidated by
the knowledge and discoveries of tomorrow."

Within the lineages Sendy read particular figures as carriers. Hermes
Trismegistus is "the god who transmitted to humans the science of the gods," and
his maxim — "what is above is like what is below" — states the cosmic
correspondence the Tradition encodes. The Hebrew and Greek streams converge:
what "the Hebrew Tradition teaches through linguistic symbolism, the Greek
Tradition has always sought to express through mathematical symbolism," the two
triangles of the Seal of Solomon (heaven pointing down, earth pointing up,
interlaced) recalling the same promised alliance that Plato's number-series in
the *Timaeus* encodes. And Sendy read his own century as the fulfilment of a
prediction the Tradition carried: the entry into Aquarius as the end of the
"times of secrecy," marked by the publication in Jerusalem of Kabbalistic texts
that "were not to be published until the approach of the fulfillment of times"
and by the convergence of modern science on what the Tradition had held.

The corpus takes Sendy's Tradition as its principal interpretive scaffold and
extends it. The Raëlian material postdates his major works — Vorilhon's first
reported contact falls in December 1973 — and supplies detail Sendy lacked: the
specificity of the Elohim as a biological civilization, the Aquarian-age embassy
mission, the wider cosmic chain. To this the corpus adds the precessional
reconstruction of *Hamlet's Mill* (Santillana and von Dechend, 1969) and
comparative material from Mesopotamian, Vedic, Buddhist, Daoist, and
Mesoamerican traditions that Sendy touched only lightly. The division of labour
is worth stating plainly: the concept and its principal moves are Sendy's; the
integration with the later canon and the wider comparative record is the
corpus's own contribution, and is offered as such.

## Comparative traditions

The nearest neighbour to The Tradition is the **perennial philosophy**
(*philosophia perennis*) — the idea, named by Agostino Steuco in 1540, revived by
Aldous Huxley in 1945, and systematized by the perennialist school of René
Guénon, Frithjof Schuon, Ananda Coomaraswamy, and Huston Smith, that a single
wisdom underlies the world's religions. The proximity is real, and so is the
difference. The perennialists locate the shared thing in a *transcendent unity of
religions* — one metaphysical Absolute glimpsed through many symbolic forms — and
their commitment is religious: the unity is a divine reality. The Tradition
locates the shared thing in a *historical* memory — one episode of embodied
contact, remembered many ways — and its commitment is materialist: the Elohim are
biological beings, not a transcendent divinity. Both notice the same convergence
across traditions; they explain it on different ground, and the framework does not
collapse the two into one.

The Tradition also overlaps the academic study of Western esotericism, where the
work of Frances Yates on the Renaissance Hermetic tradition, and of Wouter
Hanegraaff and Antoine Faivre on esotericism as a distinct and long-marginalized
domain of European intellectual history, established that this material rewards
serious study rather than dismissal. The framework shares that seriousness while
making a stronger claim than any of these scholars would — that the content
preserved is the memory of an actual event.

Set beside the world's other transmission institutions, The Tradition is one
entry in a crowded field. The Jewish *shalshelet ha-Kabbalah*, the Hindu
*guru-shishya paramparā*, the Sufi *silsila*, the Buddhist lineage transmissions
(the Zen line tracing itself back through the Indian patriarchs to the Buddha),
the Daoist ordination lineages — each is a genuine, centuries-long apparatus for
preserving knowledge across generations under conditions of restricted access.
The framework reads them as parallel receptions of a shared content; it does not
treat them as interchangeable, and their doctrinal and cultural differences are
real and are kept.

## Critical perspectives

The mainstream explanation for the cross-cultural pattern needs no appeal to a
single ancient event. Transmission lineages recur worldwide because the
pedagogical impulse is universal, because human cognition throws up similar
structures independently, and because cultures borrowed from one another across
long histories of contact — the Mediterranean exchange, the Silk Road, the
medieval transmission of Hermetic and Greek texts through the Islamic world. The
framework does not dispute that any of these operated; independent development, a
shared cognitive substrate, and diffusion plainly contribute to the record. What
it adds is a further claim — that beneath the pattern lies a real transmitted
content, the memory of actual contact — and that addition is where it parts from
the scholarly consensus.

The most careful academic engagement with Sendy himself is Stefano Bigliardi's
"A Gentleman's Joyous Esotericism," which places him within the twentieth-century
current that fed the ancient-astronaut genre while taking seriously his learning
and his distinctive, playful tone. Bigliardi reconstructs the core of the
Tradition thesis as Sendy stated it — Moses as "the custodian of an ancestral
Tradition" with a historical element (the arrival of cosmonauts Sendy first
called Theosites) and a prophetic one (humanity's later development), both keyed
to the precession — and notes that Sendy's principal works of 1963–1972 developed
the idea in parallel with, and in conceptual terms partly ahead of, Erich von
Däniken's *Chariots of the Gods* (1968). The Tradition, in short, is a specific
proposal with a real intellectual pedigree; the corpus presents it as a working
hypothesis, grounded in Sendy and extended by the later canon, and labels the
wider cross-cultural synthesis as the interpretive construction it is.
