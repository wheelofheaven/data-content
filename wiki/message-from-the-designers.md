***Message from the Designers*** — in full ***Intelligent Design: Message from the Designers*** (Nova Distribution, 2005) — is the consolidated English-language edition of the three foundational books of the Raëlian canon: *Le Livre qui dit la vérité* (*The Book Which Tells the Truth*, 1974),{{ cite(id="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", text="[2]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *The Book Which Tells the Truth* (1974)") }} *Les Extra-Terrestres m'ont emmené sur leur planète* (*Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet*, 1975),{{ cite(id="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", text="[3]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet* (1975/76)") }} and *Accueillir les Extra-terrestres* (*Let's Welcome the Extra-terrestrials*, 1979),{{ cite(id="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", text="[4]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *Let's Welcome the Extra-terrestrials* (1979)") }} together with subsequent additions.{{ cite(id="intelligent-design-message-from-the-designers", text="[1]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *Intelligent Design: Message from the Designers* (2005) — the consolidated edition") }} The three books record the two contact events at the origin of {% wiki(slug="raelism") %}Raëlism{% end %} — the December 13, 1973 encounters at the Puy de Lassolas volcanic crater near Clermont-Ferrand and the October 7, 1975 second encounter at Roc Plat — and the body of teaching {% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %} (Claude Vorilhon) reports having received across them.{{ footnote(id="1") }}

Within the Wheel of Heaven corpus, the volume functions as the **standard citation text** for the Raëlian source material. The corpus reads the comparative material through the Raëlian frame, and the Raëlian frame reaches the corpus through this specific edition: it is the principal contemporary English-language access point to the source material, the text base for the corpus's exegetical entries, and the target of the house citation convention by which other entries name the compiled canon. When a corpus entry cites "Claude Vorilhon's *Message from the Designers* (consolidating *Le Livre qui dit la vérité*, 1974; *Les Extra-Terrestres m'ont emmené sur leur planète*, 1975; and *Accueillir les Extra-Terrestres*, 1979)," this volume — and this entry — is what the convention points at.

This entry covers the volume itself: its three component books, its publication history and editions, its structure, and the conventions by which the corpus cites it. The companion entry, the {% wiki(slug="reference-corpus-of-the-raelian-canon") %}Reference corpus of the Raëlian canon{% end %}, covers the other direction — the texts, traditions, and figures the canonical books themselves quote or allude to. The two entries divide the labor deliberately: this one describes the citation text; that one maps its intertextual field.

## The title

The 2005 edition's title has two parts, and the corpus uses them differently.

**"Intelligent Design"** places the volume, at the trade-title level, within the design-argument register — the claim that terrestrial life is the product of deliberate design. The corpus reserves the bare phrase for the general design-argument topic, treated in the dedicated {% wiki(slug="intelligent-design") %}Intelligent design{% end %} entry, which also handles the volume's deliberate distinction from the religious intelligent-design movement: the canon's designers are the Elohim, an advanced civilization operating through {% wiki(slug="genetic-engineering") %}genetic engineering{% end %}, not a supernatural creator.

**"Message from the Designers"** is the operative title in corpus usage — the phrase names what the component books present themselves as: not treatises arguing a position but the record of a message delivered by its senders, through a designated messenger, at a designated moment. It is also the form the corpus's citation convention and this entry's own title use.

Within the Raëlian movement's own usage, the component books are referred to collectively as **"the Messages."** The corpus registers the devotional short form among the volume's alternative names but uses the bibliographic title in citation contexts.{{ footnote(id="1") }}

## The volume's role in the corpus

The Wheel of Heaven source program places the Raëlian books at **Tier 0** — the project's foundational canon, through which all other tiers (ancient primary sources, scholarship, scientific context, comparative traditions, critical material) are read in dialogue. The consolidated 2005 edition is the operational form that canon takes in the corpus's citation apparatus, for three reasons.

**It is the principal contemporary access point.** The combined source material has been translated into approximately thirty languages, with over two million copies sold worldwide as of the 2005 publication;{{ cite(id="intelligent-design-message-from-the-designers", text="[1]", title="*Message from the Designers* (2005) — preface statement on translation and circulation figures") }} the consolidated English edition gathered the three foundational books into the single volume through which most contemporary English-language readers meet them, distributed today through the International Raëlian Movement's official channels.{{ cite(id="rael-dot-org", text="[7]", title="rael.org — contemporary distribution point for the consolidated edition") }}

**Its translation choices matter interpretively.** Where the French originals quote the Bible chiefly through the {% wiki(slug="dhorme-bible-translation") %}Édouard Dhorme translation{% end %}{{ cite(id="dhorme-bible", text="[6]", title="Dhorme (1956-1959) — the French Bible Vorilhon consulted during the 1973 contact") }} — the version Vorilhon reports having had with him during the 1973 contact — the English omnibus uses principally the **Restored Name King James Version** (RNKJV),{{ cite(id="rnkjv", text="[5]", title="Restored Name King James Version — restores Elohim and Yahweh to the quoted text") }} which restores *Elohim* and *Yahweh* to the biblical quotations in place of "God" and "the Lord," with occasional use of the New King James Version, the American Standard Version, the Webster Bible, the Hebrew Names Version, and the Darby Translation. The canon itself notes the divergence in its editorial apparatus, and the corpus treats these translation choices as interpretive positions in their own right.{{ footnote(id="4") }}

**It stabilizes the citation target.** The three component books exist in multiple French printings, multiple earlier translations, and the corpus's own reading editions; the 2005 consolidation gives the corpus a single stable volume to resolve references against. The corpus's source record for the volume carries the id `intelligent-design-message-from-the-designers`, with the three component books carrying their own records (`the-book-which-tells-the-truth`, `extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet`, `lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials`) for locators internal to a single book.

### The Book Which Tells the Truth (1974)

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=7) %}
From very far away…
{% end %}

[*The Book Which Tells the Truth*](/library/the-book-which-tells-the-truth/) (*Le Livre qui dit la vérité*) is the foundational book of the canon, recording the December 13, 1973 first contact. In the account, Vorilhon — then a 27-year-old motor-sports journalist living in Clermont-Ferrand — walked into the Puy de Lassolas volcanic crater on a misty December morning and watched a small craft, approximately seven meters in diameter, descend silently to about two meters above the ground. The humanoid figure who emerged, approximately 1.20 meters tall with long dark hair and a beard, communicated in fluent French, and over six successive mornings delivered the teaching the book records. The figure identified himself as an officer of the {% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %}, with subsequent material identifying him as {% wiki(slug="yahweh") %}Yahweh{% end %}, president of the {% wiki(slug="council-of-the-eternals") %}Council of the Eternals{% end %}. During the contact the figure gave Vorilhon the name Raël.

The body of the book is a re-reading of the Hebrew Bible conducted through the Dhorme translation Vorilhon had brought with him. The opening chapters work through the primaeval history of {% wiki(slug="genesis") %}Genesis{% end %} chapter by chapter — creation as planetary-scale genetic engineering, the Garden of {% wiki(slug="eden") %}Eden{% end %} as the creation site, the {% wiki(slug="great-flood") %}Flood{% end %} and {% wiki(slug="noahs-ark") %}Noah's ark{% end %} read as a genetic-preservation operation, the {% wiki(slug="tower-of-babel") %}Tower of Babel{% end %} and the scattering of the creators' offspring ({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=72) %}TBWTT 2:72{% end %}), {% wiki(slug="sodom-and-gomorrah") %}Sodom and Gomorrah{% end %} — before continuing through Exodus, the fall of Jericho, the Samson narratives, Elijah's ascent, and Ezekiel's vision. The New Testament material centers on the Gospel of Matthew and the role of {% wiki(slug="jesus") %}Jesus{% end %} within the cumulative prophetic tradition.

Beyond the exegesis, the book reports the forward-looking content of the teaching: the end-of-the-world and root-of-all-religions material, the Aquarian-age program, and the request that humanity build an {% wiki(slug="embassy") %}embassy{% end %} to receive the Elohim officially. The book's title supplied the corpus term {% wiki(slug="the-truth") %}the Truth{% end %} for the message content itself. The corpus's reading edition divides the book into 7 chapters.

### Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet (1975)

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=50) %}
My name is Yahweh and I am the president of the council of the eternals.
{% end %}

[*Extraterrestrials Took Me to Their Planet*](/library/extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet/) (*Les Extra-Terrestres m'ont emmené sur leur planète*) records the second contact, dated in the corpus's principal treatment to October 7, 1975, at Roc Plat near Brantôme in the Périgord. In the account, Raël was taken aboard a craft and transported to the Elohim's home world — the **Planet of the Eternals** in Raëlian terminology, treated at length in the {% wiki(slug="elohim-home-planet") %}Elohim Home Planet{% end %} entry. The book reports the world in detail: the Council of the Eternals presided over by Yahweh — {% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=50) %}"My name is Yahweh and I am the president of the council of the eternals"{% end %} — the post-scarcity civilization of biological robots and atomic-based energy, and the earlier prophets (Moses, Elijah, Buddha, Jesus, Muhammad, among others) preserved on the home world through the Elohim's cloning-and-memory-transfer biotechnology.

The book carries the canon's principal program content: the **seven {% wiki(slug="new-commandments") %}new commandments{% end %}** — {% wiki(slug="geniocracy") %}geniocracy{% end %}, {% wiki(slug="humanitarianism") %}humanitarianism{% end %}, world government, demilitarization, science as religion, telepathic cultivation, and the metaphysical clarifications — delivered across the second contact as the political-religious program proposed for the Aquarian age. It also carries some of the canon's most-cited comparative material: the chapter on Buddhism, the direct Qur'an citations (Suras 21, 54, and 56), and the reading of Ezekiel 37's valley of dry bones as a description of the biological-creation machinery.

The opening section, "My Life Until the First Encounter," is the principal autobiographical source on Vorilhon's pre-1973 life — the 1946 Vichy birth the source frames as deliberately placed at the Aquarian-age opening, the childhood UFO experience over Ambert, the motor-sports journalism career and the founding of *Auto Pop* magazine in 1970. The corpus's reading edition divides the book into 3 chapters.

### Let's Welcome the Extra-terrestrials (1979)

{% library(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=79) %}
There is no "God", but there are the Elohim… there is no autonomous soul flying from the body after death, but there is the genetic code which allows access to eternal life.
{% end %}

[*Let's Welcome the Extraterrestrials*](/library/lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials/) (*Accueillir les Extra-terrestres*) is the third book, published in 1979. Its headline content is the **biological-parentage disclosure**: during the 1975 contact Raël was informed that Yahweh was his biological father, with the request that publication be withheld for three years to avoid what the source describes as a serious imbalance in public reception. The 1979 book published the disclosure on schedule.

The book is structured differently from its predecessors — less a contact narrative than a consolidation. It opens with a wide-ranging question-and-answer section addressing the most common questions raised after the first two messages; continues with **The New Revelations**, including the canon's extended reading of the Revelation of John as a dictation delivered through a visual-display apparatus (the {% wiki(slug="apocalypse") %}Apocalypse{% end %} read as unveiling rather than destruction); frames Raëlism explicitly as an **atheist religion** — {% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=79) %}"There is no 'God', but there are the Elohim"{% end %} — and closes with commentaries and testimonials from early Raëlians. The book also preserves the March 14, 1978 telepathic message on the parallel humanities (treated in the {% wiki(slug="cosmic-competition") %}Cosmic Competition{% end %} entry) and develops the embassy project that has organized the movement's activity since.

The testimonial section includes the follower commentary *Yes... I am Raelian*, whose enumeration of surviving "traces" (the Kojiki, the Popol Vuh, the Epic of Gilgamesh, the Mahābhārata, the Chronicle of Akakor) has been canonized by preservation across editions; its status as a follower's synthesis rather than Raël's own voice is discussed in the {% wiki(slug="reference-corpus-of-the-raelian-canon") %}Reference corpus{% end %} entry. The corpus's reading edition divides the book into 4 chapters.

## Publication history and editions

The publication history the corpus works from:

- **1974** — *Le Livre qui dit la vérité* published in French by Éditions du Message, within months of the reported December 1973 contact. Publication was followed the same year by Raël's first public talks and the founding of MADECH, the predecessor organization of the International Raëlian Movement.
- **1975/76** — *Les Extra-Terrestres m'ont emmené sur leur planète* published by Éditions du Message. The corpus's principal treatments date the book's composition to 1975 and register the 1975/76 spread in its imprint dating (see *Dating and edition notes* below).{{ footnote(id="2") }}
- **1979** — *Accueillir les Extra-terrestres* published by Éditions du Message, completing the foundational trilogy.
- **1974-2005** — the books circulate in successive French printings and in translation, reaching approximately thirty languages and over two million copies sold by 2005.
- **2005** — the consolidated English edition, *Intelligent Design: Message from the Designers*, published by Nova Distribution, gathering the three books plus subsequent additions into the movement's principal contemporary access point.{{ cite(id="intelligent-design-message-from-the-designers", text="[1]", title="*Message from the Designers* (2005) — the consolidation") }} The corpus bibliography also records a 2005 *Message from the Designers* issue under the Tagman Press imprint; the corpus cites the volume by title and year rather than by imprint.{{ footnote(id="3") }}

### Adjacent Vorilhon works outside the volume

The consolidated edition collects the foundational trilogy; it does not collect the rest of the Vorilhon bibliography. The corpus distinguishes the volume from the adjacent works, which carry their own citation identities:

- ***La Géniocratie*** (*Geniocracy*, 1978) — the elaboration of the first new commandment into a political manifesto.
- ***La Méditation Sensuelle*** (*Sensual Meditation*, 1980) — the meditation practices the source attributes to the Elohim.
- ***Oui au clonage humain*** (*Yes to Human Cloning*, 2000) — the engagement with cloning technology following the 1997 Dolly announcement.
- ***Le Maitreya*** (anthology) — the collection of Raël's teachings across the post-1973 decades, making the Maitreya identification operationally explicit.

Claims sourced to these works are cited against them directly, not against *Message from the Designers*; the volume's citation scope is the trilogy plus its own framing and supplementary material.

### Material beyond the trilogy

The consolidated edition is not a bare reprint. It carries framing and supplementary material around the three books:

- **The foreword and preface** frame the trilogy retrospectively — the foreword opens on the author's birth name and renaming ("The author of these unique writings was born Claude Vorilhon in France in September 1946. He changed his name to Rael, which means 'messenger' in Hebrew, after meeting a human being from another planet."), and the preface registers the movement's institutional maturation, including Raël's remark that the International Raëlian Movement "has become self-sustaining and could now function perfectly well without me."{{ cite(id="intelligent-design-message-from-the-designers", text="[1]", title="*Message from the Designers* (2005) — foreword and preface framing") }}
- **The 1997 addendum**, in which Buddhism is discussed as "the religion closest to the Truth" — one of the post-1979 additions the omnibus preserves alongside the trilogy.
- **The follower commentary**, most notably the *Yes... I am Raelian* section preserved within the third book's testimonial material, whose enumeration of surviving traces has been canonized by preservation across editions.
- **The editorial notes on translation**, flagging passages where the Dhorme-based French quotations and the RNKJV-based English quotations diverge, and recording the supplementary English translations drawn on.{{ footnote(id="4") }}

The edition's biblical quotations are re-based from the Dhorme French onto the RNKJV and its supplementary English translations. The re-basing is the omnibus's most consequential editorial intervention: several of the canon's exegetical moves are sensitive to which rendering a reader has in front of them, and the corpus's own philological entries flag the divergences where they matter.

## Citation conventions across the corpus

The corpus cites the volume at three levels of resolution.

**The description-level convention.** Entry descriptions and summary prose cite the compiled canon by the consolidated title with the components enumerated: "Claude Vorilhon's *Message from the Designers* (consolidating *Le Livre qui dit la vérité*, 1974; *Les Extra-Terrestres m'ont emmené sur leur planète*, 1975; and *Accueillir les Extra-Terrestres*, 1979)." The convention names the citation text and its components in one move, and links on the consolidated title resolve here.

**The source-record level.** Reference frontmatter across the wiki, articles, and timeline resolves against stable ids in the corpus's source register: `intelligent-design-message-from-the-designers` for the consolidated volume, and `the-book-which-tells-the-truth`, `extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet`, and `lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials` for the component books. The consolidated id is the conventional choice when a claim spans the trilogy or engages the omnibus's own editorial apparatus; the component ids are the conventional choice when a locator points inside a single book.

**The chapter-and-paragraph level.** The corpus's library reading editions number every paragraph, giving the canon a stable chapter:paragraph citation grammar analogous to biblical chapter-and-verse. In running prose the books are abbreviated **TBWTT**, **ETTMTTP**, and **LWTE** — so the Babel episode is cited as {% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=72) %}TBWTT 2:72{% end %}, Yahweh's self-identification as {% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=50) %}ETTMTTP 2:50{% end %}, and the atheist-religion formula as {% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=79) %}LWTE 1:79{% end %} — each reference deep-linking into the corresponding library edition. This is the citation form the corpus's close-reading work (the articles and the exegetical wiki entries) uses for canon-internal quotation.

## Reception and scholarly treatment

Outside the corpus's framework, the books are treated principally as the founding scriptures of a contemporary new religious movement. The main lines of engagement:

**New-religious-movement scholarship** provides the most sustained treatment. Susan J. Palmer's *Aliens Adored: Raël's UFO Religion* (Rutgers University Press, 2004) is the principal book-length scholarly study of the movement the books founded, drawing on extensive ethnographic fieldwork; Massimo Introvigne and CESNUR, James R. Lewis (*The Gods Have Landed: New Religions from Other Worlds*, SUNY Press, 1995), and George D. Chryssides have produced further substantial engagement. The general scholarly position treats Raël as a sincere religious figure rather than a deliberate fraud — registering the consistent five-decade pattern of behavior organized around the books' program — while not endorsing the books' claims about the 1973 and 1975 contact events, on which no scholarly consensus exists.

**UFO-religion studies** (Christopher Partridge's *UFO Religions*, Routledge, 2003; Diana G. Tumminia's *Alien Worlds*, Syracuse University Press, 2007; Mikael Rothstein) situate the books within the broader mid-century contactee tradition — Adamski, Klarer, Meier — while noting what distinguishes them within it: the systematic textual corpus, the elaborated exegetical program, and the durable institutional movement the books produced, features most contactee literature lacks.

**Journalistic treatment** across the French and international press has been generally skeptical-critical, focused less on the books' content than on the movement's controversies (the 2002 Clonaid announcement, the original symbol, the French anti-cult classifications). The corpus's position, developed in the {% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %} entry, is that mainstream scholarship has engaged the *movement* and its institutional development substantially, but the *content* of the books — the specific Genesis exegesis, the cosmological teaching, the precessional framing — only thinly; the corpus's exegetical entries work at that content level.

The corpus registers the gap honestly: the books' account of their own origin is a canon-internal claim, not a scholarly consensus position. This entry's `claim_type` is nonetheless *direct*, because the entry describes the published volume — its components, dates, editions, and conventions — rather than asserting the truth of what the volume reports.

## Relationship to the Reference corpus entry

This entry and the {% wiki(slug="reference-corpus-of-the-raelian-canon") %}Reference corpus of the Raëlian canon{% end %} entry are deliberately complementary. This entry treats the canon as a *published object*: the consolidated volume, its component books, its editions, and the conventions by which the corpus cites it. The Reference corpus entry treats the canon as a *reading subject*: the texts, translations, figures, and traditions the three books themselves quote, paraphrase, or gesture toward — the Hebrew Bible loci, the Qur'an citations, the Kabbalah passage, the trace lists. Questions about what the canon *says about other texts* belong there; questions about *which text the corpus is reading when it cites the canon* belong here.

The division matters for citation hygiene. The canon claims revelation, not derivation: the books present prior religious literature as imperfect records of earlier Elohim contact rather than as their own sources. Keeping the volume-entry and the intertextual-catalogue separate lets the corpus describe the published object precisely without importing that interpretive question into every bibliographic reference.

## Dating and edition notes

A few edition-level tensions in the corpus's own records are worth registering.

**The second book's dating.** The corpus's principal treatments date the second contact to October 7, 1975 and the second book's publication to 1975/76 — the composition following immediately on the contact, with imprint dating spanning the year boundary. The corpus's source register carries the book under a 1976 publish date, and one corpus surface summarizes the second encounter under a July 31, 1975 date. The house citation convention uses 1975 for the component book, following the description-level convention quoted above.

**The first book's imprint year.** The corpus's source register carries *The Book Which Tells the Truth* under a 1973 date — the year of the reported contact — while the corpus's narrative treatments consistently date publication to early 1974. The house convention uses 1974.

**Imprint variation on the 2005 edition.** The consolidated edition is cited across the corpus under Nova Distribution, with the Tagman Press issue also recorded in the bibliography of the {% wiki(slug="raelism") %}Raëlism{% end %} entry. Since the corpus cites the volume by title, year, and source id rather than by imprint, the variation has no effect on reference resolution.

None of these affects the citation grammar: the source ids and the chapter:paragraph grammar are stable regardless of which printing a reader holds.
