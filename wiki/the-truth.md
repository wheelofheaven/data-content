**The Truth** (French *la Vérité*; Hebrew אֱמֶת, *emet*) is the opening chapter
of Claude Vorilhon's *[The Book Which Tells the
Truth](../../library/the-book-which-tells-the-truth/)* (*Le Livre qui dit la
vérité*, 1974), the founding scripture of [Raëlism](../raelism/). In it the
[Elohim](../elohim/) officer the Hebrew Bible calls [Yahweh](../yahweh/) gives
Vorilhon a decoded reading of Genesis: creation as laboratory work, Adam and
Eve, the [Serpent](../serpent/), the expulsion, the Flood, and Babel, told back
as the compressed and half-forgotten memory of an advanced civilisation's work
on Earth. The chapter gives the book its name — the claim that this reading, and
not the supernatural surface, is what the text was always about.

By extension, "the Truth" (capitalised, *la Vérité*) is the framework's name for
the reality that chapter recovers: the record of the Elohim, their work across
the twelve precessional ages, and the contact moments preserved across the
world's traditions. On this account the record was never lost, only mystified —
carried faithfully in the religious texts but wrapped, over millennia of
pre-scientific transmission, in a supernatural vocabulary that hid what it
described. Recovering it means reading the same texts through the plain sense the
mystical overlay obscured.

## The chapter and the book

*The Book Which Tells the Truth* records what Vorilhon says took place over six
successive mornings, beginning December 13, 1973, at the Puy-de-Lassolas crater
near Clermont-Ferrand, where an occupant of a landed craft met him and dictated
a teaching. "The Truth" is the long doctrinal chapter at its centre. It is not a
philosophical essay on truth but a guided walk through the Hebrew Bible, in which
Yahweh corrects the text passage by passage and Vorilhon records the corrections.
The format is dialogue throughout — Vorilhon questions, Yahweh explains — and much
of the chapter's force comes from that back-and-forth rather than from bare
assertion. The timing is framed as deliberate: the account is given now, at the
opening of the Age of Aquarius, because only a scientific age could receive it
without turning it back into myth.

Its through-line is that the Bible is a human record of contact, garbled by the
limits of the people who preserved it. The plural noun *Elohim*, conventionally
rendered "God," is read as a genuine plural — a group, "those who came from the
sky" — rather than a single deity. The Genesis creation days become a compressed
account of deliberate biological work: a team synthesising plants, animals, and
finally humanity "in their own image," in laboratories, on a young Earth. Adam
and Eve are the first successful human results. The tree of knowledge is the
scientific understanding the creators debated sharing; the Serpent is the faction
that shared it. The expulsion from the garden is the creators withdrawing the new
humans from the research station once that knowledge was out. The later chapters
of Genesis are read the same way, as real events behind a miraculous telling: the
[Nephilim](../nephilim/) as hybrid offspring, the [Flood](../great-flood/) as a
decision to destroy a civilisation that had grown too capable, the tower of
[Babel](../babel/) as a technological advance that had to be broken up, and the
testing of [Abraham](../abraham/) as a loyalty check — the last of these a titled
section within this same chapter.

This decoded run-through is the doctrinal foundation on which the rest of the
teaching rests. Everything Raëlism goes on to ask of humanity — that it recognise
its makers, and prepare an embassy to receive them — follows from the claim
established here, that the makers were real people and the scriptures their
misremembered record. That is what makes "The Truth" the load-bearing chapter of
the book and, through it, of the movement.

The book collects Vorilhon's first contact account. Two further volumes
followed — *Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet* (1975) and *Let Us
Welcome the Extra-Terrestrials* (1979) — and were gathered with it in the
consolidated *[Message from the Designers](../message-from-the-designers/)*
(Tagman Press, 2005).

## What "the Truth" means

The chapter's title is also a claim about method. In the framework's usage "the
Truth" is not an abstract property of statements but a body of specific
historical content — what actually happened — that the traditions have carried in
disguised form. The world's words for truth already hold the two registers the
framework leans on: the Hebrew *emet* and the Old English *trēowþ* carry truth as
faithfulness and reliability, while the Greek *alḗtheia* carries it as
un-concealment, a bringing to light of what was hidden. "The Truth" is meant in
both senses at once — fidelity to what occurred, and the uncovering of it.

This is a deliberately different use of the word from the one philosophers
debate. The long argument over what truth *is* — whether it lies in a
proposition's correspondence to fact, its coherence with other beliefs, or its
practical usefulness, or whether "true" names no real property at all — asks
after the nature of truth in general. The framework brackets that question.
It reserves "the Truth" for particular content, the events themselves, and reads
the same concern for a truth preserved and awaiting recovery in other traditions'
key words — the Hebrew *emet*, the Greek *alḗtheia*, the Sanskrit *satya*, the
Arabic *al-Ḥaqq* — as parallel keepers of one memory rather than as rival
philosophies.

Two methods do the uncovering. Vorilhon's is etymological: religious words, read
back to their roots, turn out to describe something plain. He glosses *etymology*
itself (Greek *etumos*, "true," + *logos*, "science") as "the science of the
truth," and treats terms like *apocalypse* ("uncovering"), *religion* (Latin
*religare*, "that which links"), and *prophet* ("the one who reveals") as
recoverable in the same way. The second method is older and more systematic. Jean
[Sendy](../jean-sendy/), whose 1960s work anticipates much of the Raëlian
reading, set four conditions for reading the Hebrew Bible as history rather than
legend: know the Bible; know the real possibilities and limits of space flight;
hold current knowledge of biology, prehistory, history, and evolution; and refuse
"explanation by the Inexplicable," any appeal to the supernatural. Meet those,
Sendy argued, and the text acquires "the relief and coherence of a hologram."

Both methods share a self-limiting claim: the framework presents itself as
restoration, not revelation. It does not ask to be believed on the authority of a
new vision; it asks the reader to look at the same old texts and judge whether
the plain reading holds. What it offers is the reassembly of something the
traditions preserved and later forgot how to read — which is why the corpus
treats the recovery as ongoing rather than finished.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

Stated in the framework's own voice, the chapter's claim is that the Hebrew Bible
is a record of contact with a physical, mortal, technologically advanced people —
the Elohim — and that its "God" is that people, remembered as a deity once the
memory of who they were had faded. Yahweh, who speaks with Vorilhon, is not that
abstract God but a specific officer: the president of the
[Council of the Eternals](../council-of-eternals/) on the Elohim home world.

The decoded Eden turns on a political split among the creators, and the framework
is careful to keep that split distinct from the single "Devil" of later
tradition. The [Serpent](../serpent/) who tells the first humans the truth —
"your eyes shall be opened" — is read as the dissenting faction of creators who
wanted humanity to know what it was and who had made it. Their leader is
[Lucifer](../lucifer/), the "light-bringer"; for that disclosure the faction was
exiled to Earth, where it became the teacher of early humanity through the
centuries before the Flood. This is a different figure and a different quarrel
from [Satan](../satan/), an Eloha who remained on the home world leading the
faction that opposed the human project outright and pressed for its abolition.
Disclosure and abolition are two separate positions held by two separate figures;
the framework reads the later collapse of Serpent, Lucifer, Satan, and Devil into
one personified evil as exactly the kind of mystifying overlay it sets out to
strip.

The same reading runs through the rest of the chapter. The Flood is a deliberate
strike ordered by the home-world Council against a human civilisation that had
grown too capable, survived through the intervention of the exiled creators, with
the ark preserving genetic samples rather than breeding pairs. Babel is a human
technological reach alarming enough to be broken up. Sodom is a targeted strike on
a rebel centre, and the testing of Abraham the vetting of the lineage rebuilt
after it. Across all of it the framework reads one
continuous history: the Elohim's cultivation, correction, and occasional
punishment of the humanity they had made, recorded by that humanity in the only
vocabulary it had. The present age — the
[Age of Aquarius](../timeline/age-of-aquarius/) — is when the record can finally
be read plainly, which is the literal sense the framework gives to *apocalypse*:
not catastrophe but unveiling.

## Context and criticism

*The Book Which Tells the Truth* is the founding text of Raëlism and an anchor of
the Wheel of Heaven corpus, and it belongs to a wider twentieth-century lineage.
Sendy had already argued for reading Genesis as a record of visitors from the
sky; Zecharia Sitchin built a parallel case from Mesopotamian sources, Erich von
Däniken popularised the ancient-astronaut theme, and later authors such as Mauro
Biglino and Paul Anthony Wallis pressed the philological argument further. The
framework shares the interventionist premise with these writers but grounds
itself in its own contact claim and differs from each on the specifics. Whatever
one makes of that claim, the book's influence is a matter of record: it launched a
movement that grew into an international organisation and has kept this reading of
Genesis in circulation for half a century.

Mainstream scholarship reads the same texts very differently, and the framework's
reading sits alongside that work rather than displacing it. Biblical scholars
explain Genesis through source criticism and its dependence on older Near Eastern
literature — the Mesopotamian creation and flood traditions in particular — and
account for its doublets and seams as marks of composition rather than of
contact. Historical linguists do not accept the etymological method as it is used
here: several of its key glosses, including the reading of *Elohim* as "those who
came from the sky," are folk-etymological rather than standard derivations, and
the plural form has a well-established grammatical explanation. And the contact
account rests finally on one person's testimony, which is unverifiable in
principle; skeptics treat it as such. The framework's answer is not to dispute the
scholarship but to read the same seams and parallels as the signature of one real
event remembered through many channels — a reading it presents as interpretation,
offered for the reader to weigh, not as established fact.
