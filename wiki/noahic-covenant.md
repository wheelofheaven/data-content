**The Noahic Covenant** is the post-flood agreement recorded in Genesis
8:20–9:17: the arrangement sealed at the altar [Noah](../noah/) built after
leaving the ark, marked by the rainbow set in the cloud and by the promise that
the earth would never again be destroyed by flood. In the Wheel of Heaven
framework it is read not as a one-sided decree handed down by a distant God but
as a private compact between two parties who had just acted together against
orders — the exiled [Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction of creators, who built and
crewed the ark, and Noah's family, who survived the [Great Flood](../great-flood/)
aboard it. On that reading the covenant is the founding charter of
[the Alliance](../the-alliance/), the durable partnership between the creators
who sided with their creation and the human lineage they preserved, and it
stands deliberately apart from the home-world
[Council of the Eternals](../council-of-eternals/) that had ordered the
destruction of both. The principal articulation is Claude Vorilhon's *The Book
Which Tells the Truth* (1974).

## Etymology and naming

The Hebrew term is בְּרִית (*brit*) — "covenant," "alliance," "binding pact." Its
most cited etymology connects it to a root meaning "to cut," recalling the
ancient Near Eastern practice of *cutting* a covenant: sacrificial animals were
divided and the parties passed between the pieces, invoking that fate on
whoever broke faith — the rite [Abraham](../abraham/) performs in Genesis
15:9–21. A secondary proposal links *brit* to Akkadian *birītu* ("bond,
fetter"), stressing the binding character of the agreement. The Septuagint
renders *brit* with Greek διαθήκη (*diathēkē*, "covenant, testament"), the term
that later underwrites the "Old" and "New Testament" vocabulary; the Vulgate
uses Latin *foedus* and *testamentum*; the Quran speaks of covenant as ميثاق
(*mīthāq*) and عهد (*ʿahd*).

The sign of this particular covenant turns on a second word. Hebrew קֶשֶׁת
(*keshet*) means both "rainbow" and "bow" — the archer's weapon — and that
double sense carries interpretive weight in the framework's reading, treated
below.

## The Genesis 9 covenant

The covenant unfolds across three movements in Genesis 8:20–9:17.

**The altar (Genesis 8:20–22).** Noah builds an altar and offers burnt
offerings, and the text records the response: *"I will not again curse the
ground any more for man's sake ... neither will I again smite any more every
thing living, as I have done."* The seasons are guaranteed to hold — *"seedtime
and harvest, and cold and heat, and summer and winter, and day and night shall
not cease"* — a restoration of ordinary conditions after the cataclysm.

**The blessing and the commands (Genesis 9:1–7).** Noah and his sons receive
the charge of Eden renewed — *"Be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the
earth"* — and dominion over the animals, now with permission to eat meat. That
permission carries its first restriction: *"flesh with the life thereof, which
is the blood thereof, shall ye not eat."* And it carries the covenant's central
moral command, the prohibition on shedding human blood, grounded in humanity's
created likeness: *"Whoso sheddeth man's blood, by man shall his blood be shed:
for in the image of God made he man."*

**The covenant and its sign (Genesis 9:8–17).** The formal covenant is
established with Noah, his descendants, and "every living creature" that left
the ark. Its content is the "never again": *"neither shall all flesh be cut off
any more by the waters of a flood; neither shall there any more be a flood to
destroy the earth."* Its sign is the rainbow — *"I do set my bow in the cloud"*
— given "for perpetual generations."

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

**A private arrangement, not a decree.** The framework reads the altar scene as
the formalizing of a partnership between two parties who had just defied the
Council together. The creators who preserved Noah were the exiled Lucifer
faction — the dissenting minority who had carried out the [Eden](../eden/)
disclosure and been exiled for it, the same faction the serpent of Genesis 3
stands for. They are not to be confused with [Satan](../satan/), who led the
home-world abolitionists arguing that the human experiment should be ended; the
flood was the abolitionist program carried out, and the ark was mounted against
it. Noah's family were the operational partners in that rescue, and the
covenant records their joint position now that the waters had gone down.

The home-world Council is conspicuously absent from the scene. The text reports
no consultation with it, and its later reaction to [Babel](../babel/) betrays
that it was never a party: *"The people on our planet became frightened when
they heard about this. They were still observing the Earth and knew that life
had not been destroyed."* A party to the covenant would have known at the time;
the Council learns by observation, centuries on. The covenant, in short, is
local — struck on the ground between the exiled creators and the survivors, not
ratified from the home world.

**The founding of the Alliance.** This is the moment [the Alliance](../the-alliance/)
comes into being: a cross-civilizational partnership with the exiled creators as
senior partner and Noah's line as junior partner. Its terms are mutual. The
creators commit to take no part in any future destruction of humanity — a
commitment that means something precisely because they have just refused to do
so — and they acknowledge the legitimacy of human scientific progress,
reversing the very grievance the Council had used to justify the flood. The
humans commit to gratitude, to rebuilding, and to ritual recognition, which is
what the burnt offerings of Genesis 8:20 enact. Everything that follows in the
post-flood biblical narrative happens inside the political framework this
covenant establishes.

**Yahweh's post-flood vote and the "never again."** The exiled faction on the
ground could bind only itself; a guarantee that the earth would not be struck
again required a decision at the level that had ordered the strike. Here the
framework reads [Yahweh](../yahweh/) — president of the Council of the Eternals
— as the figure who secured that outcome through the Council's own politics. The
biblical "I will not again ... smite any more every thing living" is read not as
a change of divine temperament but as the record of a decision taken among the
Eternals: after the flood, humanity was argued for and allowed to continue. The
"never again" rests on that vote rather than on a softened mood, and it is the
institutional counterpart to the private compact sealed at the altar. The
promise is, notably, specific — that *a flood to destroy the earth* will not
recur — a pledge against repeating this particular kind of strike rather than a
blanket guarantee of preservation, a precision the framework reads as consistent
with a negotiated political undertaking.

**The image and the blood.** The command of Genesis 9:6 grounds the prohibition
on bloodshed in the *imago Dei* — humanity made "in the image of God." The
framework reads this likeness literally: humans were fashioned in the creators'
own image in the [Eden](../eden/) laboratories, and it is that kinship, not an
abstract sanctity, that gives the blood-prohibition its force. The dietary rule
that pairs with it — meat permitted, blood withheld — becomes, on this reading,
the practical charter of the post-flood order rather than an arbitrary taboo:
the first legislation of a community that has just been re-founded and now has
to be told how to live.

**The rainbow as a laid-down weapon.** The double meaning of *keshet* — rainbow
and war-bow — carries the covenant's central gesture. The bow that had been
turned against the earth is now hung in the cloud, set aside, no longer to be
drawn: the visible rainbow is the token of a weapon retired. That the sign
appears in the shared sky, visible to creators and humans alike, fits its
function as the emblem of a bilateral covenant rather than a message aimed at
one party. A complementary technical reading is also available — that the
post-flood atmosphere, cleared and reset to a new equilibrium, simply began to
throw the rainbow pattern as it now does, so that the renewed sky itself is the
sign that conditions had stabilized. Both are offered as readings of the text
rather than as claims the text states outright.

**A three-part political world.** The covenant leaves three actors on the board
rather than two: the home-world Council; the exiled-creator-and-human Alliance
bound by the covenant on Earth; and the broader human population of the other
six post-flood lineages, who are not parties to it. The distinction matters for
everything that follows. Many acts the Hebrew Bible ascribes to "Yahweh" or to
"Elohim" in later chapters are, on this reading, the Alliance acting on its
covenant commitments — protecting, teaching, intervening for its human partners;
others are the Council acting against the Alliance or its work. The text uses
one vocabulary for both because its authors did not have the political
distinction the framework is drawing.

## The covenant sequence

The framework reads the Noahic Covenant as the first of a line, each later
covenant a re-formation of the same Alliance for a new age:

- **The Abrahamic Covenant** (Genesis 15, 17) narrows the arrangement to a
  single lineage, with the promises of land and innumerable descendants and the
  sign of circumcision, treated in the [Abrahamic Covenant](../abrahamic-covenant/)
  entry.
- **The Mosaic Covenant** (Exodus 19–24) reformulates it for the Israelite
  people at Sinai, with the Decalogue and the covenant code as its detailed
  terms.
- **The Davidic Covenant** (2 Samuel 7) extends it to a royal dynasty and the
  messianic line.
- **The New Covenant** (Jeremiah 31:31–34, and its Christian reception) recasts
  it for the Piscean age — "written on the heart" rather than on stone.
- **The Aquarian-age covenant** anticipated in the corpus is the next
  re-formation, the one the open return at the embassy is expected to inaugurate.

Each of these specifies a particular party or a new scope while operating
within the framework the Noahic Covenant first established. The pattern the
framework reads is a repeated narrowing to a chosen lineage followed by a
re-widening toward all humanity — the shape of a single partnership carried
forward across the ages rather than a series of unrelated pacts.

## The Noahide laws in Jewish tradition

Later Jewish tradition read out of the covenant a universal ethic. The rabbinic
**Seven Laws of Noah** (שבע מצוות בני נח, *sheva mitzvot bnei Noaḥ*), set out
principally at Talmud *Sanhedrin* 56a–60a, are understood as binding on all
humanity rather than on Israel alone: prohibitions of idolatry, blasphemy,
murder, sexual immorality, theft, and eating flesh torn from a living animal,
together with the positive duty to establish courts of justice. Two of the
seven read directly off Genesis 9 — the prohibition on the "limb from the
living" (Genesis 9:4) and the prohibition on bloodshed (Genesis 9:6).
Maimonides systematized the framework in the *Mishneh Torah* (*Hilchot
Melachim* 8–10), where the *ger toshav*, the "resident alien" who keeps the
Noahide laws, holds a recognized status; in the modern period the
Chabad-Lubavitch movement has made Noahide observance the subject of an active
outreach to non-Jews.

The framework treats this rabbinic construction as a reasonable systematization
of the covenant's ethical content rather than as part of the Genesis text
itself — a later reading, in its own tradition's voice, of what the post-flood
settlement required. It notes in passing that the corpus's own Aquarian-age
ethical program, the [New Commandments](../new-commandments/), sits in the same
line of covenant articulations, without collapsing the two into one.

## Scholarly and critical perspectives

The covenant of Genesis 9 carries a large scholarly literature, and the
framework's reading is offered alongside it rather than in its place.

- **Source criticism.** Mainstream historical-critical scholarship assigns
  Genesis 9 largely to the Priestly source (P), composed in or after the
  Babylonian exile, and reads the Noahic covenant as a deliberately universal
  covenant — with all flesh, sealed by a cosmic sign — set before the
  particular covenants with Abraham and Israel (Westermann; Sarna). The
  framework does not dispute that Genesis is a composite text; it reads the
  seams as evidence of a memory carried through more than one channel.
- **Ancient Near Eastern treaty form.** Since Mendenhall and Weinfeld, scholars
  have shown that the biblical covenants share the structure of second-millennium
  Hittite suzerain-vassal treaties — preamble, historical prologue, stipulations,
  witnesses, blessings and curses. Where the mainstream reads a literary
  borrowing of a diplomatic form, the framework reads the form as fitting
  because the underlying reality was itself a political agreement between
  distinct parties.
- **Christian covenant theology.** The Reformed federal tradition (Cocceius;
  the Westminster divines) and later dispensationalism both make the biblical
  covenants the scaffolding of salvation history, the Noahic covenant marking
  God's universal forbearance toward all creation. The framework reads the same
  material as political rather than salvation-historical, and registers the
  theological systems as developments of the text rather than as reconstructions
  of the event behind it.
- **Islamic engagement.** The Quran treats covenant (*mīthāq*, *ʿahd*) as a
  recurring bond between God and the prophets, Nūḥ (Noah) among the foremost;
  the post-flood assurance to a righteous remnant is embedded in that broader
  prophetic-covenant frame rather than isolated as a distinct pact.
