**Geniocracy** (from Latin *genius*, "innate ability, natural talent," and
Greek *kratos*, "rule, power") is the Raëlian doctrine of government — a form of
democracy that weights political participation by measured intelligence. It is
the first of the seven [New Commandments](../new-commandments/) that
[Yahweh](../yahweh/) delivered to [Raël](../rael/) (Claude Vorilhon) during the
December 1973 contact and that Vorilhon recorded in
[*The Book Which Tells the Truth*](../../library/the-book-which-tells-the-truth/)
(1974). The doctrine proposes what its source calls a "selective democracy": the
right to vote reserved for those whose measured intelligence is at least ten
percent above the population average, and eligibility for public office reserved
for those at least fifty percent above it. Vorilhon set the doctrine out at
length in a dedicated 1977 book, *Géniocratie* (English: *Geniocracy: Government
of the People, for the People, by the Geniuses*), and founded the **Movement for
Worldwide Geniocracy** the same year.

## The doctrine

The name pairs two roots. *Genio-* comes from the Latin *genius* — attendant
spirit, then innate ability or exceptional talent — and *-cracy* from the Greek
*kratos*, "rule," the suffix that also yields *democracy*, *aristocracy*, and
*technocracy*. The choice of *genius* rather than *meritum* (which would give
*meritocracy*) or *techne* (which would give *technocracy*) is deliberate: the
doctrine attaches authority to raw intellectual capacity rather than to
demonstrated achievement or specialised technical skill.

The argument begins as a critique of universal-suffrage democracy. In its
present form, the source holds, democracy weighs every vote equally regardless
of the voter's capacity to understand what is at stake — *"the voice of someone
whom you might vulgarly call 'a cretin' is worth as much as that of a genius, who
has thought maturely about the way he or she is going to vote."* Across history,
Vorilhon argues, power has been held first by the physically strong, then by the
wealthy, then by politicians and military men — never by the people who actually
move civilisation forward:

> *"Whether they discovered the wheel, gunpowder, the internal combustion engine
> or the atom, the geniuses have always allowed less intelligent people in power
> to benefit from their inventions. Often such people have used peaceful
> inventions for murderous ends. All that must be changed."*

The organising image is the body. Just as the cell in the foot does not vote on
what the hand should pick up — that is the brain's task, and the foot benefits
from the brain's good decisions — political judgement should belong to those
equipped to exercise it rather than be spread evenly across the whole organism:

> *"Each person is a useful cell in this huge body we call humanity. The cell in
> your foot should not decide whether or not your hand should pick up a given
> object. It is the brain, which must decide, and if the object in question is
> good, the cell of your foot will benefit from it. It is not up to the foot to
> vote."*

The historical example is Copernicus, condemned by a Church majority that lacked
the knowledge to judge him: *"Copernicus was condemned by a majority of
incompetent people because he was the only one at that time who had a
sufficiently high level of comprehension."* Where a decision turns on
specialised knowledge, the source argues, weighting every opinion equally
produces reliably worse outcomes than expertise-weighted judgement — had the
public been polled on whether the automobile should be permitted, *"you would
still be riding in a horse and cart."*

The proposed mechanism is psychometric. Intelligence tests, applied from infancy
and recorded on an identity or voter's card, would establish each person's
intellectual coefficient and set eligibility against the two thresholds:

> *"Only those with an intellectual capacity of at least fifty per cent above the
> average should be eligible for a public post. To vote, individuals would need
> an intellectual coefficient of at least ten per cent above average."*

Vorilhon insists this is *"a totally democratic system"* — contested elections,
free political organisation, and peaceful transitions all preserved — and
stresses that it rewards intelligence rather than credentials. There are
engineers of below-average intelligence who accumulated degrees on the strength
of memory, he notes, and labourers and farm workers of no formal education whose
intelligence runs fifty percent above the average. The aim, in the source's own
naming, is *"placing the genius in power, and you may call that 'Geniocracy'."*

Because the doctrine is framed as a *selective* democracy rather than a break
with democracy, it is defined as much by what it is not. It is not monarchy or
aristocracy, whose authority is hereditary; not plutocracy, whose criterion is
wealth — the source explicitly casts the rule of "the rich, who used their money
to employ many 'brutes'" as an earlier pattern to be left behind; and not
theocracy, whose eligibility rests on religious standing. It parts from
technocracy, too, which would empower narrow technical specialists — engineers,
economists — whereas Geniocracy weights general intellectual capacity; and from
meritocracy, which rewards demonstrated achievement and credentials, whereas the
doctrine's test is measured intelligence, credentialed or not. The distinction
matters to the source, since credentials tend to track privilege while raw
intelligence, on its account, does not.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

Yahweh delivered Geniocracy as the first of seven
[New Commandments](../new-commandments/) — the Aquarian-age program the
[Elohim](../elohim/) proposed for humanity's long-term development, alongside
[Humanitarianism](../humanitarianism/) (property and inheritance),
[World Government](../world-government/),
[Demilitarization](../demilitarization/),
[Science as Religion](../science-as-religion/),
[Telepathic Cultivation](../telepathy/), and the
[Metaphysical Clarifications](../metaphysical-clarifications/). Within that
program Geniocracy is foundational: the other reforms presuppose leaders able to
grasp their long-range logic. The source makes the dependency explicit in the
Humanitarianism section — *"If geniuses are admitted to power, they will
understand the usefulness of these reforms."*

On the canon's account the doctrine is not a human invention but the standing
practice of the Elohim's own world. The preface to *Géniocratie* states it
plainly: *"These challenging concepts already apply on the planet of the Elohim.
Unless we can come up with something better, they advise us to begin preparing to
implement a similar system, since all human progress is ultimately dependent on
the work of geniuses."* Earth's adoption of Geniocracy is read, then, as the
taking-up of a cosmic-civilisational standard — offered rather than imposed, with
humanity left free to devise something better if it can.

Vorilhon carried the doctrine into a body distinct from the Raëlian Movement
itself. The **Movement for Worldwide Geniocracy** (1977) pursues Geniocracy as a
political project, while the Raëlian Movement remains a religious-cultural
institution focused on diffusing the message and building the
[Embassy](../embassy/). The two are deliberately separable: *"a person can be a
Raelian and a 'Geniocrat', just as one can be a Democrat and a Christian; one can
have a religion and a political opinion. Raelians do not have to get involved
with the Geniocratic party."*

The corpus reads Geniocracy as a direction of travel rather than a program for
tomorrow. It is what human government is to evolve toward across the
[Age of Aquarius](../timeline/age-of-aquarius/) — as intelligence measurement
grows more reliable and the cultural ground is prepared — not a system to be
imposed on present democracies. Its full realisation would depend on validated
measurement, on coordinated [World Government](../world-government/)
implementation, and on a long shift in how societies think about authority and
participation.

## Comparative traditions

Geniocracy joins a long line of proposals that would tie political authority to
knowledge or intellect, and it is best read alongside them rather than in
isolation.

The nearest ancestor is Plato's philosopher-king. The *Republic* (c. 380 BCE)
argues that political decisions, like medicine or navigation, are a kind of
expertise, and that a specially cultivated guardian class should rule — an
argument whose just-soul / just-city analogy runs parallel to Vorilhon's body
metaphor. The differences are real: Plato's guardians are formed by philosophical
education rather than measured by test, and the *Republic* is frankly
non-democratic where Geniocracy keeps elections. The Confucian tradition,
operationalised in China's imperial examination system (*keju*, c. 605–1905 CE),
offers the largest historical experiment in merit-selected government, though it
tested classical learning and moral cultivation rather than general intelligence.

Closer to the present, the twentieth-century technocracy movement (Howard
Scott's Technocracy Inc., founded 1933) would have handed governance to engineers
and scientists, and Bertrand Russell weighed — and largely resisted — the idea of
a "scientific oligarchy." The contemporary academic debate over **epistocracy**,
rule by the knowledgeable, is the closest living parallel: Jason Brennan's
*Against Democracy* (2016) argues that widespread voter ignorance makes universal
suffrage produce poor outcomes, while David Estlund's *Democratic Authority*
(2008) defends democratic legitimacy against exactly such proposals. Earlier
still, John Stuart Mill proposed plural voting in *Considerations on
Representative Government* (1861), giving the better-educated additional votes
while keeping universal suffrage — a gentler cousin of Geniocracy's hard
threshold. These convergences are probably best read as independent responses to
a shared problem; Geniocracy differs from all of them in resting its case on the
Raëlian source and in specifying exact psychometric cut-offs.

## Critical perspectives

The doctrine is, by any mainstream measure, deeply controversial, and its
objections deserve to be stated fairly.

The first is measurement. Geniocracy assumes that "intelligence" can be measured
reliably enough to gate political rights. Whether any single number captures
intelligence at all is contested: Charles Spearman's *g* factor gave the
psychometric tradition its foundation, but Stephen Jay Gould's *The Mismeasure of
Man* (1981), together with the multiple-intelligences and triarchic models of
Howard Gardner and Robert Sternberg, questions whether intelligence is unitary or
measurable in the way the doctrine requires. The Flynn effect — the sustained
twentieth-century rise in test scores — further unsettles any fixed threshold.

The second objection is historical. Proposals to attach political standing to
measured intelligence sit uncomfortably close to the eugenics movements of the
late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries — Francis Galton's hereditarian
program and its later, darker applications — and critics reasonably ask whether
an intelligence franchise could avoid reproducing those associations, or the
class and racial biases documented in the testing itself.

The third is democratic principle. Mainstream democratic theory treats equal
political liberty as basic rather than as something to be earned: Jürgen Habermas
grounds legitimacy in the equal participation of all citizens in public
deliberation, and both John Rawls and Michael Walzer reject distributing
political authority by merit. On these views a "selective democracy" is close to
a contradiction in terms.

The fourth is practical. Even granting a valid measure, critics doubt that
intelligence testing could be administered honestly in real political conditions
without capture, corruption, or manipulation by whoever controls the tests — the
perennial question of who guards the guardians. The corpus itself concedes the
force of these difficulties by casting Geniocracy as a distant developmental
target rather than an immediate reform.
