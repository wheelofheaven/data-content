**Crop circles**, or **crop formations**, are large patterns made by flattening
standing crops — most often wheat, barley, rapeseed, rye, maize, linseed, or
soy — into geometric, mathematical, or figurative designs. The modern
phenomenon appeared in southern England in the late 1970s and concentrated
heavily in Wiltshire, in the belt of chalk downland around the prehistoric
monuments of Avebury, Stonehenge, Silbury Hill, and Alton Barnes. Formations
were later reported across Europe, North America, Australia, and Japan.

The turning point came on **9 September 1991**, when two Englishmen,
**Doug Bower** (1924–2020) and **Dave Chorley** (1922–1996), confessed in the
*Today* newspaper that they had made the early formations by hand from 1978
onward.{{ cite(id="round-in-circles-schnabel", text="[1]", title="Schnabel, *Round in Circles* (1993) — journalistic history of the field across the Bower-Chorley confession") }}
They demonstrated their method — a plank swung on a rope to press the crop
flat, with alignment sighted through a hole in a baseball cap — and built a
formation for journalists in about 45 minutes; a crop-circle researcher who
examined it, not knowing its origin, pronounced it genuine.{{ footnote(id="1") }}

The mainstream position has been clear ever since, and it is well established:
crop circles are human-made. Most are the work of a small community of artists
who treat the field as a canvas, and the practice is now openly documented as a
form of land art. The Wheel of Heaven framework accepts that position and holds
it steadily. The overwhelming majority of formations are demonstrably made by
people; a small number of reported physical anomalies remain unresolved but
unproven; the alleged extraterrestrial "messages" in a few celebrated
formations are unverified; and the canon's own point of contact with the
subject is a single case, read with care rather than enthusiasm.

## History and the hoax evidence

Rings of flattened crop had been noticed in English fields for years before
anyone thought them remarkable. The modern phenomenon takes its shape from the
late 1970s, when formations began appearing around Cheesefoot Head in Hampshire
and, soon after, across the downland of Wiltshire. Reports climbed through the
1980s — from a handful a year at the start of the decade to several hundred by
its end — and the designs grew with them, from plain circles to elaborate
pictograms with rings, spurs, and satellite features. The first book-length
treatment, Pat Delgado and Colin Andrews's *Circular Evidence*
(1989),{{ cite(id="circular-evidence", text="[2]", title="Delgado & Andrews, *Circular Evidence* (1989) — the principal crop-circle study before the confession") }}
took the rising tally as an open mystery. A subculture grew up around it — the
"cerealogists," after Ceres, the Roman goddess of grain — complete with field
researchers, newsletters, dowsers, and the physicist Terence Meaden's
meteorological hypothesis (below). A notably complex pictogram near Barbury
Castle in July 1991, a triangle set with rings, marked the high point of that
pre-confession phase.

The framing collapsed weeks later. Bower and Chorley told the *Today* newspaper
that the whole wave had begun with them: two friends who, over pints in the
mid-1970s, decided to fake a flying-saucer landing near Cheesefoot Head and
kept going for fifteen years, making perhaps two hundred formations. Their
tools were plain — a four-foot plank, a loop of rope, and a cap with a sighting
wire — and their demonstration was decisive, since the circle they built for
the press was authenticated as genuine by a leading researcher before the trick
was revealed.{{ footnote(id="1") }}

The confession did not end circle-making; it professionalized it. Through the
1990s an organized art scene came into the open, led by **Circlemakers** — John
Lundberg, Rod Dickinson, Wil Russell, and Rob Irving — who had emerged from an
earlier collective provocatively named **Team Satan** (a self-chosen joke,
unrelated to the canon's [Satan](../satan/)). Circlemakers set out their craft
in Rob Irving and John Lundberg's *The Field Guide: The Art, History and
Philosophy of Crop Circle Making*
(2006),{{ cite(id="the-field-guide-crop-circle-making", text="[3]", title="Irving & Lundberg, *The Field Guide: The Art, History and Philosophy of Crop Circle Making* (2006)") }}
made commissioned formations for advertisers (Microsoft, Mitsubishi, Pepsi,
Nike, Greenpeace, the BBC) and for films including M. Night Shyamalan's *Signs*
(2002), and demonstrated their methods for journalists and documentary crews.
The formations grew accordingly: the 151-circle **Julia Set** that appeared
beside Stonehenge in July 1996, its rapid midday appearance the subject of a
lasting timing dispute; the 409-circle **Milk Hill** spiral of August 2001,
some 240 metres across and the largest ever recorded; and later
three-dimensional and mathematically encoded
designs.{{ cite(id="pringle-crop-circles", text="[4]", title="Pringle, *Crop Circles: The Greatest Mystery of Modern Times* (1999)") }}
That trajectory — rising complexity tracked step for step by a documented and
improving craft — is the core of the hoax evidence. The thing that was once
supposed to be beyond human skill turned out to be exactly what human artists
were making.

The geography reinforces the point. The concentration in Wiltshire coincides
with one of Europe's densest prehistoric ritual landscapes — Stonehenge, the
Avebury henge, Silbury Hill, the West Kennet Long
Barrow{{ cite(id="prehistoric-avebury", text="[5]", title="Burl, *Prehistoric Avebury* (1979; revised 2002)") }}{{ cite(id="avebury-biography-of-a-landscape", text="[6]", title="Pollard & Reynolds, *Avebury: The Biography of a Landscape* (2002)") }} —
and with a long-standing alternative-spiritual community drawn to it. On the
mainstream reading, the formations cluster there because the circle-makers do,
their choice of setting itself shaped by the landscape's cultural pull rather
than by anything in the soil.

## The anomalist and framework reading

A minority of researchers hold that human construction, real and dominant
though it is, does not account for everything. The most serious of these is
the **residual-anomaly program**: the **BLT Research Team** (founded in 1989 by
John Burke, William Levengood, and Nancy Talbott) and the Dutch physicist
**Eltjo Haselhoff**, who argue that crops in some formations show physical
changes hard to explain by flattening alone — stem nodes elongated as if by
brief, intense heat, iron-rich "magnetite" spherules in the soil, and altered
seed growth. Levengood published the claim in *Physiologia Plantarum* in
1994,{{ cite(id="levengood-physiologia-plantarum-1994", text="[7]", title="Levengood, 'Anatomical Anomalies in Crop Formation Plants' (Physiologia Plantarum, 1994)") }}
Haselhoff set it out for a general reader in *The Deepening Complexity of Crop
Circles*
(2001){{ cite(id="the-deepening-complexity-crop-circles", text="[8]", title="Haselhoff, *The Deepening Complexity of Crop Circles* (2001)") }}
and extended it in the same journal that
year.{{ cite(id="haselhoff-physiologia-plantarum-2001", text="[9]", title="Haselhoff, Physiologia Plantarum (2001) — peer-reviewed extension of the node-bending analysis") }}
The corpus reports this work but does not lean on it: most of the program's
output sits outside peer review, and its methods — how formations are selected,
how samples are controlled — are seriously contested. It raises questions it
has not answered; it has not shown that any formation is beyond human
making.{{ footnote(id="4") }}

Beyond the anomaly research lies the **alternative-ufological** reading, which
treats certain formations as deliberate messages. Its two set pieces both
appeared next to the Chilbolton radio telescope and at Crabwood, in Hampshire.
The **Chilbolton "Arecibo response"** (August 2001) reproduced, with changes,
the binary message Frank Drake had beamed from Arecibo toward the star cluster
M13 in 1974 — the altered version adding silicon to the listed elements and
marking three inhabited planets rather than one. The **Crabwood "alien face"**
(August 2002) paired a portrait with a disc encoding an ASCII text, decoded by
Paul Vigay as a warning: *"Beware the bearers of FALSE gifts & their BROKEN
PROMISES... There is GOOD out there. We Oppose DECEPTION. Conduit
CLOSING."*{{ cite(id="glimpses-of-other-realities", text="[10]", title="Howe, *Glimpses of Other Realities* (1993-1998) — alternative-ufological engagement with crop-circle cases") }}
These are striking objects, and no circle-making group has claimed them; but
neither has been verified as non-human by any method mainstream science
accepts, and both belong to the same tradition of unattributed formations that
skilled makers have shown themselves able to
produce.{{ cite(id="crop-circles-signs-of-contact", text="[11]", title="Andrews & Spignesi, *Crop Circles: Signs of Contact* (2003)") }}{{ footnote(id="5") }}

The Wheel of Heaven framework parts company here with the popular "crop circles
are alien signals" claim, and does so deliberately. On the canon, real contact
is not cryptic. The [Elohim](../elohim/) are an advanced human civilization
whose model of contact is open, mutual recognition — the [Embassy](../embassy/)
of *[Message from the Designers](../message-from-the-designers/)* — and whose
disclosure was long the argument of the exiled [Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction,
the [Serpent](../serpent/), who held that humanity should be told its origins
plainly. It is worth being exact about the canon here, because popular tradition
collapses the distinction: the disclosure faction is Lucifer's, and it should
not be confused with the abolitionist faction under [Satan](../satan/), which
argued not for secrecy but for ending the human project altogether. A
civilization intent on being recognized would not leave its identity to be
read out of flattened barley by hobbyists.

The canon's one direct engagement with the subject is correspondingly modest.
The foreword to *Message from the Designers* records that "not long after we
built a small scale model of the embassy... a crop circle appeared in England
which bore an astonishing resemblance to
it,"{{ cite(id="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", text="[12]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *Message from the Designers* foreword — the embassy-resemblance crop circle") }}
and the book's acknowledgments thank Colin Andrews for a 1990 aerial photograph
taken at Cheesefoot Head. The corpus treats that single resemblance as
meaningful within its own frame without generalizing from it. Alliance
communication, on this reading, runs through the contact narrative — the
13 December 1973 encounter at Puy-de-Lassolas and the 7 October 1975 transport
to the [home world](../elohim-home-planet/) — and through prophetic
transmission, not through formations in fields. Raëlian interest in crop
circles since the 1990s is cultural interest, not doctrine.{{ footnote(id="6") }}
This whole reading is offered as interpretation, and labelled as such.

## The mainstream and skeptical view

The mainstream account is straightforward and carries most of the weight of the
evidence. Crop circles are made by people. The construction methods are
documented, taught, and demonstrated on camera; the concentration in Wiltshire
tracks the concentration of circle-makers rather than anything in the ground;
and the steady rise in size and complexity follows the improving skill of a
known community of artists. Where the phenomenon was once "unexplained," it is
now, in the mainstream view, explained — a living folk-art with a thirty-year
documented history. Investigators such as Joe Nickell have argued the skeptical
case in detail, pressing the anomaly research on its sampling and controls and
finding no formation that requires a non-human
maker.{{ cite(id="real-life-x-files-nickell", text="[13]", title="Nickell, *Real-Life X-Files* (2001) — systematic skeptical investigation, crop circles included") }}{{ cite(id="nickell-levengood-skeptical-inquirer-1996", text="[14]", title="Nickell, 'Levengood's Crop-Circle Plant Research' (Skeptical Inquirer, 1996)") }}

Two once-serious alternatives have fallen away. Terence Meaden's
**plasma-vortex hypothesis** — that spinning columns of ionized air could
flatten crops into rings — was published in peer-reviewed meteorology and taken
seriously in the
1980s,{{ cite(id="the-circles-effect-meaden", text="[15]", title="Meaden, *The Circles Effect and Its Mysteries* (1989) — the plasma-vortex hypothesis") }}
but it could not survive the formations' growing geometric complexity or the
Bower-Chorley confession, and Meaden himself came to accept that most are
human-made.{{ footnote(id="2") }} The recurring appeal to a **1678 pamphlet**,
"The Mowing-Devil," as a pre-modern crop circle is weaker still: the pamphlet
describes a devil *mowing* — cutting — a field overnight, not flattening it,
and belongs to a 17th-century broadsheet tradition of moral-allegorical prodigy
tales rather than to any record of real
circles.{{ cite(id="providence-in-early-modern-england", text="[16]", title="Walsham, *Providence in Early Modern England* (1999)") }}{{ cite(id="miracles-and-the-pulp-press", text="[17]", title="Friedman, *Miracles and the Pulp Press during the English Revolution* (1993)") }}{{ footnote(id="3") }}
The 1966 **Tully "saucer nests"** in Queensland, sometimes cited as an
antecedent, are a UFO-landing ground-trace case — a separate category treated
under [Ufology](../ufology/) — rather than a crop formation.

The honest position, and the corpus's, is the one the record supports: crop
circles are overwhelmingly human-made land art; a small residue of physical
claims remains genuinely unresolved but unproven; the "messages" are
unverified; and the canon's engagement is limited to one resonant case, held
lightly.
