**Wheel of Heaven** names two things that belong together but should not be
confused. The first is a celestial image: the slow turning of the whole sky
produced by the [precession of the equinoxes](../precession/) — the roughly
25,920-year cycle across which the spring-equinox point drifts backward through
the twelve constellations of the zodiac at about one constellation every 2,160
years. That is the wheel of heaven in the literal sense: the heavens wheeling
overhead, dividing deep time into the twelve [World Ages](../world-age/). The
second is the name of this project, which takes that image as its central
metaphor and its organizing spine.

Holding the two apart matters. The wheel itself is ordinary astronomy — a real,
measurable motion of Earth's axis, recognized since Hipparchus. The project is
an interpretive framework, and the corpus of writing that states it: a
comparative reading of revelation, cosmic chronology, ancient memory, and sacred
history, built around a single working hypothesis about who first set that wheel
turning as a calendar. This entry takes the celestial wheel first, then the
framework that borrows its name, then how both stand against mainstream
scholarship.

## The turning heavens

Precession is the third of Earth's principal motions, after the daily rotation
that makes the day and the yearly orbit that makes the year. Earth's axis is
tilted about 23.4° from the plane of its orbit and is not fixed in space:
gravitational torque from the Sun and Moon on the planet's equatorial bulge
makes the axis sweep a slow cone, like a spinning top that has begun to wobble,
once every roughly 25,772 years on the modern measurement. The framework rounds
this to 25,920 years, which divides cleanly into twelve ages of 2,160 years
each.{{ footnote(id="1") }} As the axis wheels around, the point where the Sun
stands at the spring equinox slides westward through the zodiac at about one
degree every seventy-two years, and the constellation rising behind that equinox
Sun — the signature of the epoch — changes roughly every 2,160 years. One
visible token of the turning is the pole star itself: the axis pointed near
Thuban in Draco around 2,800 BCE, points near Polaris today, and will point near
Vega around 13,700 CE before the wheel carries it back again. The full astronomy
is set out in the [Precession](../precession/), [Great Year](../great-year/),
and [Zodiac](../zodiac/) entries.

The picture of the heavens as a turning wheel, or a great mill grinding, is old
and widely scattered. Giorgio de Santillana and Hertha von Dechend's *Hamlet's
Mill* (1969){{ cite(id="hamlets-mill", text="[1]", title="de Santillana & von Dechend, *Hamlet's Mill: An Essay on Myth and the Frame of Time* (1969) — the precession-in-myth thesis") }}
assembled a wide body of cross-cultural myth — the Norse and Finnish cosmic
mills (the *Grotti*, the Finnish *Sampo*), the world-tree and world-pillar that
"break" at cosmic turning points, the recurring sequences of declining ages —
and argued that these preserve, in symbolic form, a memory of precession
measured in deep antiquity. The thesis is contested (see below), but it gives
the framework its governing figure: the sky as a mill or wheel whose slow
grinding marks out the ages.

The project's name carries a second, textual echo as well. In Ezekiel's vision
the prophet describes "a wheel in the middle of a wheel" — אוֹפַן בְּתוֹךְ
הָאוֹפָן, *ofan betokh ha-ofan* ({% libref(book="ezekiel-woh", chapter=1, verse=16) %}Ezekiel 1:15–16{% end %}).{{ cite(id="ezekiel", text="[2]", title="Ezekiel 1:15–16 — the wheel-within-a-wheel vision") }}
That phrase has a long interpretive history in Jewish and Christian tradition,
most of it reading the *ofanim* as an order of heavenly beings rather than as
machinery.{{ footnote(id="2") }} The framework reads the passage more literally,
as one of several biblical descriptions of an Elohim craft rendered in the only
vocabulary its witness had. This echo is deliberately secondary: the primary
wheel of heaven is the precessional cycle, not the wheels of the *merkavah*.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

On the framework's reading — and here a canon claim can be stated plainly — the
wheel of heaven is not a mystical inheritance but an instrument. The zodiac is
the calendar the [Elohim](../elohim/), the advanced human civilization the
Hebrew Bible names with that plural word, devised to keep their bearings across
a project that ran for tens of thousands of years, and the precessional ages are
its slow hand. The Elohim arrived on Earth roughly 22,000 years ago and, across
the twelve ages of a single [Great Year](../great-year/), surveyed and prepared
the planet, synthesized life, and at last created humanity in their own
image.{{ cite(id="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", text="[3]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *The Book Which Tells the Truth* (1974) — the foundational contact account") }}
Each age carried a phase of the work: the human synthesis in the
[Age of Leo](../age-of-leo/), [Eden](../eden/) in Cancer, the
[Great Flood](../great-flood/) in Gemini, Babel and the patriarchs in Taurus,
the Hebrew prophets in Aries, the Christian era in Pisces, and the present
recovery in the [Age of Aquarius](../age-of-aquarius/).{{ cite(id="genesis", text="[4]", title="Genesis — read by the corpus as compressed memory of the Elohim project") }}

{{ diagram(name="world-ages-timeline", alt="A horizontal timeline of the twelve contiguous precessional ages from the Age of Capricorn (~21,810 BCE, project opening) to the Age of Aquarius (from 1,950 CE, the current age), with key events marked under Leo, Cancer, Gemini, Taurus, Aries, and Pisces.", caption="The precessional-age framework as a timeline: twelve contiguous ~2,160-year ages carrying the corpus's narrative from the project's opening in the Age of Capricorn to the current Age of Aquarius.") }}

Because the wheel turns far too slowly to be caught within one lifetime, the
framework holds that early cultures did not discover precession on their own but
were taught it. The teachers were the exiled creators who had sided with their
own creation and stayed on Earth — the [Lucifer](../lucifer/) faction, the party
of the Eden [Serpent](../serpent/) — and not the home-world
[Council of the Eternals](../council-of-eternals/), nor
[Satan](../satan/)'s faction, which had argued for humanity's abolition and had
no reason to instruct it. The astronomical knowledge was then set into sacred
iconography — the bull of the Age of Taurus, the ram of Aries, the fish of
Pisces — so that each age left a signature its cultures could carry forward long
after its meaning was lost. The corpus formalizes this as the
[Doubled Signature](../doubled-signature/): each age marked by both its own
constellation and its zodiacal opposite, the redundancy that lets the
astronomical content survive transmission.{{ cite(id="intelligent-design-message-from-the-designers", text="[5]", title="Vorilhon (Raël), *Intelligent Design: Message from the Designers* — the collected canon") }}

That the whole Elohim project fits inside roughly one Great Year — some 25,000
years from the human synthesis to the present transition — is, on this reading,
deliberate rather than coincidental: the wheel of heaven was chosen as the
master clock of the work. The present moment sits at the Pisces–Aquarius cusp,
which the corpus dates to about 1950 CE (with 1946 as the source-anchored
opening), the turn it reads as the age of recovery, when the memory the wheel
preserved becomes legible again. This is a framework claim, explicit in the
corpus and its Raëlian sources but not one that mainstream scholarship makes.

## The project

The Wheel of Heaven project is the framework stated at length — an interpretive
position and the growing corpus that argues it — published at
**www.wheelofheaven.world** through a wiki of several hundred entries, a library
of source texts, a narrated introduction, and a running blog. Its working
hypothesis is the one sketched above: that terrestrial life was the deliberate
scientific work of the Elohim, and that the world's religious and mythological
record preserves a decipherable memory of that work. The hypothesis is advanced
as exactly that — a working hypothesis, a basis for inquiry rather than a
settled result — and the corpus keeps four kinds of statement visibly apart:
what a source directly says, comparison across traditions, the framework's own
synthesis, and questions left open.

The reading rests on a few source families. The Raëlian canon — Claude
Vorilhon's contact accounts, beginning with *The Book Which Tells the Truth*
(1974) and collected as *Message from the Designers* — supplies the specific
content of the hypothesis. Jean Sendy's pre-Raëlian books, above all *Ces dieux
qui firent le ciel et la terre* (1969){{ cite(id="those-gods-who-made-heaven-and-earth", text="[6]", title="Sendy, *Ces dieux qui firent le ciel et la terre* (1969) — Elohim as a physical civilization") }}
and *L'ère du Verseau* (1970),{{ cite(id="the-coming-of-the-gods", text="[7]", title="Sendy, *L'Ère du Verseau* (1970) — the Aquarian-age framing and the precessional reading of the Bible") }}
give the earlier reading of the Elohim as a physical civilization and of the
Hebrew Bible through a precessional-zodiacal lens. *Hamlet's Mill* anchors the
precessional-mythology reading. And a wide body of primary religious,
mythological, and archaeological material — Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Greek,
Vedic, Mesoamerican, and beyond — supplies the comparative evidence the
framework reads as preserved cultural memory.

The project also defines itself by what it is not. It draws on Raëlian material
but is not the [Raëlian Movement](../raelism/) and asks no Raëlian commitment of
its readers. It extends Sendy's "Tradition" rather than simply restating it. It
shares its subject with popular "ancient aliens" media but not its method,
holding to a scholarly register, cited sources, and explicit epistemic labels
where that tradition tends toward spectacle. And it reads mainstream
comparative-religion and biblical scholarship in earnest, parting from it on
interpretation rather than on the textual and historical facts it establishes.

## Comparative and critical perspectives

The framework's strongest claims stand on contested ground, and the corpus keeps
the disagreement in view.

The astronomy itself is not in dispute. Precession is real, its rate is well
measured, and Hipparchus of Nicaea identified it around 129 BCE by comparing his
star positions against older ones — the mainstream date the framework accepts.
What the framework adds, and what mainstream scholarship does not grant, is that
precession was known and tracked well before Hipparchus. Its evidence for that
is *Hamlet's Mill*, whose reception has been mixed: influential in the
archaeoastronomy that grew up in the 1970s and 1980s, but treated skeptically by
most classicists and historians of astronomy, who object that its cross-cultural
parallels are too loose to demand a precessional reading and that the case for a
Neolithic discovery is unproven. The corpus adopts the thesis as interpretive
infrastructure — honestly, as an inferred reading rather than an established
fact — and attributes the pre-Hipparchean knowledge to teaching by the Elohim.

Several neighboring frameworks share the direction while differing on warrant.
Zecharia Sitchin's *The 12th Planet* (1976){{ cite(id="the-12th-planet", text="[8]", title="Sitchin, *The 12th Planet* (1976) — the Anunnaki / Nibiru reading") }}
reads the Mesopotamian Anunnaki as extraterrestrial engineers of humanity; the
Wheel of Heaven framework works principally from Hebrew Bible sources and
differs from Sitchin on interpretation more than on the basic identification.
Paul Anthony Wallis's *Escaping from Eden* (2020){{ cite(id="escaping-from-eden", text="[9]", title="Wallis, *Escaping from Eden* (2020) — a strict-translational reading of the Elohim") }}
and Mauro Biglino's strict-literal Hebrew work reach a similar reading of the
Elohim from a translational rather than a Raëlian starting point. Against all of
these stands ordinary source criticism — the Documentary Hypothesis and its
successors — which the framework does not contest: whether the Pentateuch was
assembled from several documentary sources is a separate question from what the
surviving text remembers, and both can be true at once.{{ footnote(id="5") }}

Two proposed anchors for the name deserve a caution. The connection sometimes
drawn between "wheel of heaven" and Akhenaten's Aten cult has no direct warrant
in the Amarna record and is not one the corpus treats as
established.{{ footnote(id="4") }} And the count of roughly forty prophets sent
across the ages is a figure internal to the Raëlian source, not one derived from
any single tradition's own reckoning.{{ footnote(id="3") }}
