**The Great Return** is the Wheel of Heaven framework's name for the anticipated
official arrival of the [Elohim](../elohim/) on Earth — the event the Raëlian
source material is oriented toward, and the moment the corpus reads its
comparative work as pointing to. In the source material the return is not
unconditional. Humanity must want it; must show enough unification and wisdom to
receive advanced knowledge without misusing it; must build the
[Embassy](../embassy/) — also called the [Third Temple](../third-temple/) — on
neutral ground in a country with a mild climate; and must guarantee that the
Elohim's craft will not be met by military force. When those conditions are met,
the source material promises a transfer of roughly 25,000 years of scientific
knowledge from the Elohim to the humanity they created.

The name itself is late. The foundational conditions were set out in
[*The Book Which Tells the Truth*](../../library/the-book-which-tells-the-truth/)
(1974); the phrase "Great Return" belongs to the message dated 13 December 1997,
in which [Yahweh](../yahweh/) writes that "the hour of our Great Return is near."
The framework treats the return as the culminating event of the present
[Age of Apocalypse](../apocalypse/) — the post-1945 period of recovery and
disclosure — and as the opening of the [Golden Age](../golden-age/) of
[Aquarius](../timeline/age-of-aquarius/) that follows. Its timing is left open:
the source material speaks of imminence without a calendar, and the conditional
structure makes the date depend on humanity's response rather than on a fixed
hour.

## What the source material says

The conditions for the return come from a single passage in *The Book Which
Tells the Truth*, where Yahweh tells Claude Vorilhon what would have to be true
before the Elohim arrive openly:

> If people on Earth want us to give them the benefit of our experience and help
> them gain 25,000 years of scientific knowledge, they have to show us that they
> want to meet us, and above all demonstrate that they deserve it, and that all
> this can be done without any danger to us. [...] We know that our arrival could
> improve many things and unite nations, but we have to feel that people really
> want to see us, and that they are truly starting to unify.
>
> — Yahweh, in Vorilhon, *Le Livre qui dit la vérité* (1974)

Four terms are folded together here: that humanity genuinely wants the arrival
rather than having it imposed; that it is unified and wise enough to receive
advanced knowledge responsibly; that the Elohim's craft is safe from military
forces — the same passage notes that "human warplanes have tried to chase our
crafts, taking us for enemies"; and that it understands who the Elohim are
rather than mistaking them for gods or for adversaries. The book adds the fifth,
physical condition: the Embassy, a prepared residence on neutral extraterritorial
ground where the Elohim can land and stay. The architectural specification
belongs to the [Embassy](../embassy/) entry. What matters for the return is that
the building is its precondition, not its consequence — the Elohim will not
arrive until there is a place ready to receive them.

The knowledge transfer is fixed at a figure: roughly 25,000 years, the gap the
source material places between present terrestrial science and the Elohim's own.
That number is close to one precessional [Great Year](../great-year/) (about
25,772 years on the modern astronomical reckoning) and close to the interval the
source material gives for the Elohim's presence on Earth since the original
synthesis of life. The framework reads that coincidence as calibration — a
project timed to the turning of the sky — rather than as an accident, while
noting that the source states the correspondence only in outline.

The phrase "Great Return" enters with the message of 13 December 1997, the
twenty-fourth anniversary of the first 1973 contact:

> The hour of our Great Return is near, and we will support and protect the most
> devoted ones among you. [...] May your joy in seeing the approach of our Great
> Return give you wings to overcome the last obstacles of the journey.
>
> — Yahweh, "Message of the Elohim, December 13th, 1997"

The 1997 message shifts the tone toward proximate expectation and, in the same
breath, frees the Embassy from its original site. After repeated Israeli refusals
to grant land near Jerusalem, the message authorises the Raëlian Movement to seek
extraterritorial status "from all nations on Earth," with Israel keeping a final
option. A later message of 6 August 2015 declared the Elohim's protection of
Israel withdrawn. The framework records these shifts as part of the source
material's own account and takes no position on their political content.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

On the framework's reading the Great Return is the Elohim's own return to a
creation now grown up enough to receive them. The original arrival, roughly
25,000 years ago, was one-directional: the Elohim came to an unprepared Earth to
synthesise life and shape humanity. The return reverses the relation. It comes to
a humanity that has recovered enough of the makers' capabilities — genetics,
spaceflight, the reading of its own scriptures — to meet them as something nearer
a partner than a subject. The inheritance the source promises, the 25,000 years
of knowledge, is the content of that meeting: not a conquest but a handing-over,
offered to a species now in a position to accept or decline it.

The Embassy is the hinge of the reading. Because the Elohim will not arrive
without a prepared place to receive them, the framework treats the building of
that place as the concrete form the return's preconditions take. The
[Third Temple](../third-temple/) of the Hebrew prophets and the
[New Jerusalem](../new-jerusalem/) that descends in Revelation 21 are read, in
the canon, as two older names for the same structure — the residence the Elohim
asked humanity to build so that they could land openly and stay. The return is
what happens once it stands.

The return also marks a turn in the framework's calendar. The present
[Age of Apocalypse](../apocalypse/) is, on the canon's reading, the period of
recovery and disclosure that opened around 1945 — the stretch in which humanity
reacquires the makers' capabilities and their record becomes legible again. The
Great Return is the event that closes it: the point at which recovery gives way
to open reunion and the [Golden Age](../golden-age/) of Aquarius proper begins.
The framework places the return near the Pisces-to-Aquarius transition for that
reason, with the exact moment left to depend on whether the preconditions are
met.

### The returning figure across traditions

The framework reads the Great Return alongside a pattern that recurs across the
world's religions: the expectation of a figure who returns or arrives to close
one age and open another. It sets these expectations beside the canon while
keeping each in its own terms rather than collapsing them into one.

- **Jewish tradition** awaits the **Mashiach** (Hebrew *māshîaḥ*, "anointed
  one"), the Davidic king whose coming inaugurates the Messianic Age — the
  ingathering of the exiles, the rebuilding of the Temple, and universal peace,
  systematised in Maimonides's *Mishneh Torah*.
- **Christian tradition** awaits the **Parousia** (Greek *parousia*, "arrival,
  presence"), the Second Coming of Christ, drawn from the Olivet Discourse,
  Paul's letters, and the Book of Revelation.
- **Islamic tradition** holds two linked figures: the **Mahdi**, the guided one
  who restores justice at the end of days, and the return of **Isa** (Jesus) to
  defeat the Dajjāl.
- **Buddhist tradition** awaits **Maitreya**, the future Buddha who appears once
  the present teaching has been forgotten and restores it.
- **Hindu tradition** awaits **Kalki**, the tenth and final avatar of Vishnu, who
  ends the Kali Yuga and opens a new age.
- **Zoroastrian tradition**, among the oldest of these, awaits the **Saoshyant**,
  the "bringer of benefit" who inaugurates the *Frashokereti*, the renovation of
  the world.

The framework's claim is that these expectations preserve, in the vocabulary each
tradition had to hand, an awareness of the same anticipated event — the Elohim's
return. It is careful about the strength of that claim. The traditions differ
from one another in ways that matter: a returning Davidic king is not a future
Buddha, and neither is a resurrected saviour. The framework does not flatten those
differences or insist that every return-figure is a direct memory of the same
thing. What it offers, as interpretation rather than as demonstration, is that
the recurrence of the pattern across cultures separated by great distance and
time is better read as convergence on a real expectation than as coincidence. The
broader cross-tradition case is developed in the
[List of prophets and religions](../list-of-prophets-and-religions/), and the
precessional shape of the sleeping king who wakes — Kronos, Saturn, the Once and
Future King — is treated alongside the Return in
[Hamlet's Mill](../hamlets-mill/).

## Critical perspectives

The framework's reading is its own, and neither mainstream scholarship nor the
traditions it draws on makes it. Mainstream Judaism, Christianity, Islam, and the
rest read their return-figures theologically, not as memories of an
extraterrestrial arrival, and none accepts the identification of a Raëlian
Embassy with the Third Temple or the New Jerusalem. Academic study of the
eschatological literature — the messianism of Gershom Scholem and Moshe Idel, the
Mahdi scholarship of Abdulaziz Sachedina and David Cook, the Zoroastrian work of
Mary Boyce and Anders Hultgård — reads these expectations in their own historical
settings. The entry presents the Return as canon rather than as consensus, and
leaves the two standing side by side.

The most serious objection the framework takes on is the contact-impact question:
whether the arrival of a far more advanced civilisation could be anything but
ruinous for the less advanced one. The historical analogues point the wrong way.
The Columbian encounter, the Atlantic slave trade, and the colonial conquests all
produced demographic collapse and cultural destruction rather than a peaceful
exchange of knowledge, and the pattern is consistent enough that the danger
cannot be waved away. The framework's answer is that the Great Return is built to
avoid that pattern: it is voluntary rather than imposed, conditional on
humanity's own readiness, mediated through a prepared institution rather than an
ad-hoc landfall, and undertaken by the species' own makers rather than by
strangers with no stake in its flourishing. This is offered as a genuine
difference in structure, not as a guarantee — the conditions the source sets,
above all the demand for demonstrated wisdom and unification, are themselves an
admission that unprepared contact would be dangerous.

There is also the plain fact of an unfulfilled expectation. The 1997 message
called the return "near"; nearly three decades later no Embassy has been built
with the required extraterritorial status, and no arrival has occurred. The
framework does not treat the imminence language as a dated prediction, since the
return is conditional rather than scheduled, but it does not hide the gap either.
The Embassy's construction — or its continued absence — remains the one part of
the framework open to near-term test.

The location history carries real political weight. The withdrawal-of-protection
message of 2015 and the shift away from Israel bear directly on the
Israeli–Palestinian conflict and the contested status of the Temple Mount. The
framework records these statements as part of the source material's account and
takes no side in the conflict; its own reading in fact loosens the tie to the
disputed ground, since the 1997 authorisation frees the Embassy to be built in
any consenting nation.
