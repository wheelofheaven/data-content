**Intelligent Design** (ID) is, in the broad sense, the claim that the functional
organization of living things — the way biological parts fit together toward a
working whole — is better explained by an intelligent designer than by wholly
undirected natural processes. In the narrow and more familiar sense, the term
names a specific American movement that built this claim into a structured set of
arguments, developed from the early 1990s onward by the **Discovery Institute**
and figures such as Phillip Johnson, Michael Behe, William Dembski, and Stephen
Meyer. That movement's arguments were tested in court and rejected as non-science
in the 2005 *Kitzmiller v. Dover* ruling.

The phrase carries a second, easily missed meaning here. The
[Raëlian](../raelism/) source material — *Le Livre qui dit la vérité* (1974) and
its sequels — was collected in English under the title *Intelligent Design:
Message from the Designers*, and the [Wheel of Heaven](../wheel-of-heaven/)
framework uses the words in that sense: life on Earth was intelligently designed,
by the [Elohim](../elohim/). The framework therefore agrees with the bare design
claim while standing well apart from the American ID movement. The difference is
the designer. For the movement, the designer is left publicly unnamed but
functions, on its own strategy documents, as the Abrahamic God — a supernatural
being beyond investigation. For the framework, the designer is physical: a
biological civilization that engineered terrestrial life with tools continuous
with the genetic engineering our own biology is now developing. That places the
framework's reading closer to a serious if minority scientific hypothesis —
Francis Crick and Leslie Orgel's 1973 *directed panspermia* — than to theistic
ID.

This entry keeps the three apart: the design argument and the movement that
carries its name today; the framework's own physical-designer claim; and the
verdict of mainstream science, which rejects ID as science and which the
framework does not dispute on its own terms.

## The design argument before the movement

The inference from complex, well-fitted structure to a designing mind is old.
Aristotle's natural philosophy already reasoned from the purposive organization
of living things, and Thomas Aquinas made a version of the argument the fifth of
his five ways of demonstrating God's existence (c. 1265–1274). Its most
influential statement is William Paley's *Natural Theology* (1802) and its
**watchmaker analogy**: a watch found on a heath, unlike a stone, so plainly
shows contrivance toward a purpose that one must infer a watchmaker — and, Paley
argued, every organ of every living thing shows the same contrivance to a far
greater degree. For a generation of English naturalists this was simply how
biological complexity was understood.

Two developments unsettled it. David Hume's *Dialogues Concerning Natural
Religion* (published 1779) had already pressed the argument hard through the
character Philo: the analogy between organism and artifact is weaker than it
looks; even granting a designer, the argument establishes little about the
designer's nature — it might be limited, plural, or unlike the God of tradition;
and the designer, if itself complex, would seem to need a designer in turn,
opening a regress. Then in 1859 Charles Darwin's *On the Origin of Species*
supplied a mechanism — undirected variation filtered by differential survival —
by which the appearance of design could arise without a designer. The design
argument did not vanish, but after Darwin it could no longer rest on the bare
claim that complexity requires design; it had to show why particular biological
features could not have evolved. That demand is what gave the modern movement its
shape.

## The Intelligent Design movement

The movement that now owns the phrase "Intelligent Design" is a distinct
historical thing, and it is worth separating carefully from both the older design
argument and the framework's reading.

Its immediate origin is legal. In 1987 the U.S. Supreme Court, in *Edwards v.
Aguillard*, ruled that teaching "creation science" in public schools violated the
Establishment Clause of the Constitution. The decision closed the door on
explicit creationism in the science classroom, and Intelligent Design emerged in
its place. The transition left a documentary fingerprint: drafts of the textbook
*Of Pandas and People* (1989) were revised, after *Edwards*, to swap
"creationist" for "design proponent" throughout — one botched edit produced the
hybrid word "cdesign proponentsists," later cited in court as evidence that ID
was creationism relabeled.

The movement's institutional center is the **Discovery Institute**, a Seattle
think tank founded in 1991, and its **Center for Science and Culture** (1996). An
internal strategy memo leaked in 1999, the **Wedge Document**, stated the aim
plainly: to "defeat scientific materialism" and to replace it with "the theistic
understanding that nature and human beings are created by God." However the
movement described its designer in public — deliberately unnamed, to preserve a
claim to neutrality — its own strategy identified that designer with God. This is
the decisive point of contrast with the Wheel of Heaven reading, whose designer
is physical rather than divine.

Its principal figures and arguments are well defined. Phillip Johnson's *Darwin
on Trial* (1991) set the movement's agenda. Michael Behe's *Darwin's Black Box*
(1996) introduced **irreducible complexity** — the claim that systems such as the
bacterial flagellum, needing all their parts at once to function, could not have
been assembled in selectable steps. William Dembski's *The Design Inference*
(1998) offered **specified complexity**, a proposed statistical filter for
telling design from chance. Stephen Meyer's *Signature in the Cell* (2009) argued
that the information carried in DNA is best explained by a mind. Alongside these
runs the older **fine-tuning** argument, that the physical constants of the
universe are improbably well-suited to life.

Mainstream biology has answered each in turn — irreducible complexity by the
co-option of parts that first served other functions (the flagellum's components
have homologues in the bacterial secretion system), specified complexity by
disputing how its probabilities are even to be computed, the information argument
by the ongoing, unfinished science of life's origins. Whatever one makes of the
replies, the framework's stake in these particular arguments is limited: it does
not need the strong claim that *all* biological complexity resists an
evolutionary account. Its claim is narrower, and about specific engineering
events; it is developed on its own grounds below.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework agrees with the design argument's core intuition — that the
functional complexity of terrestrial life, and of humanity above all, is the work
of intelligence rather than of undirected chemistry — and then parts from the ID
movement on almost everything else.

On the reading developed from the [Raëlian](../raelism/) source material, life on
Earth did not assemble itself from unguided chemistry. It was **designed and
synthesized** by the [Elohim](../elohim/), an advanced civilization from a world
outside the solar system, who built terrestrial plants, animals, and finally
humanity in laboratories and released them into a prepared biosphere. The
[Genesis](../genesis/) creation account is read as a compressed record of that
work — the "days" as ordered phases of engineering, the making of humanity "in
our image" as the deliberate modeling of a species on its makers — and
[Eden](../eden/) as the enclosed laboratory where the first human lineage was
made. These are foundational claims of the framework, stated here as the corpus
states them and not endorsed by mainstream science.

What separates this from the ID movement is not the design inference but the
designer. Three differences carry the weight.

**The designer is physical.** The Elohim are not a metaphysical or supernatural
cause but biological beings with a technology some tens of thousands of years
ahead of ours. Their work is, in principle, the kind of thing that could leave
traces — in the genome, in the archaeological record — and so an object of
investigation rather than an article of faith. The ID movement's designer,
identified in its own documents with God, is by definition beyond such
investigation. On this axis the framework sits closer to ordinary science than
the movement does: its designer belongs to the same natural order as
[genetic engineering](../genetic-engineering/) and [astrobiology](../astrobiology/).

**It is not anti-evolutionary.** The framework does not deny natural selection or
reject the deep-time history of life. It reads the Elohim as having engineered
particular organisms — humanity above all — on top of a biosphere that itself
involved evolutionary processes, not as having conjured every species from
nothing. Its one sharp divergence from mainstream biology is narrow and datable:
it places the synthesis of anatomically modern humans at roughly 25,000 years
ago, against the mainstream figure of some 200,000 years, and it treats that
tension openly as an unresolved question rather than smoothing it over. The
[pantropy](../pantropy/) entry develops the human-synthesis reading in detail.

**It carries no political-religious program.** The ID movement is embedded in
American religious politics; the framework is not. It reaches instead for its
scientific cognate. In 1973 Francis Crick — co-discoverer of the structure of DNA
— and Leslie Orgel proposed, in a peer-reviewed paper, that life on Earth might
have been deliberately seeded by an extraterrestrial civilization: *directed
panspermia*. Crick and Orgel left the seeding civilization abstract. The framework
supplies what they left blank — the makers, the method, and a claimed contact
history — while conceding, as they did, that the idea is not demonstrated. The
convergence in vocabulary is real and worth stating precisely: two of the leading
molecular biologists of the century entertained the general shape of the claim,
even as science rejects the framework's particular account. The point is
developed in the [astrobiology](../astrobiology/) and
[genetic engineering](../genetic-engineering/) entries.

The framework also answers Hume's old regress in a way traditional design
arguments cannot. Where classical ID halts the regress by positing a first,
uncreated designer — God — the corpus lets it run. The Elohim were themselves
engineered by an earlier civilization, which was engineered by an earlier one,
along the [Cosmic Chain](../cosmic-chain/) of makers and
[Forerunners](../forerunners/) that reaches back beyond any first link the source
material claims to know (the point is pressed in the [Infinity](../infinity/) and
[cosmic pluralism](../cosmic-pluralism/) entries). Design, on this reading, is not
the signature of a unique supreme intelligence but the ordinary work of
civilizations that reach maturity — including, before long, our own. The
framework reads today's [genetic engineering](../genetic-engineering/),
[synthetic biology](../synthetic-biology/), and
[synthetic genomics](../synthetic-genomics/) as humanity beginning to recover the
designer's craft: a marker of the [Apocalypse](../apocalypse/)-age transition, and
preparation for the [Great Return](../great-return/).

## Mainstream science and the Kitzmiller ruling

Two verdicts have to be kept apart, because the framework accepts both.

The first is mainstream science's verdict on the ID movement, which is settled:
ID is not science. The National Academy of Sciences (2008) and the American
Association for the Advancement of Science (2002) have both said as much, and the
reasons are specific. ID generates no testable predictions; its design inference
is drawn after the fact rather than used to predict anything. Its signature
arguments have been answered within evolutionary biology. It has produced no
sustained peer-reviewed research program in the decades since *Darwin on Trial*.
And its own history — the Wedge Document, the *Pandas* editing trail — makes its
claim to religious neutrality hard to credit.

Those threads were pulled together in court. In *Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School
District* (2005), eleven parents challenged a Pennsylvania school board that had
required biology teachers to present ID as an alternative to evolution. Judge
John E. Jones III — a conservative appointee — heard six weeks of testimony and
issued a 139-page ruling that ID is not science, that it is "creationism
relabeled" rather than an independent scientific position, and that teaching it in
public-school science classes violates the Establishment Clause. The decision did
not dissolve the movement, but it ended its bid for the classroom.

The framework does not contest any of this, and it takes care not to smuggle its
own claim in under the movement's. The distance it keeps from theistic ID does not
exempt its own reading from scrutiny. The standing objection to directed
panspermia in every form applies to the framework too: naming a prior
civilization does not solve the origin-of-life problem but relocates it, adding an
unobserved civilization without shrinking the mystery. There is no independent
physical evidence — no laboratory signature, no artifact, no confirmed contact —
for an engineered origin of terrestrial life, and Carl Sagan's rule that
extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence applies with full force. The
corpus rests its case on a reading of ancient texts and a contact narrative that
science does not accept as data, and it says so plainly.

What it claims is narrower, and worth holding onto. Science has itself raised the
possibility of deliberate design — tentatively, in Crick and Orgel — and the
framework states its own commitments openly, keeping the line visible between what
the canon asserts and what has been demonstrated. Mainstream science and the
framework agree that the origin and design of life is a real and unsettled
question, among the most consequential a civilization can ask. They differ on
whether it has already been answered — and on whether the answer is written in the
genome or in the texts.
