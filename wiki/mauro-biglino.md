**Mauro Biglino** (born 1950 in Turin) is an Italian translator and
author whose reading of the Hebrew Bible — strictly literal, stripped of
two millennia of theological interpretation — has made him the most
widely read figure of the contemporary reinterpretive tradition in
continental Europe. His authority rests on an unusual credential: before
he wrote a single book of his own, Biglino spent years translating the
Hebrew Masoretic Text into Italian for **Edizioni San Paolo**, the
Catholic publishing house, which published seventeen books of the Hebrew
Bible in his renderings within its interlinear *Vulgata
Ebraico-Interlineare* series. The method that emerged from that
translation desk — set aside the theology, take the words in their
concrete sense, and "pretend that the Bible is simply telling the
truth" — now carries his name; the corpus treats it at full length in
the [Biglino Method](../biglino-method/) entry. This entry gives the
biographical and bibliographical record of the man himself.

Within the contemporary landscape the corpus places Biglino beside
[Paul Wallis](../paul-wallis/) as one of the two most consequential
living voices of [neo-euhemerism](../neo-euhemerism/) — the tradition
that reads the gods of religious memory as remembered visitors. The two
figures divide the territory between them: Biglino supplies the
translation-desk philology and the Italian mass audience; Wallis, whom
the corpus registers as "the principal Anglophone popularizer of the
Biglino-adjacent approach," carries the reading into English. They
blurb each other's books and appear together on camera; Biglino's
standing endorsement of Wallis reads, "Though far apart geographically
we are spiritually close! We are a good team."

## Biography

Biglino was born in 1950 in Turin, where he has remained based
throughout his career. His formation was in religious studies and
classical languages, and his working life before the public career was
that of a professional translator — a background that matters for
understanding both his strengths and the shape of the criticism he
attracts. Biglino is not an academic biblical scholar and has never
presented himself as one; his repeated self-description is that of a
translator reporting what the words on the page say.

### The San Paolo years

The foundation of everything that followed was the commission from
Edizioni San Paolo — the publishing arm of the Society of St. Paul, a
Catholic religious congregation with substantial Vatican connections —
to translate books of the Hebrew Bible from the Masoretic Text for the
*Vulgata Ebraico-Interlineare*, a series of interlinear editions
presenting the Hebrew text with a word-for-word Italian rendering
beneath it. Seventeen books of the Hebrew Bible appeared in Biglino's
translations under the San Paolo imprint.

The interlinear format is the crux of the biography. An interlinear
translator cannot paraphrase, harmonize, or smooth: every Hebrew word
must receive its Italian counterpart in sequence, which forces a
sustained word-by-word confrontation with what the text concretely
says. Biglino's account of the experience, given in *The Naked Bible*,
is that the work "brought me into direct contact... with the very
origins, with the etymology of each individual word. Terms that, time
after time, are put back into the context of the stories. Words that
gave birth to a mosaic that is totally different from what is
traditionally told to us." It is also on record — and the corpus's
[Biglino Method](../biglino-method/) entry registers the point — that
the published San Paolo editions themselves left terms such as
*Elohim*, *kavod*, *ruach*, and the tetragrammaton in transliteration:
mainstream Catholic biblical publishing conceded, in its own apparatus,
that the conventional renderings of these words carry interpretive
weight the Hebrew does not settle.

### The break and the independent career

In 2010 Biglino published his own first book, *Il libro che cambierà
per sempre le nostre idee sulla Bibbia* ("The book that will forever
change our ideas about the Bible"), setting out in public the readings
the translation desk had produced. The relationship with San Paolo
ended after his own books began to appear — a Catholic publisher could
not keep on its Hebrew translation team an author arguing in print that
the Hebrew Bible does not speak of God. The sequence is worth stating
precisely, because it is sometimes told backwards: the credential came
first, the heterodox books second, and the separation followed the
books.

The independent career that followed made Biglino a phenomenon of
Italian public culture. *Il dio alieno della Bibbia* ("The alien god of
the Bible") followed in 2011, *Non c'è creazione nella Bibbia* ("There
is no creation in the Bible") in 2012, and the stream has continued
since — *La Bibbia non parla di Dio* (2015), *La Bibbia non è un libro
sacro* ("The Bible is not a sacred book"), and a run of later works,
increasingly in collaborative formats with co-authors ranging from the
journalist Giorgio Cattaneo (*La Bibbia nuda* / *The Naked Bible*) to a
philosopher of law (*La Bibbia non l'ha mai detto*). Several titles
reached the mainstream Italian market through major publishers; his
lectures and seminars on Hebrew Bible reading fill halls; his YouTube
and online following is large enough that Italian religious-studies
scholarship has taken his audience, not just his claims, as an object
of study. English translations, principally through **Uno Editori**,
have carried the work into the Anglophone market from the late 2010s
onward — *The Bible Is Not a Sacred Book*, *The Naked Bible* (2022),
and the consolidated *Gods of the Bible*.

## Method

The method is treated at full length in the
[Biglino Method](../biglino-method/) entry; here it needs one
paragraph. Biglino's three commitments are to work from the original
Hebrew text; to set aside the accumulated interpretation of the
Jewish, Christian, and Islamic exegetical traditions; and to read the
words literally, in their concrete sense, unless the text itself
signals otherwise. The wager is packaged in his signature formula,
*facciamo finta che* — "let us pretend that" the Bible is simply
telling the truth — a deliberately provisional framing: Biglino does
not assert that the Bible is true, he proposes reading it as if it
were and observing what coherent account results. The methodological
precedent is Schliemann's literal reading of Homer, which located
Troy; the same Schliemann-to-Bible program was articulated by Jean
Sendy in 1969, four decades before Biglino's independent formulation,
and the corpus reads the Sendy-Biglino convergence — two translators,
two languages, two decades apart, same commitments, same principal
observations — as itself evidential. Where honesty about a term's
meaning is impossible, Biglino's rule is to leave it untranslated:
"It's a question of integrity, since we do not know exactly what they
mean."

## Key readings

Biglino's principal textual claims, each attributed here to Biglino
and argued across the works cited below; several are cataloged, with
the corpus's own assessments, in the
[list of etymological readings](../list-of-etymological-readings/).

- **The Elohim are a plurality of individuals.** The grammatically
  plural [*elohim*](../elohim/) is, on Biglino's reading, exactly what
  it appears to be: a plural, denoting a group of concrete beings who
  can be counted, who divide territory, who quarrel and die (Psalm
  82's "you shall die like men"). The divine-allotment passage of
  Deuteronomy 32:8–9, where the Most High divides the nations and
  [Yahweh](../yahweh/)'s inheritance is Jacob, gives Biglino his
  picture of Yahweh as one governor among many — a reading whose raw
  data mainstream divine-council scholarship also registers, as the
  [plurality of gods](../plurality-of-gods/) entry documents.
- **The kavod is an object.** The *kavod* of Yahweh, conventionally
  "glory," is read from its root sense of weight and heaviness as a
  heavy physical thing — something that flies, lands, must be
  approached with precautions, and in Biglino's mordant phrase was
  "viewable by appointment." The reading closely parallels the one
  Wallis reached in English ("It was a machine").
- **The ruach moves through air.** *Ruach*, conventionally "spirit,"
  carries the primary sense of wind, breath, moving air; Biglino
  devotes a chapter of *Il dio alieno della Bibbia* to reading the
  *ruach* of key passages as something physically traversing the sky
  rather than an immaterial presence.
- **The tetragrammaton is a name, and its meaning is unknown.** YHWH
  is a proper name, effaced in translation by the substituted "Lord";
  Biglino declines to assign it a settled meaning and treats it as a
  designation whose referent must be reconstructed from what the text
  has him do, not from later theology. This is his standing example of
  the leave-it-untranslated rule.
- **"There is no creation in the Bible."** The claim that gives the
  2012 book its title: the verb *bara* in Genesis 1, on Biglino's
  reading, denotes separation, division, and reorganization of
  pre-existing material rather than creation from nothing — a
  [*Genesis*](../genesis/) that opens with intervention in an already
  existing world. Here Biglino's argument runs parallel to a proposal
  from inside the academy: Ellen van Wolde's peer-reviewed case that
  *bara* in Genesis 1 means "to separate," published in the *Journal
  for the Study of the Old Testament* — contested among Hebraists, but
  a mainstream demonstration that the question is real.

## Reception

### In Italy: the "fenomeno Biglino"

Biglino's popular reach in Italy has no equivalent elsewhere in the
tradition — sustained bestseller status, packed public lectures, and a
following substantial enough that the phenomenon itself has been
studied. The principal academic treatment, published in the Italian
religious-studies journal *Studi e materiali di storia delle
religioni*, analyzes the "fenomeno Biglino" through the categories of
paleoastronautics, secularization, religious individualization, and
quasi-religion — reading his audience as a post-Catholic public that
has left the Church but not the Bible, for whom Biglino's translator
persona offers a way of keeping the text while dropping the theology.
The study is cited by Biglino's own co-author, and the corpus takes it
as the serious sociological registration of his standing: whatever one
makes of the readings, the audience is a documented fact.

### Mainstream Hebraists

Academic biblical scholarship, where it has engaged Biglino at all,
has rejected his conclusions, and the objections deserve statement in
their own voice. The philological objection: *elohim*, though plural
in form, governs singular verbs and adjectives in the overwhelming
majority of its references to the God of Israel, and Hebrew grammar
knows honorific and abstract plurals — so the form alone cannot carry
the weight Biglino places on it. The literary objection: strict
literalism ignores genre, poetic convention, and the composite,
redacted character of the text's transmission; reading Ezekiel's
throne vision as an eyewitness technical report mistakes what kind of
writing it is. The professional objection: Biglino does not publish in
peer-reviewed venues, does not engage the specialist literature, and
his lexical arguments — *kavod* as flying object, *ruach* as craft —
outrun what the comparative-Semitic evidence supports. Italian
academic commentary has generally filed his conclusions under
pseudoscience and declined further engagement.

Biglino's standing reply is that he is doing translation rather than
theology or history — reporting what the words say and inviting anyone
with a lexicon to check — and that the burden of the honorific-plural
convention is itself an interpretive choice made by the tradition his
method suspends. The corpus's own position, argued in the
[Biglino Method](../biglino-method/) entry, splits the difference: the
critiques register legitimate limits of the method applied alone,
while the standoff — scholars dismissing Biglino without engaging his
specific observations, Biglino dismissing scholarship wholesale — is
unhealthy for the interpretive conversation, since several of his
observations (the grammatical plurality, the concreteness of the
theophany descriptions, the *bara* question) are ones the academy
could productively engage on its own terms.

## Relation to the Wheel of Heaven framework

The corpus's debt to Biglino is methodological and is stated openly:
the practice of reading Hebrew terms directly — *Elohim* as plural,
Yahweh as a name, *kavod*, *ruach*, and *nachash* kept in
transliteration — follows the program Biglino articulated and Sendy
articulated before him, and the corpus adopts it as necessary but not
sufficient, integrating it with the precessional framework, the
comparative sweep, and the Raëlian source-textual primacy that
Biglino's own work does not touch.

The convergence-through-independence framing the corpus applies to
Wallis applies to Biglino in modified form. Like Wallis, Biglino
arrived at the plural Elohim, the concrete *kavod*, and the
governor-Yahweh from the text, without deriving them from the canon —
and two translators reaching the same readings from the same text is
worth more evidentially than one. Unlike Wallis, however, Biglino
demonstrably knows the Raëlian material: the corpus's source record on
*Il dio alieno della Bibbia* notes [Raël](../rael/)'s texts in the
bibliography. The independence claim for Biglino is therefore narrower
and more precise — his readings are argued from the Hebrew, verifiable
against the Hebrew, and stand or fall with the Hebrew, whatever else
sits on his shelf. Where the canon states its claims as fact, Biglino
stops deliberately short: he is a self-declared agnostic who presents
his conclusions as what the text says, not as what he believes
happened, and he draws no doctrine from them. The corpus reads that
reticence not as a defect but as a division of labor — the translator
establishes what the words say; the [framework](../wheel-of-heaven/)
argues about what the words record. The three-generation lineage —
Sendy (1969), Biglino (2010–), Wallis (2020–) — and its place in the
broader tradition is mapped in the
[neo-euhemerism](../neo-euhemerism/) entry and in the Explainer
[*The Archdeacon and the Dragon*](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/).
