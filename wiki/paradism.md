**Paradism** is the Raëlian doctrine of a post-scarcity, fully automated, moneyless
society — a civilisation in which machines perform all necessary work, material
scarcity is abolished, and people are freed from wage-labour to think, create, and
fulfil themselves. In the Wheel of Heaven framework it is read as the socio-economic
shape of the [Golden Age](../golden-age/): the form Aquarian-age society takes once
automation is carried through to its conclusion, modelled on the world of the
[Elohim](../elohim/), whose home planet the canon describes as already organised
this way. Its principal source is [Claude Vorilhon (Raël)](../rael/)'s
*Extra-Terrestrials Took Me to Their Planet* (1975), consolidated in
*[Message from the Designers](../message-from-the-designers/)* (2005), where the
claim is put simply: machines can look after the daily chores by themselves, so
human beings need no longer sell their lives to earn a living.

Paradism belongs to the wider Aquarian-age programme the canon calls the
[New Commandments](../new-commandments/), alongside [Geniocracy](../geniocracy/),
which governs, and [Humanitarianism](../humanitarianism/), which reforms property
and inheritance. Where Humanitarianism restructures an economy that still runs on
money and labour, Paradism describes the further horizon in which both have become
unnecessary. The two are read as stages rather than rivals: Humanitarianism the
nearer-term transition, Paradism the long-term destination. A separate advocacy
body, the Paradism movement (paradism.org), promotes the idea as a political
proposal and is kept organisationally distinct from the Raëlian Movement, whose
own mission remains the diffusion of the message and the building of the
[Embassy](../embassy/).

## The doctrine

The core claim of Paradism is that technology has already made human toil obsolete,
and that a society organised around this fact would free people for the work they
were actually made for. Vorilhon states it directly:

> *"Science and technology can totally liberate humanity, not only from the problem
> of hunger in the world, but also from the obligation to work to live, since
> machines can quite easily look after the daily chores by themselves, thanks to
> automation."*

The argument is not for better wages or shorter hours but for the abolition of
necessary labour altogether. Vorilhon points to a trend he already saw underway in
1975 — the factory where hundreds of workers had been replaced by one person
overseeing a machine, and where, he predicts, even that one person will eventually
be unnecessary. The half-century since has borne out the direction of travel:
industrial robotics, and now artificial intelligence, have absorbed a widening
share of routine physical and cognitive work.

The point of that liberation is a positive one. Freed from labour, human beings are
to turn to what the source calls their true vocation:

> *"Machines can easily do all the chores and take care of all the work, enabling
> people to dedicate themselves to the one thing for which they were created — to
> think, create and blossom."*

Paradism therefore rejects the moral elevation of work for its own sake. Vorilhon
extends the critique to the trinity of *work, family, country* — the wartime Vichy
slogan *Travail, Famille, Patrie* — arguing that each has been used to bind people
to a labour they need not perform, and proposing in their place *fulfilment,
freedom, and universal fraternity*.

**The mechanism.** What makes a post-scarcity economy conceivable, on this account,
is the convergence of automated manufacturing, artificial intelligence, and — in the
corpus's forward reading — nanotechnology. If food, shelter, and everyday goods can
be produced on demand from common materials at negligible cost, the central economic
problem shifts. Production ceases to be the constraint; the remaining question is
how to distribute an abundance rather than how to ration a scarcity. The corpus
frames this as a change of kind, not degree: once the machines do the producing, the
economic problem becomes political — how to organise the sharing of plenty — rather
than a matter of extracting enough from human effort.

**The abolition of money.** The endpoint of the doctrine is a society without money.
Vorilhon distinguishes two stages. In the transitional phase, before automation is
complete, differentiated reward still has a place:

> *"Although everyone has the right to have sufficient means to live decently, those
> who do more for their fellow human beings have the right to receive more than those
> who do nothing for the community. This is obviously a temporary rule until human
> beings can have robots to perform all menial labor, so that they can devote
> themselves exclusively to their fulfillment, after having totally abolished money."*

Money is thus read as a rationing device for scarce goods. Where scarcity ends, the
reason for money ends with it, and a fully realised Paradism dispenses with monetary
exchange altogether.

**A third way.** The canon positions Paradism against the two dominant systems of the
twentieth century. In the "Society and Government" material the judgment is blunt:
capitalism "enslaves people to money, benefiting a few on the backs of others," while
communism "places greater importance on equality than on liberty." Paradism claims to
be neither. It does not redistribute a scarcity-bound economy, as communism proposes,
nor accept the concentration of wealth that capitalism permits; it removes scarcity
itself through automation, which the canon presents as making both extraction and
forced redistribution obsolete. Human contribution becomes voluntary and creative
rather than driven by necessity.

Two further specifics recur in the source. The first is the **biological robot** —
Vorilhon's preferred image of automated labour, a bioengineered worker rather than a
mechanical one: *"Do everything possible to create the biological robot that will
release you from all menial labor."* The second is a matching vision of **urban
form**: tall communal buildings set in open country, each capable of housing some
fifty thousand people and equipped with everything its residents need, so that
individual houses do not, in the source's phrase, "devour" nature. Both are offered
as concrete features of the paradise the doctrine names.

## Etymology and naming

The word joins *paradise* to the suffix *-ism*. *Paradise* descends from Old Persian
*pairi-daēza*, an "enclosed garden" or walled park, by way of Greek *paradeisos*; the
Septuagint used the same Greek word to render the *gan* — the garden — of
[Eden](../eden/), fixing the long association between "paradise" and the garden of
Genesis. The suffix marks Paradism as a systematic doctrine rather than a mood or a
picture, on the pattern of the other Raëlian coinages [Geniocracy](../geniocracy/)
and [Cyberparadism](../cyberparadism/).

The French source terms matter for the sense. Vorilhon writes of a *paradis
terrestre* — a *terrestrial* paradise — and titles a section *paradis sur Terre*,
"paradise on Earth." The adjective is doing deliberate work: Paradism names a
condition to be built on Earth by human hands and machines, not a heaven awaited
after death. It is an engineering programme dressed in the oldest word for the
garden, not an eschatology.

## The Wheel of Heaven reading

The framework reads Paradism as the economic face of the [Golden Age](../golden-age/)
— the age the canon expects the [Elohim](../elohim/) to help humanity reach, and
which it describes as a return, on Earth, to the conditions of the creators' own
world. Of the several characteristics the corpus assigns to that age, Paradism
carries the first two: the end of material scarcity through automated, and eventually
molecular, manufacture, and the end of human labour through machine and
artificial-intelligence substitution. Where the Golden Age names the whole realised
civilisation, Paradism names the organising principle of its economy.

Read in the canon's own voice, the doctrine is not a wish but a description of where
the [Age of Aquarius](../age-of-aquarius/) is meant to arrive. The Elohim created
humanity scientifically and have, in the Raëlian account, already crossed this
threshold themselves; the New Commandments they transmit through Vorilhon are the
programme for humanity to do the same. Paradism supplies that programme's
socio-economic content, as Geniocracy supplies its politics and Humanitarianism its
property law. The full realisation is expected to follow the return of the creators
and the transfer of advanced technological knowledge that the [Embassy](../embassy/)
is built to receive.

Like the rest of the New Commandments, Paradism is read as a directional orientation
across the long Aquarian age — roughly the span from the mid-twentieth century to the
fifth millennium in the corpus's chronology — rather than an overnight conversion.
The transitional and fully realised phases are stages along that arc: today's partial
automation, with its differentiated rewards, is understood as the early stretch of a
road whose end is the moneyless society. Whether humanity reaches that end is, in the
wider framework, bound up with how it navigates the
[Number of Man](../number-of-man/) threshold and the choice the age presents.

## Comparative currents

Paradism's structural features — automation as liberation, the end of scarcity, the
withering of money and wage-labour — recur across a long line of secular thought, and
the resemblances are close enough to be worth naming, even though the canon grounds
the idea differently from any of them.

The oldest lineage is the utopian-socialist one. William Morris's *News from Nowhere*
(1890) and Edward Bellamy's *Looking Backward* (1888) both imagine post-money
commonwealths, as do the community experiments of Fourier and Owen and the anarchist
abundance of Kropotkin's *The Conquest of Bread* (1892). These share Paradism's
destination but not its engine: they precede the age of automation and place their
hopes in reorganised human labour rather than in machines that make labour
unnecessary.

Closer in time and mechanism is the contemporary literature on post-scarcity and
automation — Peter Frase's *Four Futures* (2016), Erik Brynjolfsson and Andrew
McAfee's *The Second Machine Age* (2014), Daniel Susskind's *A World Without Work*
(2020), and the post-capitalist arguments of Paul Mason and of Nick Srnicek and Alex
Williams. The nearest single parallel is Aaron Bastani's *Fully Automated Luxury
Communism* (2019), which likewise treats extreme automation as the infrastructure of
a liberated, abundant society; the difference lies in the theoretical frame — a
Marxist one for Bastani, the Raëlian revelation for Paradism. Universal Basic Income,
in the work of Philippe Van Parijs or the campaign of Andrew Yang, converges only
partway: it is compatible with the transitional stage but keeps both money and the
market, and so falls short of the fully realised doctrine. Transhumanism and the
technological-singularity discourse of Nick Bostrom and Ray Kurzweil share Paradism's
technological optimism while pointing it elsewhere — at the enhancement of the human
being rather than the reorganisation of the economy. In popular culture, the moneyless
*Star Trek* Federation, with its matter replicators standing in for nanotechnology,
has done more than any manifesto to make a post-scarcity future imaginable.

The framework treats all of these as convergent readings of the same technological
possibility, arrived at independently. Its own distinctive claim is not the vision of
abundance, which it shares widely, but the account of where the vision comes from —
the Elohim civilisation the canon holds up as the working model — and its integration
into the single Aquarian-age programme of the New Commandments.

## Critical perspectives

Paradism inherits the objections that attach to post-scarcity utopianism generally,
and it is fairer to the doctrine to state them than to pass over them.

The first concerns automation and work. That machines displace particular jobs is not
in dispute; whether they abolish the need for human work in aggregate is very much so.
Economic historians such as Carl Benedikt Frey (*The Technology Trap*, 2019) note that
past waves of automation destroyed some occupations while creating others, and
mainstream economists remain divided on whether the current wave is different in kind.
Paradism reads displacement as a transition cost on the way to liberation; critics
reply that the hard questions — who owns the machines, and how the gains are shared —
are precisely the political problems the doctrine assumes will be solved rather than
showing how.

The second concerns money. Whether a complex economy can allocate resources without
prices is a long-standing question in economics, sharpened in the twentieth-century
"socialist calculation debate." The canon's answer is that artificial intelligence and
distributed coordination can do what prices once did; that this could work at the scale
of a planet is, on any honest account, unproven, and belongs to the *speculative*
rather than the demonstrated. The same caution applies to the nanotechnology that
underwrites the promise of near-costless goods: universal molecular manufacturing of
the kind the doctrine assumes remains a hypothesis, not an achieved technology, and
mainstream materials science has produced nothing like a general-purpose assembler.

Finally, Paradism's realisation is made to depend on the other New Commandments — on
Geniocratic governance and on Humanitarian property reform — each of which carries its
own well-rehearsed objections, treated in the [Geniocracy](../geniocracy/) and
[Humanitarianism](../humanitarianism/) entries. Read as a forecast, Paradism is
exposed on every one of these fronts. Read as the canon presents it — a directional
orientation, a description of the destination rather than a route map — it is better
understood as a normative vision of what a fully automated society could be for, and
it is on that ground that it is most defensible.
