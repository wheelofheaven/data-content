This entry catalogues reported close encounters with aerial phenomena and their occupants across the pre-modern period, from approximately 1460 BCE through 1499 CE. The catalogue follows the cataloguing work of **Jacques Vallée and Chris Aubeck** in *Wonders in the Sky: Unexplained Aerial Objects from Antiquity to Modern Times* (Tarcher/Penguin, 2009), the principal contemporary scholarly compilation of pre-modern reports, which examines approximately 500 pre-1880 incidents drawn from chronicles, religious texts, military histories, monastic annals, and other documentary sources.

The Wheel of Heaven framework reads the catalogued encounters as the documented historical record of operational Elohim and related presence on Earth across the post-Eden period of the project. The encounters are distributed across millennia and cultures; they preserve, in the regional vocabularies of the times and places where they were reported, what the contemporary vocabulary of UFO research names *close encounters*. The phenomenological continuity across the period — the same types of objects, the same patterns of behaviour, the same kinds of effects on witnesses, recurring across cultures that had no contact with one another — is read by the framework as evidence of a unitary phenomenon rather than as folkloric coincidence or parallel invention.

The application of modern UFO-research terminology to pre-modern accounts is a deliberate interpretive choice. The term *close encounter* was coined by **J. Allen Hynek** in 1972 within a conceptual frame designed for sightings of *unidentified flying objects* understood within post-1947 UFO discourse. Applying that terminology to a New Kingdom Egyptian military account, a biblical theophany, or a medieval Chinese chronicle is a neo-euhemeristic move — a claim that the phenomenon contemporary research investigates and the phenomenon ancient and medieval sources reported are the same phenomenon, just described in the conceptual vocabulary of their respective times. The methodology section below registers this terminological choice explicitly and treats the interpretive grounds for it.

The catalogue's scope ends in 1499 CE for several converging reasons: the end of the 15th century marks the beginning of the European encounter with the Americas (whose pre-1492 accounts are largely absent from the European documentary record), the broad chronological pivot before the scientific revolution's transformation of observation conventions, and the pre-modern character of the source materials that are catalogued in the early portions of the Vallée-Aubeck compilation. The catalogue does not attempt coverage of the modern UFO era (post-1947) or of the long 16th–19th-century intermediate period, which Vallée and Aubeck cover in subsequent sections of *Wonders in the Sky* and which would warrant separate dedicated cataloguing.

The reading on which the framework's interest in this catalogue depends is contested at multiple levels. Mainstream historians treat the catalogued reports variably — some as natural phenomena (comets, meteors, atmospheric optics, auroral displays), some as cultural-religious literary productions, some as authentic reports of unidentified phenomena whose specific identity remains undetermined. Mainstream UFO research (the Hynek tradition, the Vallée tradition, the Condon-era debunking tradition, the contemporary academic UFO studies that have developed since the 2017 disclosure of the U.S. Navy AATIP program) has substantively engaged the pre-modern record without reaching consensus on its interpretation. The framework's specifically neo-euhemerist reading — that the encounters preserve operational Elohim presence — is one position within this broader interpretive space, and the catalogue handles the relationships between positions transparently.

## Methodology

The catalogue's methodological commitments require explicit statement, since the field engages contested terrain at several levels: the source-criticism level (whether the reports are reliable testimony to anything), the phenomenological level (whether the reports describe a unitary phenomenon or a heterogeneous collection of unrelated events), and the interpretive level (what the phenomenon, if real, actually is).

### The Hynek close-encounter classification

The modern conceptual framework for cataloguing close encounters is J. Allen Hynek's classification, developed in *The UFO Experience: A Scientific Inquiry* (1972). Hynek, an astronomer who worked on the U.S. Air Force's Project Blue Book investigation of UFO sightings (1952–1969) and who became one of the most credentialed academic researchers of the phenomenon, distinguished encounter types by the degree of proximity and interaction:

- **CE1 — Close encounter of the first kind**: visual sighting of an unidentified flying object at close range without physical interaction
- **CE2 — Close encounter of the second kind**: physical evidence is present (ground traces, vegetation effects, animal reactions, electromagnetic interference, physiological effects on witnesses)
- **CE3 — Close encounter of the third kind**: occupants of the object are observed
- **CE4 — Close encounter of the fourth kind** (added by Jacques Vallée): abduction or other involuntary contact with occupants
- **CE5 — Close encounter of the fifth kind** (added by Steven Greer and others): direct, deliberate communication or contact

The classification was designed for *modern* sightings — sightings whose witnesses could describe what they saw in modern technological vocabulary, whose investigators could conduct interviews and physical examinations of sites, and whose reports survive in adequately documented form. Applying the classification to pre-modern accounts requires substantial interpretive judgement. The catalogue uses the Hynek classification where the surviving account contains enough specific content for the type to be determinable, with the caveat that the classification represents a contemporary interpretive lens applied to materials that originated in radically different conceptual contexts.

### The Vallée-Aubeck source and the broader Vallée corpus

The catalogue's principal scholarly source is **Jacques Vallée and Chris Aubeck**, *Wonders in the Sky: Unexplained Aerial Objects from Antiquity to Modern Times* (Tarcher/Penguin, 2009). Vallée is the foundational modern researcher of pre-modern UFO accounts; his earlier *Passport to Magonia: From Folklore to Flying Saucers* (1969) was the first sustained scholarly engagement with the parallels between modern UFO reports and pre-modern folkloric accounts of aerial encounters and abductions. Aubeck's contribution to *Wonders in the Sky* draws on his work with The Magoniax Project, which has compiled the largest known collection of pre-1947 reports. The book examines approximately 500 selected reports from antiquity through 1879 with attention to source-criticism, cross-cultural pattern identification, and the phenomenological continuity between pre-modern and modern accounts.

Vallée's broader theoretical work is important context for the catalogue. Across *Passport to Magonia* (1969), *The Invisible College* (1975), *Messengers of Deception* (1979), *Dimensions* (1988), *Confrontations* (1990), and subsequent volumes, Vallée developed a position substantively distinct from the strict Extraterrestrial Hypothesis (ETH) that dominates popular UFO discourse. Vallée's position — sometimes called the *interdimensional hypothesis* — argues that the phenomenon exhibits features (the apparent absurdity of many reports, the cultural-context-sensitivity of the manifestations, the parallels with folkloric and religious traditions) that are difficult to explain on a strict ETH account but that suggest a broader phenomenon involving consciousness, culture, and possibly non-physical dimensions. The framework's adopted reading differs from Vallée's on this point: the framework is more strictly neo-euhemerist (the encounters are read as operational Elohim presence, a physical-extraterrestrial-civilisation referent), while Vallée's position is more agnostic and more open to non-physical interpretations. The catalogue draws on Vallée's empirical compilation while adopting a different interpretive frame; this difference is registered honestly.

### The neo-euhemerist application of modern terminology

The catalogue's title and conceptual frame apply Hynek's 1972 close-encounter terminology to accounts that originated in radically different cultural and conceptual contexts. A New Kingdom Egyptian scribe documenting a "falling star" defeating the Nubians in the 15th century BCE did not conceive of unidentified flying objects in any sense recognisable to modern UFO research. A first-century Chinese chronicler reporting "a squadron of flying intruders" did not conceive of extraterrestrial visitors as a contemporary UFO researcher would. The biblical author of Ezekiel reporting the chariot vision conceived of his report as theophany — direct presence of the deity — not as observation of a technological craft.

The framework's neo-euhemerist position, treated more fully in the [List of exegetic readings](../list-of-exegetic-readings/), is that the underlying phenomenon is the same across these conceptual frames: operational Elohim presence on Earth, described in the conceptual vocabulary available to each reporting culture. The Egyptian scribe described what he saw using New Kingdom Egyptian conceptual resources (a "falling star" that defeated an enemy). The Chinese chronicler used Han-period conceptual resources (a "squadron of intruders" in the sky). The biblical author used Hebrew religious conceptual resources (a theophany with specific iconographic features). The contemporary UFO researcher uses post-1947 conceptual resources (unidentified flying objects, close encounters). The framework reads the phenomenological convergence across these conceptually divergent descriptions as evidence of a unitary referent.

This is the interpretive grounds for applying modern terminology to pre-modern accounts. The terminological choice is not a denial of the original cultural-conceptual context of the accounts; it is the explicit naming of the framework's claim that the original accounts and the contemporary research are engaging the same underlying phenomenon. Readers who do not share this interpretive position will find the terminology anachronistic; the catalogue's purpose is precisely to develop and document the position.

### The pre-1500 scope

The catalogue's chronological scope ends in 1499 CE. The scope is selected for several converging reasons.

- **The European encounter with the Americas.** The 1492 Columbus voyage and subsequent encounters opened the Americas to European documentation; pre-1492 American accounts of aerial phenomena are largely absent from the European documentary record, though substantial accounts exist in indigenous traditions whose documentation came later. The catalogue's final few entries (Columbus's 1492 light, Cabral's 1499 sighting) register the transition point.
- **The pre-scientific-revolution character of the source materials.** The catalogue's entries are drawn from chronicles, religious texts, military histories, and other pre-scientific-revolution source types. The development of systematic scientific observation in the 16th and 17th centuries transformed the conceptual context of reports about aerial phenomena; pre-1500 accounts originate in conceptual contexts where natural-philosophy categories had not yet differentiated meteorological from astronomical from religious phenomena.
- **The mechanical aerial technology question.** Pre-1500 accounts predate the development of any human-built aerial technology (the first hot-air balloon was Joseph-Michel Montgolfier's 1783 flight; the first powered aircraft was the Wrights' 1903 Flyer). The pre-1500 scope guarantees that the catalogued accounts cannot be reinterpreted as misidentified human aerial technology.
- **The Vallée-Aubeck source structure.** *Wonders in the Sky* organises its compilation by chronological periods, with the pre-1500 material constituting one natural subsection of the broader work.

The catalogue does not preclude future expansion to the 16th–19th-century intermediate period or to the modern UFO era; both are covered in Vallée-Aubeck and in the broader UFO-research literature, and the framework's interpretive engagement extends to both. The present catalogue's scope is selected for coherence rather than for theoretical exclusivity.

### Source criticism

The catalogue's entries draw from source materials of substantially varying reliability. Some entries (the biblical Ezekiel chariot vision, the New Kingdom Egyptian Karnak account of the Nubian battle) come from source traditions that have undergone substantial textual transmission with documented editorial processes. Others (single-line entries in monastic chronicles, brief notices in court histories) survive in essentially undeveloped form with limited corroboration. Others (later medieval European accounts) come from source contexts where the line between authentic report and folkloric embellishment is unclear.

The catalogue does not attempt comprehensive source criticism per entry. Readers interested in the source-criticism status of specific entries should consult Vallée and Aubeck's *Wonders in the Sky*, which provides extensive bibliographic apparatus for each case it treats. The catalogue's adopted approach is to register each report as transmitted, with the understanding that source reliability varies; structural observations about cross-cultural pattern (which depend on aggregate features of the catalogue rather than on individual case reliability) are more secure than claims about specific individual cases.

### What the catalogue does not claim

Three things the catalogue does not claim deserve explicit statement.

The catalogue does not claim that every entry represents an authentic close encounter in the framework's sense. Some entries may represent misidentified natural phenomena (comets, meteors, auroras, atmospheric optics); some may represent literary or religious productions without observational basis; some may represent embellishment of more mundane events. The catalogue's value lies in the aggregate pattern rather than in the claim that each individual entry is authentic.

The catalogue does not claim that all entries can be classified by Hynek type with confidence. Many pre-modern accounts contain insufficient detail for confident classification; the Hynek classification is applied where the surviving account supports it, with classifications noted in the catalogue's Description column.

The catalogue does not claim phenomenological uniformity across all entries. The reports span 3,000 years and many cultural contexts; they describe a substantial range of phenomena that may or may not constitute a unitary class. The framework's reading is that the cross-cultural continuity in specific features (the lights, the disk-shaped objects, the cylindrical and spherical objects, the abduction patterns, the missing-time phenomena, the heat effects, the occupant descriptions) is sufficient to support the unitary-phenomenon reading, but readers may reach different judgements.

## Structural patterns the catalogue surfaces

The catalogue's distribution across geography, chronology, and phenomenology surfaces several structural patterns worth registering.

### Geographic clustering

The geographic distribution of catalogued reports is heavily Mediterranean and East Asian. Italian peninsular reports constitute the largest single regional cluster (heavily concentrated in the prodigy literature of the Roman Republic and in the late medieval Italian chronicle tradition). Chinese and Japanese reports together constitute the second-largest cluster, drawing on the substantial imperial-court annalistic traditions of both cultures. Western European reports (British Isles, France, Germany, Iberia) constitute the third-largest cluster, with substantial expansion from the early medieval period onward as the European documentary base grew. Reports from sub-Saharan Africa, the pre-Columbian Americas, Oceania, and inner Asia are largely absent — not because the regions lacked relevant phenomena (indigenous traditions across these regions preserve substantial aerial-encounter content) but because the regions' documentary traditions were either non-textual or were not transmitted in forms that the Vallée-Aubeck compilation could access.

The clustering reflects the survival pattern of pre-modern documentary sources rather than the actual geographic distribution of the underlying phenomenon. The framework reads this distributional bias as a methodological constraint on the catalogue rather than as a phenomenological finding; if the indigenous American, sub-Saharan African, Polynesian, and other oral traditions could be integrated, the catalogue's geographic shape would substantially expand.

### Temporal clustering

The chronological distribution shows several notable temporal clusters. The Roman Republic and early Imperial period (3rd century BCE through 1st century CE) is unusually well-represented because the Roman *prodigia* recording tradition systematically catalogued anomalous events; Livy, Julius Obsequens, and other sources preserve a relatively dense record of the period. The Tang-Song transition in China (8th–12th centuries CE) is similarly dense because of the Chinese imperial annalistic tradition. The high medieval European period (12th–14th centuries CE) shows expanding documentation as European monastic and secular chronicle traditions matured. The Black Death period (mid-14th century) and the late 15th century crisis period show concentrated reporting clusters.

The framework reads the temporal clustering as partly methodological (reflecting documentary survival) and partly substantive (potentially reflecting periods of intensified Elohim operational presence). Disentangling the two contributions is difficult; the catalogue registers the clustering without proposing a confident resolution.

### Phenomenological continuity

The catalogue's most theoretically significant pattern is the *phenomenological continuity* between the pre-modern reports and the contemporary UFO record. Specific recurring features include:

- **Spherical and globular aerial objects** — reported across all eras and regions, from the Roman prodigy literature through the Tang Chinese accounts through the late medieval European reports; the same phenomenology recurs in the contemporary UFO record
- **Disc and disc-like objects** — reported as "shields," "umbrellas," "wheels," "canopies," "cartwheels" across different cultural-conceptual vocabularies; the modern "flying disc" terminology is conceptually continuous
- **Cylindrical and cigar-shaped objects** — reported as "great ships," "flying chariots," "cigar-shaped objects" (the 1347 Florence entry, the 1465 Italy entry); the modern "cigar-shaped UFO" terminology is conceptually continuous
- **Mother-and-daughter object patterns** — the 1015 Kyoto "objects emerge from mother stars" entry preserves a pattern recurrent in the contemporary UFO record
- **Multi-object formations** — triangular, linear, V-shaped, and other geometric formations recur across the catalogue
- **Aerial conflicts** — multiple objects exhibiting apparent conflict behaviour, recurring from the Roman period ("shields clashing in the sky," 103 BCE) through the late medieval period
- **CE3 occupant descriptions** — humanoid figures, child-figures, "ladies in the light," "dragon riders," and other occupant types recur across regions and centuries
- **CE4 abduction patterns** — from the Magonia tradition through the medieval child-abduction accounts, the abduction pattern is consistent across periods
- **Heat effects, physical effects** — reports of heat felt by witnesses, ground traces, physiological effects on observers occur across the catalogue
- **Water-emergence events** — aerial objects emerging from or entering bodies of water (the 1059 Fanliang lake entry, the 1185 Mount Nyoigadake entry, the 1295 I-Hing entry, the 1361 Yamaguchi entry, and others)

The framework reads the phenomenological continuity as the catalogue's principal evidential pattern. The specific features recur across cultures that had no contact with one another and across centuries separated by enormous stretches of time, with regional vocabularies varying but the underlying phenomenology constant. The framework's adopted reading is that this pattern is best explained by the unitary-phenomenon hypothesis — a single class of underlying phenomenon described in the conceptual vocabularies of the times and places where it appeared.

### The Magonia tradition as theoretical pivot

The single most theoretically significant entry in the catalogue is the c. 815 Agobard of Lyon account of the "abductees from Magonia." Agobard's report describes townspeople who had captured (and were preparing to execute) three men and a woman who claimed to have arrived from "Magonia" via cloud-ships. Agobard, a sceptical bishop, recorded the account in *De Grandine et Tonitruis* in order to mock the belief and to defend the captives from execution. The account is preserved precisely because of Agobard's scepticism — a hostile witness whose disbelief becomes the means of transmission of the account.

Vallée's *Passport to Magonia* (1969) made this account the central pre-modern parallel to the modern UFO-abduction tradition. The structural parallels are striking: a class of beings arriving from elsewhere in non-conventional vehicles ("cloud ships" / "spacecraft"), with abduction phenomena, with a sceptical reception by the broader culture, and with a persistent folkloric and (later) experiential tradition. The framework's adopted reading is that the Magonia tradition preserves authentic operational content from a period of substantial Elohim or related presence — the tradition's persistence across many subsequent centuries (the European folkloric record of "fairy abduction" and related material develops from this substrate) and its phenomenological continuity with modern abduction accounts together constitute the framework's principal evidential argument for cross-period unitary-phenomenon reading.

### The pre-Columbian Americas problem

The catalogue's coverage of pre-Columbian American reports is essentially absent until the 1492 Columbus and 1499 Cabral entries — the European-discovery transition. This is a documentary-survival artifact: indigenous American oral and pictographic traditions preserve substantial aerial-encounter content, but the survival of pre-Columbian content into the European documentary base depended on post-conquest collection (which began only in the 16th century and which was substantially mediated by Spanish and Portuguese clerical agendas). The Vinland account (c. 1050) is the catalogue's sole substantial pre-Columbian entry, preserved through the Norse saga tradition. The catalogue's geographic shape would change substantially if pre-Columbian American content could be integrated; future editorial passes could expand in this direction.

## Open questions

The catalogue surfaces several methodological and substantive open questions.

- **The boundary between authentic preservation and folkloric embellishment.** For many entries, the line between an authentic report and folkloric or hagiographic elaboration is unclear. The Christian-tradition entries associated with saints (Saint Francis at La Verna, the Caravaca tradition, the Santiago de Compostela tradition) have substantial hagiographic interest at stake in the encounter content; reliable separation of the underlying observational content from the religious framing is difficult and may not be possible for some entries.
- **The natural-phenomenon hypothesis for specific entries.** Many entries are plausibly identifiable with specific natural phenomena. The 1054 daytime "bright circle" coincides with the SN 1054 supernova that produced the Crab Nebula and was widely observed across Eurasia; the 1178 Gervase of Canterbury "cosmic catastrophe" has been tentatively identified with the formation of the lunar crater Giordano Bruno; many catalogue entries describe meteors, comets, parhelia, lenticular clouds, ball lightning, or other identifiable natural phenomena. The catalogue does not attempt to filter such cases; the framework reads the aggregate catalogue as preserving genuine operational content alongside an unknown fraction of natural-phenomenon entries.
- **The source-criticism unevenness.** Some entries draw from well-attested source traditions (the Roman *prodigia* literature, Josephus, the Chinese imperial annals); others draw from late chronicles, monastic annals, or hagiographic compilations whose source-criticism status is weaker. A more developed catalogue would index entries by source-reliability tier; the present catalogue treats reliability uniformly with the understanding that aggregate patterns are more secure than individual case claims.
- **The pre-1500 cutoff's selectivity.** The catalogue stops at 1499 CE. The intermediate period (1500–1947 CE) contains substantial reports treated by Vallée-Aubeck in subsequent sections of *Wonders in the Sky* and elsewhere. A complementary catalogue extending into the early modern and modern periods would be a natural extension.
- **The framework's relationship to Vallée's interdimensional hypothesis.** Vallée's principal interpretive contribution (the interdimensional hypothesis, with its emphasis on consciousness, cultural-context-sensitivity, and possible non-physical dimensions) differs from the framework's neo-euhemerist position. The framework reads the encounters as operational Elohim presence specifically; Vallée reads them more broadly. A more developed treatment of the relationship between the two positions, with attention to the specific points of agreement and disagreement, would be valuable for the corpus's broader interpretive work.
