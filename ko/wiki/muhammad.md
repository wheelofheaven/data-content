**Muhammad ibn Abdullah** (Arabic <span class="arabic">مُحَمَّد ٱبْن عَبْد ٱللَّٰه</span>, *Muḥammad ibn ʿAbd Allāh*; c. 570 – 8 June 632 CE) was the Arabian prophet who founded the religious community that became **Islam**, the reciter of the **Qur'an** over a 22-year period of revelation (c. 610–632 CE), and the political leader who unified the Arabian Peninsula under the *ummah* polity during the last decade of his life. He is recognised by approximately one quarter of the contemporary human population — roughly 1.9 billion Muslims worldwide — as the final messenger (*khātam al-anbiyāʾ*, the "Seal of the Prophets") of the prophetic lineage that includes Ibrahim (Abraham), Musa (Moses), Dawud (David), Isa (Jesus), and the broader sequence of figures named in the Qur'an and the Hadith literature. Islam, the religious community he founded, is the second-largest religious tradition in the contemporary world after Christianity and is the principal religious affiliation of approximately fifty contemporary nation-states.

The Wheel of Heaven framework reads Muhammad as the **thirty-ninth of the forty prophets** the Raëlian source material describes the Elohim as having sent across human history (with Joseph Smith as the fortieth pre-Raël prophet, and Claude Vorilhon (Raël) himself as the fortieth and final messenger in the Raëlian counting). The framework reads several specific events in the Muhammad biography as preserving operational content about Elohim contact during the late **Age of Pisces** (the corpus's precessional reckoning places the Pisces period from c. 95 BCE through c. 2065 CE; Muhammad's prophetic career falls in the early portion of this age). The principal framework-relevant events include the **Cave of Hira revelation event** (c. 610 CE, with the *mal'akh* / *malak* Jibrīl as the named contact figure), the **Isrāʾ wa-l-Miʿrāj** (the Night Journey from Mecca to Jerusalem followed by an ascent through the seven heavens — operationally parallel to the Enoch and Elijah translation events), the **22-year revelation period** as a sustained-contact protocol, and Vorilhon's reported 1975 meeting with Muhammad among other prophetic figures on the Elohim planet during the Council of the Eternals account.

The corpus engages Muhammad as one of the operational-contact figures whose biographical record preserves authentic content about the Elohim's terrestrial activities, while recognising that the Islamic religious tradition's reception of Muhammad is the principal contemporary interpretive context within which his significance is understood, and that the framework's neo-euhemerist reading is one interpretive position alongside the broader religious-traditional reception. The entry treats the mainstream Islamic-tradition biographical content with the historical precision the subject warrants before developing the framework reading.

## Etymology of the name

The Arabic **<span class="arabic">مُحَمَّد</span>** (*Muḥammad*) derives from the triliteral root **<span class="arabic">ح-م-د</span>** (*ḥ-m-d*, "to praise"). The name is the passive participle in the intensive form (*Form II*), giving the meaning "the Praised One" or "He who is praised greatly." The same root also produces the related names ***Aḥmad*** (<span class="arabic">أَحْمَد</span>, "most praised"), ***Maḥmūd*** (<span class="arabic">مَحْمُود</span>, "praised"), and ***Ḥāmid*** (<span class="arabic">حَامِد</span>, "praising"), and the verbal noun ***ḥamd*** (<span class="arabic">حَمْد</span>, "praise") used liturgically in the Islamic phrase *al-ḥamdu li-llāh* ("praise be to God"). The Qur'an refers to Muhammad by the variant *Aḥmad* in Surah 61:6, in a passage Islamic tradition reads as Jesus's prophecy of the coming of Muhammad.

The English transliteration *Muhammad* is the standard form recommended by most contemporary scholarly conventions; older European literature uses *Mohammed*, *Mahomet* (medieval Latin), *Mahound* (medieval polemical), and various other forms. The corpus uses *Muhammad* as the standard transliteration.

Muhammad is the most common given name in the world, borne by approximately 150 million living individuals across the contemporary Muslim world and the diaspora. The name's commonness is itself a feature of the Islamic naming tradition, in which naming a son after the Prophet is regarded as a meritorious act.

## In Islamic tradition

The biographical record of Muhammad in the mainstream Islamic tradition is among the most extensively developed historical-biographical sequences of any religious founder. The principal source materials include the Qur'an itself (which is generally read in Islamic tradition as preserving Muhammad's own recitations of the revealed text rather than as a biography), the *Sīrah* literature (the principal biographies, beginning with **Ibn Isḥāq**'s *Sīrat Rasūl Allāh* c. 760 CE, surviving principally in the redaction of **Ibn Hishām** c. 833 CE), the *Maghāzī* literature on Muhammad's military campaigns, the **Hadith** collections compiled in the 9th century CE, and the **Tafsīr** (Qur'anic exegesis) tradition. The standard biographical chronology presented below is the consensus position of the Islamic tradition; specific details vary across the source materials, and modern academic historical-critical scholarship has substantively engaged the question of how reliably the late-source materials preserve early-7th-century historical content.

### Pre-Islamic Arabia and Muhammad's lineage

Muhammad was born in **Mecca** in approximately **570 CE** (the traditional "Year of the Elephant," named for the abortive Aksumite military expedition against Mecca led by the Ethiopian governor Abraha that the Islamic tradition dates to the same year). He was born into the **Banū Hāshim** clan of the **Quraysh** tribe — the dominant tribal group of Mecca and the custodians of the **Kaʿba** sanctuary at the centre of the city. The Quraysh had developed Mecca into a regional trade and pilgrimage centre across the previous several generations; Muhammad's grandfather **ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib** was the chief of the Banū Hāshim and a prominent Meccan civic figure.

Muhammad's father **ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib** died before his son's birth, possibly during a trading journey to Syria. His mother **Āminah bint Wahb** died when Muhammad was approximately six years old. Muhammad was raised first by his grandfather ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib (who died c. 578 CE when Muhammad was approximately eight) and subsequently by his paternal uncle **Abū Ṭālib**, who would remain his principal protector throughout his Meccan period until Abū Ṭālib's death in 619 CE.

As a young man, Muhammad worked in the Meccan trade networks, travelling on caravan journeys to Syria and other regional destinations. He developed a reputation in Meccan society for honesty and reliability, earning the epithets *al-Ṣādiq* ("the truthful") and *al-Amīn* ("the trustworthy"). At approximately age 25 (c. 595 CE), he was hired as a commercial agent by **Khadīja bint Khuwaylid**, a wealthy widow approximately 15 years his senior; their successful commercial collaboration led to Khadija's proposal of marriage. Muhammad and Khadija were married c. 595 CE and remained monogamously married until Khadija's death in 619 CE. The marriage produced several children, of whom four daughters (Zaynab, Ruqayyah, Umm Kulthūm, Fāṭimah) survived to adulthood; the two sons (al-Qāsim and ʿAbd Allāh) died in infancy.

### The Cave of Hira and the first revelation

In the years before his 40th birthday (i.e., approximately 605–610 CE), Muhammad developed a contemplative practice that involved periodic retreats to the **Cave of Hira** (<span class="arabic">غَار حِرَاء</span> *Ghār Ḥirāʾ*) on **Jabal al-Nūr** (<span class="arabic">جَبَل ٱلنُّور</span>, "the Mountain of Light"), approximately 3 km northeast of Mecca. The cave is a small natural opening — approximately 3.5 m long, 1.3 m wide at its widest point, and 2 m high — located near the summit of the mountain. Muhammad would retreat to the cave for periods of solitary meditation, typically during the month of Ramadan, returning to Mecca periodically for supplies.

In approximately **610 CE**, during one of these retreats, Muhammad experienced the **first revelation event** that the Islamic tradition treats as the inaugural moment of his prophetic career. The Hadith preserves the event as follows (in the version transmitted from Muhammad's later wife ʿĀʾisha through the chain of narrators preserved in *Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī* and *Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim*):

- The angel **Jibrīl** (<span class="arabic">جِبْرِيل</span>, the Arabic form of Gabriel; identified in Islamic tradition with the same figure who appears as Gabriel in the biblical narratives) appears to Muhammad in the cave
- Jibrīl commands Muhammad: ***Iqraʾ*** (<span class="arabic">ٱقْرَأ</span>, "Recite!" or "Read!")
- Muhammad replies that he cannot recite (*mā anā bi-qāriʾ*, "I am not a reciter" — the phrase has been interpreted variously, with much of the tradition treating it as referring to Muhammad's status as *ummī*, conventionally rendered "unlettered")
- Jibrīl seizes Muhammad and embraces him forcefully (*fa-ghaṭṭanī ghaṭṭan shadīdan*), then releases him, repeating the command
- The exchange is repeated three times
- After the third embrace, Jibrīl recites the verses now preserved as **Surah al-ʿAlaq** (Q 96:1–5):

> Recite in the name of your Lord who created — created man from a clinging clot. Recite, and your Lord is the most generous, who taught by the pen, taught man what he did not know.
>
> — Qur'an 96:1–5 (the first revealed verses, on the standard chronological reading)

The traditional date is the night of **27 Ramadan** (or alternatively 17, 19, 21, 23, or 25 Ramadan — the precise night is contested within the tradition) of the year corresponding to approximately August 610 CE in the Gregorian calendar. The night is known as **Laylat al-Qadr** (<span class="arabic">لَيْلَة ٱلْقَدْر</span>, "the Night of Decree" or "the Night of Power"), and is treated in Qur'an 97 as "better than a thousand months." Muhammad was at the time approximately 40 years old.

According to the Hadith, Muhammad descended from the cave shaken by the experience, hearing a voice from the sky addressing him as *Rasūl Allāh* ("Messenger of God") and a vision of Jibrīl spanning the horizon. He returned home to Khadija in distress, asking to be covered ("*zammilūnī, zammilūnī*"); Khadija comforted him and brought him to her cousin **Waraqa ibn Nawfal**, a Christian relative familiar with the earlier scriptures, who reportedly confirmed Muhammad's experience as a prophetic call.

Following the initial revelation, the tradition reports a *fatra* — a period during which revelation paused. Subsequent revelations resumed and continued at intervals across the next 22 years.

### The Meccan period (610–622 CE)

Muhammad's early preaching in Mecca, beginning approximately three years after the first revelation, emphasised **monotheism** (*tawḥīd*, the absolute unity of God), the moral judgment of the human soul after death, social justice and care for the poor, and Muhammad's status as a messenger in continuity with the earlier prophetic figures (Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus). The early Meccan community of followers grew slowly: the tradition lists Khadija as the first convert, followed by Muhammad's cousin and ward **ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib** (then approximately 10 years old), his adopted son **Zayd ibn Ḥāritha**, and his close friend **Abū Bakr** (a Meccan merchant who would become the first caliph after Muhammad's death).

The Meccan establishment increasingly opposed Muhammad's preaching, which was perceived as threatening the Kaʿba's status as a pilgrimage centre for the broader Arabian polytheistic tradition. The opposition intensified after Muhammad's public preaching began (c. 613 CE) and included verbal harassment, social ostracism, and physical persecution — particularly of socially marginal converts who lacked tribal protection. Several specifically named early converts were tortured for their adherence to Islam, including **Bilāl ibn Rabāḥ** (an Abyssinian slave subsequently emancipated by Abū Bakr) and the family of **Yāsir ibn ʿĀmir** (Muhammad's followers Sumayyah and ʿAmmār).

In response to the persecution, Muhammad authorised an emigration of approximately 80 followers to the Christian kingdom of **Aksum** (Ethiopia) c. 615–616 CE — the first *hijra*. The emigrants were welcomed by the Aksumite king (the *Najāshī*, traditionally identified with Aṣḥama ibn Abjar), who refused Meccan demands for their return.

The Meccan persecution intensified during the period 616–619 CE, with the Banū Hāshim and Banū al-Muṭṭalib subjected to an economic and social boycott. The boycott was eventually lifted, but the year 619 CE — known to subsequent Islamic tradition as the **Year of Sorrow** (*ʿĀm al-Ḥuzn*) — brought the deaths of both Khadija (Muhammad's wife of 24 years and his principal personal support) and Abū Ṭālib (his uncle and tribal protector). With Abū Ṭālib's death, Muhammad lost his Meccan tribal protection; the leadership of the Banū Hāshim passed to his uncle **Abū Lahab**, who was hostile to Muhammad's mission and withdrew the clan's protection.

### The Isrāʾ wa-l-Miʿrāj (the Night Journey and Ascension)

Among the most theologically and (in the framework's reading) operationally significant events of the Meccan period is the **Isrāʾ wa-l-Miʿrāj** (<span class="arabic">ٱلْإِسْرَاء وَٱلْمِعْرَاج</span>, "the Night Journey and Ascension"), traditionally dated to the year 621 CE (a year before the Hijra). The event is referenced briefly in Qur'an 17:1 ("Glory to Him who took His servant by night from the Sacred Mosque to the Farthest Mosque whose precincts We have blessed") and developed extensively in the Hadith and *Sīrah* literature.

The traditional narrative describes the event in two distinct phases:

**The Isrāʾ (Night Journey).** Muhammad is awakened at night by the angel Jibrīl and brought to a riding animal called **al-Burāq** (<span class="arabic">ٱلْبُرَاق</span>, "the lightning" or "the dazzling," etymologically from *barq* "lightning"), described in the Hadith as "a white animal smaller than a mule, larger than a donkey, that placed its hoof at the limit of its sight." Muhammad is carried by al-Burāq from Mecca to the *al-Masjid al-Aqṣā* ("the Farthest Mosque," identified in subsequent Islamic tradition with the Temple Mount in Jerusalem) — a journey of approximately 1,200 km accomplished in a single night.

**The Miʿrāj (Ascension).** From Jerusalem, Muhammad ascends through the **seven heavens**, meeting at each level a specific prophet of the previous tradition: traditionally identified as Adam in the first heaven, Yaḥyā (John the Baptist) and ʿĪsā (Jesus) in the second, Yūsuf (Joseph) in the third, Idrīs (commonly identified with Enoch) in the fourth, Hārūn (Aaron) in the fifth, Mūsā (Moses) in the sixth, and Ibrāhīm (Abraham) in the seventh. Beyond the seventh heaven, Muhammad reaches the **Sidrat al-Muntahā** ("the Lotus Tree of the Utmost Boundary") and the divine presence, where the five daily prayers (*ṣalāt*) are established for the Muslim community.

The Isrāʾ wa-l-Miʿrāj is theologically foundational for Islamic prayer practice and for the broader tradition's account of Muhammad's status. In the framework's reading, the event preserves operational content about a translation/ascent event comparable to the Enoch and Elijah translations in the Hebrew Bible — a topic developed in the framework section below.

### The Hijra and the Medinan period (622–632 CE)

In 622 CE, following the deterioration of Muhammad's position in Mecca and following negotiations with delegations from the city of **Yathrib** (located approximately 320 km north of Mecca), Muhammad authorised his followers to emigrate to Yathrib in stages, and finally undertook the migration himself with his companion Abū Bakr. The event — known as the **Hijra** (<span class="arabic">ٱلْهِجْرَة</span>, "the emigration") — became the foundational event of the Islamic calendar, with the year 622 CE designated **1 AH** (*Anno Hegirae*, "Year of the Hijra"). Yathrib was subsequently renamed **al-Madīnah** ("the city," short for *Madīnat al-Nabī*, "the City of the Prophet") and remains the second-holiest city in Islam.

In Medina, Muhammad established the **Constitution of Medina** (the *Ṣaḥīfat al-Madīnah*) — a foundational political document organising the relationships among the various tribes and religious communities (the Muslim emigrants from Mecca, the Medinan converts, the existing Medinan Jewish tribes, and other groups) under a single political framework. The Medina polity is widely treated in Islamic and academic scholarship as one of the earliest constitutional polities in the historical record.

The Medinan period was marked by increasing political and military conflict with the Meccan Quraysh:

- **Battle of Badr** (March 624 CE / 17 Ramadan 2 AH): the first major military engagement between the Muslim community and the Meccan Quraysh; a decisive Muslim victory despite numerical inferiority
- **Battle of Uhud** (March 625 CE / 3 AH): a Meccan counter-attack that produced a Muslim military setback; Muhammad himself was wounded
- **Battle of the Trench** (*Ghazwat al-Khandaq*, March-April 627 CE / 5 AH): a Meccan-led coalition siege of Medina, repulsed when the Muslims constructed a defensive trench around the city
- **Treaty of Hudaybiyya** (March 628 CE / 6 AH): a ten-year peace agreement between Muhammad and the Quraysh, granting the Muslims the right to perform pilgrimage to Mecca
- **Conquest of Mecca** (January 630 CE / 8 AH): following a Quraysh violation of the Hudaybiyya treaty, Muhammad led an army to Mecca, taking the city with minimal resistance; the Kaʿba was purified of polytheistic idols and rededicated as a centre of Islamic worship
- **Battle of Hunayn** (January 630 CE): a further engagement against tribes opposed to the Meccan capitulation; a Muslim victory
- **Tabuk Expedition** (October 630 CE / 9 AH): a Muslim military expedition toward the northern frontier with the Byzantine Empire; no engagement occurred
- **Farewell Pilgrimage** (March 632 CE / 10 AH): Muhammad's final pilgrimage to Mecca, including the delivery of the famous Farewell Sermon at Mount Arafat

Throughout the Medinan period, the Qur'anic revelations continued. The Medinan surahs are generally longer, more legislative in character, and more focused on community governance than the Meccan surahs (which tend to be shorter, more lyrical, and focused on monotheism and eschatology). The revelations continued through Muhammad's final illness; the final revealed verses are traditionally identified with Qur'an 5:3 ("Today I have perfected for you your religion and completed My favour upon you and approved Islam as your religion") or Qur'an 2:281.

### Death and succession

Muhammad died at **Medina on 8 June 632 CE** (12 Rabi al-Awwal, 11 AH), aged approximately 62, following a brief illness. He was buried at the site of his death, in the room of his wife ʿĀʾisha — a location now within the **al-Masjid al-Nabawī** (the Prophet's Mosque) in Medina, the second-holiest site in Islam.

Muhammad's death precipitated the **succession crisis** that ultimately divided the Muslim community into the **Sunni** and **Shia** traditions:

- The **Sunni** tradition holds that Muhammad did not formally designate a successor and that the community correctly selected **Abū Bakr** as the first **caliph** (*khalīfat rasūl Allāh*, "successor of the Messenger of God"), followed by ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb, ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān, and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (the four "Rightly Guided Caliphs," the *Rāshidūn*)
- The **Shia** tradition holds that Muhammad designated ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (his cousin and son-in-law) as his rightful successor at the **Ghadīr Khumm** event during the return from the Farewell Pilgrimage, and that the *imāmate* properly passed through ʿAlī's lineage

The succession dispute developed across the subsequent decades and became the principal historical-theological division of the Islamic tradition, persisting to the present.

### The Qur'an and the Hadith

The principal textual legacy of Muhammad is the **Qur'an** (<span class="arabic">ٱلْقُرْآن</span> *al-Qurʾān*, "the Recitation"), 114 surahs (chapters) ranging from very short (the shortest is 3 verses) to substantial (the longest, Surah 2 al-Baqarah, contains 286 verses). The Qur'an is held in Islamic tradition to be the literal word of God transmitted through Jibrīl to Muhammad in the Arabic language, preserved unchanged through the recitation tradition and the subsequent textual canonisation under the third caliph **ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān** approximately 20 years after Muhammad's death (c. 650 CE). The Uthmanic recension is the basis for all subsequent Qur'anic textual transmission, with the standard contemporary text essentially identical to the Uthmanic original (with minor variations across the *qirāʾāt* — the recognised recitation traditions).

The **Hadith** (<span class="arabic">ٱلْحَدِيث</span> *al-Ḥadīth*, "the Reports") tradition comprises the reported sayings, actions, and tacit approvals of Muhammad, transmitted through chains of narrators (*isnād*) and compiled in canonical collections in the 9th century CE. The principal **Sunni** collections — the *Kutub al-Sittah* ("the Six Books") — are: *Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī* (compiled by Muhammad ibn Ismāʿīl al-Bukhārī, d. 870 CE), *Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim* (compiled by Muslim ibn al-Ḥajjāj, d. 875 CE), and the four *Sunan* collections (*Sunan al-Nasāʾī*, *Sunan Abī Dāwūd*, *Jāmiʿ al-Tirmidhī*, *Sunan Ibn Mājah*). The principal **Shia** collections include *al-Kāfī* (compiled by al-Kulaynī, d. 941 CE), *Man Lā Yaḥḍuruhu al-Faqīh* (al-Ṣadūq, d. 991), *Tahdhīb al-Aḥkām* (al-Ṭūsī, d. 1067), and *al-Istibṣār* (al-Ṭūsī).

The Qur'an and the Hadith together constitute the *Sunnah* — the broader normative tradition deriving from Muhammad's prophetic mission — and form the textual foundation for **Islamic law** (*fiqh*) and the various Islamic legal schools (*madhāhib*) that developed in the centuries following Muhammad's death.

### Subsequent significance

Muhammad's significance in the subsequent Islamic tradition extends across virtually every aspect of Muslim life and thought. He is the model of personal conduct (*uswah ḥasanah*, "the beautiful example") whose specific practices are emulated in matters of worship, family life, and social conduct; the recipient of the final revelation that supersedes the previous Abrahamic scriptures while confirming their substantial authenticity; the foundational political leader whose Medinan polity model has informed Islamic political theory; the central figure of Islamic devotional life through the practice of *ṣalawāt* (the invocation of blessings on Muhammad in prayer); and the eschatological figure who will intercede for the Muslim community on the Day of Judgment (the *shafāʿah* tradition).

The contemporary Islamic world includes approximately 1.9 billion adherents across the Sunni majority (approximately 85-90%) and the Shia minority (approximately 10-15%), with smaller groups including the Ibadi, the Ahmadiyya (whose status as Muslims is contested in the broader tradition), and various Sufi orders that operate within or alongside the principal denominations.

## In the Wheel of Heaven framework

The framework's reading of Muhammad is developed within the broader Raëlian source-material account of the **forty prophets** the Elohim are described as having sent across human history. The Raëlian source material identifies Muhammad as one of these prophets and treats his prophetic career as part of the broader operational-contact lineage that the corpus tracks across the Hebrew Bible, the Christian New Testament, the Buddhist tradition, and the broader cross-cultural prophetic record. The framework's reading is developed in several connected sections below.

### Muhammad in the forty-prophets lineage

The Raëlian source material describes the Elohim as having sent approximately forty prophets across human history, each providing operational and ethical content appropriate to the specific time, place, and cultural context of their reception. The traditional Raëlian count places Muhammad as the **thirty-ninth** of the pre-Raël prophets, with **Joseph Smith** (founder of the Latter-day Saint movement, c. 1830 CE) as the fortieth pre-Raël prophet and Claude Vorilhon (Raël) himself as the fortieth and final messenger of the prophetic lineage (the "Seal" of the prophets in the Raëlian counting, paralleling the Islamic identification of Muhammad as the *khātam al-anbiyāʾ*).

The framework's adopted reading does not propose a definitive list of all forty prophets; the corpus's [List of prophets and religions](../list-of-prophets-and-religions/) catalogues the principal candidates across the major traditions with the framework's source-tier classification applied. The principal pre-Muhammad prophets the framework reads as historically attested operational-contact figures include: **Enoch** (antediluvian period), **Noah** (deluge period), **Abraham** (early second-millennium BCE, Age of Aries beginning), **Moses** (13th century BCE, Age of Aries middle period), **Elijah** (9th century BCE, Age of Aries late period — treated in the dedicated [Elijah](../elijah/) entry), **Ezekiel** (6th century BCE, Age of Aries-Pisces transition), **Zoroaster** (date contested, possibly mid-second millennium BCE or 6th century BCE), **the Buddha** (5th century BCE, early Age of Pisces), **Jesus** (1st century CE, early Age of Pisces) — treated in the dedicated [Jesus](../jesus/) entry — and **Muhammad** (7th century CE, mid Age of Pisces).

The framework reads the prophetic lineage as continuing across the precessional ages with content appropriate to the developmental stage of each period. Muhammad's role within the lineage is the bringing of the operational message to the Arabian peninsula and the broader Semitic-cultural region during the late 6th and early 7th centuries CE — a period in which the framework reads the broader operational-contact tradition as having developed its mature form across the Christian, Jewish, Zoroastrian, and other regional traditions, with the Islamic revelation extending the operational message to communities previously outside the principal Abrahamic textual lineage.

### The Cave of Hira revelation as operational contact event

The framework reads the **Cave of Hira revelation event** (c. 610 CE) as preserving authentic content about an operational contact event between Muhammad and an Elohim representative — the figure named in the Islamic tradition as Jibrīl (Gabriel). The framework's reading depends on several specific features of the canonical Hadith account:

- **The named messenger figure (Jibrīl).** The Arabic *Jibrīl* is the cognate of the Hebrew *Gavriʾel* (גַּבְרִיאֵל, "Mighty One of El"), the named *mal'akh* who appears in the biblical books of Daniel (8:16, 9:21) and Luke (1:19, 1:26). The framework reads the Jibrīl/Gabriel figure across the Hebrew, Christian, and Islamic traditions as preserving the operational identity of a specific Elohim representative or class of representative — possibly an individual operational agent across the three traditions, possibly a specific operational role with multiple agents at different times. The cross-tradition continuity of the name and the broader operational characteristics (messenger, herald of prophetic missions, appearance to selected human contacts) is read by the framework as one of the principal cross-traditional confirmations of the operational lineage.
- **The specific physical setting (a mountain cave).** The Cave of Hira on Jabal al-Nūr is consistent with the broader operational pattern the framework reads across the prophetic record: elevated, isolated, often cave-based contact locations (Moses at the cleft of the rock on Horeb, Elijah at the cave on Horeb, the Hebrew prophets often at "high places," the broader cross-cultural mountain-of-the-gods motif catalogued in the [List of mythemes and mythological motifs](../list-of-mythemes-and-mythological-motifs/)). The framework reads the recurring elevation-and-isolation pattern as operationally significant rather than as conventional religious topos — preserving content about the practical requirements of the operational contact protocol.
- **The physical pressure / embrace.** The Hadith account specifically reports that Jibrīl seized Muhammad and embraced him forcefully three times. The physical-contact element distinguishes the Hira event from purely auditory or visionary contact accounts and is operationally specific. The framework reads this as preserving authentic content about the contact event, with the specific operational purpose of the physical contact (transferring information directly, establishing a communications link, performing some operational preparation) left underdetermined by the canonical account.
- **The *iqraʾ* command.** The first command — "Recite!" or "Read!" — is operationally significant in the framework's reading. The framework reads the command as preserving content about the operational mode of the revelation: not a passive reception of a vision but an active recitation by Muhammad of content being delivered. The pattern is consistent with the broader operational pattern across the Hebrew prophetic tradition (the prophets typically deliver content in first-person form, indicating active reception and verbal transmission rather than passive recording).
- **The named witness (Khadija and Waraqa).** The Islamic tradition preserves the canonical narrative through Muhammad's report to Khadija and her subsequent consultation with Waraqa ibn Nawfal — a Christian relative familiar with the prior scriptural tradition. The framework reads the witness chain as supporting the operational reading: the event was reported immediately, was verified against the prior scriptural tradition by a knowledgeable contemporary, and was preserved through the chain of immediate witnesses rather than being reconstructed later from purely subjective material.

### The Isrāʾ wa-l-Miʿrāj as translation/ascent event

The framework's most operationally significant reading of the Muhammad biography concerns the **Isrāʾ wa-l-Miʿrāj** — the Night Journey from Mecca to Jerusalem followed by the ascent through the seven heavens. The framework reads the event as preserving operational content about a translation/ascent event comparable to the Enoch translation (Genesis 5:24), the Elijah translation (2 Kings 2:11), and the post-resurrection ascension of Jesus (Acts 1:9–11) — a recurring operational pattern in the broader prophetic record involving the bodily removal of human contact figures from terrestrial existence into the Elohim's broader operational sphere.

Several features of the Isrāʾ wa-l-Miʿrāj account support the operational reading:

- **The specific Mecca-Jerusalem journey.** The Islamic tradition is explicit that Muhammad travelled from Mecca to Jerusalem — a journey of approximately 1,200 km — and back in a single night. This is operationally specific in a way that purely visionary accounts typically are not. The framework reads the journey as a documented aerial transport event, with the specific craft (described in the tradition as **al-Burāq**) functioning as the operational vehicle.
- **Al-Burāq as operational craft.** The al-Burāq description — "a white animal smaller than a mule, larger than a donkey, that placed its hoof at the limit of its sight" — has been variously interpreted in the Islamic tradition. The "hoof at the limit of its sight" detail is operationally interesting: a vehicle whose movement is described as reaching "the limit of sight" with each step would, in modern operational vocabulary, be a vehicle moving at extreme speed. The framework reads the al-Burāq description as preserving operational content about an aerial craft, with the "animal" description reflecting the 7th-century Arabic conceptual vocabulary available to Muhammad for describing what he experienced.
- **The seven heavens structure.** The ascent through the seven heavens, with named prophetic figures at each level, is one of the most theologically developed accounts of any post-terrestrial location in the broader prophetic record. The framework reads the seven-heavens structure as preserving operational content about the broader Elohim spatial organisation, possibly corresponding to orbital and post-orbital locations or to the broader structure of the Elohim's operational sphere. The presence of the named prior prophets (Adam, Yaḥyā/John, ʿĪsā/Jesus, Yūsuf/Joseph, Idrīs/Enoch, Hārūn/Aaron, Mūsā/Moses, Ibrāhīm/Abraham) at the various levels is read by the framework as preserving content about the continuing presence of prior contact figures in the Elohim's broader sphere, consistent with the translation events recorded for Enoch and Elijah and consistent with the Vorilhon source material's account of meeting prior prophets on the Elohim planet (discussed below).
- **The Sidrat al-Muntahā.** The "Lotus Tree of the Utmost Boundary" beyond the seventh heaven is preserved in the Qur'an (53:13–18) as the specific location of Muhammad's reception of additional revelation. The framework reads the Sidrat al-Muntahā as the operational boundary of Muhammad's translation experience — possibly corresponding to the limit of the immediate operational sphere accessible from Earth, possibly to a specific operational location.

The framework's [List of close encounters](../list-of-close-encounters/) does not currently catalogue the Isrāʾ wa-l-Miʿrāj because the catalogue's scope ends in 1499 CE while the event is dated to c. 621 CE — well within the catalogue's pre-modern scope. The corpus's editorial pass on the close-encounters catalogue should include the Muhammad ascension as one of the principal early-medieval CE4/CE5-type events alongside the abductions of Elijah, Ezekiel, and the various intervening figures.

### The 22-year revelation period as sustained-contact protocol

The Qur'an's 22-year transmission period (c. 610–632 CE) represents an unusually sustained operational-contact relationship between Muhammad and his contact figures. The framework reads this duration as operationally significant in several ways:

- **The duration matches the typical operational-contact lifetime of other framework-identified prophets.** Moses's prophetic career extended approximately 40 years (the wilderness period); Elijah's documented prophetic career extended approximately 20 years; the Hebrew classical prophets (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel) typically operated across 30-50 year periods. The 22-year Muhammad period is consistent with the broader pattern.
- **The revelation episodes are described in the Islamic tradition with substantial operational specificity.** Different revelation contexts (the *asbāb al-nuzūl*, "occasions of revelation") are preserved in the Tafsīr tradition, describing the specific situations in which particular Qur'anic verses were delivered. The framework reads the *asbāb al-nuzūl* literature as preserving operational content about the contact pattern across the 22-year period: specific events triggered specific revelations, suggesting an ongoing dialogue-and-response relationship rather than a fixed prepared text being released in installments.
- **The continuing operational involvement.** Muhammad's reported continuing interaction with Jibrīl across the 22-year period — including the standard tradition that Jibrīl reviewed the entire revelation with Muhammad annually during Ramadan, and twice during the final Ramadan before Muhammad's death — supports the framework's reading of sustained operational contact rather than a single revelation event.

### The Vorilhon 1975 meeting with Muhammad

The framework's most direct source-material connection between Muhammad and the broader Raëlian operational account is Vorilhon's reported **1975 second-contact event** — the journey to the Elohim planet described in *Les extra-terrestres m'ont emmené sur leur planète* (1976). During this contact, Vorilhon describes being brought to a location identified as the **Council of the Eternals** (*le Conseil des Éternels*) and dining with several figures whom Yahweh identifies as the principal previous prophets of the broader Earth-mission lineage — including Jesus, Muhammad, the Buddha, Moses, and others.

The Vorilhon source material includes descriptions of the figures present at the Council of the Eternals meal. The descriptions are not always individually attributed to specific named figures in the surviving source material, but Vorilhon describes the table arrangement including: "a young bearded man, very handsome and very slim" with "a mysterious smile and an expression filled with fraternal feeling"; "to his right was a man with a noble face sporting a black beard that was very thick and very long"; "to his left was a more corpulent man with an Asian face. He had a shaven head." Subsequent Raëlian tradition has typically identified the young slim bearded man as Jesus, the noble-faced man with the thick black beard as Muhammad, and the corpulent man with the Asian face and shaven head as the Buddha, though the specific identifications in the original source material are mediated by Yahweh's broader statement that the assembled figures are the prior prophets rather than by individual one-to-one labelling.

The framework reads the Council of the Eternals account as supporting the broader operational pattern: the prior prophetic figures, having completed their terrestrial missions, are translated (in the Enoch-Elijah-Ascension pattern) into the Elohim's broader operational sphere where they continue to exist as ongoing operational presences. The Vorilhon account places Muhammad within this broader operational community of prior prophets — consistent both with the Islamic tradition's status of Muhammad as the final prophet of the Abrahamic lineage and with the framework's reading of the broader cross-traditional prophetic pattern.

### The Qur'an's preservation of operational vocabulary

The framework reads the Qur'an as preserving substantial operational vocabulary that parallels the operational vocabulary the corpus tracks across the Hebrew Bible. Several specific terms warrant attention:

- ***Malak* / *malāʾikah* (angels).** The Arabic *malak* (plural *malāʾikah*) is the direct Arabic cognate of the Hebrew *mal'akh* — "messenger" — that the corpus's broader work reads as preserving operational content about Elohim representatives or operational agents. The Qur'an's *malāʾikah* terminology preserves the same operational referent as the Hebrew *mal'akh* terminology, with Jibrīl as the principal individually-named agent across both traditions.
- ***Jinn*.** The Arabic *jinn* is the Qur'anic category of intelligent non-human beings, described as having been created from "smokeless fire" (Qur'an 55:15) and as having free will and moral responsibility paralleling that of humans. The framework's reading of the *jinn* is open: the category may preserve operational content about specific non-human contact entities, may reflect pre-Islamic Arabian traditional categories integrated into the Qur'anic worldview, may be a class of entity distinct from both humans and Elohim in the broader operational sphere, or some combination. The dedicated [Jinn](../jinn/) entry treats the question in more detail.
- ***Rūḥ* (spirit/breath).** The Arabic *rūḥ* is the direct cognate of the Hebrew *ruaḥ* — "spirit, breath, wind" — that the corpus tracks across the Hebrew Bible (particularly *ruaḥ Elohim* in Genesis 1:2 and elsewhere). The Qur'an's use of *rūḥ* terminology — particularly *al-Rūḥ al-Amīn* ("the trustworthy spirit," Qur'an 26:193) and *Rūḥ al-Qudus* ("the spirit of holiness," Qur'an 16:102 and elsewhere) for Jibrīl — preserves the operational continuity with the Hebrew *ruaḥ* tradition.
- ***Sakīna*.** The Arabic *sakīna* (Qur'an 2:248, 9:26, 9:40, 48:4, 48:18, 48:26) is the cognate of the Hebrew *shekhinah* — the "indwelling presence" that the Hebrew tradition associates with the Ark of the Covenant and the Tabernacle/Temple. The framework's reading of the Hebrew *shekhinah* (developed in the broader corpus work on the Ark of the Covenant) extends to the Qur'anic *sakīna*.
- ***Ḥijāb*** (in the operational sense of "veil" or "barrier" rather than the contemporary dress sense). The Qur'an's various uses of *ḥijāb* terminology, including in the context of the prophetic communications, preserve operational content about the conditions of contact between the human and Elohim spheres.

The framework reads the Qur'an's operational vocabulary as deeply continuous with the broader Hebrew-Christian operational vocabulary, with the Arabic forms providing additional philological evidence of the underlying operational referents that the framework's broader work tracks across the prophetic tradition.

## Connections to the broader framework

The Muhammad entry connects to several specific aspects of the broader corpus's interpretive work.

**The forty-prophets lineage.** Muhammad's position as the thirty-ninth of the forty prophets places him within the broader lineage the corpus tracks across the [List of prophets and religions](../list-of-prophets-and-religions/). The lineage's structural features — periodic operational contact with selected human figures across the precessional ages, with content appropriate to each period — are developed across the dedicated entries on the principal prophets.

**The cross-traditional prophetic continuity.** Muhammad's recognition of Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, David, Jesus, and other prior prophetic figures within the Qur'anic tradition is read by the framework as the Islamic tradition's preservation of the broader cross-traditional prophetic continuity that the corpus tracks. The Qur'an's specific treatments of these figures, while differing in detail from the Hebrew and Christian narratives, generally affirm the broader operational lineage and the recurring contact pattern.

**The translation/ascent pattern.** Muhammad's Isrāʾ wa-l-Miʿrāj joins the Enoch translation, the Elijah translation, and the post-resurrection ascension of Jesus as the principal documented translation/ascent events in the broader prophetic record. The framework reads the recurring pattern as evidence of a specific operational practice — the bodily translation of selected human contact figures into the Elohim's broader operational sphere — that recurs across the major traditions with sufficient consistency to support a unitary-phenomenon reading.

**The Jibrīl/Gabriel cross-tradition figure.** The named messenger figure who appears to Muhammad at Hira (Jibrīl) is the same operational agent who appears in the Hebrew Bible's Daniel narratives (Gabriel) and in the Christian New Testament's Annunciation narrative (Gabriel to Mary; Luke 1:26–38). The framework reads the cross-tradition Gabriel figure as one of the principal continuing operational agents across the three Abrahamic traditions, with operational responsibilities including the announcement of prophetic missions and the delivery of revelation content.

**The Age of Pisces context.** Muhammad's prophetic career falls in the early-to-middle Age of Pisces (the corpus's precessional reckoning places the Pisces period from c. 95 BCE through c. 2065 CE). The framework reads the Age of Pisces as the period of consolidation and broad cultural transmission of the operational message across the major civilisational regions — with the Christian tradition extending the message into the Mediterranean and European spheres (early Pisces), the Islamic tradition extending the message into the Arabian and broader Afro-Asian spheres (middle Pisces), and the subsequent developments (the medieval and early-modern transmission of the major traditions, the modern emergence of new operational-contact events) representing the continuing development of the Pisces-period operational pattern.

**The Council of the Eternals account.** The Vorilhon 1975 meeting with the assembled prior prophets, including Muhammad, is one of the principal source-material accounts of the continuing operational presence of the prior prophetic figures. The Council of the Eternals account is referenced across multiple corpus entries; the dedicated [Council of the Eternals](../council-of-the-eternals/) entry develops the operational reading in detail.

## 페트라 가설 (비판적으로 관여하는 수정주의)

캐나다 연구자 **댄 깁슨(Dan Gibson)**과 주로 결부되는 한 수정주의 고고학 가설은, 초기 이슬람의 원래 성지가 히자즈의 **메카**가 아니라 요르단 남부의 버려진 나바테아 수도 **페트라**였으며, 성소와 순례, 그리고 흑석(黑石)이 7세기 말과 8세기 초에 이르러서야 남쪽으로 이전되었다고 주장한다. 이 주장은 여러 갈래의 정황이 수렴하는 데 근거한다 — 최초의 모스크들이 대략 이슬람 첫 세기 동안 페트라를 향해 잡은 방향(*qibla*), 프톨레마이오스의 *지리학(Geography)*에 메카가 등장하지 않는다는 점, 알아즈라키가 기록한 카바의 불규칙한 사변형 측정치, 그리고 꾸란 아랍어와 바로 그 문자가 나바테아 아람어에서 유래했다는 점. 이 가설은 [하니피야](../hanafiyya/) — 가장 이른 사료들이 새 신앙의 자기 이해로 지목하는 "아브라함의 종교" — 와 연결되는데, 그 종교의 아브라함과 이스마엘의 제단을 페트라의 중심에 위치시킴으로써 그렇게 한다.

이 가설에 대한 프레임워크의 입장은 **채택이 아니라 관여**이며, 위에 서술한 이 항목의 주류 및 프레임워크 설명은 다시 쓰이지 않고 그 가설과 나란히 의도적으로 그대로 남겨져 있다. 전기적 내용 — 무함마드의 메카 탄생, 메카 인근의 히라 동굴, 메카–예루살렘 밤의 여행 — 은 이슬람 전통 자체의 서사와 그에 대한 프레임워크의 기존 독해를 반영한다. 깁슨의 가설은 논쟁적인 수정주의이다: 이슬람 천문학 사학자 데이비드 A. 킹(David A. King)은 이를 거부하며(그는 초기 키블라들을 페트라를 겨냥한 것이 아니라 카바를 향한 민속천문학적 "성스러운 방향" 정렬로 읽는다), 이 가설은 주류 이슬람학 합의 밖에 놓여 있고, 라엘리안 정경의 일부도 아니다. 코퍼스가 이를 다루는 까닭은, *만약* 그것이 성립한다면, 프레임워크가 이미 이슬람의 기원에서 읽어내고 있는 하니피야 복원에 구체적인 지리를 제공하기 때문이다 — 초기 이슬람을 물고기자리 시대의 아브라함적 회복 운동으로 보는 독해를 대체하는 것이 아니라 더 예리하게 다듬으면서 말이다. 키블라, 언어학, 반론 자료를 포함한 전면적 논의는 [페트라](../petra/)와 [나바테아인](../nabataeans/) 항목, 그리고 해설 기사 "[최초의 모스크들은 메카가 아니라 페트라를 향했다](/articles/the-first-mosques-faced-petra-not-mecca/)"에서 전개된다.

## Open questions

The Muhammad entry surfaces several open questions for the framework's broader interpretive work.

- **The specific operational technology of the Cave of Hira contact.** The Hadith account of the physical embrace by Jibrīl is operationally specific but underdetermined. The framework's reading treats this as preserving authentic content about the contact event without proposing a specific operational mechanism; the question of what the physical contact accomplished operationally (information transfer, communications-link establishment, some other function) remains open.
- **The al-Burāq description.** The specific identification of al-Burāq as an aerial craft is the framework's adopted reading, but the Islamic-traditional descriptions are ambiguous between literal animal, hybrid being, and operational vehicle interpretations. The framework's reading is open on the specifics of the al-Burāq's technological form.
- **The seven-heavens structure of the Miʿrāj.** The framework reads the seven heavens as possibly preserving operational content about the broader Elohim spatial organisation, but the specific mapping (whether the seven heavens correspond to orbital locations, to broader cosmological structure, to a phenomenological account of the ascent experience, or to some combination) is treated as open.
- **The status of the Qur'an as preserved revelation.** The framework reads the Qur'an as preserving authentic operational content from Muhammad's 22-year contact period, but the specific question of how the textual transmission preserved the operational content across the recitation tradition and the Uthmanic canonisation is treated as a separate question. The framework does not require either the strong Islamic-traditional position (the Qur'an as literally identical to the divine archetype) or the strong skeptical-academic position (the Qur'an as substantially edited and reformulated across the early period) — the framework's reading depends on the broad preservation of operational content rather than on specific textual-criticism conclusions.
- **The relationship between the framework's reading and the Islamic-traditional reception.** The framework's neo-euhemerist reading of Muhammad as one of forty operational-contact figures sits alongside the Islamic-traditional reception of Muhammad as the final messenger of God. The framework's adopted position does not require Muslim adherents to abandon their traditional understanding; rather, the framework reads the operational content of the Muhammad biography as continuous with the broader operational lineage the corpus tracks, while recognising that the traditional religious reception is the principal contemporary interpretive context for the figure.
- **The Joseph Smith position in the lineage.** The Raëlian source material's identification of Joseph Smith as the fortieth pre-Raël prophet (and consequently as Muhammad's immediate prophetic successor) is a specifically Raëlian position that diverges from the Islamic-traditional doctrine of Muhammad as the final prophet. The framework's adopted position on the question is open: whether the Smith identification preserves authentic operational content or reflects a specifically Raëlian interpretive framework is treated as an open question for further work.
