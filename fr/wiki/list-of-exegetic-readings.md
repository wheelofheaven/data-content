This entry catalogues the Wheel of Heaven framework's adopted neo-euhemeristic readings of religious, mythological, and traditional terms — its glossary of operational readings, presented as a navigable reference instrument. The corpus's interpretive method here is the one applied throughout the wiki and the long-form chapter material: each term's surface content is translated into operational terms consistent with the source material's account of the Elohim civilisation and the Earth project, on the working hypothesis that the relevant traditions preserve, in varying states of clarity, historical content that the operational translation recovers.

The catalogue serves three functions. First, it is a navigation index for the wiki's coverage of individual terms, with the short-form framework reading of each term pointing readers toward the dedicated entry where one exists. Second, it is a transparent statement of the framework's adopted positions on each term, presented at catalogue scale so that the corpus's interpretive method can be assessed against its full output rather than against single instances. Third, it is a reference instrument for the corpus's broader interpretive work, providing the operational vocabulary that the chapter material draws on.

The catalogue is paired with two other reference lists that together cover the corpus's interpretive instruments: the [List of mythemes and mythological motifs](../list-of-mythemes-and-mythological-motifs/), which catalogues recurring cross-cultural narrative patterns and the framework's readings of them, and the [List of etymological readings](../list-of-etymological-readings/), which catalogues specific philological-historiographic readings of individual words and phrases. The present catalogue covers the framework's readings of specific terms and referents rather than the structural motifs or the etymological roots; the three catalogues together constitute the framework's interpretive infrastructure at reference-list scale.

The reading on which the framework's interest in neo-euhemeristic exegesis depends is not contested. The neo-euhemeristic interpretive tradition exists as a documented current of modern thought, with substantial published literature going back at least to Brinsley Le Poer Trench's *The Sky People* (1960), and the framework is one specific instance within that tradition. What is contested is the substantive correctness of the readings themselves — whether the operational translations the framework offers do in fact recover the historical content of the relevant traditions, or whether the readings are post-hoc impositions of a contemporary interpretive frame on materials that were originally about something else. The corpus's adopted position is that the convergence between the framework's readings and the source material's specific account, combined with the cross-cultural coherence of the readings across traditions and the alignment with the broader corpus's interpretive synthesis, is more parsimoniously explained by the framework's reading than by the alternatives. The reading is open to revision; the catalogue below presents the corpus's currently adopted positions.

## Methodology

The neo-euhemeristic interpretive method has a specific philosophical history and a specific contemporary lineage that the catalogue draws on. Both deserve explicit treatment, since the method's substantive content depends substantially on its lineage.

### Classical euhemerism

The term *euhemerism* derives from the Greek mythographer **Euhemerus of Messene** (c. 330 – c. 260 BCE), whose lost work *Hiera Anagraphē* (*Sacred Inscription*) developed the interpretive hypothesis that the gods of Greek mythology had been historical kings and benefactors of humanity, deified by their grateful subjects after their deaths and gradually transformed by tradition into the supernatural figures of subsequent religious life. Euhemerus's specific framework — that the gods were deified mortals — was widely discussed in late antiquity and adopted by several early Christian apologists as a polemic against pagan religion (the gods of the pagans had been mere humans; the God of Christianity was the true and only deity). The reception was contested but the method survived, and the term *euhemerism* became the standard name for any interpretive approach that reads myths about gods as mythologised accounts of historical persons.

The classical method has several characteristic features. It treats myth as historically derivative rather than as primary religious discourse. It identifies specific historical referents (persons, places, events) behind mythological figures and narratives. It accounts for the supernatural content of myths as the product of tradition's elaboration rather than as the original content. And it preserves the truth-value of the myth at the historical level while declining the truth-value at the supernatural level. These features carry forward into the contemporary neo-euhemeristic tradition.

### Neo-euhemerism

*Neo-euhemerism*, sometimes called *ufological euhemerism* in the academic literature on the ancient-astronaut discourse, is the modern interpretive tradition that extends euhemerism by identifying the historical referents of religious and mythological content not as deified mortals but as visiting non-terrestrial intelligences. The neo-euhemeristic position is that the gods of various traditions were historical, that their reported actions were operational rather than supernatural, and that the historical referents are recoverable through careful operational re-reading of the relevant texts.

The modern neo-euhemeristic lineage that the framework draws on has several principal figures:

- **Brinsley Le Poer Trench**, 8th Earl of Clancarty, published *The Sky People* (1960) — among the earliest modern works to read Genesis specifically as a neo-euhemeristic document, identifying the Elohim of Genesis 1 as one creator-civilisation and Jehovah of Genesis 2 as another, and proposing that the contradiction between the two Genesis creation accounts reflects two different creator interventions rather than a literary aporia. Trench's work predates both Sendy and von Däniken and is the earliest substantial work in the modern lineage.

- **Jean Sendy** published *La Lune, clé de la Bible* (*The Moon: Outpost of the Gods*, 1968), *Ces dieux qui firent le ciel et la terre* (*Those Gods Who Made Heaven and Earth*, 1969), and *L'Ère du Verseau* (*The Age of Aquarius*, 1970) — the foundational works of the specifically philological-historiographic wing of the neo-euhemeristic tradition. Sendy's method is more textually careful than the broader ancient-astronaut literature; he reads the Hebrew Bible's specific lexical choices, syntactic features, and narrative details for their operational content, identifying *Elohim* as a plural creator-civilisation, Genesis 1 as a compressed account of the Earth project's preparatory and biological phases, and the broader Hebrew tradition as preserving authentic content despite subsequent theological transformation. Sendy is the corpus's principal scholarly antecedent.

- **Erich von Däniken** published *Erinnerungen an die Zukunft* (*Chariots of the Gods?*, 1968), which became the popular ancient-astronaut tradition's most widely read text. Von Däniken's approach is more associative and less philologically careful than Sendy's, but its global cultural impact established the ancient-astronaut interpretive frame as a recognised position in popular discourse. The framework's adopted positions are substantially more aligned with Sendy than with von Däniken, though the broader cultural recognition of the neo-euhemeristic frame derives substantially from von Däniken's work.

- **Zecharia Sitchin** published *The 12th Planet* (1976) and twelve subsequent volumes in the *Earth Chronicles* series, developing a specifically Sumerian-source neo-euhemerism that identifies the Anunnaki of the Mesopotamian sources as the creator civilisation. Sitchin's lexical and philological claims are substantially contested within Assyriological scholarship, and the framework's adopted readings differ from Sitchin's on several specific points (most importantly, the framework reads humanity as a Late-Pleistocene synthesis from the Age of Leo, not as a Sitchin-style modification of an existing hominid stock from a much earlier period). The Sitchin tradition is nonetheless part of the broader neo-euhemeristic lineage and informs the corpus's broader interpretive work.

- **Claude Vorilhon (Raël)** published *Le Livre qui dit la vérité* (1974), *Les extra-terrestres m'ont emmené sur leur planète* (1976), and subsequent volumes, collectively the Raëlian source material. Vorilhon's text differs from the rest of the neo-euhemeristic lineage in two important respects: it is presented as a first-person contact narrative rather than as a textual reconstruction, and it provides a substantial body of specific operational content (about the Elohim civilisation, the cosmic chain, the future history of humanity, the Earth project's organisation) that the textual-reconstruction tradition can only partially access. The Raëlian source material is the corpus's principal source.

- **Mauro Biglino**, an Italian biblical translator who worked on Catholic Church publications in the 1990s and early 2000s before publishing independent neo-euhemeristic work from approximately 2010 onward, has developed a strict-philological neo-euhemerism that reads the Hebrew Bible without theological overlay. Biglino's principal works include *La Bibbia non è un libro sacro* (*The Bible Is Not a Sacred Book*, 2012) and *La Bibbia non parla di Dio* (*The Bible Does Not Speak of God*, 2015). The framework draws on Biglino's specific philological readings of Hebrew terms and his strict-operational interpretive method.

- **Paul Wallis**, a former Anglican Archdeacon who left the church in approximately 2020 to develop a neo-euhemeristic reading of the Hebrew Bible from a Christian-clerical interior perspective, has published *Escaping from Eden* (2020), *The Scars of Eden* (2021), and subsequent works developing a specifically Christian-engaged version of the neo-euhemeristic method. Wallis's contribution is the application of the method to the broader Christian textual tradition, including the New Testament.

The framework's adopted positions draw substantially from Vorilhon (as primary source) and Sendy (as principal scholarly antecedent), with selective use of Biglino and Wallis on specific philological and theological points and selective engagement with the broader Trench–von Däniken–Sitchin tradition where their specific readings align with the framework's broader synthesis.

### The framework's interpretive method

The framework's neo-euhemeristic exegesis has six characteristic moves that the catalogue applies systematically across its rows.

**First, de-supernaturalisation.** Each term's supernatural content is re-read as an operational description of activities performed by the Elohim civilisation. The "speaking the world into existence" of Genesis 1 is re-read as the design-and-synthesis programme; the "burning bush" of Exodus 3 is re-read as a specific Elohim-presence event; the "heavenly host" of various traditions is re-read as the council of the Elohim. The de-supernaturalisation move is the foundational interpretive operation of the entire method.

**Second, operational translation.** The de-supernaturalised content is translated into the operational vocabulary of contemporary science and engineering, where appropriate. The creation of humanity is translated into the contemporary vocabulary of synthetic biology and synthetic genomics (treated in those entries); the deluge is translated into the operational vocabulary of orbital intervention; the various theophanies are translated into the operational vocabulary of localised technological encounter.

**Third, cross-source synthesis.** Each term's reading is checked against the broader corpus's interpretive synthesis — against the Raëlian source material, against the comparative-religion data, against the historical and archaeological record, against the contemporary scientific picture. A reading is adopted when it coheres with the broader synthesis; readings that would require revision of the broader synthesis are flagged for further consideration.

**Fourth, preservation of authentic content.** The framework reads each tradition as preserving authentic historical content, even where the surface theological frame has substantially transformed that content. The interpretive move is to identify the preserved content rather than to dismiss the tradition as inauthentic. Where a tradition's content cannot be operationally translated, the framework reads this either as authentic content the framework has not yet successfully recovered, or as content that originates in non-Elohim sources (cultural-syncretic, theological-development, or otherwise).

**Fifth, registration of source attribution.** Each reading in the catalogue is attributed to the specific point in the framework lineage where it originates. The catalogue's *Source* column registers whether a given reading is foundational to Vorilhon, originates with Sendy, is adopted from Biglino or Wallis, derives from the broader corpus's interpretive synthesis, or constitutes a framework-original reading not previously developed in the lineage.

**Sixth, epistemic humility.** The framework readings are best-fit hypotheses about what each term preserves. The catalogue's adopted positions are open to revision; alternative readings within the framework's broader interpretive space are recognised where they exist.

### What the catalogue does not claim

Three things the catalogue does not claim deserve explicit statement.

The catalogue does not claim that the framework's readings exhaust the meaning of the relevant terms. Many religious and mythological terms carry substantial spiritual, ethical, and cultural meaning beyond their operational-historical content; the framework's readings address the operational-historical content specifically and leave the other dimensions to the traditions' practitioners and to the broader religious-studies literature.

The catalogue does not claim that traditions whose terms are read on the framework as preserving authentic content are thereby authoritative in their subsequent development. The framework reads many traditions as authentic in origin but substantially transformed by interpretive development; the catalogue's readings concern the original content rather than the subsequent transformation.

The catalogue does not claim methodological uniformity across all the readings. The strength of the framework's confidence varies substantially across the entries below. Some readings are foundational to Vorilhon and well-supported by the corpus's broader synthesis; others are tentative framework-original readings that may require revision. The catalogue presents the adopted readings without uniformly distinguishing the confidence level, but the source-attribution column gives readers a way to assess the readings' provenance.

## Structural patterns the catalogue surfaces

The catalogue's distribution of readings surfaces several structural patterns that the framework's interpretive work depends on.

### The clustering of readings around specific source-material content

The framework readings cluster heavily around several specific source-material claims: the Elohim's plural-creator structure (multiple entries across the cosmological and persons categories), the seven-phase Earth project organisation (the *yamim* readings in the cosmological category), the cosmic-competition pattern (the Lucifer/Satan and serpent readings), the deluge-and-preservation pattern (the Noah and ark readings), the cultural-transmission programme (the patriarchal and Mosaic readings), the Aquarian-age disclosure pattern (the eschatological readings). The clustering is not arbitrary — it reflects the source material's principal content areas, which the catalogue's framework readings systematically engage.

### The pre-existing referents the framework recovers

A striking pattern across the catalogue is that the framework readings typically translate terms into operational referents that *already exist* in the contemporary scientific and engineering landscape, rather than into hypothetical or speculative referents. Creation translates into the contemporary discipline of synthetic biology (treated in the [life engineering](../life-engineering/) and [synthetic biology](../synthetic-biology/) entries). Resurrection translates into the operational capability of biological reconstitution from preserved genetic material (a capability whose lower bound is now demonstrated in contemporary terrestrial science). Heaven translates into the originating planet of the Elohim civilisation (a referent consistent with contemporary astrobiology and exoplanetary science). The framework reads this pattern as one of the structural features supporting the broader interpretive synthesis — the readings are not arbitrary metaphorical translations but operational translations into referents whose existence is independently demonstrated by contemporary scientific work.

### The preservation pattern across traditions

The catalogue's coverage extends across many traditions (Hebrew, Christian, Islamic, Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Vedic, broader Indo-European, Mesoamerican, and others). The framework readings show that the same specific operational content is preserved, in varying states of transformation, across many of these traditions independently. The cross-traditional preservation pattern is the basis of the framework's broader claim that the source material is recovering historical content rather than constructing a contemporary interpretive frame — the convergence is more parsimoniously explained by shared historical reference than by parallel modern interpretation.

### The relationship to the etymological and motif catalogues

The present catalogue is structurally complementary to the [List of etymological readings](../list-of-etymological-readings/) and the [List of mythemes and mythological motifs](../list-of-mythemes-and-mythological-motifs/). The etymological catalogue addresses the philological-historiographic level: what individual words in the original languages mean, recovered through careful philology and operational re-reading. The mythemes catalogue addresses the structural-comparative level: what recurring cross-cultural narrative patterns preserve. The present catalogue addresses the referential level: what specific terms refer to operationally. The three catalogues together constitute a triple instrument for the framework's interpretive work, with each providing access to a distinct layer of the underlying material.

## Open questions

The catalogue surfaces several methodological and substantive open questions.

- **The boundary between authentic preservation and post-hoc interpretation.** The framework's readings are claimed to recover authentic historical content from the relevant traditions. The risk inherent in any interpretive method is that the readings impose a contemporary frame on materials that were originally about something else. The framework distinguishes itself from arbitrary post-hoc interpretation through three commitments: the readings align with specific source-material content rather than with general interpretive convenience; the readings cohere across many traditions independently rather than requiring tradition-by-tradition adjustment; and the readings translate into operational referents that exist independently in the contemporary scientific landscape rather than into speculative or merely metaphorical referents. The cumulative weight of these commitments, on the framework's reading, supports the authentic-preservation interpretation; the question remains open whether the commitments succeed in distinguishing the framework's readings from sophisticated post-hoc imposition.
- **The unevenness of confidence across the catalogue.** Some readings in the catalogue are foundational to Vorilhon, well-supported by the corpus's broader synthesis, and convergent with multiple independent lines of evidence (the Elohim reading, the *yom*-as-precessional-age reading, the deluge reading). Other readings are more tentative — framework-original readings, single-source readings, or readings whose specific operational content is partly speculative. The catalogue presents the adopted positions without uniformly distinguishing confidence levels; the source-attribution column gives readers a way to assess provenance, but a more developed confidence-coding system might be valuable in future editorial passes.
- **The treatment of competing framework readings.** For several terms in the catalogue, the broader neo-euhemeristic tradition has produced multiple distinct framework readings (the Sitchin-tradition reading of the Anunnaki versus the Sendy-tradition reading of the Elohim; the Sitchin-tradition reading of human modification from a hominid stock versus the Raëlian reading of de novo human synthesis; the various readings of specific Genesis chapters). The catalogue's adopted positions reflect the corpus's specific synthesis but do not always engage the competing readings in detail. A more developed treatment would map the framework readings within the broader interpretive space.
- **The integration of contemporary scientific advance.** Several readings in the catalogue translate religious-mythological content into operational referents in contemporary scientific disciplines (synthetic biology, synthetic genomics, etc.). As those disciplines continue to advance — as the contemporary capability extends toward the scale the source material attributes to the Elohim — the operational referents of the framework readings will themselves change. The catalogue is to that extent a snapshot of the readings under current scientific conditions, and is open to revision as those conditions develop.
- **The treatment of non-Abrahamic traditions.** The catalogue's coverage is more developed for the Hebrew Bible and broader Abrahamic traditions than for the Vedic, Egyptian, Mesoamerican, and other major traditions. The framework readings for those traditions exist in the corpus's broader interpretive work but are not yet systematically catalogued at the same level of granularity. The catalogue is open to substantial expansion in this direction.
