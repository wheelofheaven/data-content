## Etymology and naming

### "Paradise" — from Old Persian "pairi-daēza"

The English *paradise* derives from Greek παράδεισος (*paradeisos*), itself a loanword from Old Persian *pairi-daēza* (*pairi-*, "around"; *daēza*, "wall"), meaning "enclosed garden" or "walled park." The Persian term originally designated the royal hunting parks and pleasure gardens of the Achaemenid Empire — substantial enclosed landscapes featuring trees, water features, and assembled wildlife. The Hebrew Bible adopts the loanword as פַּרְדֵּס (*pardes*) in late biblical and post-biblical Hebrew (Nehemiah 2:8; Ecclesiastes 2:5; Song of Songs 4:13), designating substantial garden-orchard content.

The Septuagint Greek translation renders the Hebrew גַּן (*gan*, "garden") of Genesis 2-3 with παράδεισος (*paradeisos*) — establishing the substantial Greek and subsequent Latin/European tradition of treating the Garden of Eden as substantial "paradise." Subsequent Christian theological development extended *paradeisos* to designate:

- The substantial Genesis 2-3 Garden of Eden (the originary paradise)
- The substantial intermediate post-mortem state of the righteous (Luke 23:43)
- The substantial substantial eschatological New Jerusalem state (Revelation 2:7, 22:1-2)

The Arabic فِرْدَوْس (*firdaws*) preserves the substantial Persian/Greek etymology and designates the highest level of Islamic *Jannah* (paradise).
