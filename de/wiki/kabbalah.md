The **Kabbalah** (Hebrew: קַבָּלָה, *Qabbalah*, "received tradition") is the principal Jewish mystical-esoteric tradition — a body of teachings on cosmology, theology, anthropology, and ethical-spiritual practice transmitted across approximately three millennia within Jewish religious-textual tradition and developed through several distinct historical-textual phases. The tradition's own self-designation registers its character: *Kabbalah* derives from the Hebrew root **קבל** (*q-b-l*, "to receive"), with the term naming the body of teachings received and transmitted across the **chain of tradition** (*shalshelet ha-Kabbalah*). The Kabbalistic textual corpus comprises several principal works developed across the historical phases: the foundational ***Sefer Yetzirah*** (Book of Formation) of late antiquity; the medieval ***Sefer ha-Bahir*** (Book of Brightness, late 12th century); the ***Zohar*** (Book of Splendor, late 13th century, attributed to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai but composed principally by Moses de León in Castile); the **Lurianic synthesis** of sixteenth-century Safed (developed by Isaac Luria and transmitted principally through Hayyim Vital); the **Hasidic developments** of eighteenth-century Eastern Europe; and various twentieth-century academic and renewal phases.

The Kabbalah's principal cosmological framework articulates the relationship between the transcendent infinite divine source — the ***Ein Sof*** (אֵין סוֹף, "without end" or "the infinite") — and the created world through ten emanations called ***sephirot*** (סְפִירוֹת, singular *sephirah*, "enumeration" or "counting"), operating across four worlds (***olamot***: *Atziluth*, *Beriah*, *Yetzirah*, *Asiyah*). The ten *sephirot* are: **Keter** (Crown), **Chokhmah** (Wisdom), **Binah** (Understanding), **Chesed** (Mercy / Loving-kindness), **Gevurah** (Severity / Strength), **Tiferet** (Beauty / Harmony), **Netzach** (Eternity / Victory), **Hod** (Splendor / Glory), **Yesod** (Foundation), and **Malkhut** (Kingdom / Sovereignty). They are arranged within the diagram known as the ***Etz Chaim*** (Tree of Life), with three pillars (left, right, central) and twenty-two paths connecting the *sephirot* — paths corresponding to the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet. The Lurianic synthesis added the framework of cosmic catastrophe (*shevirat ha-kelim*, "the breaking of the vessels") and cosmic repair (*tikkun olam*, "repair of the world"), articulating the cosmos as the result of an originating crisis whose ongoing repair is the proper work of human beings within the cosmic order.

The Wheel of Heaven framework registers the Kabbalah as **"the closest book to the truth"** of any religious tradition — the most pointed framework-positive assessment given to any single religious-textual tradition in the corpus. The Yahweh-articulated source position: *"It is not only in the Bible and the Gospels that there are traces of the truth; testimonies can be found in practically every religion. The Kabala especially is one of the richest in testimonies."* The framework's distinctive analytical contribution articulates several specific features of the Kabbalistic tradition that, on the corpus's reading, preserve actual alliance-transmitted content with notable fidelity. The Kabbalistic *sephirot* cosmology preserves an articulated **structured plurality** of distinct emanations with specific attributes, functions, and relationships — a cosmology that, on the framework's reading, "maps with surprising accuracy onto the actual alliance structure: multiple specific beings with specific functions, collectively constituting the 'divine' from the human perspective but distinct as individuals within their own civilization." The Kabbalistic chain-of-tradition (*shalshelet ha-Kabbalah*) framing preserves a substantial historical claim about the antiquity of the tradition — reaching back through Moses to prehistoric origins, in the articulation of Alexandre Safran (Chief Rabbi of Geneva) that Jean Sendy's work adopts: *"The Kabbalah exceeds, in antiquity, the Revelation of Sinai; it dates back to prehistoric times. Moses only introduced it into the history of Israel."* The Kabbalistic esoteric-transmission protocol — the careful pedagogical-initiatic transmission within specific Jewish circles across the medieval and early modern centuries — preserved the content against the kind of distortion that more widely distributed traditions often suffered under the pressure of strict monotheism.

## Etymology and naming

The tradition has several principal designations operating across multiple linguistic-religious contexts.

### "Kabbalah" as principal designation

The English term **Kabbalah** is the principal scholarly-academic and contemporary religious-traditional designation. The term derives directly from the Hebrew **קַבָּלָה** (*Qabbalah*).

The Hebrew **קַבָּלָה** (*Qabbalah*) is a verbal noun derived from the Hebrew root **קבל** (*q-b-l*, "to receive"). The noun's literal sense: "reception" or "received tradition" or "that which is received." The term thereby names the tradition by its principal mode of transmission rather than by its content — registering the pedagogical-initiatic character of the tradition, in which the teaching is received from a teacher rather than independently discovered.

The Hebrew root **קבל** has substantial semantic depth:
- **קבל** as "to receive" (the principal verb)
- **קבלה** as "reception, receipt, tradition" (the principal noun)
- The same root produces the modern Hebrew קַבָּלָן (*kabbalan*, "contractor"), through the substantive sense of one who "receives" a contract
- The same root produces קבלת שבת (*kabbalat Shabbat*, "receiving the Sabbath"), the substantive Friday-evening liturgy

The Kabbalistic tradition's own self-articulation through the *q-b-l* root registers the tradition as essentially received — passed down from teacher to student across the chain of tradition rather than constructed through individual rational reflection. This contrasts with the broader rationalist Jewish-philosophical tradition (Maimonides and the broader medieval Jewish philosophical tradition) which operated principally through rational discursive engagement.

### "Cabala" / "Cabbala" as older European spelling

The variant **Cabala** (or **Cabbala**) operates as the older European-language spelling, principally derived from the Latin transliteration tradition. The variant has substantial Renaissance-and-early-modern usage, particularly within the Christian Kabbalah / Cabala tradition (Pico della Mirandola, Johannes Reuchlin, the broader Renaissance Christian engagement). The Sendy works (composed in French) preserve the older European spelling **Cabale** consistently.

### "Qabalah" as Hermetic / Western-esoteric spelling

The variant **Qabalah** (with initial *Q*) operates principally within the Hermetic / Western-esoteric tradition, registering the substantial appropriation of Kabbalistic content within broader Hermetic-magical-occultist framing across the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries (Mathers, Crowley, the broader Golden Dawn tradition). The substantial difference in spelling registers the substantial difference in tradition-positioning: **Kabbalah** for the Jewish-traditional content, **Cabala** for the Christian-traditional content, **Qabalah** for the Hermetic-esoteric content.

### Hebrew internal designations

Within the Jewish tradition, the Kabbalah has several substantial internal designations:

- **קַבָּלָה** (*Qabbalah*) — the principal designation registered above
- **שַׁלְשֶׁלֶת הַקַּבָּלָה** (*shalshelet ha-Qabbalah*, "the chain of Tradition") — the substantial designation registering the tradition's transmissional character
- **תּוֹרַת הַסּוֹד** (*torat ha-sod*, "the secret teaching") — registering the esoteric-confidential character
- **חָכְמָה פְּנִימִית** (*chokhmah penimit*, "inner wisdom") — registering the esoteric-internal character
- **חָכְמַת הַנִּסְתָּר** (*chokhmat ha-nistar*, "wisdom of the hidden") — alternative designation registering the esoteric character
- **מַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה** (*ma'aseh merkavah*, "work of the chariot") — designation registering the substantial Ezekiel-vision-tradition content
- **מַעֲשֵׂה בְּרֵאשִׁית** (*ma'aseh bereshit*, "work of creation") — designation registering the substantial Genesis-creation-tradition content

The various Hebrew internal designations register different aspects of the broader tradition: *Qabbalah* the transmissional aspect; *torat ha-sod* the esoteric-confidential aspect; *ma'aseh merkavah* and *ma'aseh bereshit* the substantial early-Jewish-mystical content within the broader tradition.

## Conventional understanding

The Kabbalah has substantial mainstream scholarly engagement across several distinct disciplines: Jewish studies, history of religion, comparative mysticism, philosophy, and various adjacent fields.

### The historical-developmental phases

Mainstream scholarship articulates the Kabbalah's development across several substantial historical phases.

**The foundational period (late antiquity)**. The earliest Kabbalistic-tradition texts emerge in late antiquity. The principal text from this period is the **Sefer Yetzirah** (Book of Formation), composed sometime between the second and the sixth centuries CE (scholarly dating substantially debated). The *Sefer Yetzirah* presents a brief but dense cosmological text articulating the creation of the world through "thirty-two paths of wisdom" — the ten *sephirot* (the term's first attested usage in this context) and the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet. The *Sefer Yetzirah* operates as the foundational text for subsequent Kabbalistic development, with substantial commentary tradition extending across approximately fifteen centuries.

The broader late-antique Jewish mystical context includes the **Hekhalot literature** (literature of the heavenly halls), composed principally between the second and the seventh centuries CE, which describes mystical ascent through seven heavenly palaces to behold the divine throne. The Hekhalot literature operates as substantial prefigurement of the subsequent Kabbalistic tradition, with various specific motifs (the heavenly journey, the divine throne-vision, the encounter with angelic beings) preserved across the subsequent tradition.

**The medieval emergence (12th-13th centuries)**. The Kabbalah as a distinct identifiable tradition emerges principally in southern France (Provence) and northern Spain (Catalonia, then Castile) in the late twelfth and thirteenth centuries. The principal texts:

- **Sefer ha-Bahir** (Book of Brightness, late 12th century, anonymous): the substantial early-medieval text articulating the *sephirotic* structure with substantial mythical-symbolic content
- **The Provençal Kabbalists** (12th century): including Isaac the Blind (c. 1160-1235) and his teaching circle, who developed the early-medieval *sephirotic* theology
- **The Geronese school** (early 13th century): including Azriel of Gerona, Ezra of Gerona, and Nahmanides (Moses ben Nahman, 1194-1270), who developed substantial systematic Kabbalistic theology
- **The Castilian school** (late 13th century): including Moses de León (c. 1240-1305), the principal author of the *Zohar*

**The Zohar**. The ***Zohar*** (Book of Splendor, late 13th century) is the principal Kabbalistic text — substantial multi-volume corpus written principally in Aramaic and presented as the teachings of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai (a 2nd-century Tannaitic rabbi) and his disciples. Mainstream scholarship (following Gershom Scholem's foundational analysis) attributes the principal authorship to Moses de León in late-13th-century Castile, with the *Zohar* operating as substantial creative pseudepigraphic composition rather than as authentic 2nd-century material. The *Zohar*'s structure: a running commentary on the Pentateuch (with substantial systematic engagement with the Genesis through Deuteronomy material), supplemented by various distinct sections (the *Idra Rabba*, the *Idra Zuta*, the *Sifra di-Tzniuta*, various others) and substantial homiletic-narrative material.

**The Lurianic synthesis (16th century)**. The substantial Lurianic Kabbalah developed principally in sixteenth-century **Safed** (in the Galilee region of the Land of Israel), through the teaching of **Isaac Luria** (1534-1572, known by the acronym **ARI**, "the Lion"). Luria himself wrote almost nothing; his teachings were preserved principally through his disciple **Hayyim Vital** (1542-1620), who composed the substantial *Etz Chaim* (Tree of Life) systematizing the Lurianic content. The Lurianic synthesis articulated several principal innovations:

- **Tzimtzum** (צִמְצוּם, "contraction" or "withdrawal"): the doctrine that the *Ein Sof* contracted to create empty space (*chalal*) within which creation could occur
- **Shevirat ha-kelim** (שְׁבִירַת הַכֵּלִים, "the breaking of the vessels"): the doctrine of cosmic catastrophe in which the original *sephirotic* vessels could not contain the divine light and shattered, scattering divine sparks throughout the lower worlds
- **Tikkun olam** (תִּקּוּן עוֹלָם, "repair of the world"): the doctrine that the proper work of human beings is to gather and elevate the scattered divine sparks, repairing the cosmic order
- **Gilgul ha-neshamot** (גִּלְגּוּל הַנְּשָׁמוֹת, "transmigration of souls"): substantial Lurianic articulation of soul-transmigration

The Lurianic synthesis became the dominant Kabbalistic framework for subsequent Jewish mystical tradition.

**The Hasidic developments (18th century onward)**. The Hasidic movement, founded by **Israel ben Eliezer** (the **Baal Shem Tov**, c. 1700-1760) in eighteenth-century Eastern Europe, developed a popularized form of Lurianic Kabbalah with substantial practical-pietistic emphasis. The substantial Hasidic literature includes the various rebbe-traditions (Chabad / Lubavitch, Breslov, Satmar, and many others), with substantial textual production extending across approximately three centuries.

**Modern academic and renewal phases (20th-21st centuries)**. The twentieth century saw substantial academic engagement with the Kabbalah principally through Gershom Scholem (treated under *Modern reinterpretations* below), with substantial continuing scholarly engagement across multiple distinct disciplinary contexts. Various twentieth- and twenty-first-century renewal movements (Jewish Renewal, the broader Kabbalah Centre, various other contemporary engagements) have produced substantial popularization and contemporary application.

### The principal cosmological-theological content

The Kabbalistic tradition articulates substantial cosmological-theological framework operating across several principal components.

**The *Ein Sof***. The **Ein Sof** (אֵין סוֹף, "without end" or "the infinite") is the Kabbalistic designation for the transcendent unmanifest divine source — that which exceeds all designation, all attribute, all conceptualization. The *Ein Sof* operates as the apophatic-transcendent ground from which the *sephirotic* emanations proceed, with the *Ein Sof* itself being substantively beyond direct human relation.

**The ten *sephirot***. The ten emanations through which the *Ein Sof* expresses itself in relation to creation:

1. **Keter** (כֶּתֶר, "Crown"): the highest *sephirah*, often identified with the *Will* of the divine, the threshold between the *Ein Sof* and manifestation
2. **Chokhmah** (חָכְמָה, "Wisdom"): the second *sephirah*, the active principle, often associated with the masculine
3. **Binah** (בִּינָה, "Understanding"): the third *sephirah*, the receptive principle, often associated with the feminine
4. **Chesed** (חֶסֶד, "Mercy" / "Loving-kindness"): the fourth *sephirah*, the principle of expansion and giving
5. **Gevurah** (גְּבוּרָה, "Severity" / "Strength"): the fifth *sephirah*, the principle of restraint and judgment (also called *Din*, "Judgment")
6. **Tiferet** (תִּפְאֶרֶת, "Beauty" / "Harmony"): the sixth *sephirah*, the principle of balance, often associated with the divine name *Yahweh* and with the figure of Jacob/Israel
7. **Netzach** (נֶצַח, "Eternity" / "Victory"): the seventh *sephirah*, the principle of endurance
8. **Hod** (הוֹד, "Splendor" / "Glory"): the eighth *sephirah*, the principle of acknowledgment
9. **Yesod** (יְסוֹד, "Foundation"): the ninth *sephirah*, the principle of connection and transmission
10. **Malkhut** (מַלְכוּת, "Kingdom" / "Sovereignty"): the tenth *sephirah*, the principle of manifestation and reception, often identified with the *Shekhinah* (the divine presence) and the figure of Israel/the Community

**The four worlds**. The *sephirotic* structure operates across four distinct worlds:

1. **Atziluth** (אֲצִילוּת, "Emanation"): the world of pure divine emanation, closest to the *Ein Sof*
2. **Beriah** (בְּרִיאָה, "Creation"): the world of creation, of the throne and the highest angels
3. **Yetzirah** (יְצִירָה, "Formation"): the world of formation, of the angelic hosts
4. **Asiyah** (עֲשִׂיָּה, "Action" / "Making"): the world of action, of physical creation

**The *Etz Chaim* diagram**. The *sephirot* are arranged in the **Tree of Life** diagram with three pillars:
- **Right pillar** (the Pillar of Mercy): Chokhmah, Chesed, Netzach
- **Left pillar** (the Pillar of Severity): Binah, Gevurah, Hod
- **Central pillar** (the Pillar of Equilibrium): Keter, Tiferet, Yesod, Malkhut

The *sephirot* are connected by twenty-two paths corresponding to the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet, producing the substantial diagrammatic-symbolic structure that has become the principal visual representation of Kabbalistic cosmology.

### Sendy on the Kabbalah

Jean Sendy's substantial engagement with the Kabbalah operates principally through the work of **Alexandre Safran** (1910-2006), the Romanian-born rabbi who served as Chief Rabbi of Romania (1940-1947) and subsequently as Chief Rabbi of Geneva (1948-1998). Safran's *La Cabale* (Payot, 1960) provides substantial systematic articulation of the Kabbalistic tradition that Sendy adopts.

The Sendy / Safran articulation registers several specific positions that align substantially with the broader corpus framework:

- **The Kabbalah's pre-Mosaic antiquity**: *"The Kabbalah exceeds, in antiquity, the Revelation of Sinai; it dates back to prehistoric times. Moses only introduced it into the history of Israel."* Safran's articulation registers the tradition's extension beyond the Sinai event, with prehistoric origins.
- **The chain of tradition**: *"shalshelet ha-Kabbalah"* — the substantial transmission claim from Moses through Joshua, the Elders, the Prophets, the Great Assembly, and onward.
- **The cosmic-renewal content**: *"Man will renew the acts recounted at the beginning of Genesis"* — substantial alignment with the framework's Cosmic Chain content articulating that humans will themselves become creators.
- **The two-worlds correspondence**: the substantial correspondence between celestial and earthly worlds, with their substantial unification through human action.

The Sendy engagement registers the Kabbalah as substantially preserving alliance-transmitted content within Jewish religious-textual tradition, with Safran's substantial Jewish-tradition authority providing additional credibility for the broader framework engagement.

## In primary sources

The framework's principal primary-source material is contained in the Yahweh-delivered passage in *The Book Which Tells the Truth* (1974), with substantial subsequent corpus development.

### The principal "At the Roots of All Religions" passage

The principal source-material passage establishing the framework's specific reading appears in *The Book Which Tells the Truth* (1974), in the chapter "The End of the World," section "At the Roots of All Religions." Yahweh's articulation:

> *"It is not only in the Bible and the Gospels that there are traces of the truth; testimonies can be found in practically every religion. The Kabala especially is one of the richest in testimonies, but it would not have been easy for you to get hold of one. If one day you can find a copy, then you will be able to see that there are a great number of allusions to us."*

The passage establishes several principal components:

**1. The cross-traditional preservation of truth.** The opening clause registers that "traces of the truth" are preserved across multiple religious traditions beyond the Bible and Gospels. The Kabbalah is named as one specific instance of this broader cross-traditional preservation.

**2. The Kabbalah's exceptional richness.** The phrase "the Kabala especially is one of the richest in testimonies" registers the Kabbalah's exceptional position within the broader cross-traditional landscape. The framework reading: the Kabbalah is not merely one tradition among many but the specifically richest tradition for traces of the truth.

**3. ### Der *Shi'ur Qomah*: der Text hinter der Aussage vom „der Wahrheit am nächsten kommenden Buch"

Die Passage vom „der Wahrheit am nächsten kommenden Buch" steht nicht für sich allein. Im selben Atemzug nennt Jahwe zwei konkrete Zahlenwerte — die „Höhe des Schöpfers", 236.000 *parasangs*, und die „Höhe seiner Fersen", 30 Millionen — und schreibt sie *„dem Hohelied (V)"* zu. Diese Werte sind real, doch stammen sie **nicht** aus dem *Sohar* oder aus dem oben behandelten *sephirotischen* Material. Sie stammen aus einem bestimmten, älteren Text: dem ***Shi'ur Qomah*** (hebräisch שִׁעוּר קוֹמָה, *šiʿur qômāh*, „das Maß der Gestalt"), einem Werk der **vorkabbalistischen Hekhalot- / Merkava-Literatur**, in dem der Engel Metatron die kosmischen Ausmaße des göttlichen Körpers Glied für Glied diktiert, jedes in *parasangs* gemessen und mit einem geheimen Namen benannt. Die korrekte Identifikation des Quelltextes löst eine seit Langem bestehende Quellenspannung im Korpus auf: Die „Kabbala", die die Passage zitiert, ist keineswegs die mittelalterliche klassische Kabbala, sondern die spätantike mystische Schicht, die ihr vorausgeht.

Die Zahl 236 wird durch *gematria* aus Psalm 147,5 erzeugt (*gadol adoneinu ve-rav koaḥ*, „Groß ist unser Herr und reich an Macht", dessen Buchstaben in der Summe 236 ergeben); der „Sohlen"-Wert von 30 Millionen parasangs und die verkürzte Gesamtsumme „236.000" sind beide in der Handschriftenüberlieferung bezeugte Varianten (Letztere überliefert vom karäischen Gelehrten al-Qirqisānī aus dem zehnten Jahrhundert). Der Text geht dem *Sohar* um Jahrhunderte voraus — von Gershom Scholem ins zweite Jahrhundert datiert, von Martin Samuel Cohen in die geonische Zeit des sechsten bis siebten Jahrhunderts, während Peter Schäfer der Auffassung ist, dass in dieser fließenden Literatur „der eine Text eine Illusion ist" — und er gelangte innerhalb des *Sefer Raziel ha-Mal'akh* (Amsterdam, 1701, Folio 37b) in den Druck. Genau dieser Druck ist der Grund, weshalb eine Passage aus einem esoterischen hebräischen Korpus als ein „Buch" beschrieben werden konnte, von dem man sich „ein Exemplar beschaffen" könnte.

In der Lesart des Rahmens liefert Jahwe den fehlenden Schlüssel: Der *parasang* wird als die Strecke neu definiert, die das Licht in einer Sekunde zurücklegt (~300.000 km), womit die beiden Werte zur Entfernung der [Elohim-Heimatwelt](../elohim-home-planet/) von ihrem eigenen Stern (~70,8 Milliarden km) und von der Erde (~0,95 Lichtjahre) werden. Die Maßangaben werden nicht als mystische Hyperbel gelesen, sondern als verzerrte Aufzeichnung zweier realer Entfernungen, bewahrt von einer Tradition, die getreulich rezitierte, was zu verstehen sie aufgehört hatte.

Die vollständige Behandlung — eine genaue Lektüre des Textes durch die projekteigene Übersetzung, der dreiseitige Datierungsstreit, die mainstream-Lesart der „Maßangabe-als-Apophase" und die Gegenlesart des Kanons sowie ein neuer Befund dazu, wie die exakte Form der Passage (die Fehlübersetzung *talons*/„Fersen" für das hebräische „Sohlen" und die Zitierung „Cantique des Cantiques (V)") auf Henri Sérouyas 1957 erschienenes Taschenbuch *La Kabbale* über Scholems *Major Trends* zurückgeht — findet sich im Explainer [**Das Buch, das der Wahrheit am nächsten kommt**](/articles/the-book-closest-to-the-truth/) und in der [*Shi'ur Qomah* — Wheel of Heaven Translation](/library/shiur-qomah-woh/).
