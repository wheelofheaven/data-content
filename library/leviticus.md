**Leviticus** (Hebrew: <span class="hebrew">וַיִּקְרָא</span>,
*Vayikra* — "And He called") is the third book of the Torah and
the legal heart of what source critics call the Priestly source.
It is, in genre terms, the most technical of the five books — a
manual of ritual specification, purity categories, and priestly
protocol.

## Structure

- **Sacrifices (chapters 1–7)** — burnt offerings, meal offerings,
  peace offerings, sin offerings, trespass offerings, with detailed
  procedural specifications.
- **Priestly consecration (chapters 8–10)** — the ordination of
  Aaron and his sons, and the immediate death of Nadab and Abihu
  for "strange fire."
- **Purity regulations (chapters 11–16)** — clean and unclean
  animals, the postpartum purity period, skin diseases, bodily
  discharges, and the annual Day of Atonement (chapter 16).
- **Holiness Code (chapters 17–26)** — ethical and ritual laws
  culminating in the promise of blessing for obedience and curse
  for disobedience.
- **Vows and valuations (chapter 27)** — the appendix on
  redemption prices.

## Why this matters for the Wheel of Heaven reading

Leviticus is the densest book in the Hebrew Bible for
**specification language**. The verbs are procedural. The nouns
are categorical. If the Wheel of Heaven frame reads Genesis 1 as
compressed engineering specification, Leviticus is the long-form
operations manual that the same kind of authorial voice might
have produced — exhaustive procedural detail for maintaining a
clean environment around a powerful, dangerous, and easily
contaminated presence.

The purity regulations in chapters 11-15 also have a distinctly
**hygienic** register — dietary categories that correlate with
disease risk, isolation protocols for infectious skin conditions,
quarantine procedures for houses with mold. Whether read as
advanced proto-epidemiology or as ritual categories with
incidental hygienic function, the texts are strikingly
procedural for an ancient document.
