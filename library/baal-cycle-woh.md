**The Baal Cycle (Wheel of Heaven Translation)** is the project's
**first Ugaritic-source text** and **first Levantine-tradition text**.
The cycle is preserved on six clay tablets discovered at the royal
palace of Ugarit (Ras Shamra, modern Latakia, Syria) in the 1929–1931
excavations led by Claude Schaeffer. The tablets are scribed in the
Ugaritic alphabetic cuneiform script — a 30-letter Northwest-Semitic
abjad rendered with cuneiform-shaped wedges on clay, distinct from
the syllabic Sumero-Akkadian cuneiform of Mesopotamia. The cycle was
likely composed in the 14th–13th centuries BCE by the scribe Ilimilku
son of ʿAttenu.

The Baal Cycle is the most extensively-preserved Late Bronze Age
mythological composition from anywhere in the eastern Mediterranean,
and the central comparative-philology document for understanding the
Northwest-Semitic background of the Hebrew Bible. The six-tablet
narrative covers Baʿlu's conflicts with Yammu (Sea) and Môtu (Death)
and his ascension to kingship over the gods. Cross-corpus parallels
include: Yahweh-vs-Yam ({% wiki(slug="psalm-74") %}Ps 74:13–14{% end %},
Ps 89:9–10, Isa 27:1); El/Elohim and the Northwest-Semitic divine
council; *bn ʾilm* = *bnei elohim* (Gen 6:2, Job 1:6, Deut 32:8
LXX/4QDeut^j); ʾAṯirat = Asherah; Lôtanu = Leviathan (the cleanest
NW-Semitic Chaoskampf cognate); Môtu = Hebrew *mavet*; the
storm-theophany at Sinai; and F. M. Cross 1973's reading of Psalm 29
as a "Yahwehized" Baʿlu hymn.

**Chapter 1 — KTU 1.1 + 1.2 (Baʿlu vs Yammu).** The sea-conflict
episode: the assembly of the gods; Yammu's demand for tribute; the
messengers' arrival; the duel; Baʿlu's victory with two divine weapons
named Yagrush "Driver-out" and Ayamur "Striker" (forged and
speech-act-named by Kothar-wa-Khasis the divine craftsman); the
proclamation of Baʿlu's kingship. The text is severely fragmentary in
places — KTU 1.1 has only column iv substantially preserved (~28
lines), and KTU 1.2 column ii is broken away entirely. The famous
*šm bny yw* line at KTU 1.1 IV 14 (the proto-YHWH candidate) is
handled with explicit philological humility: three named-scholarly
readings (de Moor 1971, Cross 1973, Smith 1994) preserved without
adjudication.

**Chapter 2 — KTU 1.3 + 1.4 (the palace-building cycle).** ʿAnat's
bloody battle (the most explicit warrior-goddess passage in the
NW-Semitic corpus); her threat to ʾIlu the head-of-pantheon;
ʾAṯirat-of-the-Sea's intercession with ʾIlu on Baʿlu's behalf;
Kothar's seven-day construction of the cosmic palace on Mount Ṣapūnu
(Jebel al-Aqra, the 1759m peak ~40 km north of Ugarit); the famous
window-debate (Baʿlu initially refuses a window "lest Yamm see," then
reverses himself); Baʿlu's voice from the palace (*ytn qlh b ʿrpt*,
*qdš tr ʾarṣ* — direct cross-corpus to {% wiki(slug="psalm-29") %}Ps
29{% end %}'s seven-fold *qol YHWH* thunder-theophany); the proleptic
challenge to Môt.

**Chapters 3 (KTU 1.5–1.6) — pending.** The conflict with Môtu /
Death: Baʿlu is swallowed by Death, his sister Anat ritually destroys
Môt with sickle / winnowing-fan / millstone, and Baʿlu returns to
life. The dying-and-rising-storm-god narrative.

The translation is a best-effort reconstruction from KTU³
(Dietrich-Loretz-Sanmartín 2013), Smith 1994 Vol 1, Smith & Pitard
2009 Vol 2, Pardee 2003 *Context of Scripture* 1.86, Gibson 1978
*Canaanite Myths and Legends* (2nd ed.), and Ginsberg 1955 *ANET*
pp. 129–142. Verification against KTU³ is pending; corrections
welcome.

**The original Ugaritic alphabetic cuneiform is rendered directly in
the source file** — a project first. Unlike Sumero-Akkadian logographic
cuneiform (where back-rendering requires sign-by-sign lookup via the
OGSL mapper), Ugaritic's 30-letter alphabet maps 1:1 to the Unicode
Ugaritic block (U+10380–U+1039F), so both the strict KTU
transliteration AND the original-script rendering ship in the source
file from day one.

**Lines with commentary carry a bracketed marker `[ ]` at the end of
the translated line. Click the marker to open the commentary panel.**
The interlinear view shows the cuneiform alongside the transliteration
when toggled on. Cross-corpus glossary entries are linked from each
relevant line; the Baal Cycle has been the single largest source of
central-glossary growth in any text-pair (31 new central entries + 1
overlay-promoted + 11 overlay across chapters 1 and 2, including F. M.
Cross 1973's Psalm-29-as-Yahwehized-Baʿlu-hymn entry, the Hurowitz 1992
palace-temple-building typology cluster, Day 1985's Lôtanu-Leviathan
cognate-cluster, the Heiser-thesis seventy-sons-of-ʾAṯirat ↔ Deut 32:8
activation, and the window-of-heaven cluster).

860 lines preserved across 24 segments (KTU 1.1 cols i–vi + KTU 1.2
cols i–iv + KTU 1.3 cols i–vi + KTU 1.4 cols i–viii), with substantial
lacunae in the more damaged columns. Translation is signed off pending
critical-edition verification — the *Editor's Reports* (one per chapter)
in the source repository document the conditions of the reconstruction.
