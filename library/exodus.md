**Exodus** (Hebrew: <span class="hebrew">שְׁמוֹת</span>, *Shemot* —
"Names") is the second book of the Torah. It picks up where Genesis
leaves off — the descendants of Jacob in Egypt — and carries the
narrative through the liberation under Moses, the wilderness
journey to Mount Sinai, the mountain theophany, the giving of the
Law, and the construction of the Tabernacle as a portable meeting
place between the divine and the people.

## Structure

Exodus is traditionally read in three blocks:

- **Liberation narrative (chapters 1–15)** — Israel in Egyptian
  bondage, the birth and call of Moses, the ten plagues, the
  Passover, the crossing of the Reed Sea, and the Song of the Sea.
- **Wilderness journey (chapters 15–18)** — manna, quail, water
  from the rock, the battle with Amalek, the visit of Jethro.
- **Sinai and covenant (chapters 19–40)** — the theophany at the
  mountain, the Ten Commandments, the Book of the Covenant, the
  golden calf incident, and the detailed instructions for building
  the Tabernacle.

## Why this matters for the Wheel of Heaven reading

The Sinai theophany in Exodus 19 is one of the strangest passages
in the Hebrew Bible in phenomenological terms — cloud, fire,
smoke, thunder, trumpet blasts, the mountain shaking, and strict
instructions that no one approach the mountain or touch it "lest
the LORD break forth upon them" (Exod 19:22). Whether read as
myth, poetic compression, or the Wheel of Heaven frame's
compressed memory of contact, the scene is unmistakably an
account of a numinous encounter with something powerful,
bounded, and dangerous to approach without protocol.

The Tabernacle chapters (25-40) have their own Wheel of Heaven
interest: they describe in exhaustive technical detail the
construction of a specific enclosure with specific materials and
dimensions, intended as a place where the divine presence
(*kavod*) can "dwell" among the people. The genre of the text
shifts from narrative to specification.
