**Eridu Genesis** is the modern name for the Sumerian narrative
preserved on the Nippur tablets CBS 4561 and CBS 4611 — the
oldest known account of paradise, the creation of humanity, and
the flood. The tablets predate the Babylonian *Enuma Elish* and
the Akkadian *Atrahasis*, and provide the earliest surviving
parallels to the Genesis primeval history.

## About this edition

- **Coverage:** 7 columns (6 from CBS 4561 + 1 fragment from CBS 4611), 257 paragraphs total
- **Translation:** Stephen Langdon, *Sumerian Epic of Paradise, the Flood, and the Fall of Man* (Publications of the Babylonian Section, Vol. X, No. 1, University of Pennsylvania, 1915), public domain
- **Quality note:** OCR extraction from Archive.org. The Langdon edition itself is heavily reconstructed — many lines on the original tablets are broken, and Langdon's bracketed restorations are preserved here as `[...]`. Marker lines indicate sections obliterated on the tablet. Quality is partial; later passes may refine OCR errors and adopt cleaner editorial brackets.
- **Reference format:** `EG {column}:{line}`

## About the name

The label "Eridu Genesis" was coined by Thorkild Jacobsen in his
1981 article *The Eridu Genesis* (Journal of Biblical Literature
100/4) to describe the narrative complex preserved across the
Nippur tablet and related fragments. Langdon, working sixty-six
years earlier from the same source material, called it the
"Sumerian Epic of Paradise, the Flood, and the Fall of Man." Both
names refer to the same underlying tradition.
