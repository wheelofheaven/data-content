**Atra-ḫasīs** (Akkadian *Atra-ḫasīs*, "exceedingly wise") is the
Old Babylonian epic that survives most fully in three tablets copied
in the reign of Ammi-ṣaduqa (mid-17th century BCE). It narrates the
labour of the lesser gods, their rebellion, the creation of humanity
to take over that labour, the overpopulation crisis met first by
plague and drought and then by the Flood, and the survival of
**Atra-ḫasīs**, the sage warned by **Enki/Ea**. Atra-ḫasīs is the
direct Akkadian counterpart to Sumerian **Zi-ud-sud-du** / Ziusudra
and to the later **Utnapishtim** of *Gilgamesh* Tablet XI.

## About this edition

- **Status:** Scaffold only. Translation source pending.
- **Reference format:** `ATR {tablet}:{line}`
- **Original language:** Akkadian (Old Babylonian)

The standard scholarly edition is W.G. Lambert and A.R. Millard,
*Atra-ḫasīs: The Babylonian Story of the Flood* (Oxford, 1969),
which is not public domain. Candidate PD sources include George
Smith's partial readings of the Atra-ḫasīs tablets in
*The Chaldean Account of Genesis* (1876) and any pre-1929
reconstruction that may surface. Final source will be recorded
here once selected.
