**Atra-ḫasīs (Wheel of Heaven Translation)** renders Tablet I of the
Old Babylonian flood-and-creation epic directly from the Akkadian. It
opens on the famous line *inūma ilū awīlum* — "When the gods, like man,
bore the labor" — and follows the lesser gods (the Igigi) through forty
years of digging the Tigris, the Euphrates, and the Apsû until they
burn their tools and rise in the night against Enlil.

The tablet's core is an **anthropogony**: to relieve the gods of their
toil, Enki has a single god slaughtered — Wê-ila, "the god who had
*ṭēmu*," intelligence — and his flesh and blood mixed into clay by the
womb-goddess Nintu. From that mixture fourteen pieces are pinched off,
seven male and seven female, and humankind is made — expressly to carry
the *dullu*, the labor of the gods. The *ṭēmu*/*eṭemmu* pun (the slain
god's reason becomes the "spirit" that persists in humans) and the
Wê+ila → *awīlu* pun make the creature a compound of clay and divinity.

This is a **moat text**: no usable public-domain English translation
exists, so the project translates its own. The Akkadian is a
**best-effort reconstruction** (Lambert–Millard 1969 line numbering;
eBL L.1.1 and SEAL as collation aids) with sign-level verification
pending. The clay-and-divine-blood, made-to-serve motif stands here in
its own terms; the comparative reading — its relation to Genesis 2:7's
dust-and-breath, and to the wider Wheel of Heaven frame — is developed
in the encyclopedia and the Explainers, not imposed on the text.
Initial scope is Tablet I; the plagues and the flood (Tablets II–III)
remain to come.
