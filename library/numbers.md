**Numbers** (Hebrew: <span class="hebrew">בְּמִדְבַּר</span>,
*Bemidbar* — "In the wilderness") is the fourth book of the
Torah. Its English name reflects the two census lists that
bracket the book (chapters 1 and 26); its Hebrew name reflects
the actual setting — forty years of wilderness journey between
Mount Sinai and the edge of the Promised Land.

## Structure

- **At Sinai (chapters 1–10:10)** — the first census, tribal
  arrangements around the Tabernacle, Levitical duties, the
  Passover observance, the silver trumpets.
- **From Sinai to Kadesh (chapters 10:11–12)** — the journey
  begins, Moses's father-in-law Hobab, the sending of the spies.
- **At Kadesh and the forty years (chapters 13–19)** — the
  twelve spies, the rebellion at the refusal to enter the land,
  the forty-year penalty, Korah's rebellion, the red heifer.
- **To Moab (chapters 20–25)** — Miriam's death, the bronze
  serpent, Sihon and Og, and the Balaam oracles (chapters 22–24),
  which are among the strangest passages in the Torah.
- **At Moab (chapters 26–36)** — the second census, inheritance
  laws, the appointment of Joshua, the boundaries of the
  Promised Land.

## Why this matters for the Wheel of Heaven reading

Two things in Numbers are especially relevant to the Wheel of
Heaven frame.

**The Balaam oracles (22–24)** are a first-person narrative in
which a non-Israelite prophet is repeatedly contacted by YHWH,
attempts to curse Israel and is prevented, and instead delivers
a series of blessings that include the strange "star shall come
forth out of Jacob" oracle (24:17), read messianically by later
Jewish and Christian tradition and with Wheel of Heaven interest
for its celestial imagery.

**The wilderness provisions** — manna described as "like
coriander seed, and the appearance thereof as the appearance of
bdellium" (11:7), water from the rock, the pillar of cloud and
fire — read as a sustained record of being supported in a hostile
environment by something external and intentional. Whether as
myth or as compressed memory, the narrative structure is
unmistakably "a people being managed through a wilderness by a
presence that supplies their needs."
