**Enki and Ninhursag (Wheel of Heaven Translation)** is rendered from the
ETCSL composite transliteration (ETCSL 1.1.1, CC0 base) under a documented
WoH lens, with the source and the English translation displayed in
parallel.

The composition is one of the foundational Sumerian creation-and-paradise
myths. Dilmun is described as the pure land where no animal preyed and no
human suffered illness or old age; Enki orders Utu to bring water from
below; the land becomes fertile. A series of divine birth-sequences
follow; the eight plants grow; Enki eats them and falls ill in eight
body-parts; Ninhursag curses him but is appeased and creates eight
healing-deities from her body — one for each ailing part of Enki. The
text pairs with the Hebrew Eden tradition (Gen 2–3) on multiple
comparative-mythology axes: paradise-as-precondition, the rib / *Ninti*
motif (the goddess of the rib / lady of life), the forbidden-plants
motif, divine illness from forbidden food, and paradisal healing.

256 lines are preserved across 6 small in-line lacunae (totaling ~25
missing lines); ~91% of the composition is preserved — considerably
more complete than the Sumerian Flood Story.
