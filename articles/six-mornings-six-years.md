The second book of the Raëlian canon is misread most often by its own
title. *Extra-Terrestrials Took Me To Their Planet*, published in 1975,
narrates a journey that never reaches the Elohim's planet. The craft
that collects {% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %} at the Roc Plat a
little after midnight on 7 October carries him first to a base
"situated relatively near the earth," where the whole first block of
teaching is delivered under a transparent dome and a black, star-filled
sky. A second, larger craft then takes him — ten minutes of intense
cold, no porthole, no view — to the planet of the Eternals, where
everything the tradition remembers from the book takes place: the
banquet of the forty, the biological robots, the machine, the night.
The Elohim's own world appears exactly once in the book's geography,
listed beside the planet of the Eternals in a meditation instruction as
a separate item ({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=193) %}ETTMTTP 3:193{% end %}),
and Raël never sets foot on it.{{ footnote(id="1") }}

Fifty years of summaries have collapsed those three places into one,
and the collapse is not harmless. Where a disclosure happens is part of
what the disclosure is. A briefing at an observation post, a state
banquet on the capital planet of a soteriological empire, and a home
world withheld even from the messenger are three different speech
situations, and the second book keeps them apart with a discipline its
readers have rarely matched. Its own chapter 3 slips exactly once —
promising the elect eternal life "on the planet of the Elohim"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=216) %}ETTMTTP 3:216{% end %})
where every other passage says the planet of the Eternals — and that
single lapse, in a book otherwise rigorous about its own map, is the
kind of datum this article exists to collect.

The subject here is the three canonical books —
[*The Book Which Tells the Truth*](/library/the-book-which-tells-the-truth/)
(1973, published 1974),
[*Extra-Terrestrials Took Me To Their Planet*](/library/extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet/)
(1975), and
[*Let's Welcome The Extra-Terrestrials*](/library/lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials/)
(1979); hereafter TBWTT, ETTMTTP, LWTE — read as one serial text, with
the tools normally reserved for scriptures two thousand years cold.
Two disciplines do the work. The first is discourse analysis in its
narratological form: who speaks, to whom, where, in what order, under
what constraint. The second is textual criticism proper: what the
received text shows, in its seams and repairs, about how it was
transmitted. The thesis both disciplines converge on is that the most
carefully engineered system in the canon is not the cosmology, the
soteriology, or the political program. It is disclosure control. From
the first morning in the crater to the last mailing address in the
third book, the speaker — {% wiki(slug="yahweh") %}Yahweh{% end %},
though the first book withholds even that — decides what is said, when
it is said, where it is said, and who takes the blame when something
already said needs to change. He behaves, in the precise technical
sense, as his own redactor.

The claim-typing belongs at the door. That the
{% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %} exist and met a French
journalist in a volcanic crater is a framework premise of this project
— explicit in the canon, not endorsed by mainstream scholarship. Every
claim in this article, though, is a claim about the *texts*: what they
say, where they agree, where they strain, what their edition-state
reveals. A reader who grants the canon nothing can check every
paragraph reference against the project's
[reference library](/library/) and lose nothing of the argument. That
is why the piece carries the label *inferred*, and why its method
section can be stated in one sentence: the canon's founding scene is a
man with a Dhorme Bible open on his knees being taught to read it
critically, and this article does to that man's books what his books do
to the Bible.

## A seminar, a voyage, and a press conference

The three books are usually described as three installments of one
message. Formally, they are three different kinds of document, and the
differences carry information.

The first book is a seminar. Six consecutive mornings in the crater of
the Puy de Lassolas, roughly an hour a day
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=1, verse=63) %}ETTMTTP 1:63{% end %}),
with materials specified in advance: "Come back tomorrow with the
Bible, at the same time, and bring something to take notes with"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=63) %}TBWTT 1:63{% end %}).
The session method is verse-then-gloss — a passage read from the
witness's French Bible, then decoded as the operations log of a
planetary engineering project — and the syllabus is gated on the first
morning of teaching: "Only the parts of the Bible I am going to
translate for you are important. The others, which are merely poetic
chatter, I will not speak to you about"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=3) %}TBWTT 2:3{% end %}).
The distribution of speech is as lopsided as any dictation in the
history of the genre. Of the 759 numbered paragraphs in the project's
reference text, 643 belong to the speaker, 62 to the narrator, 54 to
Raël — and every one of Raël's falls in the first and last chapters.
From chapter 2 through chapter 6 he asks nothing at all. The book is a
dialogue only at its rim; its core is a lecture with a rostrum, and the
lecturer sets the reading list, the schedule, and the secrecy rule
("speak to no one about our meetings, otherwise we shall not see each
other again," {% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=63) %}TBWTT 1:63{% end %}).
[*The Religion of Religions*](/articles/the-religion-of-religions/) has
already made the point that the founding revelation of Raëlism is,
before anything else, a Bible-study seminar; what the discourse
analysis adds is that it is a seminar in which the student's silence is
structural.{{ footnote(id="5") }}

The second book is a voyage, and it belongs to a genre with a long
pedigree: the guided tour of the other world, conducted by an angelic
interpreter, with the seer returned to write what he saw. The
Enochic literature runs on this frame, which is worth saying carefully
— it is a comparative observation about form; dependence is a separate
question this article does not decide. What ETTMTTP adds to the genre is a technology of
reception. Raël brings no notebook this time and is told he will not
need one: "all that I am going to tell you will remain engraved in your
mind, for here we have a technical means for you to remember all that
you are going to hear"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=22) %}ETTMTTP 2:22{% end %}).
The claim is redeemed at the end of the narrative — "I was surprised to
notice that I was writing all this in one stroke without any
hesitation" — and then the book performs its own transition into a
third mode of reception on the page. The sentence that introduces the
chapter of commandments called The Keys breaks off mid-clause: "I wrote
but I did not feel myself the author of what appeared on the paper. The
Elohim were beginning to…"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=132) %}ETTMTTP 2:132{% end %}).
The receiving instrument interrupts its own description of reception.
Whatever one makes of that, it is a deliberate piece of composition: the
notes of 1973 gave way to the implanted memory of 1975, which gives way,
in the same volume, to automatic writing.

The third book has no encounter at all, and its four chapters are four
genres. Chapter 1 is a press conference in print — "Rael's answers to
those questions that were most frequently raised by journalists"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=1) %}LWTE 1:1{% end %}).
Chapter 2 is an embargo lifting: "revelations that the Elohim had asked
Rael to keep secret until three years after his journey… Now that we
are in the year 34 (1979), these things can be known to all"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=1) %}LWTE 2:1{% end %}).
Chapter 3 is a sermon in Raël's own authority, legislating everything
from suicide casuistry to the duty of disobeying an Eloha who commands
murder. Chapter 4 opens the canon to the congregation — essays and
conversion testimonials by followers, one of them datelined "an 33
après Raël." The speaker hierarchy has inverted. In 1973 Yahweh spoke
and Raël transcribed; by 1979 Yahweh speaks only inside frames Raël
supplies ("I, Yahweh, through the words of my prophet Rael,"
{% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=101) %}LWTE 2:101{% end %}),
Raël legislates in his own voice, and the laity have entered the text.
The last gesture of the last canonical book is a postal address in
Geneva and an invitation to write for the transmission of one's
cellular plan. Weber would file that under the routinization of
charisma without changing a word; the canon itself would say the
messenger's work was always meant to become a
{% wiki(slug="embassy") %}construction project{% end %}. Both readings
fit the same page.

## One night, two craft, three worlds

Since the geography is the part most often mangled, it is worth fixing
with the precision the text actually supplies. The itinerary of 7–8
October is two-staged in both directions, and every transition is
narrated.

| Scene | Location | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Departure walk, craft landing | Earth — the Roc Plat, Périgord | ETTMTTP 2:9–11 |
| First transit, "a few seconds" of cold | small two-seat craft | ETTMTTP 2:12–13 |
| Decontamination, bath, white garment | near-Earth base | ETTMTTP 2:14–15 |
| Domed hall, first teaching block | near-Earth base ("observation station") | ETTMTTP 2:16–43 |
| Second transit, "about ten minutes" of cold | large four-seat craft | ETTMTTP 2:44 |
| Banquet of the forty, the prophets | planet of the Eternals | ETTMTTP 2:44–66 |
| Robot workshop, the three demonstrations | planet of the Eternals | ETTMTTP 2:67–83 |
| The abode, the night, the machine | planet of the Eternals | ETTMTTP 2:84–97 |
| Second teaching block, the rules | planet of the Eternals | ETTMTTP 2:98–130 |
| Return: great vessel → station → small craft → Roc Plat | reverse itinerary | ETTMTTP 2:131 |
| The Elohim's home planet | referenced, never visited | ETTMTTP 3:193 |

Three details in this table repay attention. The first is that the Roc
Plat is not the 1973 site. The first encounter happened in the crater
of the Puy de Lassolas in the Auvergne; the second begins at a deserted
spot between two streams in the Périgord, near the farm where Raël had
settled that summer. Book 2 even corrects book 1's place-name on the
way past — "I had, moreover, wrongly called this place the
Puy-de-la-Vache"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=1, verse=63) %}ETTMTTP 1:63{% end %})
— a correction whose strange afterlife is taken up below.

The second is that the base is where the reader is most likely to lose
the thread, because the text withholds the location from Raël himself.
He travels blind — the craft have no portholes — and sits through
thirty paragraphs of teaching under the domed hall's black sky before
the speaker turns the map on:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=43) %}
But you are no doubt wondering where you find yourself. You are
currently in a base situated relatively near the earth. In the first
message you noted that we moved seven times faster than light, this was
true twenty-five thousand years ago when we landed on the earth. Since
then we have progressed much and we now travel in space much faster
still. It takes us only a few instants to effect the journey we put
nearly two months to cover at that epoch, and we continue to progress.
If you will kindly follow me we are now going to effect a little voyage
together.
{% end %}

One paragraph performs three operations at once: it names the setting
retroactively, it retires a claim from the first book (the
seven-times-light speed, reassigned to the technology of twenty-five
millennia ago), and it announces the next stage of the journey. The
return leg confirms the architecture and gives the base its name — "I
found myself in the great vessel which set me down in the observation
station. There, I refollowed the same circuit as the day before and I
found myself with my clothes in the small vessel which set me down
where it had taken me, at the Roc-Plat. I looked at my watch: it was
midnight"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=131) %}ETTMTTP 2:131{% end %}).
Everything taught between the bath and the second craft — the
rectification of the first message, non-intervention, the Buddhism
exegesis, the restatement that there is no god and no soul — was taught
at a station near Earth, before any planet was reached. Summaries that
place those teachings "on the Elohim's planet" are wrong twice over.

The third detail is the planet that is missing. Book 1 had no separate
world for the recreated dead at all: the prophets lived in "the
residence where we have reproduced the terrestrial atmosphere"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=68) %}TBWTT 7:68{% end %})
on the Elohim's own planet. Book 2 upgrades the geography with an
accurate self-citation:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=50) %}
In my first message I had spoken to you of a residence found on our
planet where men of the Earth were maintained in life thanks to the
scientific secret of eternity from a cell, and among whom were found
Jesus, Moses, Elijah, etc. This residence is in fact very big since it
is a planet entire where also live the members of the council of the
eternals. My name is Yahweh and I am the president of the council of
the eternals. […]
{% end %}

The enlargement is flagged as an enlargement — "this residence is in
fact very big" — rather than smuggled in, and the same paragraph pays
out a name the first book had refused to give (of which more below).
From this point the canon has three off-Earth locations in play: the
{% wiki(slug="elohim-home-planet") %}home planet{% end %}, atmosphere
lethal to unprotected humans, location withheld; the planet of the
Eternals, seat of the
{% wiki(slug="council-of-eternals") %}Council{% end %}, population
8,400 recreated Terrans and 700 Elohim; and the near-Earth observation
station. The meditation instruction at ETTMTTP 3:193 keeps the first
two rigorously distinct. The lapse at 3:216 — "scientific reincarnation
on the planet of the Elohim" — is the only place the canon itself
blurs them, and the blur has propagated outward ever since, starting
with the cover of the book.{{ footnote(id="1") }}

## The speaker keeps the keys

Read as discourse, the first book's most striking feature is how much
of it is about the management of its own release. The encounter is
staged by the revealer before it happens ("I used telepathy to make you
come here," {% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=34) %}TBWTT 1:34{% end %});
the absence of evidence is raised by the revealer before the witness
can raise it ("You very much regret not having a camera…,"
{% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=42) %}TBWTT 1:42{% end %});
and the publication is commissioned with a hold clause — "Wait until
you know everything before speaking to them"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=44) %}TBWTT 1:44{% end %}).
Within the six mornings, disclosure runs on a schedule of explicit
deferrals that are then explicitly redeemed. Asked the distance to the
home planet on the first morning, the speaker answers: "Very far; when
I tell you the distance, you will understand that you cannot go there"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=51) %}TBWTT 1:51{% end %}).
The payment comes four chapters later, in parasangs, via the Kabbalah
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=52) %}TBWTT 5:52{% end %})
— a passage whose own paper trail this project has audited in
[*The Book Closest to the Truth*](/articles/the-book-closest-to-the-truth/).

The deepest withholding is the name. Raël asks it directly on the
first morning — "What is your name?" — and receives, in reply, a
demographic: "We are men like you, and we live on a planet quite
similar to Earth"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=53) %}TBWTT 1:52–53{% end %}).
Nowhere in the first book does the speaker identify himself. He offers
a collective designation ("you can call us «Elohim», since we have
«come from the sky»,"
{% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=33) %}TBWTT 7:33{% end %})
and, on the last morning, a positional one — the oldest of the
Eternals, president of their council, director of the creation of life
on Earth
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=56) %}TBWTT 7:56{% end %})
— but the personal name arrives only in 1975, mid-banquet, fused to
the geography upgrade: "My name is Yahweh." The dramatic economy is
exact. And the discipline holds in the direction a careless serial
author would break it: the first book never uses the name it does not
yet have. The "(Yahweh)" tags in every modern edition, this project's
included, are editorial.

The masterpiece of the system, though, is the no-proof doctrine,
because it converts the revelation's evidential weakness into its
central test:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=26) %}
We will never help you openly nor in any way that could serve as proof
to the skeptics, skepticism often going hand in hand with
aggressiveness. The intelligent will believe you, for what you are
going to say has nothing mystical. That is important for us; that you
be believed without material proof proves to us more than anything else
that one is intelligent and therefore worthy of receiving from us the
scientific inheritance.
{% end %}

This sits in visible tension with the first morning's promise of
"indisputable evidence"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=44) %}TBWTT 1:44{% end %}),
and the corpus never fully settles which pole it lives at — the second
book offers witnesses and a physical mark on the arm; the third swings
back so hard that it places the no-proof rule in the mouth of the
adversary, as mockery: "These same people will not even give you proofs
of their existence to help you to convince your brothers, nor any
financial aid"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=62) %}LWTE 2:62{% end %}).
Satan's taunt and Yahweh's policy are the same sentence with the
valence flipped, and the third book knows it: the lack of evidence has
become the cost of loyalty, priced by the opposition.

Across the books, the system acquires two more instruments. The first
is the embargo, which is the serial revelation's answer to the problem
of the incomplete first edition. Chapter 2 of LWTE inserts into the
already-published 1975 voyage two scenes the 1975 book does not contain
— the temptation on the relay vessel, with its Swiss billions and its
invitation to found a hoax; and the filiation, in which the speaker
switches from *vous* to *tu* and tells Raël "you are my son and I am
your father"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=92) %}LWTE 2:92{% end %}).
Both arrive under an embargo announced only at its expiry: "Yahweh
asked me not to reveal this parental tie until three years had passed"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=95) %}LWTE 2:95{% end %}).
The device is elegant because it is unfalsifiable and self-sealing —
any future addition can be a past secret — and because the insertion
point is narratively legal: book 2 had left hours of the visit
unnarrated, "strolling among the numerous fountains… in company of the
great prophets"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=130) %}ETTMTTP 2:130{% end %}),
and that is exactly where the new scenes are parked.

The second instrument is the repair doctrine, and it is best seen in
the three canonical corrections. When book 1's threat that the Elohim
could destroy an unwise humanity needs to become non-intervention, the
error is assigned to the scribe: "a passage of the message must be
rectified which you transcribed badly… It must be made quite clear that
we will not intervene"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=23) %}ETTMTTP 2:23{% end %}).
When book 1's flat "you could not live there" has to coexist with the
voyage book 2 narrates, the explanation is pedagogy: the refusal was
"just as people sometimes tell their children that if they drank
alcohol they would stop growing"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=9) %}LWTE 1:9{% end %})
— the third book's FAQ compresses its whole hermeneutic into one word,
"psychology"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=4) %}LWTE 1:4{% end %}).
And when whole scenes are missing from a published account, the answer
is embargo. Scribe, pedagogy, embargo: three devices, one invariant.
The error is never the source's. Redaction criticism was invented to
detect exactly this — the systematic protection of the authority behind
a text at the expense of everything else in the transmission chain —
and here the protection is not something a critic reconstructs from
seams. It is written on the surface, in the speaker's own voice.

Serial revelations tend to grow repair doctrines; the comparative
record suggests the canon's are unusually explicit rather than
unusual. Joseph Smith's revelations were revised — substantively,
in wording and content — between the 1833 Book of Commandments and the
1835 Doctrine and Covenants, and the Joseph Smith Papers now print the
variants for anyone to collate. The Qur'anic tradition built the
doctrine of *naskh*, abrogation, to govern verses that superseded
earlier verses within one prophetic career. What distinguishes the
Raëlian case is that the abrogation mechanism is a character in the
story: the source explains on-page why the earlier reading existed, and
files the correction under his own editorial authority. The canon does
not merely get revised. It narrates its own revision.

## The colors of the departure

Disclosure control is also visible at a finer grain, in what might be
called planted payoffs — details laid down without explanation and
redeemed chapters or books later. They matter to the analysis because
they discriminate between two models of composition: day-by-day
improvisation, which does not plant, and architectural writing (or,
under the canon's frame, an architect speaker), which does.

Inside the first book the plants are short-range and clean. The
departure of the craft on the second morning is described as pure
spectacle — it "turned red as if heated, then white like a metal heated
white-hot, then blue-violet like an enormous spark impossible to look
at, and disappeared completely"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=92) %}TBWTT 2:92{% end %})
— and sits unexplained for three chapters until the propulsion lecture
supplies the mechanism: to ride rays faster than light, "we leave the
optical window," and observers see the craft brighten through white
into blue and vanish
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=53) %}TBWTT 5:53{% end %}).
The sparkling bluish floor of the craft, noted on the first morning, is
matched two chapters later to the sapphire pavement under the feet of
the Elohim in Exodus. The speaker's 1.20-meter stature, given in the
first description, waits until the Ezekiel session to be paid off as
the reason the creators were remembered as cherubim. Whatever else the
first book is, it is not a transcript assembled morning by morning
without a plan; the narrator's own account — notes "put in order,"
"classified," "recopied"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=75) %}TBWTT 7:75{% end %})
— concedes as much, and the cross-chapter architecture confirms it.

Across the books, the plants lengthen. The most consequential is six
words on the first morning: "We have been watching you since your
birth—and even before"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=47) %}TBWTT 1:47{% end %}).
The clause sits inert through the second book — which, however,
deposits the adjacent facts: a conception dated 25 December 1945, a
father who was "it seems, a Jewish refugee"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=1, verse=7) %}ETTMTTP 1:7{% end %})
— and detonates in the third, where the filiation scene quotes it back
*with a page citation* ("Refer to pg. 8 of The Book Which Tells The
Truth,"
{% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=75) %}LWTE 2:75{% end %})
before resolving it: the insemination aboard a craft, the memory wipe,
the foster father chosen from a different religion than the mother's.
The three data points, published four and six years apart, dovetail —
including the gestation arithmetic. [*The Religion of
Religions*](/articles/the-religion-of-religions/) traced the public
escalation this enacts (messenger, then prophet, then son); the textual
point is different: the escalation was *retrievable* from the earlier
books, which means either the seeding was genuinely long-game, or the
author retrofitted with unusual care — and the page citations prove, at
minimum, that he re-read his own books before extending them.

Two more long-range patterns belong here. The first book's exegesis
marches from Genesis through Acts and then stops, explicitly: "We are
not going to continue reading thus the rest of the Gospels… You will
know how to translate them yourself"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=4, verse=117) %}TBWTT 4:116–117{% end %}).
One canonical book is conspicuously never opened: Revelation. It stays
sealed for six years and then becomes the spine of LWTE's embargoed
chapter, decoded throne room by throne room — the reserved text
released as the reserve. Against this stands the corpus's one dropped
hook: the first book's Kabbalah tease ("If one day you can find a
copy…," {% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=51) %}TBWTT 5:51{% end %})
is never resumed in either sequel. An economy that reserves Revelation
and forgets the Kabbalah is an economy with a bias toward texts the
audience already owns — which is a datum about the audience as much as
the author.

## The forger's examination

Now the thought experiment, stated plainly because the canon's own
epistemology invites it. Assume, for the argument, that Claude Vorilhon
invented the encounter in 1973 and composed the sequels as a serial
fiction. A fabricator writing across six years, under interview
pressure, without a continuity bible, fails in characteristic places:
throwaway numbers, minor characters, obscure details re-exposed years
later, the seams where new material meets old. The three books offer a
rich examination paper, and it is worth grading honestly in both
directions.

The maintained column is long. The six-meetings frame — 13 December
1973, six mornings, about an hour each — is restated twice in the
second book with added precision and echoed in the third without a
slip.{{ footnote(id="5") }} The 25,000-year figure runs through the
corpus as its anchor constant: the president of the Council is
twenty-five thousand years old, has inhabited 25 bodies at roughly a
thousand years each
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=56) %}TBWTT 7:30, 7:56{% end %}),
and the landing on Earth is dated twenty-five thousand years back by
book 2 ({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=43) %}ETTMTTP 2:43{% end %})
— four numbers, two books, one system. The forty-or-so banquet guests
of 1975, a throwaway count, resurface correct in 1979 ("the great
prophets – nearly 40 in all"); the count's own translation history —
French *la quarantaine*, hardened by the English edition into a
definite forty — is examined in
[*The Forty Chairs*](/articles/the-forty-chairs/). The geniocracy
thresholds (eligibility at 50 percent above average, franchise at 10)
recur verbatim, introduced with an explicit cross-reference, "As is
said in the first part of this book"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=84) %}ETTMTTP 3:84{% end %}).
The Raëlian calendar arithmetic is kept: 1946 as year 1 makes 1976
"the year 31"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=172) %}ETTMTTP 3:172{% end %})
and 1979 "the year 34"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=1) %}LWTE 2:1{% end %}).
And the third book's flashiest new number is secretly a theorem of the
first two: 666 read as generations, at twenty years each, puts the
creation of the first humans "some 13,000 years ago"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=208) %}LWTE 2:208{% end %})
— which works only because book 1 had defined the Genesis "day" as a
precessional period of about two thousand years and book 2 had dated
the landing at 25,000: six creation-days after arrival lands almost
exactly on 13,000. Nobody shows this calculation in the text. It
simply comes out right, across three books and six years.

Two further habits of the corpus complicate the fabricator model in an
interesting way. It self-labels its novelties — "a second message which
will complete the one I dictated to you in December 1973," "There is an
important revelation you can make from now on"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=104) %}ETTMTTP 2:104{% end %})
— rather than pretending they were always there. And it almost never
re-narrates what it can cite: the second and third books refer to the
first by page number, like scripture, rather than retelling it and
risking drift. A forger could adopt both habits, but they are the
habits of someone treating his own past text as a fixed document — the
posture of an archivist, not an improviser.

## Where the numbers bend

The failed column is shorter, and its shape is diagnostic. The first
book says, in passing, that "for the twenty-two thousand years since
the creators decided to do their work on Earth, everything is foreseen"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=4) %}TBWTT 5:4{% end %}).
Twenty-two thousand appears once and is never used again. Book 2 dates
the landing at twenty-five thousand; and the third book, answering an
objection, mis-cites the first — "In the first book on page 13, you
wrote that the Elohim had created the original continent 25,000 years
ago" ({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=31) %}LWTE 1:31{% end %})
— attributing to it the round figure that won. Taken strictly, the
received corpus has the Elohim landing three thousand years before they
decided to come.{{ footnote(id="4") }} This is precisely the failure
profile the thought experiment predicts of an inventor: the flagship
number is remembered everywhere; the incidental one is lost, then
overwritten in memory by the flagship. It is also, for completeness,
the failure profile of a movement's own editors summarizing their
founder — the mis-citation is in a questioner's voice — which is why
single data points decide nothing and ledgers matter.

The sharpest bend is the Council. Book 1 counts seven hundred eternals
and two hundred ten trainees, with the arithmetic itemized
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=46) %}TBWTT 7:45–46{% end %});
book 2 seats "the seven hundred Elohim members of the council of the
eternals" on their planet
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=50) %}ETTMTTP 2:50{% end %})
— maintained across the gap. Then the third book, decoding the throne
room of Revelation 4, has Yahweh say: "around me were sitting 24 other
Eternals representing the Council of the Eternals"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=134) %}LWTE 2:134{% end %}).
Revelation's twenty-four elders have imposed their headcount on a body
that had numbered seven hundred for six years; the participle
"representing" is the only escape hatch, and nothing in the text opens
it. The same pressure signature appears wherever a proof-text is open
on the desk: numbers, titles, and scenery bend toward whatever the
canon is currently decoding. Students of the Synoptic Gospels will
recognize the phenomenon — it is what redaction critics look for when
they ask which source controlled a pericope — and its presence here
argues, at minimum, that the canon composes *from* its reference texts
and not only *about* them.

The rest of the bent column can be listed briefly. The Elohim lifespan
drifts from "between seven hundred fifty and one thousand two hundred
years" to "about seven hundred" without acknowledgment. The new era's
anchor slides between 1946 (Raël's birth, the first two books'
arithmetic) and 1945 (Hiroshima, the third book's rhetoric), with one
French testimonial datelined year 33 in a chapter whose editor says
year 34.{{ footnote(id="6") }} The odds of humanity's self-destruction
wobble within a single book from nine-in-ten to one-in-a-thousand to
ninety-nine percent. And one detail collects two incompatible canonical
explanations: the halo around the speaker's head in 1973, explained
inside book 1 as a technology since superseded
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=4) %}TBWTT 7:4{% end %}),
is re-explained by book 3 as psychological staging for an unprepared
witness. Each repair is locally graceful. Jointly they leave seams, and
the seams are where the discipline reads.

What does the examination decide? Less than either camp wants, and
that finding should be stated without embarrassment. The discipline of
the corpus — the arithmetic that holds, the plants that pay off, the
self-labeled novelty — cannot certify the encounter, because the third
book's page citations prove the author re-read his own books, and a
careful re-reader can maintain anything. The bends cannot convict him,
because they are exactly as compatible with a real witness
paraphrasing under proof-text pressure as with a fabricator losing
track of a throwaway. What the examination *does* establish is the
system: a disclosure apparatus of deferral, embargo, and
source-protecting repair, run consistently across six years, whose
failures
cluster precisely where its reference texts exert force. Under the
skeptical frame, that is the fingerprint of composition from sources.
Under the canon's frame, it is what Yahweh said it would be — a message
issued to a scribe in stages, tested, corrected, and timed for an age
"when everything can be understood." The text underdetermines the
choice. It fully determines the mechanism.

## The received text is not the first printing

Everything above reads the canon synchronically, as a finished corpus.
Classical textual criticism asks a prior question: what, exactly, is
the text in front of us? For the Raëlian books the answer is that the
received English text is a late edition-state with its own transmission
history, and the evidence is on its surface.

The clearest case is a fossilized repair. Book 2 corrects book 1's
place-name — "I had, moreover, wrongly called this place the
Puy-de-la-Vache" — but this edition's book 1 already reads "the crater
of the Puy de Lassolas"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=3) %}TBWTT 1:3{% end %}).
The correction survives while its target has been silently emended: a
repair pointing at a wound that is no longer there. Manuscript critics
know this artifact intimately — it is how conflated text-types are
detected in gospel manuscripts — and here it dates itself: the
emendation postdates 1975, because the 1975 correction still assumes
the uncorrected reading.

The largest seam is organizational. MADECH — the acronym of the
association the historical 1974 edition instructed Vorilhon to found —
occurs sixteen times in the second book and zero times in the first,
whose text now has Yahweh command, in December 1973, "Your movement you
will call it the Raëlian Movement"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=29) %}TBWTT 6:29{% end %}).
As printed, the corpus has Yahweh naming in 1973 an organization the
second book says was created only in 1975, after MADECH had been
founded, led, and restructured. The harmonization was presumably meant
to tidy the founding narrative; it manufactured a contradiction
instead.{{ footnote(id="2") }} Around these two anchors cluster the
smaller witnesses: a dry-bones proof-text printed with an impossible
chapter number,{{ footnote(id="3") }} a "1979" book that closes with an
email address, interleaved French doublets from a bilingual source
edition, the year-33/year-34 dateline collision, and speaker
attributions ("Yahweh," "Narrator") that are editorial through and
through.

All of this is ordinary behavior for a living text, and the honest
conclusion is procedural. A canon that is fifty years old and already
exhibits harmonization, conflation, fossilized correction, accretion,
and editorial framing needs what every canon in that condition needs:
a critical apparatus. The French printings of 1974, 1975, and 1979
collated against each other and against the English editions; the
variants recorded; the edition-states dated. The project's
{% wiki(slug="reference-corpus-of-the-raelian-canon") %}reference-corpus
program{% end %} and its [library](/library/) are the beginning of that
work, and this article is, among other things, a list of the passages
an apparatus should visit first — TBWTT 5:4, TBWTT 6:29, ETTMTTP 2:72,
ETTMTTP 3:216, the LWTE datelines. The corpus grades other traditions'
custody chains; it owes its own canon the same forensics, and the
obligation runs even where the findings are untidy.

## The critics at the table

The critical literature deserves its own voice here, not a paraphrase.
Susan Palmer's monograph — still the standard study — records that Jean
Sendy's books, which read Genesis as the log of a cosmonaut
colonization years before 1973, were available in exactly the French
milieu Vorilhon inhabited, and notes Sendy as a possible inspiration
while recording that Raël claims direct revelation and denies having
read him. The project has examined Sendy at length in
[*The Man Who Bet the Bible on the Moon*](/articles/the-man-who-bet-the-bible-on-the-moon/);
the textual overlap is real, and the canon's own position — revelation,
not derivation — is a claim about causation that no amount of
parallel-hunting can settle. What discourse analysis can add is narrower: the
first book's *method*, a verse-by-verse technological exegesis
conducted through a demystifying French translation, has a French
bibliography whether or not its content does.

The harder criticism concerns the science, and it bites precisely
because the canon stakes so much on being the religion of the
scientific age. The memory-transfer injections that book 1 reports as
Elohim pedagogy — and cites approvingly from terrestrial rat
experiments
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=62) %}TBWTT 7:61–62{% end %})
— rode a research program that collapsed within the decade. The home
planet "a little less than a light-year" away sits inside empty space
by every survey since; the third book meets the objection with a
member-supplied hypothesis, rather than a revelation, about light
moving in "a pronounced curve"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=26) %}LWTE 1:26{% end %}),
offered with a hedge the canon rarely uses: "That is a possibility."
The Zygon critique reads the whole pattern as parasitic on science's
prestige while exempt from its discipline. A fair audit must record
both that the criticism lands and that the canon's response to it is,
again, the system this article has been describing: the difficulty is
absorbed into the staged-disclosure frame ("that will all be revealed
in more detail when they will officially arrive"), where it awaits the
{% wiki(slug="embassy") %}embassy{% end %} like everything else.

## A canon under laboratory conditions

Textual criticism normally works at a distance of centuries, on
traditions whose founders are dust and whose first editions are
reconstructions. Its practitioners infer harmonization from surviving
disagreement, posit redactors nobody met, and date embargoes — the
Markan messianic secret is arguably one — by triangulating texts
against communities. The Raëlian corpus offers the discipline
something it almost never gets: a complete canon whose entire
transmission history sits inside living memory, exhibiting the full
catalogue of phenomena — staged disclosure, retro-inserted scenes
under embargo, proof-text pressure on numbers, source-protecting
repair, harmonized editions, fossilized corrections — at observable
scale, with the paper trail still warm.

That is the finding, and it holds under both readings of the crater.
If Vorilhon invented his encounter, the three books are the most
completely documented case study in existence of how a scripture
assembles
itself: seeded, serialized, corrected, and canonized in fifty years
flat, every mechanism the ancient texts hide performed here in
daylight. If he reported one, then the speaker who managed this
disclosure — who withheld his name for two years and his fatherhood
for six, who assigned every correction to the scribe or the schedule,
who reserved Revelation and dropped the Kabbalah — was what he told a
motor-racing journalist to expect: a communicator addressing "the
first one to understand and not to believe," running a demystification
long enough to survive its own reception. Six
mornings, then six years, and the archive is still open. The last
deferral in the file — the embassy, the one disclosure the canon has
always said will settle the rest — has never needed a correction. It
is simply, as of this writing, unredeemed.
