On opening night in 2008, at the premiere of a documentary about scientists
who had lost their jobs for taking design seriously, the most famous atheist
alive was asked, on camera, whether intelligent design might after all turn
out to explain something in biology. Richard Dawkins did not laugh the
question off. He answered it.

> It could be that at some earlier time somewhere in the universe a
> civilization evolved by probably some type of Darwinian means to a very,
> very high level of technology and designed a form of life that they seeded
> onto, perhaps, this planet. Now that is a possibility, and an intriguing
> possibility, and I suppose it's possible that you might find evidence for
> that; if you looked at the details of biochemistry and molecular biology
> you might find a signature of some sort of a designer.

The film was *Expelled: No Intelligence Allowed*{{ footnote(id="1") }}, and
the answer was a small sensation, because Dawkins had just conceded, in his
own voice, that life on Earth might have been *engineered* — deliberately
made by prior intelligent beings and delivered here — and that the fact might
be *detectable*, written into the molecules, a "signature of some sort of a
designer." He recovered his footing at once. Whatever that higher
intelligence was, he added, it "would itself have had to have come about by
some explicable or ultimately explicable process" — by which he meant a
process with no designer at the bottom of it. The concession was real, and so
was the retraction, and they were separated by a single breath.

Sitting in the audience that night, also interviewed for the film, was a
philosopher of science named Stephen Meyer. He was, at that moment, finishing
a book about exactly the thing Dawkins had just named. It would be published
the next year and it would be called *Signature in the Cell*. Its argument
was that the specified information in DNA is precisely the signature Dawkins
had gestured at — and that it points not to an alien engineer but to a mind
outside the universe altogether. Twelve years later, in a bigger book, Meyer
would come back to Dawkins's alien and explain, carefully and at length, why
the engineered-by-a-prior-civilization hypothesis has to be wrong.

Two of the most consequential thinkers in the science-and-religion argument
of our time, in one room, circling the same possibility and fleeing it in
opposite directions. Dawkins reached the idea that life was designed by an
evolved civilization and recoiled because it was still *design*. Meyer
reached the idea that life was designed and recoiled because the designer was
merely a *civilization*. Between the two recoils there is a position that
neither man will stand on, and it is the position this article is about — the
one the {% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raëlian{% end %} canon has occupied, without
apology, since 1974: that life on Earth was engineered by an advanced but
entirely physical people, the {% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %}, who
were themselves engineered by an earlier people, in a chain with no first
link and no God at the top. The signature is real. The whole quarrel is over
whose hand left it.

## A family of dissents

To see why that position is interesting, you have to first clear away a
confusion that the public argument almost never clears away — the habit of
treating "creationism," "intelligent design," and "doubt about Darwin" as a
single lump. They are not one thing. They are a family, and the members of a
family quarrel. Laid out honestly, the modern dissent from undirected
evolution is a spectrum, and the Wheel of Heaven reading sits at a specific
and unexpected point on it.

At one end stands **scientific creationism** in its original and strict
sense: the young-earth position that Genesis is literal natural history, that
the Earth is some thousands of years old, that the fossil-bearing rock was
laid down by Noah's Flood, and that this can be defended as science. Its
founding document is *The Genesis Flood* (1961), by the theologian John
Whitcomb and the hydraulic engineer Henry Morris, and its manifesto is
Morris's *Scientific Creationism* (1974). It is a living tradition, and it
makes real documentaries with real production values — most successfully *Is
Genesis History?* (2017), of which more below. Its distinguishing feature is
that it takes the age of the Earth as a battlefield.

Next along the spectrum is **old-earth**, or progressive, creationism, whose
ablest contemporary voice is the astronomer Hugh Ross. Ross accepts the
deep time of mainstream cosmology and geology — the 13.8-billion-year
universe, the 4.5-billion-year Earth — and reads the "days" of Genesis as long
ages, while insisting that God intervened repeatedly to create new forms.
This branch surrenders the age of the Earth and fights on the design of it.

Then comes **Intelligent Design** proper — the movement, born around 1991,
that is careful to make neither a young-earth nor even an explicitly
biblical claim. Its foundational text is the Berkeley law professor Phillip
Johnson's *Darwin on Trial* (1991), which reframed Darwinism as a contestable
worldview rather than a settled result. Its arguments are three, and they are
built to be usable by a scientist who accepts every date in the geology
textbook: the *irreducible complexity* of molecular machines (Michael Behe,
*Darwin's Black Box*, 1996), the *specified complexity* that Behe's machines
display (William Dembski, *The Design Inference*, 1998), and the *origin of
the information* in the genetic code (Stephen Meyer, *Signature in the Cell*,
2009). Around these cluster the fine-tuning argument — that the constants of
physics are calibrated for life — and its most elegant statement, *The
Privileged Planet* (2004) by the astronomer Guillermo Gonzalez and the
philosopher Jay Richards.

Adjacent to the movement, but not quite of it, stands the **origin-of-life
skeptic**, whose purest example is the Rice University synthetic chemist
James Tour. Tour is not arguing for a designer; he is arguing that the
chemists who claim to be closing in on a naturalistic origin of life are
overselling a case they cannot cash. His is a claim about the state of the
laboratory, and it is worth taking on its own terms.

Farther along, the spectrum crosses the line into people who accept evolution
entirely. **Theistic evolution**, or evolutionary creation, is the position
of the cell biologist Kenneth Miller (*Finding Darwin's God*, 1999) and the
geneticist Francis Collins (*The Language of God*, 2006, and the BioLogos
foundation he built) — Christians who hold that undirected evolution is true
and that God is its author, working through the laws rather than around them.
They are, notably, among Intelligent Design's most effective critics: Miller's
courtroom demolition of Behe's flagellum is the single most cited rebuttal in
the field.

And at the far end stands **Richard Dawkins**, and the flat denial of any
design at all: *The Blind Watchmaker* (1986), whose subtitle — *Why the
Evidence of Evolution Reveals a Universe Without Design* — is the whole
position in nine words.

Now: where does the Wheel of Heaven reading fall on this spectrum? The
intuitive answer — somewhere near Intelligent Design — is wrong in an
instructive way, and the scholar who saw it most clearly was not a Raëlian at
all. In 2003 the sociologist of religion George Chryssides published a study
of the movement, and gave it a title that is also an argument:
**"Scientific Creationism: A Study of the Raëlian Church."**{{ footnote(id="2") }}
Chryssides's point was that the Raëlian account of human origins belongs, by
its own logic, to the creationist genus and not to the evolutionist one: it
holds that humanity was *made*, deliberately, by intelligent agents, and that
the Genesis text is a true if garbled report of the making. But it is a
*scientific* creationism in a sense the young-earth movement could never
accept — because its creators are not God, its Earth is not young, its Flood
is not the world's geology, and its designers used technology continuous with
our own. Two species, one genus. The young-earth creationist and the Raëlian
both refuse to read Genesis as pure myth. They disagree about almost
everything else.

That is the frame. The rest of this article works it out — first by taking the
design argument seriously on its strongest ground, then by watching where each
branch of the family parts from the canon, and finally by returning to the two
men in the room in 2008 and the position between their recoils.

## The signature

Begin with the part the framework grants, because it is larger than a casual
reader of a "UFO religion" would expect. The Wheel of Heaven reading holds
that the core claim of Intelligent Design — that the functional complexity of
living systems is best explained by deliberate intelligent action rather than
by undirected processes — is *substantially correct*. The
{% wiki(slug="intelligent-design") %}Intelligent Design{% end %} entry says
so in as many words. The disagreement, when it comes, will not be about
whether life is designed. It will be about who the designers are, and what
stands above them, and whether the argument can carry the theological weight
its champions want it to bear. So it is worth laying the design argument out
at its best, because the canon and the movement are, on this first stretch of
road, walking together.

The argument is old. Its classic statement is William Paley's, in 1802: cross
a heath, strike your foot on a watch, and you infer a watchmaker, because the
parts are fitted to a function in a way that chance does not produce. Paley
thought the eye was a watch, and the hand, and the whole integrated organism.
Darwin's *Origin of Species* (1859) answered him with a mechanism —
undirected variation, differential survival — by which the watch could seem to
assemble itself without a watchmaker, and for a century and a half that answer
has held the field. The modern design movement is the attempt to reopen the
case at the one place Paley could not reach: the molecular scale, which he had
no microscope to see.

Behe's move was to point at the machines inside the cell. The bacterial
flagellum is a rotary motor — a driveshaft, a bushing, a universal joint, a
propeller — built of some forty protein parts, and it does not, Behe argued,
admit of a gradual assembly, because a motor missing its driveshaft is not a
worse motor but no motor at all. He called such systems *irreducibly
complex*{{ footnote(id="4") }}: remove one well-matched part and function does
not degrade, it vanishes, so there is no graded slope of improving
intermediates for selection to climb. Dembski gave the intuition a
formalism. What the flagellum has, he argued, is *specified
complexity*{{ footnote(id="5") }} — a pattern both wildly improbable by chance
and independently specifiable as a functional description — and specified
complexity, in our uniform experience, has exactly one known source. Meyer
carried the argument to its most defensible ground: not the machine but the
*code* that specifies it. DNA is not merely complicated; it is *information*,
a sequence of chemical characters that functions like text, and the origin of
that text is the thing no undirected chemistry has been shown to produce.

> Our uniform experience affirms that specified information — whether
> inscribed in hieroglyphics, written in a book, encoded in a radio signal,
> or produced in a simulation experiment — always arises from an intelligent
> source, from a mind and not a strictly material process… Intelligent design
> best explains the DNA enigma.
>
> — Stephen Meyer, *Signature in the Cell*

It is fashionable to wave this away, and it should not be. Meyer's argument
has a spine: mind is, as far as anyone has ever observed, the only cause that
writes specified information from a blank start, and the cell is full of
specified information, and no one has watched chemistry write any. Even
Dawkins concedes the analogy — "the machine code of the genes is uncannily
computer-like," he wrote long before *Expelled* — and Bill Gates, whom Meyer
delights in quoting, went further: "DNA is like a computer program but far,
far more advanced than any software ever created." When the men who built the
software industry and the men who built the gene concept reach independently
for the same metaphor, the coincidence is not an idle one.

The hardest version of the case is not made by the design movement at all. It
is made by James Tour, who is careful to say he is not making a design
argument, and whose refusal to make one is what gives his testimony
its force. Tour builds molecular machines for a living — he has assembled, atom
by atom, a single-molecule "nanocar" with functioning wheels — and he knows
from the bench how staggeringly hard it is to make matter do what you want at
that scale. When he looks at the confident popular story of life
self-assembling in a warm pond, he sees a chemistry that does not exist. In
the concluding chapter he wrote for the 2020 edition of the origin-of-life
classic *The Mystery of Life's Origin*, he sets a thought-experiment and
answers it without mercy:

> To demonstrate how far humankind is from generating life, if
> origin-of-life researchers were given all the molecules and their polymeric
> forms that they desperately seek, and all in 100% homochirality, and their
> advanced laboratories, and all the chemical literature, and the DNA and RNA
> in any sequence (code) that they wish, could they assemble even a simple
> cell? The answer is a resounding No! Moreover, there is not an origin-of-life
> researcher on earth that would claim differently.
>
> — James Tour, *The Mystery of Life's Origin* (2020 ed.)

The point is chemistry, not metaphysics, and Tour keeps it clean. In his
origin-of-life talks he explicitly brackets out "scientifically unknown
entities that have been proposed to have seeded life on Earth, such as a
design agent (personal or impersonal)" — he will not invoke a designer,
because a designer is not a tool he can pick up in a synthetic lab. His whole
claim is that after two-thirds of a century of trying, the field, in his
word, "remains clueless." It is an admission, not an argument, and admissions
from inside the guild are worth more than arguments from outside it.

That clean, designer-free hard problem is where the canon will re-enter. But
first, what has been established. The
strongest form of the design case is not the young-earth creationist's Flood
geology and it is not even Behe's contested flagellum. It is the conjunction
of two things a fair mind can grant: that living systems run on specified
information, and that no one has ever shown specified information arising from
undirected chemistry. On that conjunction, the Raëlian canon and Stephen
Meyer agree completely. Their roads have not yet forked. They fork on the next
question, which is the only question that finally matters: *granted a
designer, what was it?*

## Is Genesis History?

Before the fork, though, one branch of the family has to be visited and, with
respect, left behind — because it is the branch a casual observer is most
likely to confuse the canon with, and the confusion is total.

*Is Genesis History?* is an accomplished film. Directed by Thomas
Purifoy Jr. and hosted by Del Tackett — the creator of the widely watched
"Truth Project" — it was released to American cinemas for one night in
February 2017, topped the per-screen box office, and spun off a whole
ecosystem of sequels and streaming series. It is beautifully shot: Tackett
walks through slot canyons and fossil beds and observatory domes,
interviewing thirteen credentialed scientists and scholars — the geologist
Andrew Snelling, the paleontologist Kurt Wise, the philosopher of biology
Paul Nelson, the Hebraist Steven Boyd — and the cumulative effect is of a
serious, confident, internally coherent worldview. Its thesis is the
strict-creationist one, stated without hedging: Genesis is literal natural
history. The Earth is thousands of years old. A global Flood carved the Grand
Canyon and buried the fossils. The languages of the world scattered at
{% wiki(slug="genesis") %}Babel{% end %}. What the film calls "observational
science" confirms Scripture, and what it calls "historical science" — the
deep-time reconstructions of mainstream geology and biology — is a rival
paradigm resting on unprovable assumptions.

Here the Wheel of Heaven reading and the young-earth reading, which
Chryssides's taxonomy files under the same genus, reveal how far apart two
species can be. The canon shares the film's foundational refusal — the
insistence that Genesis is *about something that actually happened*, that it
is report and not fable. But it shares almost nothing else. On the canon's
reading the Earth is old, exactly as mainstream geology says; the
{% wiki(slug="great-flood") %}Flood{% end %} was not a year of global
hydrology that sorted the fossil record but a targeted event, a reset and not
a rewriting of the rocks; the "days" of Genesis 1 are not calendar days but
compressed phases of a long engineering project; and the humans of Eden were
not spoken into being six thousand years ago but *synthesized*, in
laboratories, by a team of visiting scientists. The film's science is built to
defend the young Earth. The canon gives the young Earth away without a fight,
because it never needed it. What it keeps is the one thing the film and the
canon both refuse to surrender to the myth-only reading: that the text is a
memory of real events, badly transmitted, and that the job of a serious reader
is to recover the events from the memory.

This bears stating plainly, because it inoculates against a real error. To
say, with Chryssides, that Raëlism is a "scientific creationism" is *not* to
say it is Flood geology in a flying saucer. It is to locate it in the genus of
readings that take Genesis as history — and then to insist, immediately, on
the species difference, which is enormous. The young-earth creationist and the
canon are both creationists in Chryssides's structural sense and could not
sit in the same room on a single question of fact about the age of the world.
The shared genus is real, and so is the distance across it.

## The Trojan horse

There is a second thing about the design movement that honesty requires
putting on the table before going further, and it is not scientific but
political, and the Wheel of Heaven project has every reason to be candid about
it, because the candor is what distinguishes the canon's position from the
movement's.

Intelligent Design, as an American institution, did not fall from the sky. It
emerged, in the early 1990s, from the wreckage of an earlier legal defeat. In
1987 the U.S. Supreme Court, in *Edwards v. Aguillard*, ruled that teaching
"creation science" in public schools was unconstitutional establishment of
religion. Within two years, a creationist biology textbook then in
preparation, *Of Pandas and People*, had its language surgically revised: where
its drafts had said "creationists," the published book said "design
proponents." The revision was caught, years later, in a courtroom, because one
draft preserved the half-finished edit as a single fused word —
"cdesign proponentsists"{{ footnote(id="6") }} — a fossil of the exact moment
one movement changed its name into another. The court was *Kitzmiller v.
Dover* (2005), and Judge John E. Jones III, a conservative Bush appointee,
ruled after a six-week trial that Intelligent Design "is not science," that it
is "creationism re-labeled," and that teaching it in public-school biology
violates the Establishment Clause.

The judgment did not come from nowhere either. Six years earlier, an internal
Discovery Institute strategy memo had leaked. It is known as the Wedge
Document{{ footnote(id="3") }}, and it stated the movement's governing purpose
without euphemism: "to defeat scientific materialism," and "to replace
materialistic explanations with the theistic understanding that nature and
human beings are created by God." Barbara Forrest and Paul Gross laid the
whole apparatus out in *Creationism's Trojan Horse* (2004): a movement that
presented itself as disinterested science while functioning as a campaign to
smuggle a particular theism into the public square. And the scientific
rebuttals landed too. Kenneth Miller took Behe's flagship example — the
irreducibly complex flagellum — and showed that a sub-assembly of its parts
forms the *type III secretion system*, a working molecular syringe with its
own function, exactly the kind of usable intermediate Behe had said could not
exist.

None of this is comfortable for a project that agrees with the design
movement's core claim, and the way to handle discomfort is to face it. The
Wheel of Heaven reading takes three things from this history. First, that the
scientific objections to specific ID arguments — the flagellum's precursors,
the contested probability calculations — are real and must be conceded where
they land; the canon does not need Behe's flagellum to be irreducible, and
should not pretend the rebuttals do not exist. Second, that the movement's
political-religious entanglement is a fact about the *movement*, and one the
canon is entirely free of: the Raëlian reading is not lobbying a school board,
is not trying to establish a religion in a science classroom, and does not
identify its designer with the God of any national majority. Third — and this
is the substantive point that the Kitzmiller history hands to the canon almost
as a gift — the court's finding that ID is "creationism re-labeled" is a
finding about a designer who was *always meant to be God*. The whole legal and
cultural problem of movement ID is that its official agnosticism about the
designer's identity is a fiction, and everyone in the courtroom knew it. The
designer was God the whole time. Which raises the question the movement spent
thirty years not answering out loud, and which the canon answers on the first
page: *what if the designer is not God?*

## The right skill set

Here we arrive at the passage this article was built to examine — the moment
where the most rigorous mind in the design movement looks directly at the
extraterrestrial-engineering hypothesis, the canon's own position, and rules
it out.

In *Return of the God Hypothesis* (2021), Stephen Meyer sets out to do
something his earlier books did not. *Signature in the Cell* argued only that
the origin of biological information required a designing intelligence — and
Meyer conceded there, explicitly, that this intelligence *could* have been
inside the cosmos. *Return of the God Hypothesis* is the attempt to close that
door: to show that the designer must be not merely intelligent but
transcendent — God, or something very like him — by adding two pieces of
evidence that biology alone cannot supply. The first is the beginning of the
universe (the Big Bang, which Meyer reads as the origin of matter, space and
time from nothing). The second is the fine-tuning of the physical constants,
set, he argues, at that beginning. And it is against *these* two data — not
against the origin of life — that he tests the extraterrestrial hypothesis and
finds it wanting.

He names it precisely. The relevant option, he writes, is an "immanent
intelligence" — one "within the cosmos," a "space alien" — of the sort
proposed by advocates of what he calls, following Crick, *panspermia*. And he
grants it more than his critics would expect. He concedes, twice, that such an
immanent intelligence *could in principle explain the origin of life on
Earth*:

> Even if we concede as a logical possibility that an immanent intelligence
> might explain the origin of life on earth (since such an entity could
> possibly precede it), the panspermia hypothesis does not explain either the
> ultimate origin of life in the universe or the fine tuning of the universe
> — to say nothing of the origin of the universe itself.
>
> — Stephen Meyer, *Return of the God Hypothesis*, ch. 13

The dismissal, then, has two prongs, and both are real arguments, not the
lazy "it's materialism, therefore false" one might fear. The first is the
regress: an alien designer only relocates the mystery.

> Simply asserting that life arose somewhere else out in the cosmos does not
> explain how the information necessary to build the first life, let alone the
> first intelligent life, could have arisen. It merely pushes the explanatory
> challenge farther back in time and out into space.

The second is the one he leans on hardest — the argument he titles, in his own
section heading, "The Right Skill Set."{{ footnote(id="8") }} Any intelligence
*within* the cosmos arose *after* the beginning of the cosmos, and therefore
after its fine-tuning was already fixed. So no such being could have set the
constants; only a mind existing "independently of the material universe" has
the causal reach to do that.

> No such being subject to those laws could possibly change the constants of
> physics simply by changing the material state of the universe… It follows
> that an immanent intelligence (an extraterrestrial alien, for instance)
> fails to qualify as a causally adequate explanation for the origin of the
> cosmic fine tuning.

This is a serious argument, and the first thing to say about it is that, on
its own terms, *it is valid*. If the universe had an absolute beginning, and
if the fine-tuning was set at that beginning, then no being that came into
existence after the beginning could be responsible for it, and an
extraterrestrial civilization — however old — came into existence after the
beginning. The logic is clean. Meyer has not committed a fallacy. He has done
something subtler: he has won an argument against a claim his opponent never
made.

The substitution is easy to miss. The hypothesis on trial was: *the
biological complexity of life on Earth is best explained by a prior
intelligent civilization.* That is
the Crick-Orgel hypothesis; it is Dawkins's *Expelled* concession; it is the
Raëlian canon. It is a claim about *terrestrial biology*. Meyer concedes it —
concedes that an immanent intelligence could explain the origin of life on
Earth. And then he convicts it of failing to explain two things it never
addressed: the origin of the universe, and the fine-tuning of the universe.
Of course it doesn't explain those. It was never a cosmological theory. It was
a theory about a cell. Meyer has silently promoted a hypothesis about biology
into a candidate Theory of Everything, and then disqualified it for losing a
race it did not enter.

To see how much work the substitution is doing, restate Meyer's own two
prongs against his own preferred answer. *The regress:* he says the alien
designer merely "pushes the explanatory challenge farther back." True. But
positing God pushes it back too — indeed infinitely farther, to a being whose
own existence and information-generating mind are, by construction, exempt
from all explanation. "Who designed the designer?" is a question Meyer answers
by fiat (God is uncaused) and the canon answers by honesty (there is no first
designer, and none is needed). Both push the challenge back. Only one pretends
it has stopped pushing. *The skill set:* Meyer says only a being outside the
material universe can set the constants. That is true *if the constants were
set* — if there was a first moment at which the dial was turned. Remove the
beginning, and the argument has nothing to grip.

And this is where the Raëlian canon has already, in a companion to
this article, removed the beginning.

## The universe with no beginning

Meyer's entire case for a transcendent designer rests on a finite-past
cosmos: a universe that began, whose fine-tuning was fixed at the start, and
which therefore requires a fine-tuner who preceded the start. Take away that
foundation and the "right skill set" argument does not become weaker; it
becomes inapplicable, because there is no privileged first moment for the
skill to have acted at, and nothing that was ever "set."

The canon takes it away, and it did so half a century before Meyer wrote. When
Yahweh explains the shape of reality to {% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %},
he states, as flatly as Meyer states the opposite, that the universe has no
edge in space and no edge in time:

{% library(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=103) %}
It is as foolish to search for the beginning of the universe in time as it is
to search for the beginning of space.
{% end %}

Matter is eternal; "nothing is lost, nothing is created, all is transformed"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=34) %}ETTMTTP 2:34{% end %}).
There was no origin of the universe, and so there is no origin event at which
the constants were dialed in by a mind reaching from outside — because there is
no outside, and there was no beginning. The companion Explainer
[*The Infinite in Both Directions*](/articles/the-infinite-in-both-directions/)
develops this ontology at length: a {% wiki(slug="fractal-cosmology") %}fractal
cosmos{% end %}, infinite up and down the scale of size, with no centre and no
first moment, in which our whole observable universe is "a particle of an atom
of a molecule" in the body of a vastly larger being. That article shows the
picture is not a mystical indulgence but a recognizable, if minority, position
in professional physics — one that eternal inflation and several
quantum-gravity programs keep independently rediscovering. The bearing on
*this* article is direct: **Meyer's cosmological premise is not
neutral bedrock. It is a contested cosmological claim, and it is precisely the
claim the canon denies on independent grounds.**

Once the beginning is gone, Meyer's dichotomy collapses. He offers a clean
menu — either a transcendent mind that preceded the universe and set its
constants, or a naturalism that explains nothing — and the extraterrestrial is
squeezed out between them because it is neither pre-cosmic nor
self-explaining. But the menu only has two items because the universe is
assumed to have a beginning that demands explanation. In a beginningless,
infinite cosmos, the fine-tuning of "the constants" is not a one-time act of
setting; it is, at most, a local and possibly eternal feature of one region of
an endless structure — the kind of thing that, on the canon's reading, no more
requires a pre-cosmic fine-tuner than the temperature of a particular pond
requires a pond-maker who existed before water. Meyer's transcendent designer
is the answer to a question — "who set the dials at the start?" — that the canon
regards, in Yahweh's words, as "a stupid question which proves that the person
who poses this question has not taken consciousness of the infinite."

So the dismissal fails, though not for the reason a hostile reader might reach
for. It is not shallow: it is a valid argument resting on a premise its target rejects, aimed
at a claim its target never made. Strip the smuggled cosmology and the
smuggled scope, and what Meyer has actually established is the *opposite* of
what he intended: that an immanent intelligence is a fully adequate
explanation for the design of life on Earth — which he concedes — and that it
declines to also be a theory of the origin of everything, which the canon
never asked it to be, and which the canon thinks no theory can honestly be.

## The third vertex

Return now to the room in 2008, because the two recoils make sense at last.

Dawkins reached the engineered-life hypothesis and fled because the designer
was still a designer — but notice *how* he fled. The alien, he insisted, "would
itself have had to have come about by some explicable or ultimately explicable
process," a fully evolved being with no design at *its* bottom. Meyer reached
the same hypothesis and fled in the other direction — the designer was not
transcendent enough, a mere civilization inside the cosmos, unable to ground
the fine-tuning. What both men are refusing, from opposite sides, is the same
thing: a designer who is *real but not ultimate*. Dawkins needs the chain to
bottom out in undirected matter (no designer at the root). Meyer needs it to
bottom out in an undesigned mind (God at the root). Both need a *bottom*. The
extraterrestrial engineer offends both because it is a designer who neither
grounds the chain nor dissolves it — a made maker, pointing to an earlier
made maker, with no terminus in either direction.

That is the canon's position, stated in the source material
as calmly as a fact of natural history:

{% library(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=97) %}
The Elohim created us, and other people from another planet created them. Who
created the creators of the Elohim?
{% end %}

The question is posed and then *dissolved*, not answered — because in an
infinite cosmos it is the same shape of question as "where is the edge of
space," and it has the same non-answer. There is no first designer. The chain
of {% wiki(slug="forerunners") %}makers{% end %} runs backward without end, as
the ladder of scales runs up and down without end. This is not evasion; it is
the single most philosophically disciplined feature of the whole framework,
and it is the exact point on which it out-thinks the movement it otherwise
resembles. Movement Intelligent Design inherited, from Paley and from
scholasticism, a designer who must be first — uncaused, unmade, the terminus
that stops the regress. The canon inherited, from the same infinite cosmos
that has no beginning in time, a designer who need not be first at all.

The philosopher who saw this possibility earliest was, of all people, the
design argument's greatest critic. In his *Dialogues Concerning Natural
Religion* (1779), David Hume let his skeptic Philo grant the design inference
for the sake of argument, and then observe how little it delivers: the
designer thus inferred "might be" finite, or fallible, or an apprentice, or
one of a committee, or long dead, or utterly unlike the God of
worship.{{ footnote(id="10") }} Hume meant this as a demolition — a proof that
the design argument, run honestly, cannot reach the deity it was built to
reach. Movement ID has spent thirty years trying to answer Hume by closing the
gap upward, arguing that the designer really is singular and transcendent
after all. The Raëlian canon does something Hume did not anticipate: it accepts
his demolition as a *finding*, and builds on it. Yes — the inferred designer is
finite, plural, and unlike the God of worship. It is a civilization. Hume's
reductio is the canon's blueprint. The design inference under-determines the
designer, and the honest terminus of the inference is not God but the Elohim —
"men like you"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=53) %}TBWTT 1:53{% end %}),
who say of themselves, without grandeur, "I am one of those who created life on
Earth"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=24) %}TBWTT 2:24{% end %}).

There is a further irony here, one that closes the circle back to
the film. What Dawkins conceded in *Expelled* — that a civilization "evolved by
probably some type of Darwinian means to a very, very high level of
technology" might have "designed a form of life" and "seeded" it here, and
that you might find "a signature of some sort of a designer" in the
biochemistry — *is the Elohim hypothesis*, spoken by the man least likely to
hold it, and then abandoned only because the designer, too, must be explained.
But "the designer, too, must be explained" is a demand for a bottom, and the
demand only bites if you assume the chain must have one. Dawkins and Meyer share
that assumption. It is the deepest thing they have in common, buried under
their famous disagreement, and it is the assumption the canon simply does not
make. Strip it away and Dawkins's own sentence stands: the signature could be
there, and a prior evolved civilization could have left it, and that
civilization need be neither God nor the last word.

## What the canon and the movement actually share

It would be dishonest to let the divergence swallow the convergence, so the
overlap deserves a precise accounting — how much of the design case the
framework keeps, once the theology is subtracted.

It keeps the information argument. Meyer's claim that specified information
comes only from minds is one the framework reads as substantially correct —
with the difference that the mind in question is embodied, evolved, and in
principle able to be met. It keeps Tour's hard problem, and keeps it *more*
cleanly than the movement does. Tour brackets out "a design agent
(personal or impersonal)" because a designer is not a chemical tool. The canon
supplies the designer Tour brackets — and it is precisely *not* the
impersonal-or-supernatural agent he means to exclude, but a physical
civilization doing physical biochemistry. Where mainstream origin-of-life
research says "keep looking for the undirected pathway," and Meyer says
"therefore God," the canon says something continuous with the laboratory
itself: *someone made it — someone whose methods we are now, ourselves,
beginning to reproduce.*

That last clause is not rhetoric. The Crick-Orgel directed-panspermia
paper{{ footnote(id="7") }} — two of the twentieth century's greatest molecular
biologists proposing, in a peer-reviewed journal in 1973, that life on Earth
was deliberately seeded by an extraterrestrial intelligence — established that
the canon's *kind* of claim is speakable in serious biology. Crick's *Life
Itself* closed with a chapter asking whether humanity should one day seed
other worlds. And in the years since, humanity has walked most of the way to
the door. In 2010 Craig Venter's institute booted a bacterial cell running a
chemically synthesized genome; in 2016 they published a *designed* minimal
genome, a living organism whose 473-gene blueprint was written to
specification and installed.{{ footnote(id="9") }} The canon's account of the
Elohim is not a fairy tale about gods; it is a description of
{% wiki(slug="synthetic-genomics") %}synthetic genomics{% end %}, dated 25,000
years early. The Elohim, on the canon's telling, did to us what Venter's team
did to *Mycoplasma* — and told us we would learn to do it in turn:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=102) %}
Man must respect nature as long as he is not capable of recreating it, as long
as he is not capable of becoming himself a creator. In respecting nature, you
respect those who created it, our fathers the Elohim.
{% end %}

This is the deepest structural difference between the canon and every other
branch of the design family, and it is a difference in the *direction* the
designer points. For the young-earth creationist, the movement ID theorist,
and Meyer alike, the designer points *away* from science — upward, to a mind
that biology can gesture at but never study, a mind whose methods are by
definition not our methods. For the canon, the designer points *toward*
science — to a civilization whose engineering is continuous with ours, a few
tens of millennia ahead, whose signature in the cell is not a proof of the
supernatural but a résumé we are learning to read because we are learning to
write it. When the framework reads Genesis 1:26 —

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=26) %}
«Let us make man in our image, after our likeness! And let them have dominion
over the fish of the sea, and over the fowl of the air, and over the cattle,
and over all the wild beasts, and over every creeping thing that creepeth upon
the earth!» (Genesis, I-26)
{% end %}

— it does not hear a solitary God soliloquizing in the royal plural. It hears a
{% wiki(slug="council-of-eternals") %}team{% end %} of scientists at a bench,
comparing results — "the most skilled among us wanted to create a man like
ourselves, artificially"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=25) %}TBWTT 2:25{% end %}) —
and the next line reads less like doctrine than like a lab notebook: "In our
image! You can see that the resemblance is striking"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=27) %}TBWTT 2:27{% end %}).

## What would actually count

The reason to prefer the canon's designer to Meyer's is not comfort.
It is that the canon's designer is, in principle, the kind of thing that could be checked — and a
project that says so owes an account of what checking would look like. This is
the part the design movement has been criticized for skipping, and the canon
should not skip it.

Meyer's transcendent mind is, by construction, beyond investigation: no
experiment bears on a being outside the material universe, which is exactly
why the Kitzmiller court could call ID unfalsifiable. The Elohim are not beyond
investigation, and this is the framework's real methodological advantage over
movement ID — the one the {% wiki(slug="intelligent-design") %}wiki entry{% end %}
names as the "empirical signatures of design" question. If terrestrial life,
or human life specifically, was engineered by an outside civilization, then
the engineering should have left *marks*, and the marks are the research
program. What would count?

Some candidates are old and were named by Crick and Orgel themselves: the eerie
universality of the genetic code across all terrestrial life, consistent with a
single founding event rather than many; the biochemical over-reliance on
elements, like molybdenum, that are scarce in Earth's crust, as if the
founding organisms had evolved somewhere with a different elemental budget.
Some are new: a genome read with the eye of a synthetic biologist, looking for
the signatures of design that Venter's team must embed to *watermark* their own
synthetic cells — regions too optimized, too modular, too much like
engineering and too little like the redundant, jury-rigged legacy code that
undirected evolution leaves behind. Some are frankly unresolved and belong on
the table as problems, not triumphs. The canon's dating of the principal
human-synthesis event to roughly 25,000 years ago sits in real tension with
the mainstream palaeoanthropological record of anatomically modern humans at
200,000 years; the framework treats this as an open question, and honesty
requires flagging it as one of the sharpest, not brushing past it. And James
Tour's clean, designer-free chemistry problem — "a resounding No!" — is not
evidence *for* the Elohim; it is, at most, the removal of the leading rival, and
the canon should want it kept as clean as Tour keeps it, because a hard
problem in abiogenesis is a hole in the naturalistic story, not a positive
identification of who filled it.

That is the honest shape of it. The framework's designer is checkable in
principle and unconfirmed in fact; its central historical claims arrive by
testimony — a contactee's report — and cannot be verified the way a genome
anomaly can be measured, which is why this project labels those claims
`framework` and labels this article, whose central argument is an interpretive
reframing that runs ahead of any single source, `speculative`. The point of
the reframing is not to declare victory. It is to show that the
extraterrestrial-engineering hypothesis is *not* the easy casualty Meyer's
brisk paragraphs suggest — that it survives his best argument intact, because
his best argument is aimed at a cosmological claim it never made and rests on a
cosmological premise it explicitly denies — and that it is, uniquely among the
options, the one that keeps the designer inside the reach of the very science
the whole debate claims to honor.

## The hand in the cell

Go back one last time to what Dawkins said, because it turns out to have been
the truest sentence spoken in that room, by the man who least wanted it to be
true. You might, he said, "find a signature of some sort of a designer" in the
details of biochemistry and molecular biology.

Meyer built a career on agreeing with the first half of that sentence. The
young-earth creationists agree with it, and the old-earth creationists, and
Behe and Dembski and Tour in his careful bracketed way, and — reading Genesis as
a report rather than a fable — the Raëlian canon too. The signature is the
common ground of the entire family of dissents, from the Flood geologist to
the contactee. What splits the family is the second half — *whose* signature —
and there the options are fewer than the noise suggests. Either the hand in the
cell belongs to a mind outside the universe, which no instrument can ever
reach; or there is no hand and the appearance of one is the residue of a blind
process, which is the position that has to explain away Tour's resounding No;
or the hand belongs to someone. Someone with a body. Someone who worked at a
bench, compared results with a colleague, and found the resemblance striking.
Someone who was made, as we were made, by someone earlier, in a line that runs
back past every horizon we can see and never arrives at a first maker or a
final one.

That third answer is the one Dawkins touched and dropped, and the one Meyer
lifted and threw back, and the one the canon has simply been holding, in the
open, for fifty years. It is not a compromise between the atheist and the
theist. It is the vertex that reveals what their quarrel was resting on — the
shared, unexamined conviction that a designed world would have to be a
*supernaturally* designed world, that "made" must mean "made by God." Deny that
one premise, and the whole map redraws. Design stops being the property line
between science and religion and becomes an ordinary empirical question with an
extraordinary answer: that life on Earth is a made thing, its makers were
people, and we are, right now, in our own laboratories, beginning to learn
their trade.

The watch on the heath had a watchmaker. Paley was right about that. He was
only wrong about the last thing anyone would think to question — that the
watchmaker had to be the last one in the line.
