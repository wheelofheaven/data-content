Sometime in the last centuries of antiquity — the exact date is one of the
things this essay is about: a Hebrew writer set down the height of God.
He did it in units. The figure is delivered by an angel, sworn like an
affidavit, and priced in *parasangs*{{ footnote(id="1") }}, the Persian
league a traveler's feet knew as an hour's walk: from the seat of glory
upward, 118 myriads; from the seat of glory downward, 118 myriads; the height
entire, 236 myriads of thousands. The text goes on to measure the soles, the
ankles, the neck, the crown of the head, each with its secret name, and then,
as if anticipating the reader reaching for a slate, it defines its terms: a
parasang is three miles, a mile ten thousand cubits, a cubit three spans —
and a span is the fullness of the whole world.

Nineteen or so centuries later, in December 1973, a twenty-seven-year-old
French motor-racing journalist named Claude Vorilhon —
{% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %} — reported that a being called
{% wiki(slug="yahweh") %}Yahweh{% end %} spent six days explaining the Hebrew
Bible to him inside a volcanic crater in the Auvergne. In the book he
published the following year, the being pauses over one tradition above all
the others:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=51) %}
Traces of the truth — there are not only in the Bible and the Gospels;
testimonies are found in practically all the religions. The Kabbalah notably
is one of the books richest in testimonies, but it would not have been easy
for you to procure one. If one day you can find a copy, you will be able to
observe in it a very great number of allusions to us. In particular a
description in the Song of Songs (V) of the planet of the creators as well as
the distance that separates it from the Earth. It is said there that the
«height of the creator» is 236,000 «parasangs» and that the «height of his
heels» is 30 million «parasangs».
{% end %}

Three paragraphs later comes the flat superlative that gives this essay
its title: *"The Kabbalah is the book closest to the truth"*
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=54) %}TBWTT 5:54{% end %}).

Most of the claims in the Raëlian canon are readings — a laboratory behind
{% wiki(slug="genesis") %}Genesis{% end %}, a reset behind the
{% wiki(slug="great-flood") %}flood{% end %}. This one is a citation. It
names a corpus, a chapter, two numbers, and a unit, and every element can be
checked. Doing the checking honestly takes the whole length of this article,
because the trail runs through the strangest text in Jewish mysticism, a
three-way scholarly fight about its age, a millennium of theological
embarrassment about its contents, and — at the very end — a cheap French
paperback with a telltale translation error. The destination is worth
stating up front: **the passage Yahweh quotes exists.** It is called the
*Shi'ur Qomah* (Hebrew שִׁעוּר קוֹמָה, *šiʿur qômāh*, "the Measure of the
Stature"), and the Wheel of Heaven project has translated it in full:
[*Shi'ur Qomah* — Wheel of Heaven Translation](/library/shiur-qomah-woh/).
What the numbers *mean* is exactly the point at which the mainstream and the
canon part ways, and this article will mark that line when it comes to it.

## What the Kabbalah is — and is not

The canon's own phrasing contains a puzzle, and it begins with the word. The
{% wiki(slug="kabbalah") %}Kabbalah{% end %} is not a book. Hebrew
קַבָּלָה (*qabbalah*) means "reception," "that which is received," from the
root ק־ב־ל (*q-b-l*), "to receive" — an ordinary, working verb of the
language (a *kabbalah* is a shop receipt in modern Israel; *kabbalat
Shabbat* is the Friday-evening liturgy that "receives" the Sabbath).
Applied to a body of teaching, the word makes a specific and audacious
claim: this knowledge was never composed, deduced, or discovered. It was
*handed over* — mouth to ear, teacher to student, under restriction, for
centuries. The same verb opens the Mishnah's famous chain of custody:
*"Moses received [kibbel] the Torah at Sinai and transmitted it to Joshua,
Joshua to the Elders, the Elders to the Prophets, and the Prophets to the
Men of the Great Assembly"* (Pirkei Avot 1:1). The tradition's
self-description — שַׁלְשֶׁלֶת הַקַּבָּלָה, *shalshelet ha-Kabbalah*, "the
chain of the Tradition" — reaches back through
{% wiki(slug="moses") %}Moses{% end %} and keeps going. Alexandre Safran
(1910–2006), who was Chief Rabbi of Romania through the war years and then
Chief Rabbi of Geneva for half a century, put the traditional claim in a
sentence: *"The Kabbalah exceeds, in antiquity, the Revelation of Sinai; it
dates back to prehistoric times. Moses only introduced it into the history
of Israel."* Rabbinic Judaism institutionalized the caution as much as the
content. The early rabbis recognized two esoteric curricula — מַעֲשֵׂה
בְּרֵאשִׁית (*ma'aseh bereshit*), "the work of creation," the secrets
behind the first chapter of Genesis, and מַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה (*ma'aseh
merkavah*), "the work of the chariot,"{{ footnote(id="3") }} the secrets
behind Ezekiel's throne-vision — and the Mishnah forbids expounding the
second even before a single student, unless he is wise and understands of
his own accord (Hagigah 2:1).

When the received teaching finally crystallized into books, it did so late
and in stages. The סֵפֶר יְצִירָה (*Sefer Yetzirah*, "Book of Formation"),
a cosmological tract of only a few thousand words composed somewhere
between the second and sixth centuries CE, builds the universe out of
"thirty-two paths of wisdom" — the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew
alphabet plus ten primordial numbers it calls סְפִירוֹת (*sefirot*) — and
contains no measurements of God; modern scholarship has even read its
insistence that the divine "measure is ten" as a quiet rebuke to the
measurement tradition. The *Sefer ha-Bahir* ("Book of Brightness")
surfaces in Provence at the end of the twelfth century and turns the
*sefirot* into living divine powers. The זֹהַר (*Zohar*, "Book of
Splendor"), the vast Aramaic masterpiece the word "Kabbalah" conventionally
evokes, was composed in Castile in the 1280s — presented as the teachings
of a second-century sage, though modern scholarship attributes it
principally to Moses de León — and it articulates the mature system: the
unknowable infinite Godhead, אֵין סוֹף (*Ein Sof*, "Without End"),
unfolding toward the world through ten *sefirot* arranged as the
{% wiki(slug="tree-of-life") %}Tree of Life{% end %}. Isaac Luria's circle
at Safed rebuilt the system once more in the sixteenth century around
cosmic catastrophe and repair. This is the library the word "Kabbalah"
normally names — and none of it is the source of Yahweh's quotation. In
all its bulk, the *Zohar* gives the old measurement tradition exactly one
passing glance (Zohar II 175b–176a). If "the Kabbalah" meant the famous
books, the canon's citation would fail.

It does not fail, because there is an older stratum — and describing it
requires a word most readers will not have met.

## Before the Kabbalah: the palace literature

Between roughly the third and eighth centuries CE — the dating fight gets
its own section below — anonymous Jewish mystics produced a body of
writings that scholarship calls the **Hekhalot literature**, from
הֵיכָלוֹת (*hekhalot*), "palaces" or "halls."{{ footnote(id="2") }} These
texts describe the ascent of the initiate through seven concentric
heavenly palaces, each with armed angelic gatekeepers, to the throne-room
of God — the מֶרְכָּבָה (*merkavah*), the wheeled chariot-throne that the
prophet Ezekiel saw by the river Chebar. With a
perversity the texts never explain, the adepts call this ascent a
*descent*: they are יוֹרְדֵי מֶרְכָּבָה (*yordei merkavah*), "descenders
to the chariot." The traveler needs equipment — hymns to recite at each
gate, the names of the doorkeepers, seals to display — and the literature
is, in large part, that equipment: page after page of unpronounceable
angelic names, adjurations, and liturgy. Alongside the ascent texts stand
rituals that run the journey in reverse, compelling an angel *down* to
earth to grant the one gift this culture prized above every other:
total, permanent, unforgettable knowledge of Torah. The principal works
carry names like *Hekhalot Rabbati* ("The Greater Palaces"), *Hekhalot
Zutarti* ("The Lesser Palaces"), *Merkavah Rabbah* ("The Great Chariot"),
and *3 Enoch* — and all of them are written in the names of the great
second-century rabbis{{ footnote(id="4") }} Ishmael and Akiva, who serve
the tradition as its two licensed travelers: Akiva, in the Talmud's famous
legend, the only one of four sages to enter the *pardes* — the orchard,
paradise — and come out whole.

Two things about this literature matter for our trail. The first is its
standing. Gershom Scholem (1897–1982) — the Berlin-born historian who
emigrated to Jerusalem in 1923, took up the first university chair ever
devoted to Jewish mysticism, and built the modern academic field around
his own bibliography — spent a career insisting that this material was no
heretical fringe: "the bearers of these speculations were at the very
center of rabbinic Judaism in tannaitic and talmudic times... The gnosis
we are dealing with here is a strictly orthodox Jewish one." The second is
its physical condition. The Hekhalot texts survive in medieval manuscripts
that refuse to settle into fixed books; the same material circulates in
different arrangements, under different titles, from one manuscript to the
next. Embedded in this shifting corpus, quoted by it, circulating through
it, is the measurement text we are hunting. And the canon's category
slip — calling a tradition a "book" you might "procure a copy" of — turns
out to be a clue rather than an error, because the measurement text *did*
become a book you could procure: from 1701 onward it was carried inside
the printed editions of the סֵפֶר רָזִיאֵל הַמַּלְאָךְ (*Sefer Raziel
ha-Malakh*, "Book of Raziel the Angel"), a grimoire whose own framing
legend says the angel Raziel delivered it to Adam, the first man. The
recension of the *Shi'ur Qomah* that the Sefaria library prints today is
reproduced from exactly there — folio 37b of the Amsterdam 1701 edition.

## The Measure of the Stature

The *Shi'ur Qomah* announces itself as testimony — a deposition, angelically
notarized. Rabbi Ishmael reports the oath of the angel
Metatron{{ footnote(id="8") }}:

{% library(book="shiur-qomah-woh", chapter=1, verse=2, interlinear=true) %}
R. Yishmael said: Metatron, the Great Prince of Testimony, said to me: I
testify with this testimony by YHWH, the Elohim of Israel, the living and
enduring God, exalted and great, lofty and uplifted, who rescues us and
redeems us and delivers me from every evil thing.
{% end %}

The text's name states its business. שִׁעוּר (*shiʿur*) is "measure,"
"quantity," "amount" — the same word a modern Israeli uses for a school
lesson; קוֹמָה (*qomah*) is "stature" or "height" — in modern Hebrew, the
floor of a building — and in the Aramaic of the period it can mean simply
"body." *Shiʿur qomah*: the measure of the stature; or, as Scholem
preferred to hear it, the measure of the body. The question the text
answers is posed inside it, in Rabbi Ishmael's own voice:

{% library(book="shiur-qomah-woh", chapter=1, verse=9, interlinear=true) %}
R. Yishmael says: how great is the measure of the stature of the Holy One,
blessed be He, who is concealed from all creatures.
{% end %}

And the answer arrives under oath:

{% library(book="shiur-qomah-woh", chapter=1, verse=3, interlinear=true) %}
From the seat of his glory upward, 118 myriads, and from the seat of his
glory downward, 118 myriads. His height is 236 myriads of thousands of
parasangs.
{% end %}

The symmetry — 118 up, 118 down — is deliberate, and so is the idiom, and
following both requires two facts about how Hebrew handles numbers. First:
the Hebrew of this literature writes no numerals. Quantities are spelled
out in words, stacked from named blocks — the אֶלֶף (*elef*), one
thousand, and the רִבּוֹא (*ribbo*), the myriad of ten
thousand.{{ footnote(id="5") }} "His height is 236 myriads of thousands of
parasangs" — רוּמֵיהּ מָאתַיִם וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה רִבְבוֹת אֲלָפִים
פַּרְסָאוֹת — therefore unpacks as 236 × 10,000 × 1,000: two billion, three
hundred sixty million parasangs. Second: every Hebrew letter doubles as a
number (א = 1, ב = 2, and so on up to ת = 400), which means every Hebrew
word is also a sum — the interpretive art called
gematria.{{ footnote(id="7") }} That is where the 236 comes from. Psalm
147:5 praises God as גָּדוֹל אֲדוֹנֵינוּ וְרַב כֹּחַ (*gadol adoneinu
ve-rav koaḥ*), "Great is our Lord and mighty in power" — and the letters of
*ve-rav koaḥ* count out as ו 6 + ר 200 + ב 2 + כ 20 + ח 8 = 236. The
mystics read the verse as if it said: *Great is our Lord — and his measure
is 236*. From that seed the text unfolds downward and outward, limb by
limb, in the order a temple attendant might wash a statue: soles, ankles,
calves, thighs, loins, neck, skull, beard, eyes, shoulders, arms, fingers,
toes — each with a dimension, and each with an unpronounceable name. The
soles come first:

{% library(book="shiur-qomah-woh", chapter=1, verse=10, interlinear=true) %}
The soles of his feet fill the whole world entire, as it is said: 'Thus said
YHWH: the skies are my throne and the land is my footstool.'
{% end %}

In the *Sefer Raziel* recension{{ footnote(id="6") }} — the printed one, the
procurable one — the height of those soles is שְׁלֹשׁ רִבְבוֹת אֲלָפִים
פַּרְסָאוֹת (*shalosh rivevot alafim parsa'ot*): three myriads of
thousands, thirty million parasangs. And in the Sefer Haqqomah recension —
translated from Oxford MS 1791 by Martin Samuel Cohen, the American
rabbi-scholar whose Jewish Theological Seminary dissertation grew into the
text's standard modern study and, in 1985, its first critical edition —
the same line reads: "The height of His soles is 30,000,000 parasangs; its
name is Parmeseh." There is Yahweh's second figure, sitting in the
manuscripts. The first figure is stranger and more interesting. The critical
text says 236 myriads of thousands — 2.36 billion. Yahweh says 236,000. But
the tradition itself already carries the truncated form: the tenth-century
Karaite{{ footnote(id="11") }} polemicist al-Qirqisānī, quoting the text in
order to mock it, reports that "His height from the sole of His foot up to
His entire stature comprises 236,000 parasangs." Copyists and hostile
witnesses had been dropping the myriads for a thousand years before anyone
in the Auvergne did. That truncation returns, with consequences, in the
provenance audit at the end of this article.

The unit itself carries a history and, in Hebrew, a pun. The parasang —
פַּרְסָה (*parsah*), plural פַּרְסָאוֹת (*parsa'ot*) — is a loanword from
Persian *farsang*, the league of the Achaemenid royal roads, and it
entered rabbinic Hebrew as the Talmud's everyday unit of long distance.
But the same three root letters, פ־ר־ס, also spell the Hebrew for a hoof
or the sole of a foot — so the recensions can say, in what is either an
accident of homonymy or a scribe's quiet joke, that the *parsot* of his
feet measure so many *parsa'ot*. And the text totals itself, in a
summation that abandons precision altogether:

{% library(book="shiur-qomah-woh", chapter=1, verse=39, interlinear=true) %}
It turns out that the whole measure entire is a great-many-myriads of
myriads of thousands of parasangs in height, and a thousand thousand
myriads of parasangs in breadth.
{% end %}

Two more features of the text matter for everything that follows. The first
is its conversion table. Someone — the text stages it as Metatron
pre-empting Rabbi Ishmael's arithmetic — defines the unit:

{% library(book="shiur-qomah-woh", chapter=1, verse=31, interlinear=true) %}
Declare the reckoning of the parasangs — how great they are. Each and every
parasang is three miles; each and every mile is 10,000 cubits; each cubit is
⟨…⟩ spans by his span; and his span is the fullness of the whole world
entire.
{% end %}

These are divine parasangs. Each one is built out of spans, and each span is
a universe wide. The second feature is the promise attached to knowing all
this — the most-quoted lines of the text in the whole later tradition, and
on their face the strangest:

{% library(book="shiur-qomah-woh", chapter=1, verse=41, interlinear=true) %}
R. Yishmael said: I and R. Akiva are guarantors in this matter — that
whoever knows this measure of our Creator and the praise of the Holy One,
blessed be He, is assured that he is a child of the world to come; provided
only that he recites it as Mishnah every single day.
{% end %}

Two sages standing surety, in the technical legal sense, for the salvation
of anyone who *recites the measurements daily*. Whatever else this text may
be, it is a practice.

## Song of Songs, chapter five

Why does the canon say the description sits "in the Song of Songs (V)"? At
first glance this is another category slip — the Song of Songs is a biblical
book, eight chapters of love poetry, and no parasang appears anywhere in it.
Its fifth chapter contains the bride's praise of her beloved's body:

{% library(book="song-of-solomon", chapter=5, verse=10, verse_end=15) %}
My beloved is white and ruddy, the chiefest among ten thousand. His head is
as the most fine gold... His hands are as rings of gold set with beryl: his
body is as ivory work overlaid with sapphires. His legs are as pillars of
marble, set upon sockets of fine gold.
{% end %}

The link runs deeper than a glance shows. Second-century tradition read the
Song's beloved as God himself, seen by all Israel at the Red Sea and at
Sinai. And the *Shi'ur Qomah* itself quotes Song
5:10–16 — verbatim, and in the Merkavah Rabbah recension the verses are even
rearranged out of biblical order to match the sequence of the limbs just
measured. The connection is inside the text, not imposed on it. On the
strength of it, Scholem argued — together with Saul Lieberman (1898–1983),
the Jewish Theological Seminary professor whom his peers regarded as the
greatest Talmudist of the century — that the *Shi'ur Qomah* began life as
esoteric midrash on Song of Songs 5; Lieberman called it outright "an
early midrash" on Song 5:10–16. Scholem added a beautiful indirect argument: Origen (c. 185–253), the most
learned Christian scholar of his age, writing in Caesarea around 240 CE,
reports that the Jews reserved
four scriptural texts "for the very end" of a student's education — the
beginning of Genesis, the beginning and end of Ezekiel, and the Song of
Songs. The first three are the known esoteric syllabus of the Mishnah. The
Song's presence on the restricted list makes sense, Scholem argued, only if
by Origen's day it was already the scriptural anchor of the measurement
doctrine.

Cohen, who produced the critical edition, pushed back hard: the God of the *Shi'ur Qomah* "has neither golden head nor
ivory loins nor marble calves"; the Song describes its beloved head-down
while the *Shi'ur Qomah* measures feet-up; and the Song's verses function in
the text as liturgical framing, quoted like the Psalms that surround them,
never as prooftexts for a single measurement. On Cohen's reading, the Song
lent the text legitimacy and atmosphere; it did not generate it. The dispute
is live and unresolved, and this project preserves it rather than settling
it. What matters for our purposes is narrower and firmer: from the second
century onward, the tradition itself tied the vision of the divine body to
Song of Songs chapter 5 — so that when Scholem came to summarize the
doctrine for modern readers, he did it in a single sentence that fused the
two: the fragments describe the body of the Creator *"in close analogy to
the description of the body of the beloved one in the fifth chapter of the
Song of Solomon... the height of the Creator is 236,000 parasangs —
according to another tradition, the height of His soles alone is 30 million
parasangs."* That single sentence of Scholem's turns out to be the hinge of
the provenance question this article closes with.

## How old is the text?

The dating question deserves a section of its own, because the canon's aside
— *"it would not have been easy for you to procure one"* — happens to be the
one claim every party to the scholarly fight would countersign.

Scholem dated the doctrine to the second century CE. His evidence is a
chain of early shadows: the Gnostic Marcus (a disciple of Valentinus, active
around 170 CE), whose system of a divine "body of Truth" with letter-named
limbs Scholem read as a borrowing from Jewish sources; Justin Martyr,
reporting around 160 CE — as normative Jewish teaching, not as heresy — that
God has shape and organs; the Slavonic *2 Enoch*, likely first century,
whose Enoch says *"I saw the extent of the Lord, without measure and without
image and without end"*; and the Origen argument above. Hugo Odeberg
(1898–1973), the Swedish theologian at Lund who gave *3 Enoch* its name
and produced its first edition in 1928, took the parallel traditions back
further still, into the first two centuries. Cohen, in 1983, dismantled
the early dating piece by piece — the Marcus parallel dissolves on
inspection, the Origen report is secondhand — and rebuilt the case for
composition in Babylonia in the sixth or seventh century, early in the age
of the geonim{{ footnote(id="9") }}: late enough to quote the completed
Talmud, early enough that Eleazar ha-Kallir, the great liturgical poet of
Byzantine Palestine, already alludes to the text and to its psalm-verse.
Peter Schäfer (b. 1943) — the historian of Judaism at Berlin and later
Princeton whose 1981 *Synopse zur Hekhalot-Literatur* printed the major
manuscripts side by side in parallel columns and forced the field to see
how fluid they are — then questioned whether there was ever a single text
to date at all:
the *Shi'ur Qomah* materials "are found scattered in various places
throughout the manuscripts... and have evidently never reached the stage of
a unifying, let alone a final redaction." The search for the original, he
concluded, is "methodologically obsolete." His deepest maxim about this
whole literature — manuscripts that refuse to settle into books — is that
*"the one text is an illusion."*

A working range, honestly stated: traditions with plausible roots in the
second century (some would say the first), a composition perhaps as late as
the seventh, textual fluidity throughout, and no critical edition until
1985. At every point in that range the text was esoteric — restricted by the
Mishnah's own rules, recited by initiates, copied in closed circles,
condemned or hidden for most of the centuries in between. And by 1973 it
had still never been published in French translation; the audit in the
final section found only summaries. As an aside delivered in a volcano
crater to a sports journalist, *"it would not have been easy for you to
procure one"* is, at minimum, well informed.

## The angel who fills the world

Before turning to what the measurements mean, one figure in the text needs
a proper introduction, because he shows how far this tradition could take
a body — and how carefully it policed the limit. *3 Enoch*'s hero is
Enoch, the antediluvian patriarch of Genesis who "walked with God, and was
not, for God took him." In the Hekhalot telling, what God took him *for*
was transfiguration: Enoch becomes Metatron, highest of the angels. "I was
raised and enlarged to the size of the length and width of the world," the
angel testifies in Odeberg's translation; he receives seventy-two wings,
each spanning the world, and three hundred sixty-five eyes, each like the
sun; a throne is built for him resembling the Throne of Glory; and — most
dangerously, on the warrant of Exodus 23:21, "for my name is in him" — he
is called יהוה הַקָּטָן, "the lesser YHWH." Our text knows this figure
intimately and handles him with precision:

{% library(book="shiur-qomah-woh", chapter=1, verse=56, interlinear=true) %}
And this youth — his stature fills the world, and the Holy One, blessed be
He, calls him 'youth.' And this youth is Metatron, Prince of the Presence.
{% end %}

A servant whose stature fills the world, in attendance on a Master whose
*span* is the world: the *Shi'ur Qomah* keeps the two bodies rigorously
distinct. The measured body of the great enumeration belongs to the
Creator — the text names him יוֹצֵר (*yotzer*), "the Fashioner" — and
never to the angel, however cosmic the angel's stature. Metatron is styled
נַעַר (*na'ar*), "youth" or "servant-lad," precisely to hold the rank
line. The Talmud remembers what happened to the one mystic who let the
distinction blur: Elisha ben Avuyah, seeing Metatron enthroned in heaven,
exclaimed "perhaps — God forbid — there are two powers!", and came down
the arch-heretic of rabbinic memory, renamed *Aḥer*, "the Other One"
(b. Hagigah 15a). A tradition this anxious about a second body in heaven
was never going to be casual about the first one.

## What the tradition says the numbers mean

Before the canon's reading, the tradition's own — stated with full force,
because it is powerful, and because the entire later history of Judaism's
relationship with this text turns on it.

The key observation was already made above: the text defines its own unit,
and the definition detonates the measurements. If a span is the width of
the universe, a cubit is three universes, a mile is thirty thousand, a
parasang ninety thousand — and 2.36 billion such parasangs is a quantity
arithmetic can write but no mind can picture. Cohen did the multiplication
soberly and concluded that the final tally comes to "twelve quadrillion
universe-lengths," a figure that "was not meant as an empirically correct
figure... but rather as an essentially inconceivable notion upon which the
mystic might focus for the sake of his meditative technique." Scholem read
the same feature as controlled paradox: "the tremendous dimensions make any
contemplation illusory"; the anthropomorphism is pushed until "all
measurements fail, and the strident anthropomorphism is suddenly and
paradoxically transformed into its opposite: the spiritual." The text
itself says as much at its center. Having priced the beard at a myriad and
a half of parasangs, it stops dead before the face:

{% library(book="shiur-qomah-woh", chapter=1, verse=19, interlinear=true) %}
His beard, a myriad and 1,500 parasangs. Its name is HADRAQMASYA. And the
appearance of the face and the appearance of the cheeks is like the form
of a breath and in the shape of a soul; no creature is able to recognize
it.
{% end %}

The dissent should be heard too: David Halperin, whose *The Faces of the
Chariot* (1988) remains the fullest account of how the merkavah tradition
grew, argued that the anthropomorphism means what it says — that these
authors believed in a God with a real, colossal body, and that modern
readers spiritualize the text to spare their own embarrassment. Schäfer
answered him directly, and drew the opposite conclusion most sharply. The
point of the "completely absurd calculations,"
he wrote, "is to demonstrate that God cannot be conceived of in human
categories: he, 'as it were,' is like a human being and yet hidden... God is
transcendent and immanent, at the same time hidden and revealed." What the
mystic actually takes away is the *names*: "the 'nonsense' is the message:
everything consists of names." On this reading the *Shi'ur Qomah* is
apophatic{{ footnote(id="10") }} theology conducted by overdose — liturgy
and theurgy, in Cohen's summary phrase, a daily recitation whose reward the
two tannaim guarantee.

The tradition paid a heavy price for the text all the same. The Karaites
brandished it as proof of rabbinic corporealism. The geonim answered
delicately — Sherira Gaon (c. 906–1006), who led the ancient Babylonian
academy of Pumbedita, replied to an anxious query from the Jews of Fez:
"our Creator is too high and sublime to have organs and measurements in
the literal sense; rather, these are words of wisdom that cannot be
conveyed to everyone." Moses ben Maimon — Maimonides, medieval Judaism's
supreme legal codifier and philosopher — had in his youth treated the text
as an authoritative work awaiting interpretation; he later deleted the
reference from his Commentary on the Mishnah and issued one of the most
violent book-verdicts in Jewish history: "I never thought that
this came from the Sages... it is undoubtedly no more than the work of a
Byzantine preacher. All in all, it would be a highly meritorious deed to
snuff out this book and to destroy all memory of it" (Responsa, ed. Blau,
no. 117). The Kabbalists rescued it by transformation: for the *Zohar*'s
circle and for Moses Cordovero (1522–1570), the great systematic
theologian of Safed Kabbalah, whose own boldest speculative work proudly
reused the title *Shi'ur Qomah* — the measured limbs became the ten
*sefirot*, the body became the Tree of
Life, and the scandal became the watchword of a mysticism that,
in Scholem's words, "found it an honor, rather than an embarrassment, to
speak about the Shi'ur Komah." The measurements themselves — the actual
numbers — were quietly allowed to fall silent.

## The canon's counter-reading

Here is the line, marked as promised: everything above is philology and
theology on the mainstream's own terms. What follows is the Wheel of Heaven
frame, which reads the text against the grain of fifteen centuries of
spiritualization.

The canon's position is that the numbers went silent because nobody could
any longer hear what they were saying. On Yahweh's reading, the *Shi'ur
Qomah* is a garbled record of two real distances, preserved by a tradition
that faithfully recited what it had stopped understanding — and he supplies
the missing key, the unit:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=52) %}
The parasang, which, like the parsec, is a unit of measure, equals the
distance that light travels in one second, that is, about 300,000
kilometers. Our planet is at 30 million parasangs, that is, about nine
thousand billion kilometers, or a little less than a light-year.
{% end %}

The arithmetic is checkable. Thirty million light-seconds is
8.99 × 10¹² kilometers — 0.95 light-years, "a little less than a light-year,"
exactly as the book says; for scale, mainstream astronomy places the nearest
known star, Proxima Centauri, at 4.25 light-years. The second figure is
handled in the next breath: the 236,000 parasangs are the distance from the
{% wiki(slug="elohim-home-planet") %}planet of the creators{% end %} to its
own sun — 70.8 billion kilometers, says the canon, which is 473 astronomical
units, a planet orbiting roughly sixteen times farther out than Neptune,
around "a big star" whose greater luminosity would make such an orbit
habitable. And the anatomical language gets a decoding rule of its own: the
"height of the heels" is *"the distance at which his heels rest on a
planet"* ({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=53) %}TBWTT 5:53{% end %})
— the body of the Creator stands, so to speak, with its feet on Earth and
its seat of glory at home, and the limb-measurements are stations on a
route. Where the mystical tradition saw a body too vast to picture, the
canon sees something closer to a navigational mnemonic wearing a body's
shape — the one form in which two interstellar distances could survive
three thousand years of oral transmission inside a culture that had lost
the referent.

Read from inside the frame, several features of the text snap into focus.
The measurements are delivered as sworn *testimony*, by a named witness,
with a guarantor formula — the genre of a deposition, not a hymn. The
tradition transmitting it insisted, against its own theological comfort,
that the numbers were the payload: recite them daily, exactly, as Mishnah.
The figures come with a unit-conversion table — a thing love poetry does
not need. And the wild variance of the figures across recensions — 236,000
in Qirqisānī, 2.36 billion in the critical text, 237 in the Jewish
Encyclopedia's transcription, 30 million for the soles here, 6,000 there —
is, on the canon's reading, precisely the degradation profile of numerical
data copied for centuries by scribes to whom the quantities meant nothing.
The mainstream explanation for that variance — ordinary textual fluidity in
a literature Schäfer showed never had a fixed text at all — is simpler and
should be stated plainly. Both readings predict the same manuscripts. That
is what makes the passage a genuine interpretive fork rather than a
checkmate for either side; the canon's reading is speculative in exactly
the sense this project uses the word, and it is also, verse for verse, the
only reading on offer that explains why a liturgical text would carry a
conversion table.

One more feature deserves its weight. Of all the world's scriptures — and
the canon surveys many — this is the only one that assigns the divine a
magnitude in named units and then defines the unit. The Raëlian claim that
the Kabbalah is "the book closest to the truth" is, at bottom, a claim
about that singularity: everyone else's theophanies came through as
imagery; this tradition's came through as *data*, however corrupted. Even
the tradition's own chain-of-custody story — a teaching older than Sinai,
received rather than composed, delivered to Adam by an angel according to
the very book that carried the fragment into print — rhymes with the
canon's account of what it actually was: information handed down from
{% wiki(slug="elohim") %}elsewhere{% end %}, guarded by people who knew it
was important long after they had forgotten why.

## What could Claude Vorilhon have known in 1973?

The claim also permits a provenance test. If the *Shi'ur Qomah* was esoteric,
untranslated into French, and textually chaotic, then a checkable question presents itself: could a
racing journalist with no Hebrew have gotten those two numbers from a
library rather than from a being in a crater? This project went looking —
systematically, book by book, in the sources a French reader of 1973 could
actually have held. The results, stated with their evidence:

**The scholarly package existed, in English, from 1941.** Scholem's *Major
Trends in Jewish Mysticism* contains, on one page, every element of the
canon's citation: the Song of Songs chapter 5 framing, the height of the
Creator as 236,000 parasangs (Scholem used the truncated Qirqisānī-style
figure), and "according to another tradition, the height of His soles alone
is 30 million parasangs." The French translation (Payot) appeared in 1950.
Scholem's Hebrew has *soles* — no heel in sight.

**The Jewish Encyclopedia (1906) had the figures with a variance.** Its
"Shi'ur Ḳomah" article gives the soles at 30 million and the height as "237
myriad times 1,000 parasangs" — 237, a transcription variant that does not
match the canon's 236,000. English-language, and a poor textual fit.

**Vulliaud (1923) discussed the text without any figures.** Paul
Vulliaud, the French Catholic painter and esotericist, defends the
"Schiour Qoma" as allegory in two passages of his two-volume *La Kabbale
juive* and never prints a number. **Jean Sendy** — the French writer whose paleoastronautic
reading of Genesis is the canon's closest published predecessor, and who
devoted a full chapter to the Cabala — never cites the *Shi'ur Qomah* or any
parasang figure in *La Lune, clé de la Bible* (1968) or *Ces dieux qui
firent le ciel et la terre* (1969); both were checked in full text, as were
Robert Charroux's Kabbalah chapters. (Sendy's earlier *Les Cahiers de cours
de Moïse*, 1963, could not be obtained for this audit and remains an open
item.)

**And then there is Sérouya.** Henri Sérouya, a Jerusalem-born philosopher
who spent his Paris career writing on mysticism and had published a
scholarly volume on the Kabbalah with Grasset in 1947, distilled his
subject into *La Kabbale*, no. 1105 in the Que
sais-je?{{ footnote(id="12") }} series — in print continuously from 1957,
revised 1967, available in any French railway-station bookshop for a few
francs. In it he compresses Scholem's page into one paragraph. In it, the
hyperbolic dimensions of the Creator's body "ont leur analogie dans le
Cantique des Cantiques (V)": the height of the Creator is "236 000
parasanges," and, for another tradition, *"la hauteur de ses talons est de
30 millions de parasanges."* *Talons* — heels. The Hebrew says soles
(*parsot raglav*); Scholem's English says soles; Sérouya, condensing,
wrote heels. The canon says heels. It also cites the chapter exactly as
Sérouya does — "(V)," Roman numeral, in parentheses — and reproduces
Sérouya's collapse of Scholem's careful "in close analogy to the Song of
Songs" into the claim that the description is *in* the Song of Songs, which
is false of the biblical book and true of nothing except this specific
chain of summaries. A shared number can be a coincidence. A shared
translation error, a shared citation format, and a shared category mistake,
stacked in a single short paragraph, strongly suggests filiation. It is the
same reasoning philologists use on the *Shi'ur Qomah*'s own manuscripts. To this project's
knowledge — and we searched for a precedent — this identification of the
canon passage's proximate textual profile has not been published before;
treat it as this essay's own finding, offered with its evidence.

What the audit did *not* find matters just as much. No pre-1973 source —
not Scholem, not Sérouya, not the encyclopedias, not the paleoastronautic
literature — equates the parasang with a light-second. The historical
parasang is six kilometers of Persian road; the
parsec{{ footnote(id="13") }} is an unrelated coinage of 1913; the leap to
"the distance light travels in one second," which turns a devotional text
into two coherent astronomical distances, appears for the first time in
*The Book Which Tells the Truth* itself. The skeptical reading of the audit
is straightforward and should be given without flinching: the canon's
citation looks like a paperback's summary of Scholem, heels-error included,
with an original numerological flourish on top. The canon-internal reading
is also available, and the canon itself supplies it: Yahweh tells Vorilhon
he was chosen in part because he lived in "a country where new ideas are
well received"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=46) %}TBWTT 1:46{% end %})
— a messenger meets his sources where his culture keeps them, and
a being explaining an esoteric Hebrew text to a Frenchman in 1973 might
reasonably quote it in the only form the Frenchman's world had transmitted
it, truncations, talons, and all. What the reader does at this fork is the
reader's affair; the project's obligation was to map it.

## The wager of the guarantors

Seen whole, the story has a strange symmetry. A text
older than the Kabbalah proper spends fifteen centuries being recited,
denounced, allegorized, and rescued — everyone from Sherira Gaon to
Cordovero agreeing, whatever else they disagreed about, that its numbers
must not mean what they say. Modern scholarship, at its best, turned that
instinct into a profound reading: measurement as the deliberate wreckage of
measurement, the parasang table as a machine for breaking the mind's grip,
the names as the real cargo. The Raëlian canon arrives from outside the
whole tradition and takes the opposite wager: the numbers mean exactly what
they say — only the *referent* was lost, the unit misfiled, the body a
container for a route. Between those wagers this project does not
adjudicate; it translates the text, preserves the disputes, and marks the
lines. But it is worth noticing which single verse both wagers leave fully
intact. Two sages once stood surety that whoever *knows this measure* — who
carries the numbers forward, understood or not — is assured of the world to
come, provided only that he recites them every day. For fifteen hundred
years, somebody always did. Whatever the measurements are — liturgy,
theurgy, telemetry — the guarantee, at least, was honored: the numbers
survived long enough for the twentieth century to argue about them, and for
a book published in 1974 to call the tradition that carried them the
closest one to the truth.
