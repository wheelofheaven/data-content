On Christmas Eve of 2007, at a family dinner in California, an
eighty-eight-year-old physicist named Edward Lovick leaned over to a
journalist — his relation by an improbable chain of marriages — and said,
*"Have I got a good story for you."* Lovick had spent three decades making
aircraft invisible to radar at Lockheed's Skunk Works, much of it at a
place in the Nevada desert whose name the government would not say aloud.
The journalist was Annie Jacobsen. The book she built from that dinner
conversation, *Area 51* (2011), opened with a promise that has governed
everything she has written since: "This book is a work of nonfiction. The
stories I tell in this narrative are real. None of the people are
invented."

What she has written since amounts to a shelf. *Area 51* was followed by
*Operation Paperclip* (2014), on the Nazi scientists America imported;
*The Pentagon's Brain* (2015), a history of DARPA that made her a Pulitzer
Prize finalist; *Phenomena* (2017), on the government's own decades of
classified research into extrasensory perception; *Surprise, Kill,
Vanish* (2019), on CIA paramilitary operations; *First Platoon* (2021),
on biometric war; and *Nuclear War: A Scenario* (2024), a minute-by-minute
reconstruction of how deterrence ends, which spent months on the
bestseller lists and is now being developed for film. Her method has never
varied: named sources on the record wherever possible, Freedom of
Information Act requests by the dozen, declassified paper checked against
living memory. Her subject has never varied either, though it took the
shelf some years to make this obvious. Annie Jacobsen writes the history
of the machinery the United States built after 1945 — the weapons, the
secrecy that grew around the weapons, and the people consumed by both.

This project reads that shelf with unusual attention, for a reason worth
stating at the outset. The Raëlian canon — the corpus received by
{% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %} in 1973 and the foundation of
everything Wheel of Heaven does — dates the decisive period of human
history from the same year Jacobsen's shelf documents. It calls the era
that opened with the Hiroshima detonation the Age of
{% wiki(slug="apocalypse") %}Apocalypse{% end %}, and it makes two claims
about it: that *apocalypse* means revelation, the age in which everything
hidden can finally be understood{{ footnote(id="1") }}, and that the same
age poses a single, terminal question — whether a species that has just
acquired the means of its own destruction can master the aggression that
would trigger it. Jacobsen has never engaged the canon. Nothing in her
books or interviews suggests she has heard of it, and everything in her
temperament — a reporter who checks warhead counts with the Federation of
American Scientists — suggests she would file it, unread, with the
mythologies her Area 51 sources spent careers hiding behind. That
independence is precisely what makes her useful here. When a secular
national-security reporter, working from FOIA releases and on-record
interviews, spends twenty years documenting the same three-act structure
the canon asserted in 1973 — a bargain, a veil, and an exam — the
convergence tells you something, whatever you decide it tells you.

## The unveiling business

Jacobsen's *Area 51* carries an epigraph from Horace: "Time will bring to
light whatever is hidden; it will cover up and conceal what is now shining
in splendor." It is as close as she comes to a credo, and it is worth
pausing on how exactly it describes her trade. The postwar security state
invented a new ontology for its secrets. Under
the Atomic Energy Act of 1946, information about nuclear weapons became
*born classified* — secret from the moment of its conception, no stamp
required, no reviewing officer, no sunset. "It is from the Atomic Energy
Act of 1946 that the concept 'born classified' came to be," she writes in
the prologue to *Area 51*, adding that the Atomic Energy Commission
controlled the base "with terrifying and unprecedented power." Against the
common belief in automatic declassification she is blunt:

> There is a broad misconception in America that there exists some kind of
> automatic declassification system that requires the government to reveal
> its secret programs after thirty or fifty years. In reality, the most
> damaging programs often remain classified for as long as they can be
> kept secret.
>
> — *Operation Paperclip*, ch. 23

Her books are the counter-force: applications of legal pressure and
patient interviewing to a system engineered never to open. In *Operation
Paperclip* she describes prying a file called "List of Personnel Involved
in Medical Research and Mercy Killings" out of a Harvard archive whose
access card read "Restricted Until 2015." In *Area 51* she reports that a
presidential advisory committee under Bill Clinton was denied access to
EG&G's records "on the grounds that the president did not have a
need-to-know." A working reporter, in other words, operates at what her
latest book calls the razor's edge of the legally knowable, and every book
she writes moves the edge.

The canon has a name for this labor. Its reading of *apocalypse* as
*unveiling* is set out in the source material in so many words — "the
'Age of Revelation', that is to say, the age when everything can be
understood, and not the 'end of the world'"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=8) %}LWTE 1:8{% end %})
— and the age is given a precise opening date{{ footnote(id="2") }}. The
Elohim state that they chose their last messenger *because of* the bomb:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=47) %}
Lastly, we decided to choose someone after the first atomic explosion,
which took place in 1945, and you were born in 1946. We have been watching
you since your birth—and even before.
{% end %}

Within the frame, then, the detonation over Hiroshima opened two processes
at once: a countdown and a disclosure. Everything hidden would begin
coming to light — the plurality of the {% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %}
under the singular "God," the operational content of the old texts, and,
more mundanely, the archives of the states that had just acquired the
creators' own signature weapon. Jacobsen would put it differently in every
particular. But her shelf is what the unveiling of a weapons state looks
like when it is performed with subpoenas instead of testimony, and the
three books this article now takes in turn — *Operation Paperclip*,
*Area 51*, *Nuclear War: A Scenario* — map onto the age's structure with
a neatness the canon's editors could not have improved.

## The bargain

*Operation Paperclip* is the earliest chapter of the story,
chronologically: 1945, the rubble of the Reich, and the sixteen hundred
German scientists and technicians the United States decided it would
rather employ than prosecute. Jacobsen's prologue states her subject with
the directness of a charge sheet: "This is a book about Nazi scientists
and American government secrets. It is about how dark truths can be hidden
from the public by U.S. officials in the name of national security."
Twenty-one men carry the narrative; eight of them, she notes, had worked
directly alongside Hitler, Himmler, or Göring.

The book's moral center is the tunnel complex at Nordhausen, where the
V-2 — the world's first ballistic missile, ancestor of every delivery
vehicle in *Nuclear War: A Scenario* — was assembled by concentration-camp
prisoners:

> The prisoners worked twelve-hour shifts, seven days a week, putting
> together V-weapons.… The dead were replaceable. Humans and machine parts
> went into the tunnels. Rockets and corpses came out.
>
> — *Operation Paperclip*, ch. 1

Roughly half of the sixty thousand men sent to Nordhausen died there.
Wernher von Braun, an SS major who visited the tunnels ten times in the
winter of 1945, ended the arc of the book as the avuncular television
evangelist of the American space age — his 1955 Disney broadcast *Man in
Space* drew an estimated 42 million viewers — and then as the architect of
the Saturn V, with the Mittelwerk's former operations director, Arthur
Rudolph, as his deputy on the program. Von Braun's own retrospective
conscience, offered to a *New Yorker* writer, is quoted without softening:
"We felt no moral scruples about the possible future use of our
brainchild.… It was simply a question with us of how the golden cow could
be milked most successfully." When the Ford White House weighed giving him
the Medal of Freedom, an adviser's note — "frankly he has gotten as good
as he has given" — settled the matter; Rudolph, threatened at last with a
war-crimes trial, surrendered his citizenship and left the country in
1984. The deeper cases are darker still: Otto Ambros, IG Farben's sarin
co-inventor and the manager of the Buna plant at Auschwitz, convicted at
Nuremberg of mass murder and slavery, released under American clemency in
1951 with "his finances fully restored," and later engaged as a consultant
by the Department of Energy — the renamed Atomic Energy Commission.
Jacobsen's governing question is asked by two Manhattan Project physicists
she quotes, Hans Bethe and Henri Sack, protesting the program in 1947:
"Do we want science at any price?"

The canon's interest in this story is exact rather than general. Its
sixth chapter opens with a diagnosis of the same pathology the Paperclip
bargain institutionalized — genius harnessed by the less wise for
destruction:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=3) %}
Whether he discovers the wheel, gunpowder, the internal-combustion engine,
or the atom, the genius has always made the power of men less intelligent
than himself benefit from his inventions, often using peaceful inventions
for murderous ends. This must change!
{% end %}

The proposed remedy, {% wiki(slug="geniocracy") %}geniocracy{% end %},
belongs to a later section of this article. What belongs here is the
canon's treatment of individual responsibility inside a chain of command,
because it converges with Jacobsen's central question — does accomplishment
cancel crime? — while answering it more severely than any denazification
board did. The second volume of the source material groups the pilot of
the Hiroshima aircraft with the war criminals of the camps, refuses the
defense of orders in both cases, and then extends the refusal forward, to
the missileers of the present:

{% library(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=3, verse=58) %}
The man who launched the bomb on Hiroshima was only following orders. In
every powerful country, there are people who are ready to launch nuclear
missiles in good faith and with a clear conscience, knowing that they are
only 'doing their duty in executing the orders received'. They are
responsible for their actions! … the man who delivers a bomb on a city is
more responsible than the one who made the decision.
{% end %}

Jacobsen closes *Operation Paperclip* in the living room of Gerhard
Maschkowski, a survivor of the Auschwitz Buna plant Ambros managed. She
asks what lasts. "He pushed back the sleeve on his shirt and showed me his
blue-ink Auschwitz tattoo. 'This lasts,' he said. 'But it is also a record
of [the] truth.'" Her verdict on the program stays interrogative — "When,
for a nation, should the end justify the means? These are questions that
can only be answered separately, by individuals" — and the restraint is
honest, because her evidence has already answered. The bargain worked. The
rockets flew, the moon was reached, and the men of the tunnels died with
their case unheard. What the bargain purchased, though, is the detail the
canon would have a reader hold on to: the United States acquired, in a
single decade and partly from the servants of a death-cult state, the
missile and the warhead together — the exact pairing that the rest of
Jacobsen's shelf, and the rest of this article, is about.

## The veil

*Area 51* looks like a book about a place and is actually a book about a
mechanism. The place — a dry lake bed annexed to the Nevada Test Site,
where 105 nuclear weapons were fired above ground and 828 below between
1951 and 1992 — hosted the U-2, the A-12 OXCART, the captured MiGs, the
stealth prototypes. The mechanism is the one her subtitle calls
*uncensored history*: the way a security state, by refusing to explain
what people see in the sky, manufactures a mythology it can then hide
behind.

Her fourth chapter documents the process with the CIA's own paper. As
soon as the U-2 began flying from the base in 1955, sightings poured in;
the aircraft flew at altitudes commercial pilots thought impossible, its
silver wings catching the sun long after ground level had gone dark.

> By 1957, according to the CIA monograph "CIA's Role in the Study of
> UFOs," the U-2s accounted for more than half of all UFO sightings
> reported in the continental United States.
>
> — *Area 51*, ch. 4

The Agency's response was a standing three-part policy she quotes from an
internal memo: keep files on UFOs, deny keeping files on UFOs, and
quietly divide the sightings its own aircraft could explain from the rest.
Air Force officials told Congress the files did not exist while Project
Blue Book's analysts, a handful of whom held the necessary clearances,
matched public reports against classified flight logs. "It didn't take
long for the public to become convinced that the CIA was covering
something up," Jacobsen writes, "which, of course, it was." By the OXCART
years the loop had closed completely: FBI agents met airliners to collect
secrecy oaths from passengers who had seen the craft, and since almost no
Air Force investigator was cleared for the program, the government's own
UFO desk was cataloguing the government's own aircraft as unknowns. The
CIA historian Gerald Haines, in the monograph she relies on, concedes the
structural result: the concealment itself "contributed greatly to later
charges of a CIA conspiracy and cover-up."

For this project the significance of that record cuts in two directions
at once, and honesty requires stating both. Read deflationarily, *Area
51* is the best documented account in print of how much of the postwar
saucer mythology was terrestrial hardware plus institutional silence — a
caution the corpus's own {% wiki(slug="ufology") %}ufology{% end %} entry
already carries. Read the other way, it establishes, from the state's own
archive, the premise of the corpus's
{% wiki(slug="military-cover-up-of-exobiology") %}military cover-up{% end %}
entry: that official denials in this domain carry no evidentiary weight
whatsoever, because the deniers spent five decades demonstrably lying, by
policy, about exactly this subject. A reader who wants to dismiss the 2017–2024
disclosure cycle — the Navy videos, the ODNI reports, the Grusch
testimony — with "the government would have told us" has *Area 51* to
answer to. The canon, it should be said, needs nothing from Roswell
either way: its contact event is Clermont-Ferrand, 1973, six mornings of
conversation, and the corpus treats the 1947 crash agnostically. Which
clears the ground for the audit this article owes.

### The audit: the engineer's story

The final chapter of *Area 51* made the book famous and nearly sank it.
An anonymous EG&G engineer — one of five, he told Jacobsen, cleared onto
a program she says explains the base's whole numerological mystique —
described receiving, in 1951, the remains of the Roswell crash: a saucer
"remotely piloted," bearing Cyrillic block letters, and containing
child-sized aviators, "grotesquely deformed, but each in the same manner
as the others," two of them still alive. The engineers were told the
children were the work of Josef Mengele, engaged by Stalin to produce
convincing aliens; the craft was to land in America and disgorge them,
triggering a *War of the Worlds* panic that would paralyze early-warning
radar and humiliate Truman. Asked why Truman buried it, the engineer gave
the answer that darkens the whole book: "Because we were doing the same
thing.… We performed medical experiments on handicapped children and
prisoners.… People were killed. In this great United States."

The story must be audited with the same discipline this project applies
to its own materials, and the audit is short, because the story fails
quickly. It rests on a single anonymous source, then in his late eighties,
reporting a hearsay chain two links deep — what his boss said a
"government superior" said. It requires Mengele to have worked for Stalin
in the late 1940s, and Mengele's postwar trail is among the best
documented of any fugitive's{{ footnote(id="8") }}: Bavaria, Argentina,
Paraguay, Brazil, a drowning at Bertioga in 1979, an exhumation, a DNA
match. Jacobsen knows this — she interviewed Gerald Posner, Mengele's
principal biographer, for the book — and her own endnotes carry the
concession the chapter's prose withholds: "One cannot rule out the
possibility that the elite EG&G engineers were given false information as
a means of coercing them into participating in a morally reprehensible
program." That sentence, buried in the apparatus, is almost certainly the
truth of the matter. Somebody was told a story in 1951; the story served
its teller; the mainstream account of the debris{{ footnote(id="9") }} has
no need of it. As history of the crash, the chapter does not survive.

What survives is the same thing that survived the audit of Paul Wallis's
Akhekh etymology in [an earlier article in this
series](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/): the structure
underneath the failed particular. Strip the Mengele decoration away and
the engineer's story asserts three things *Area 51* proves independently —
that Roswell's remains went into the Atomic Energy Commission's
compartments, beyond even presidential reach; that states deliberately
seed and exploit flying-saucer belief as cover ("by overfocusing on
Martians," as Jacobsen puts it, people "would pay less attention to other
UFO realities"); and that the national-security apparatus of the period
was willing to conduct monstrous human experiments under the atomic
umbrella, a fact the declassified radiation-experiment record has since
placed beyond argument. Her closing verdict on the secret-keeping itself —
"this kind of secret-keeping — of this kind of secret — is the work of
totalitarian states, like the one we fought against for five decades" —
stands whatever crashed at Corona. So does the book's last image, which
could serve as this project's method statement: "What has been revealed in
this book is like a single bread crumb in a trail. There is so much more
that remains unknown."

## The exam

*Nuclear War: A Scenario* is the book the first two were preparing, and
it is unlike anything else in the literature of its subject. Jacobsen
interviewed some four dozen principals — a former secretary of defense, a
former STRATCOM commander, the designer of the first thermonuclear
device, the FEMA director, the climatologists of nuclear winter, a Secret
Service director, the archaeologist of Göbekli Tepe — and built from
their answers a single fictional war, clocked to the second, every beat
of which is sourced to policy, physics, or precedent. Her author's note
sets the stakes without decoration: "The nuclear war scenario proposed in
this book could happen tomorrow. Or later today." Her purpose, stated in
the acknowledgments, is equally plain: "The fundamental idea behind this
book is to demonstrate, in appalling detail, just how horrifying nuclear
war would be."

The scenario runs as follows. North Korea launches a single
intercontinental missile at the Pentagon, without warning and for reasons
never given — "a Bolt out of the Blue," the attack the system's own
planners fear most. American satellites see the launch within seconds.
Under the standing policy of Launch on Warning{{ footnote(id="3") }},
whose logic former defense secretary William Perry states to her on the
record — "Once we are warned of a nuclear attack, we prepare to launch.
This is policy. We do not wait." — the machinery of retaliation begins
moving while the missile is still climbing. The president, who holds sole
launch authority and asks no one's permission{{ footnote(id="4") }}, is
given roughly six minutes to choose a strike package from the Black Book
carried beside him. Jacobsen reaches here for the sentence Ronald Reagan
left in his memoirs: "Six minutes to decide how to respond to a blip on a
radar scope and decide whether to release Armageddon! How could anyone
apply reason at a time like that?" The interceptors fail — the United
States fields forty-four of them, tasked with a feat the Missile Defense
Agency's own spokesperson describes as "akin to shooting a bullet with a
bullet," a force Jacobsen's verdict dismisses as "mostly for show." The Pentagon is destroyed; a
submarine-launched second warhead ruins a California reactor. And then
the scenario turns on its hinge: the American counterstrike, eighty-two
warheads bound for North Korea, must overfly Russia, because the
geometry of the Earth allows nothing else. Russian early-warning
satellites — the same constellation that generated the Petrov false alarm
of 1983{{ footnote(id="10") }} — misread the launch. A president in a
Siberian bunker, distrustful of American explanations, empties his
arsenal. At minute 72, roughly a thousand warheads begin landing on the
United States. "There is no such thing as a small nuclear war" is the
Washington aphorism the book exists to underline, together with its
corollary from the STRATCOM commander she quotes in her opening pages:
"The world could end in the next couple of hours."

Part V follows the soot. Built on the modern nuclear-winter
literature{{ footnote(id="6") }} — Toon and Robock are named
interviewees — it tracks temperatures falling by double digits,
agriculture failing across the northern hemisphere, the ozone layer
thinning until, in her sentence, "the sun's warming rays are now killer
ultraviolet rays," and an estimated five billion dead of famine within
two years. Ten thousand years of
agriculture end; the survivors return to foraging under a hostile sky.
The register of the book, it should be noted, has been criticized — one
reviewer called the prose pulp-thriller apocalyptic, another faulted it
for offering no disarmament program — and both charges have some
purchase. Neither touches the sourcing.

What a reader of this project cannot miss is that the canon set this
exam paper, in outline, fifty years before Jacobsen sat it. Its texts
attack the doctrine of deterrence by name — the 1975 message warns
against "those… who are partisans of the accumulation of atomic weapons
under pretext of 'dissuasion'"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=39) %}ETTMTTP 2:39{% end %})
— and Jacobsen's whole book is an engineering answer to the question she
plants in her opening chapters: "deterrence keeps the world safe from
ever having a nuclear war. But what happens if deterrence fails?" The canon's first volume, in a chapter titled "The atomic bombs,"
has Yahweh answer Raël's direct question about the danger:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=6) %}
Yes, a great danger. But that will allow us, in case of need, not to have
much to do to destroy this civilization if men do not grow wiser. Perhaps
they will destroy themselves. … If men no longer want to be exposed to
this peril, it suffices for them to take the atomic weapons away from the
military.
{% end %}

The policy content of that last sentence deserves a beat of attention. In
1973 it was the position of a claimed contactee; in the 2010s and 2020s,
taking weapons off hair-trigger status, ending Launch on Warning, and
qualifying sole presidential authority became the published program of
William Perry — Jacobsen's most-cited living source — and a caucus of
former commanders. The two epistemologies could not be further apart. The
policy asks converge to the letter.

### The scenario John saw

There is a second convergence, stranger and more specific, and it is the
reason this article treats *Nuclear War: A Scenario* as a text in the
comparative-religion sense. The canon's third volume devotes a long
section to decoding the Book of
{% libref(book="revelation", chapter=6, verse=12) %}Revelation{% end %} —
within the frame, a vision shown to John "through a visualizer similar to
your television sets" of the events awaiting the Age of Apocalypse — and
the decode is, verse by verse, a nuclear-war scenario. The sixth seal:

{% library(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=155) %}
The sixth seal represents the final danger for Humanity, the greatest
danger, the one which could destroy it completely: the atomic war. The
'great earthquake' is the explosion itself, the 'black sun' is the
darkening of the sky by the mushroom cloud, and the fallout dust… will
darken the moon. 'The sky receded as a scroll' is when the clouds are
chased abruptly by the sudden surge of hot air coming from the explosion.
The men who hide in the rocks of the mountains are the people who rush
into antinuclear shelters.
{% end %}

The seventh seal, the same text continues, "represents the final
cataclysm, a worldwide atomic war, which ends with the destruction of all
life on Earth"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=162) %}LWTE 2:162{% end %});
the locusts of the ninth chapter are read as aircraft "loaded with atomic
bombs," their scorpion sting the radiation poisoning of the fallout zone
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=182) %}LWTE 2:182{% end %}).
Whatever one makes of the hermeneutics — and this project labels the
canon's foundational claims `framework` precisely because mainstream
scholarship does not endorse them — the literary fact is arresting on its
own. The corpus and the reporter wrote the same book. One is a
first-century vision decoded in 1979 as a blow-by-blow account of atomic
war; the other is a 2024 blow-by-blow account of atomic war that reaches
compulsively for first-century language. Jacobsen's pages are saturated
with it: her prologue is titled "Hell on Earth"; her submarines are "the
handmaidens of the apocalypse" — "it is the astonishing speed with which
ballistic missile submarines can launch nuclear weapons, and hit multiple
targets nearly simultaneously, that makes them the handmaidens of the
apocalypse" — her grid collapse is an electric Armageddon, her Washington
officials measure their days in it, and at the scenario's 28-minute mark
she reaches past her genre entirely, to W. Somerset Maugham's *The
Razor's Edge* and the verse Maugham took from the Katha Upanishad for his
epigraph, quoted in her pages without attribution: "The sharp edge of a
razor is difficult to pass over; thus the wise say the path to Salvation
is hard."
A secular book about missile flight times, finding that the only adequate
vocabulary is scriptural, is itself a datum about what kind of event a
nuclear exchange is.

The canon's own framing of the exam is the place where its stakes are
highest and its voice most direct. In 1975 the Elohim issued a correction
to the first message — they will not intervene, in destruction or in
rescue of the many:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=23) %}
Humanity is now arriving at a turning point of its history and its future
depends now only on itself. If it knows how to master its aggressiveness
towards itself and towards the environment in which it finds itself, it
will attain the golden age of interplanetary civilization, in universal
happiness and blossoming. If, on the contrary, it lets itself go to
violence, it will destroy itself by itself either directly or indirectly.
{% end %}

And the same message generalizes the exam to a law — the canon's version
of a Great Filter, stated fourteen years before the phrase entered the
academic literature:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=25) %}
This is, in a way, a natural selection, on the cosmic scale, of the
species capable of escaping their planet. Only those who perfectly master
their aggressiveness can attain this stage. The others self-destruct as
soon as their scientific and technological level permits them to invent
weapons powerful enough for that. This is why we never fear the beings
who come from elsewhere to contact us. Thousands of contacts have
confirmed this absolute rule in the universe: the beings capable of
escaping their planetary system are always pacific.
{% end %}

The corpus even quantifies the odds — "one chance in one hundred that
your Humanity will not self-destroy"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=103) %}LWTE 2:103{% end %})
— and instructs its readers to act as if the chance were live. Jacobsen
declines to give odds. Her sources give them for her. Perry, recalling the
night in 1979 when a training tape convinced NORAD's computers that
Soviet missiles were inbound, tells her that "right now, we are closer to
having a nuclear war happen, even by accident, than we were during the
Cold War"; her own summary of the Washington consensus is a sentence the
canon could have dictated: "So here we are now. Teetering at the
edge—perhaps even closer than ever before."

## The next archaeologists

The last pages of *Nuclear War: A Scenario* perform a maneuver no other
book in its genre attempts, and it is the maneuver that earns Jacobsen
her place in this project's comparative file. After the soot settles, she
jumps forward twenty-four thousand years — twice the age of settled
civilization — and asks who might dig. Her guide is Michael Morsch, one
of the two archaeologists who in October 1994 recognized what lay buried
at Göbekli Tepe{{ footnote(id="7") }}: a monumental complex built by
people the textbooks called hunter-gatherers, deliberately interred, and
holding its silence for twelve millennia. The buried site becomes her
figure for us. Future humans, if there are any, may stand over the
melted grid of our cities exactly as Schmidt and Morsch stood over the
carved pillars, asking the questions Morsch tells her archaeology cannot
answer: "We can tell you what they ate," he says of the Göbekli Tepe
builders; "we can tell you what animals they hunted, but we can't tell
you what they were thinking. Or what happened to them." Her book's final
judgment is placed there, in the deep future, among the myths our ruins
will seed — these are its last lines:

> With time, after a nuclear war, all present-day knowledge will be gone.
> Including the knowledge that the enemy was not North Korea, Russia,
> America, China, Iran, or anyone else vilified as a nation or a group.
>
> It was the nuclear weapons that were the enemy of us all. All along.
>
> — *Nuclear War: A Scenario*, Part V, "24,000 Years Later"

Set beside that ending, now, a passage the canon published in 1979 as a
throwaway thought experiment — an aid for readers struggling with the
idea that Genesis records laboratory work:

{% library(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=14) %}
Imagine that an atomic war breaks out tomorrow, and all life on Earth is
destroyed. Then 10,000 years later, extra-terrestrials settle down to
create new living organisms…
{% end %}

The symmetry is close to perfect, and it is worth being precise about
what each side claims. Jacobsen's epilogue is a warning: this could
happen, and if it does, the record of who we were will decay into
mythology. The canon's position is that a version of it *already
happened* — that the {% wiki(slug="great-flood") %}Great Flood{% end %} of
Genesis was a strike ordered from the Elohim's home world against a
creation judged too dangerous, executed with the same class of weapon
Jacobsen's scenario spends, and survived only because a dissenting
faction preserved the seed of every species in orbit:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=58) %}
So they decided, from their distant planet, to destroy all life on Earth
by sending nuclear missiles. But the exiles, forewarned of the matter, had
asked Noah to build a rocket that was to orbit the Earth during the
cataclysm, containing a couple of each species to be safeguarded.
{% end %}

A companion article in this series, [*The Flood Was a Reset, Not a
Punishment*](/articles/the-flood-was-a-reset-not-a-punishment/), reads
the Mesopotamian flood corpus through that claim at full length, down to
Ea's recorded protest that a flood, unlike a lion or a famine, takes
everyone. What Jacobsen contributes to the file, without intending to, is
the phenomenology of the aftermath. Her Part V describes survivors under
a blackened sky, the return to foraging, the loss within generations of
the knowledge of what the flash had been. The canon's texts describe the
same epistemic wreckage from the far side: survivors of a technological
cataclysm transmitting what they saw in the only vocabulary left to them,
until boats on mountaintops harden into a story about rain
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=54) %}LWTE 1:54{% end %}).
Jacobsen herself reaches for the pattern in miniature: she recounts how
Robert Plot, director of Oxford's Ashmolean Museum, confronted with a
dinosaur femur in 1677, drew it for a learned journal and identified it
as the bone of a giant — an accurate record filed under the only frame
available. Her point is that deep time humiliates interpretation. The corpus's point,
across a hundred wiki entries, is the same one run in reverse: the
world's flood myths, sky-people traditions, and
{% wiki(slug="babel") %}scattered-builder stories{% end %} may be exactly
such catalogue entries — accurate records filed under the wrong frame,
waiting for re-description.

One further passage belongs in this section, because it closes the loop
between the exam and the examiners. Asked about their own religion, the
Elohim of the canon describe themselves as survivors of the same test —
and as orphans of teachers who failed it:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=25) %}
Our only religion is human genius. We believe only in that, and we
particularly love the memory of our own creators, whom we have never seen
again and whose world we have not been able to find again. They must have
disappeared. They had taken the precaution of putting into orbit around
our planet an immense craft containing all their knowledge, which landed
automatically on our planet when their world was destroyed. Thanks to
them we took up the torch. This torch that we would like to see taken up
by the Earth.
{% end %}

The same protocol, the text adds, is prepared for us "in case our world
were annihilated"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=27) %}TBWTT 7:27{% end %}).
Within the frame, the chain of creators and creations that this project's
{% wiki(slug="cosmic-chain") %}cosmology{% end %} describes is punctuated
by exactly the event Jacobsen's book clocks: worlds that end at their own
hands, leaving a knowledge-ark and a silence. Her epilogue imagines our
entry in that ledger. The canon claims the ledger has entries already.

## Where the readings part ways

A comparison that found only agreement would be advertising, and the
divergences here are deeper than the ones that separate the canon from a
Wallis or a Sitchin, because Jacobsen shares no premise with it at all.

**She is not a fellow traveler.** Nothing in Jacobsen's work asserts,
implies, or flirts with extraterrestrial contact. Her Area 51 reporting
is the most effective deflation of saucer mythology in the popular
literature; her Roswell chapter, whatever its faults, replaces aliens
with a Soviet hoax; *Phenomena* ends its survey of the government's
psychic programs in measured agnosticism about small effects. Read
cold, her shelf is a naturalist's history of the very period the canon
calls supernatural in origin — and a reader who takes her books as
somehow corroborating the canon's framework claims has misunderstood
both. What her work establishes is narrower and, for that reason,
firmer: the age's timeline, its machinery, and its stakes are exactly
what the canon says they are, on the record of the state's own archive.
The frame around those facts is where the two part company, completely.

**The enemy.** Jacobsen's final sentence indicts the hardware: the
weapons "were the enemy of us all." The canon disagrees, and the
disagreement is load-bearing. Its texts treat the atom as morally
neutral — the Elohim power their own civilization with it, "applied
gently"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=6) %}TBWTT 7:6{% end %})
— and locate the danger one level up, in the aggression of the species
holding it. The "beast" of Revelation, on the corpus's decode, "is simply
the use of nuclear energy for murderous means"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=205) %}LWTE 2:205{% end %})
— the use, and not the energy. The distinction has consequences.
A world that abolished the warheads while keeping its aggression would,
on the canon's terms, fail the exam by other means; the exam is about
the examinee. Jacobsen's own reporting supplies quiet support for the
canon's ordering of causes: every close call in her file — Petrov's five
phantom missiles, the NORAD training tape, the misread rocket of 1995 —
was resolved or nearly lost on the temperament of individual humans, with
the hardware performing exactly as built.

**The program.** Reviewers faulted *Nuclear War: A Scenario* for
prescribing nothing, and the criticism, while fair as far as it goes,
mistakes the book's office. Jacobsen is a diagnostician; she ends where
the x-ray ends. The canon is prescriptive to a degree that embarrasses
even its sympathizers: total planetary disarmament as the explicit
pass-condition of the exam
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=205) %}LWTE 2:205{% end %});
the removal of atomic weapons from military custody
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=6) %}TBWTT 7:6{% end %});
personal, non-delegable responsibility for every hand in the launch
chain, with the texts proposing that the names of those who hold the keys
be published so that they know they will answer for their turn of it
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=3, verse=70) %}LWTE 3:70–71{% end %});
and {% wiki(slug="geniocracy") %}geniocracy{% end %}, the canon's
institutional answer to the scene at the center of Jacobsen's book. Her
scenario turns on six minutes of one unadvised mind under the worst
cognitive conditions ever engineered{{ footnote(id="5") }}. The canon's
sixth chapter proposes, in effect, that a civilization which entrusts
Armageddon to whoever wins an election has chosen its decision-makers by
the wrong filter. A reader need not endorse the remedy to notice that
Jacobsen's reporting states the disease in the canon's exact terms.

**Hope.** The deepest divergence is structural. Jacobsen's book has the
shape of tragedy: the machine, once triggered, runs to the end, and her
survivors inherit myth and ash. The canon's Age of Apocalypse is a fork,
with the second tine described in detail — the
{% wiki(slug="golden-age") %}Golden Age{% end %}, the
{% wiki(slug="great-return") %}return of the creators{% end %}, an
{% wiki(slug="embassy") %}embassy{% end %} built to receive them — and a
soteriology for the failure branch: the faction that once saved Noah
stands ready to save "the people who follow" the last messenger and
repopulate a devastated Earth
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=23) %}ETTMTTP 2:23{% end %}).
Within the canon's own dramatis personae, the wager even has a named
adversary: the {% wiki(slug="satan") %}Satan{% end %} faction of the
{% wiki(slug="council-of-eternals") %}Council of the Eternals{% end %},
which holds that synthetic species are irredeemably violent and regards
humanity's final test as "the last opportunity for Satan to prove that he
was right"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=204) %}LWTE 2:204{% end %}).
Every launch officer in Jacobsen's chapters is, on that reading, an
unwitting witness for one side of a wager older than the species. She
would not accept the sentence. Her book is, nonetheless, the most
complete brief either side of it has ever been handed.

**How each knows what it claims.** As always in this series, the last
divergence is the epistemic one, and it should be stated without
softening. Jacobsen's method is the gold standard of secular
verification: named sources, documents, adversarial fact-checking, a
notes apparatus that runs to a hundred pages. The canon's foundational
claims arrive by testimony — one man's report of six conversations — and
cannot be verified by any archive; that is exactly why this project
labels them `framework` and labels this article's comparative reading
`inferred`. The two methods meet only in their subject matter. But the
meeting is real. The reporter working outward from the declassified
record and the corpus working downward from claimed revelation describe
the same age, the same weapon, the same compressed decision, the same
two exits. Convergence of that specificity does not validate a frame.
It does mark the territory where the frames must be compared.

## The torch and the clock

The canon's texts close their discussion of the atomic bombs with a
sentence addressed, in effect, to the civilization Jacobsen reports on:
the Elohim state that they took up the torch of their own vanished
creators, and that they would like to see the torch taken up by the
Earth. Jacobsen closes her war with a clock: at the seventy-second
minute, her narration returns to the New Mexico desert valley where the
first device was fired in July 1945, a place the Spanish had named
Jornada del Muerto — the Journey of the Dead Man — and lets the name
stand as prophecy: "How the story of nuclear weapons began is how it will
end." A torch and a clock are not the same instrument, and the difference
between them is the whole distance between the two bodies of work this
article has read together. A clock runs one way. A torch is a thing
handed on.

Her shelf, though, earns a place on this project's shortest list of
essential outside reading, for a reason that has nothing to do with
agreement. The corpus claims that in 1945 humanity entered an age of
revelation whose content is a test. Annie Jacobsen, sharing none of the
frame, has spent her career producing the test's documentation: the
bargain that armed it, the veil that hid it, the machinery that will
grade it in seventy-two minutes. "A nuclear crisis is not a worst-case
scenario," she writes; "it is *the* worst-case scenario." The canon said
the same thing in its own voice fifty years ago, added that the graders
are watching, and put the pass-rate at one in a hundred. Between her
epilogue's future archaeologists, puzzling over what happened to us, and
the canon's claim that such archaeologists have stood over such ruins
before, there remains exactly one difference that matters: whether
anyone survives who can still read the file. Jacobsen wrote hers so that
the question stays hypothetical. On that project, at least, the reporter
and the canon are on the same side of the wager.
