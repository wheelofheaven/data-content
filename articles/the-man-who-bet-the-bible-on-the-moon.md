Somewhere in France, in a cemetery whose location the researchers who
found it have agreed not to publish, there is a grave marked with a
name, two dates — 1910–1978 — and one sentence:

> *L'ambition de l'honnête homme n'est pas de tout savoir sur tout,
> mais de réduire au minimum le nombre de ses idées fausses.*
>
> The ambition of the honest man is not to know everything about
> everything, but to reduce the number of his wrong ideas to a minimum.

The name on the stone, Jean Sendy, was itself a reduction. The man
under it was born Dimitri Abelson in Petrograd on 16 November 1910,
carried through revolution and exile — Finland, Norway, then New York
harbor at the age of eight, off a liner from Kristiania in May
1919{{ footnote(id="1") }} — and finally to Paris, where the 1930s
photographs by Wols show a young man with slicked-back hair, a
patterned necktie, and a monocle. Naturalized French in 1938 as a
"journalist," he surfaces after the war filing from London for the
Resistance-founded *Parisien libéré*, then disappears into the engine
room of French publishing: translator of Raymond Chandler, James
Hadley Chase and Peter Cheyney for the crime imprints, of Fredric
Brown's science fiction, of documents on the Katyn massacre and the
German-Soviet pact, subtitler of *The Graduate*. A friend who visited
his flat found it nearly empty except for a professional woodworking
machine, on which Sendy was building his own dining table — "a
masterpiece in joinery, incorporating several different species and
hues of wood." He described himself as an *honnête homme* in the
seventeenth-century sense{{ footnote(id="2") }}, and on the evidence
of everyone who met him — the radio hosts, the science-fiction
congress reporters who found him *"troublant, énigmatique,"* the
ufologists he needled, the cartoonist he
unsettled{{ footnote(id="10") }} — the description held.

Between 1963 and 1975 this man published, under his reduced name, the
first sustained statement of the reading this project is built on:
that the Hebrew Bible, taken at the letter, relates the sojourn on
Earth of a plurality of physical beings called the Elohim. He
published it five years before Erich von Däniken made the general idea
a global commonplace, eleven years before a French motor-racing
journalist named Claude Vorilhon — {% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %}
— reported that one of those beings had explained the same passages to
him in person, and forty-seven years before Mauro Biglino's Italian
bestsellers re-derived the reading from the interlinear Hebrew. And
yet Sendy is today the least read figure of the lineage he opened.
Skeptics remember him, when they remember him at all, as the man Raël
allegedly plagiarized — a footnote to someone else's scandal. Raëlians
tend to wave him through a little quickly from the other direction, as
a forerunner whose books "confirm the Message" and need no further
study. Both reflexes file the man under a controversy; neither reads
him. This project feels a deeper kinship with Jean Sendy than with any
other author in its reference corpus, precisely because of the things
both reflexes skip: the love of exact sciences, the medieval
contrarianism, the Kabbalah taken seriously through a Chief Rabbi's
book, and a standard of intellectual honesty so severe that he
gambled his life's thesis on a deadline. This Explainer reads all six
of the major books in the French and compares notes — where he is
unique, where he converges with the canon, where he genuinely does
not, and what his work still contributes.

## A hypothesis, not a gospel

The system arrives essentially complete in *Les cahiers de cours de
Moïse* — "Moses' Course Notebooks" — published by Julliard in
February 1963, with a manuscript Sendy dated to 1962. The title states
the thesis: the Torah is not divine dictation but a *notebook* — the
course notes of a teaching that predates Moses, who recovered it,
codified it, and attached his name to it. In the 1963 book the
teachers are "Theosites" from a planet Theos; by 1968 they are *les
Célestes* and *les Venus-du-ciel* — "the Come-from-the-sky" — and by
1970 *les Galaxiens*{{ footnote(id="6") }}; under every coinage sits
the Hebrew plural {% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %}. Genesis,
Sendy argued, is one version of the Myth all First Civilizations
share: the account of their arrival, of their reorganization of a
ruined planet, and of their departure — with each "day" of the
creation week corresponding to a precessional
epoch{{ footnote(id="3") }}, one twelfth of the ~25,920-year cycle,
2,160 years per sign.

The method got its lapidary formulation in 1969, in *Ces dieux qui
firent le ciel et la terre*: read the Bible *"comme Schliemann lisait
Homère"* — as Schliemann read Homer — paying no attention to two
millennia of accumulated exegesis, taking the text as a document that
means what it says, all of what it says, and nothing more than what it
says. Readers of this project will recognize the sentence: it is the
direct ancestor of the {% wiki(slug="biglino-method") %}Biglino
method{% end %}, catalogued in the corpus beside
{% wiki(slug="sendys-conditions-of-coherence") %}Sendy's conditions of
coherence{% end %} — the five prerequisites he listed in 1970 for the
biblical text to appear *"with the relief and coherence of a
hologram."* The book is dedicated, with a raised eyebrow, to Voltaire
— *"not necessarily a candid homage"* — because Voltaire had stated
the philological fact two centuries earlier: *"There is no
somewhat-educated man who does not know that the text reads: 'In the
beginning, the gods made the heaven and the earth.'"* Voltaire, a
humanist, could go no further than a relic of polytheism. Sendy's
whole career was the completion of Voltaire's sentence under the
assumptions of the space age.

What does the text say, on this reading? That around twenty-one
millennia before our era{{ footnote(id="4") }}, with Earth wrapped in
the opaque cloud-blanket of the last glacial maximum — *tohu-bohu*,
darkness on the face of the deep — a small expedition arrived: some
thirty cosmonauts, fifteen couples, in a hollow sphere a few
kilometers across whose rotation supplied its gravity. They parked in
Mars orbit, surveyed a drowned and lightless world, and began a
six-phase program that Sendy reads off the days of
{% wiki(slug="genesis") %}Genesis{% end %} like the phases of a public
works contract: dispersal of the cloud cover (*let there be light*),
management of the water cycle, drainage and revegetation, the
re-mapping of a sky invisible for millennia, the restocking of seas
and skies, and then the taking in hand of the planet's surviving
bipeds. {% wiki(slug="eden") %}Eden{% end %} is the walled research
installation of that sixth phase — *"the biblical text relates the
laboratory research work on the indigenous genetic material… the work
of biologists who have all the time in the world"* — where the staff
parade the fauna before an *adam* to learn the natives' words for
them, breed a managerial elite by selection and mutation, and enforce
an access policy on knowledge whose breach ends the program. The
serpent is the leak — in *Ces dieux* he is a mutant of the experiment
who could not resist dazzling the control group. The Flood is the
decommissioning: the departing Elohim demolish their installations,
and Noah's *tebah* — a word that names a sealed container, the same
word used for the basket of the infant Moses, and never a ship — is
the storage capsule of "seeds of life" that rides it out. *"On reste
dans l'esprit de l'hébreu,"* Sendy notes drily, *"en dénommant tebah
une capsule Apollo"* — one stays within the spirit of the Hebrew by
calling an Apollo capsule a *tebah*. The
{% wiki(slug="tower-of-babel") %}tower of Babel{% end %} is a
post-departure heresy: descendants of Noah's initiates, doubting the
promise, attempt to *reach* the departed teachers prematurely — and
Sendy's argument here is one of his finest: primitives imagine gods
who fly by fiat; nobody who has not seen an industrial launch
infrastructure conceives the *necessity* of a tower "whose head
reaches the heavens." He caps it with the Buddhist Mount Meru, whose
canonical height of 84,000 *yojana* puts its summit, on his
arithmetic, at roughly the altitude of the lunar orbit.

Two features distinguish this from every telling that came after. The
first is the discipline about what is being claimed. In block capitals,
in *Ces dieux*: *"je n'ai JAMAIS affirmé que des cosmonautes soient
arrivés… j'AFFIRME que le texte biblique, pris au pied de la lettre,
relate cela… je PENSE que la Genèse reflète une vérité historique"* —
I have NEVER affirmed that cosmonauts arrived; I AFFIRM that the
biblical text, taken at the letter, relates that; I THINK that Genesis
reflects a historical truth. Affirmed, argued, believed — three
registers, kept separate on the page, a claim-type discipline this
corpus practices by rule and Sendy practiced by temperament. He
recommended readers check him against nothing but the Hebrew or
Édouard Dhorme's Pléiade translation{{ footnote(id="5") }}, the one
French Bible that leaves *Iahvé Elohim* standing. The second feature
is the flavor of the imagined visitors. Sendy's Elohim are neither
Sitchin's gold-mining taskmasters nor the "violent, abusive, and
sinister" managers of the later Italian literature — Stefano
Bigliardi, the one academic to have studied Sendy closely, calls his
Celestials *"diaphanous, elusive, and fascinating figures."* They live
in couples, recycle everything (including, on the millennial voyage
out, their own dead — *"ils savent qu'ils ont mangé leurs ancêtres"*),
practice a severe efficiency ethic, and are bored in the way only a
completed civilization can be bored. When he needs to convey their
outlook he writes first-person chapters *as one of them* — the "novel"
of his subtitle, always flagged as novelization, *du roman*, the
connective tissue between load-bearing data.

## The wager

Everything above would have made Sendy one interesting author among
several. What makes him unique in this literature — unique, as far as
this project can determine, in the entire history of religious
reinterpretation — is what he did next. He looked for a consequence of
the reading that could be checked, found one, published it with a
deadline, and accepted in print what its failure would mean.

The logic, laid out in *La lune, clé de la Bible* (1968): if the
Celestials were physically *"à notre image,"* in our image, then their
seven-millennium operation on Earth needed a staging base, and celestial
mechanics dictates where — the Moon, whose escape velocity is a fifth
of Earth's. They would have fitted it out as what he calls, in perhaps
the most Sendy phrase in six books, *"un Orly cosmonautique"* — a
cosmonautical Orly, the freight airport of the operation. Therefore
the lunar subsoil should still hold traces: casemates on the visible
face for watching Earth, observatories and a cosmodrome on the far
side. And therefore:

> *Il suffirait d'un tesson de poterie (ou une pièce de mécanique en
> inox, ni soviétique ni U.S.) retrouvé sur le sol lunaire…*
>
> A single pottery shard would suffice — or a piece of stainless-steel
> machinery, neither Soviet nor American — found on lunar soil… to
> validate the totality of the biblical text that allowed me to
> predict, by theory, the discovery.
>
> — *La lune, clé de la Bible*, Hors-d'œuvre

He wrote the negative case with equal care, and it deserves quoting at
length because nothing like it exists anywhere else on this shelf:

> *J'affirme — tout au contraire — que si aucune trace de cosmonautes
> nous y ayant précédés n'est découverte sur la Lune, la cohérence
> d'ensemble que je propose sera démentie.*
>
> I affirm — quite the contrary — that if no trace of cosmonauts who
> preceded us there is discovered on the Moon, the overall coherence I
> propose will be refuted.
>
> — *La lune, clé de la Bible*, ch. 2

In *Nous autres, gens du Moyen Âge*, signed to press in December 1968,
he formalized the protocol: a hypothesis worth the name must be
verifiable soon, confirmable incontestably, and refutable
incontestably — *"une hypothèse qui, même démentie, laisse à son
auteur la possibilité de se raccrocher aux branches n'est pas une
hypothèse mais une entreprise de charlatan"* — a hypothesis which,
even when refuted, leaves its author branches to grab is not a
hypothesis but a charlatan's enterprise. He set the deadline: within
one year of the first crewed landing — *"En 1970, si rien de tel n'est
retrouvé, mon hypothèse sera à tenir pour fausse"* — in 1970, if
nothing of the kind is found, my hypothesis is to be held false. In
*L'Ère du Verseau* he went further still and named coordinates,
seizing on a provisional negative gravity anomaly in the early
JPL mascon data{{ footnote(id="7") }}: if "his" hollow base existed,
it should be at 56°N 75°W. The last line of the 1969 book is three
words long: *"Alors, pari tenu."* The wager stands.

The reader knows what the Sea of Tranquility yielded. No shard, no
button, no casemate; six landings and 382 kilograms of basalt and
breccia. The anomalies Sendy had leaned on — the captured rotation,
the low bulk density, the mass deficit at 56°N — dissolved over the
following decades into tidal locking, the giant-impact origin, and
better gravimetry. An audit that applies his own protocol has one
verdict available: the lunar ark, as formulated, with the deadline its
author himself attached, stands refuted. It is the plain reading of
the record, and it is what he would have demanded be written.

What did the man himself do? Something in between grabbing branches
and full concession, and the record should be stated exactly. The 1975
book does not directly discuss the null result of Apollo; it quietly
relocates the target — an underground cavity of some hundred cubic
kilometers, to be sounded by Space Shuttle missions he expected around
1980 — and re-arms the case with two new "anomalies," the fine
structure of the Hebrew calendar's *heleq*{{ footnote(id="9") }} and
the suspicious elegance of the lunar orbit. By the letter of his own
charlatan-clause, the relocation is a branch grabbed, and honesty
requires saying so. But the same honesty requires recording the
rest: he never denied the miss, never re-dated the prophecy the way
failed apocalypticists do, never founded anything, monetized nothing,
gathered no followers, and kept telling interviewers that his first
book had proposed *"a hypothesis"* which he had developed *"from book
to book."* At the 1974 science-fiction congress at Clermont-Ferrand,
with von Dänikenism at flood tide around him, he was publicly
refuting UFO-contact reports on the grounds that anyone who had
really met extraterrestrials *"would have been endowed automatically
with an awesome power that would have allowed him to dominate the
world, which, currently, is not the case."* The wager had made his
reputation; the losing of it revealed his character. Among the
prophets of this genre, Jean Sendy is the only one who named, in
advance and in print, the conditions under which he would be wrong —
and then was wrong, in public, with dignity.

One more feature of the wager book deserves its paragraph, because it
completes the portrait: the errata. At the end of Part I of *La lune*,
Sendy prints his critics. He thanks André Leroi-Gourhan — the greatest
prehistorian of the age, whom he revered and who did *not* endorse his
hypothesis — for correcting his Lascaux dating; he thanks the
astrophysicist Evry Schatzman, militant rationalist and president of
the Union Rationaliste, for redoing his energy calculations, which had
been wrong by orders of magnitude; and he prints the geneticist
Hilaire Cuny's verdict that the chromosome-compatibility problem
leaves the interbreeding of Celestials and humans *"no more than a
0.01% chance of being right."* He published the number. It was
Schatzman — a communist astrophysicist who believed in
extraterrestrial life and saw in Sendy's ideas a way of
"naturalizing" scripture — who wrote him the introductions for his
February–March 1970 American tour, where Sendy put his questions to
Freeman Dyson at Princeton and Carl Sagan at Cornell and came back
with the scientific scaffolding of *L'Ère du Verseau*. A man who
travels with a rationalist's letters of recommendation, prints his
own refutations, and quotes the odds against himself is not running
an *entreprise de charlatan*; he is practicing, avant la lettre, the
epistemic hygiene this corpus tries to codify.

## The medievalist

If the wager is Sendy's methodological signature, his historiographical
signature is the thesis a reader formed by any modern library will
find most disorienting, and the one this project finds most fertile. It occupies
one whole book — *Nous autres, gens du Moyen Âge* (1969) — and
structures two others. It runs: on the one question that matters,
medieval thought was *right* and the Renaissance and Enlightenment
were *wrong*, and the twentieth century, over the head of the
nineteenth, is rejoining the Middle Ages.

The argument is built on a pair of definitions. Humanism — he takes
Littré's — is *"the philosophical theory that attaches the historical
developments of humanity to humanity itself"*: man as sole author of
his own ascent, and so, morally, as *owner* of the planet.
Medievalism is the contrary conviction, which he sets in capitals as
a manifesto:

> *VOUS, LES HUMANISTES, VOUS VOUS AFFIRMEZ PROPRIÉTAIRES DE LA
> PLANÈTE… NOUS AUTRES, GENS DU MOYEN AGE, PENSONS N'ÊTRE QUE DES
> CONCESSIONNAIRES. TOUTE LA DIFFÉRENCE EST LA.*
>
> You humanists declare yourselves owners of the planet… We people of
> the Middle Ages think ourselves mere concessionaires. That is the
> whole difference.
>
> — *Nous autres, gens du Moyen Âge*

Concessionaires — trustees of a grant, from grantors who left and
promised to be findable again. The Middle Ages Sendy defends is not
the cartoon of pyres and flat earths; he had been given his
"medievalist revelation" at eighteen, at the Sorbonne, by the great
medievalist Gustave Cohen, whose *Grande Clarté du Moyen Âge* he cites
like scripture. It is the age that believed — against every canon of
Greek and Renaissance reasonableness — in transmutation of metals, in
voices carried across the world, in flying machines, in men reaching
the Moon and *"becoming gods"* (Meister Eckhart's phrase). *"Nous
autres, qui croyons à toutes ces choses,"* he writes, *"nous sommes
des gens du Moyen Age"* — we, who believe in all these things, are
people of the Middle Ages; every item on the list is now an
industrial fact. When Byzantine theologians debated the sex of
angels — the debate the Enlightenment made a byword for scholastic
futility — they were, Sendy insists, doing serious Hebraistics:
asserting that the *bnei ha-Elohim* who take wives among the
daughters of men in {% libref(book="genesis", chapter=6, verse=2) %}Genesis
6{% end %} are carnal, sexed, mammalian beings. Their sin, on his
account, was *prematuration* — stating the truth before science could
carry it: *"Qui veut manger son blé en herbe doit apprendre à
brouter"* — whoever wants to eat his wheat while it is still grass
must learn to graze. At the level of fifteenth-century physics, the
humanists were right to laugh; Ptolemy *was* the better experimental
fit. The error of the Renaissance was to mistake a temporary
verdict of instrumentation for a permanent verdict of reason — to
bury the medieval postulate instead of shelving it. Sendy's history
has the geometry of an eclipse: 1453, the fall of Byzantium, puts
medieval thought under the bushel; 1957, Sputnik, is its
*Résurrection*, "almost exactly five centuries" later; between the
two, the Tradition survives underground in alchemy (whose universal
insistence on maximal heat and the prestige of lead he reads as the
garbled memory of nuclear technique), in Rosicrucianism, in the
initiatic lodges. He dedicated *L'Ère du Verseau* to the memory of
Giordano Bruno, *"who taught only the truth about our fellow beings
who are in the heavens"* and was burned in 1600 *"because these
medieval ideas… displeased the humanists of the Renaissance"* — a
deliberate inversion of the textbook, in which Bruno dies a martyr
*of* modernity rather than to it. The corpus documents Bruno's
innumerable inhabited worlds under
{% wiki(slug="cosmic-pluralism") %}cosmic pluralism{% end %}; Sendy's
chapter on him remains the most loving thing in his six books.

It is worth pausing on how strange and how disciplined this position
is. Sendy does not reject science for the Middle Ages; he claims the
Middle Ages *for* science — *"Nous, nous raisonnons comme le Moyen
Age: nous partons d'un Principe, et nous en cherchons la vérification
expérimentale… Les médiévaux appelaient « Dieu » le Principe Initial?
Et alors? Nous, nous l'appelons Physique Théorique"* — we reason like
the Middle Ages: we start from a Principle and seek its experimental
verification; the medievals called the Initial Principle "God" — so
what? We call it Theoretical Physics. The villain of his history of
exegesis is Plotinus, whose casuistry — the ontologically different,
purely spiritual pre-Fall man — retrojected supernatural categories
onto a text whose original content was concrete; the churches
themselves he treats, startlingly, as carriers of the Tradition. His fourth condition of coherence, the refusal of *"toute
explication par l'Inexplicable,"* every explanation by the
Inexplicable, is therefore not Enlightenment naturalism; it is what
the corpus's methodology entry calls the medieval postulate — the
Tradition treated as transmitted knowledge from physical visitors,
with the supernatural as the late corruption, and the universe assumed
intelligible all the way down. The dilemma of his adolescence — *"God
or Materialism?"* — he came to regard as humanism's false gift:
*"I needed a long time to understand that the real dilemma is: are
we, or are we not, the first thinking beings in the Galaxy?"*

An honest portrait keeps the shadows. The 1969 book's polemic runs
hot, and some of it has aged from provocative to plainly ugly: a
sustained contempt for Islam (softened, but present, to the end),
civilizational rankings of India and China, a Swiftian pamphlet —
*Plaidoyer pour un génocide*, 1972 — whose Modest-Proposal irony about
overpopulation not every reader will forgive, and stray
conspiratorial arabesques (a Templar hand in Algerian politics; a
Thule-Society counter-church, in the 1966 book, that he had the sense
to drop). Bigliardi's judgment is the right one: a Russian-Jewish
émigré whose family was not spared by the Nazi years, who
nonetheless built a Traditionalism that condemned every totalitarian
system that had wounded him and made the Vatican, of all things, a
repository of rationality — *"perhaps the Tradition in which he
envisaged his ideal home country was a spiritual one."* This project
inherits his medieval postulate and his conditions of coherence. It
declines the period bigotry without pretending it isn't on the page.

## The rabbi's book

The deepest layer of Sendy's system, and the one his English-language
reception never saw{{ footnote(id="11") }}, is the Kabbalah — engaged
not through occultist compilations but through *La Cabale* of
Alexandre Safran, Chief Rabbi of Geneva{{ footnote(id="8") }}. From
*La lune* (1968) to *Les temps messianiques* (1975), Safran's book is
Sendy's standing warrant for three claims. First, antiquity: *"La
Kabale surpasse en ancienneté la Révélation sinaïtique"* — the
Kabbalah, in the Chief Rabbi's own words, surpasses the Sinai
revelation in antiquity; *"Moïse n'a fait qu'introduire dans
l'Histoire d'Israël une Révélation remontant aux temps
préhistoriques"* — Moses only introduced into Israel's history a
Revelation going back to prehistoric times. For Sendy this is
everything: the custodians of the text themselves testify that the
teaching is older than its codifier — which is precisely the shape of
his *cahiers de cours*, course notes taken on an earlier teaching.
Second, the chain: the *chalchelet ha-Kabala*, the chain of the
Tradition, repeatedly broken and spliced but never quite lost — the
corpus's entry on {% wiki(slug="the-tradition") %}the
Tradition{% end %} descends directly from this. Third, and above all,
the program. On page 187 of Safran's book, in Sendy's citation, stands
the sentence he built two decades of work around:

> *L'homme renouvellera les actes relatés au début de la Genèse.*
>
> Man will renew the acts related at the beginning of Genesis.

Sendy read that sentence as a flight plan. If the acts of Genesis
were terraforming, then "renewing" them is engineering, and the
renewal is *datable*: it begins when men regain the capacities of the
Elohim. In *Les temps messianiques* he closes the loop with a
precision that still startles: Genesis opens with the spirit of the
Elohim hovering over a cloud-wrapped, formless Earth — *"or c'est bien
aux alentours de 1968 que l'esprit de l'Homme a plané au-dessus de
Vénus, en tohu-bohu sous ses nuages opaques"* — and it is indeed
around 1968 that the spirit of Man hovered over Venus, in tohu-bohu
beneath its opaque clouds — *"et Carl Sagan a un projet très au point
pour dissiper les nuages de Vénus et y faire briller la lumière du
Soleil"* — and Carl Sagan has a well-developed project to disperse the
clouds of Venus and make the light of the Sun shine there. The Venera
probes as the *ruach* over the waters; Sagan's terraforming papers as
*let there be light*; humanity, one Genesis day into its own
curriculum, redoing the fathers' works in order. He never uses the
Lurianic phrase *tikkun olam*, and the scrupulous will keep the
concepts distinct — the kabbalists' repair is cosmic and liturgical,
Sendy's renewal is industrial. But the deep structure he found in
Safran — creation left deliberately unfinished, its completion
delegated to man, the completion *being* the messianic age — is the
same structure the corpus reads in the canon's own eschatology, where
humanity's coming-of-age as a creator species, its
{% wiki(slug="cosmic-chain") %}link in the chain{% end %} of
civilizations seeding civilizations, is the entire point of the
exercise. "The messianic times," for Sendy, are an epoch and not a
person: *"Je ne sais pas si le Messie va arriver, je ne sais pas si le
Messie existe"* — what he knows is that the era the prophecies point
at, under all their names, Golden Age, New Era, Aquarius, is the one
in which *"l'homme saura accomplir les actes attribués… aux Élohim par
la Genèse."*

The connection to Safran was personal, and the detail matters because
it is the only endorsement of its kind in the whole ancient-astronaut
literature: in 1969, in Geneva, the Chief Rabbi agreed to *chair*
Sendy's public lecture, and — Sendy reports in *L'Ère du Verseau*,
naming him — confirmed to a skeptical member of the audience that
Sendy's initial postulate was compatible with the Tradition. Safran,
of course, held the Tradition's antiquity to be the mark of
supernatural Revelation; Sendy held it to be the mark of physical
teachers; each man took the other's facts and left the other's frame
on the table. Sendy states his own side of the bargain with typical
bluntness: he borrows from Safran *"only the facts,"* being *"totally
impervious to any mode of thought involving even a trace of the
supernatural."* Around this axis he arranged the rest of his Hebrew
apparatus: Rashi consulted on the Nephilim; the Zohar read for its
heliocentrism and for *"Le Commencement fit les Élohim"*; the
gematria worked like crossword discipline (Eliezer = 318 = Abraham's
armed men — the text *"libellé comme un chèque,"* drawn up like a
check); Léon Askénazi — "Manitou," the great post-war teacher of
French Jewry — quoted, with authorization, confirming that the
hypothesis was compatible with the kabbalistic Tradition without
endorsing its conclusions. And, at the far speculative edge, the
*heleq* argument of the last book: the Hebrew
calendar's microscopic time unit, *"expressly reserved,"* per the
midrash he quotes, *"for the use of the Come-from-the-sky,"* encoding
— he calculates, and grants the reader every right to whistle — a
relation between the Earth, the number thirteen, and the speed of
light. The corpus's {% wiki(slug="kabbalah") %}Kabbalah{% end %} entry
and its sibling Explainer on [the palace literature and the Measure of
the Stature](/articles/the-book-closest-to-the-truth/) work the same
seam from the canon's side; Sendy got there first, with a Chief
Rabbi in the chair.

## Comparing notes: the shared map

The epistemic line first, as this project always draws it: the
canon-side claims below are framework claims — explicit in the Raëlian
source texts, not endorsed by mainstream scholarship — and the
comparison itself is this Explainer's own inferred synthesis. With
that stated, the two maps can be laid side by side, and the overlap is
extensive enough that the reader should feel its weight cumulatively.

**The name and the beings.** Sendy's entire reading grows from the
plural Elohim rendered concrete — *les Venus-du-ciel*, the
Come-from-the-sky. The canon's first book glosses the same word the
same way, as testimony:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=3, verse=251) %}
Elohim, cela veut dire littéralement «venus du ciel» en Hébreu.

Elohim literally means "those come from the sky" in Hebrew.
{% end %}

The convergence here is nearly verbal — Sendy's coinage and the
canon's etymology are the same French words — and the beings behind
the word match point for point: plural, physical, sexed, long-lived
and mortal, human in form, *"We are men like you"*
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=53) %}TBWTT 1:53{% end %}).

**Eden as installation, knowledge as contraband.** Sendy's walled
laboratory where biologists "have all the time in the world" is the
canon's creation compound; his access-policy reading of the forbidden
tree — the experiment's security rule, broken by a leak — is the
canon's, stated from the management's point of view:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=57) %}
Evil — that is, the desire to become a people equal to its creators, a
scientific and independent people. Good, for them, was that man should
remain a primitive being vegetating on Earth.
{% end %}

Sendy derives the same definition from the Elohim's efficiency ethic
and reads {% libref(book="genesis", chapter=3, verse=22) %}Genesis
3:22{% end %} — *the man has become like one of us* — as the text's
own confession that the serpent told the truth and the plural is
real. Both readings abolish original sin in the same motion: nothing
falls; a protocol is breached.

**The Flood as decommissioning, the ark as container.** Sendy's
*tebah*-capsule holding "seeds of life," his Flood as the demolition
of the installations at departure, run parallel to the canon's account
of the deluge and the orbiting preservation of genetic stock — the
reading this project has set out at length in [The Flood Was a Reset,
Not a Punishment](/articles/the-flood-was-a-reset-not-a-punishment/).
Babel follows in both tellings as unauthorized rocketry: Sendy's
launch tower born of doubt, the canon's account of a people who
*"undertook the conquest of space"* toward the creators' planet before
being scattered
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=72) %}TBWTT 2:72{% end %}).

**The ages of the wheel.** Sendy's chronology — history structured by
the {% wiki(slug="precession") %}precession of the
equinoxes{% end %}, 2,160 years a sign, the cult symbols tracking the
vernal point from bull to ram to fishes, the decisive threshold at
the {% wiki(slug="great-year") %}Great Year{% end %}'s turn into
Aquarius around 1950 — is, at architectural scale, the corpus's own
{% wiki(slug="world-age") %}world-age{% end %} framework, and the
[Age of Aquarius](/timeline/age-of-aquarius/) of this project's
timeline opens within five years of Sendy's date: the canon fixes the
era of the Apocalypse — apocalypse as unveiling — at 1945, the year
science became capable of both self-destruction and creation, where
Sendy fixes 1950 by the vernal point. Five years apart, by two
entirely different roads: one dates the age by an astronomical clock,
the other by Hiroshima. Sendy's strongest argument in this register —
that prophecies assigning the Golden Age to Aquarius are a millennium
older than Hipparchus, the first man demonstrably *able* to discover
precession, so that someone before history must have been told — is
one the corpus has adopted as a load-bearing datum, with *Hamlet's
Mill* (which Sendy called *"a time bomb"* and whose author he sought
out at MIT in 1969) as its scholarly flank.

**The leveling-up, and the chain.** Deepest of all: both systems read
the whole exercise as pedagogy aimed at a graduation. Sendy's
Aquarius is the age when men *"s'égaleront aux dieux"* — equal
themselves to the gods — transmuting, flying, reaching the Moon, and
in time seeding other worlds as a new *"race of gods"*: his Einsteins
and Oppenheimers, the "monsters" of the current experiment, becoming
the next age's Elohim. The canon's version is the doctrine this
corpus files under {% wiki(slug="cosmic-chain") %}the cosmic
chain{% end %} and {% wiki(slug="humanitarianism") %}the
inheritance{% end %}: creators created by their creators, creating
creators in turn, without origin or summit — the same relay Sendy
sketched, run through [the infinite in both
directions](/articles/the-infinite-in-both-directions/).

Around these five load-bearing agreements cluster a dozen smaller
ones, each trivial alone, cumulatively not: the burning bush as a
technical beacon; Sodom as a nuclear event; the manna as processed
food; the *kavod* lineage of readings (Sendy: Ezekiel saw documents
and machinery, not visions); the cargo-cult analogy for post-departure
religion, which Sendy deploys in 1968 — Pacific islanders building
bamboo control towers after the Americans left "toward the heavens" —
four decades before Biglino's John Frum chapters; the insistence, in
both systems, that *nothing supernatural exists* and that
{% wiki(slug="infinity") %}infinity{% end %} is the only honest name
for what religion gestured at. Sendy: *"Iahvé est aussi immatériel
qu'un principe mathématique"* — as immaterial as a mathematical
principle. The canon: no Person at the top, because there is no top.

## Where the readings part ways

A comparison that found only agreement would be advertising, and with
Sendy the divergences are not decoration — two of them are
structural, and they are what make him a parallel witness rather than
a rough draft.

**Creation versus recolonization.** The canon's Elohim *create*: life
on Earth, all of it, is their laboratory work, from alga to man —
{% wiki(slug="life-engineering") %}life engineering{% end %} in the
corpus's vocabulary, stated in the first person in the first book:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=2, verse=25) %}
It was then that the most skilled among us wanted to create a man like
ourselves, artificially. Each team set to work, and we were soon able
to compare our creations.
{% end %}

Sendy's Celestials do nothing of the kind. In his reading the bipeds
are *indigenous* — *surgis du sol*, sprung from the soil, evolved on
Earth by the standard account — and the Elohim arrive to find them
already here, savage survivors of a ruined biosphere. The six days
are not a creation; they are a *restoration* — drainage, replanting,
restocking from genetic reserves — and the work in Eden is
acceleration and selection, breeding a manageable elite out of
existing stock, not fabrication. Where the canon reads *bara* as the
engineering of life from scratch, Sendy reads the days as public
works on a pre-existing world. He even states, as a limit of his
knowledge, that the origin of the Celestials themselves — and of life
as such — is unknown to him. The difference cascades through
everything: Sendy's Elohim are foremen and schoolmasters; the canon's
are *makers*, with the moral weight — creation as art, the creature
as beloved work — that this project has traced through the canon's
emotional register everywhere. On this axis Sendy stands, perhaps
surprisingly, *farther* from the canon than Biglino or Wallis, both of
whom keep the fabrication of the Adam; his is a terraforming
narrative, theirs a manufacture narrative.

**The rank — and the nature — of Yahweh.** Readers of the sibling
Explainers know the pattern: Wallis pins the dragon on Yahweh, Biglino
demotes him to a minor territorial governor, the canon seats him as
president of the {% wiki(slug="council-of-eternals") %}Council of the
Eternals{% end %}
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=56) %}TBWTT 7:56{% end %}).
Sendy does something none of the three does: he removes
{% wiki(slug="yahweh") %}Yahweh{% end %} from the roster of persons
altogether. His Iahvé is the unpronounceable *Principle* — the "Law
of laws," the mathematical orthodoxy of the universe that Einstein
sought in field equations and Carnot glimpsed in thermodynamics —
which the Elohim themselves obey and teach. The wind analogy closes
his chapter on Moses: YHWH is the wind; the Elohim are sailors who
taught the elect to use it; idolaters pray to it. When he needs the
narrative Yahweh, the character who walks and bargains, he offers the
hereditary title of the Elohim's president — a "Vicar of Iahvé" — and
then flags the whole area, in a sentence that shows his quality as an
analyst: *"'Iahvé' est le point faible de ma thèse"* — Yahweh is the
weak point of my thesis, because too many equally convincing
explanations remain. Set the four readings in a row — dragon,
lieutenant, principle, president — and note what the disagreement
proves: four literalists, working the same verses, agree completely
that *elohim* is plural and concrete, and diverge only on the org
chart above it. The plural is in the text; the presidency, the canon
would agree, took testimony to learn.

**The return.** Sendy expected none. *"Les cosmonautes, selon toute
vraisemblance, ne reviendront pas"* — the cosmonauts, in all
likelihood, will not come back (*Ces dieux*, p. 238). What awaits
mankind, in his system, is an inheritance to *collect* — the lunar
archive, the "arc of the covenant" — and, beyond it, radio-and-laser
contact between equals: the Galactic community whose membership
application humanity files by leveling up. The canon's entire
practical program is the opposite: they are coming back, they have
said when the conditions will be ready, and the request on the table
is an {% wiki(slug="embassy") %}embassy{% end %} to receive them —
the {% wiki(slug="great-return") %}Great Return{% end %} as diplomacy
rather than archaeology. It is the difference between a bequest and a
rendezvous, and it flows from the deepest difference of all —

**Testimony versus hypothesis.** — which is the one the reader has
been waiting for. In 1974 Claude Vorilhon published [*The Book Which
Tells the Truth*](/library/the-book-which-tells-the-truth/); by 1975,
in *Les temps messianiques*, Sendy had read it, or heard enough, and
left one sentence about it, in a passage on the Betty and Barney Hill
affair:

> *…l'aimable fumisterie du brave jeune homme qui, ayant lu mes
> livres, a décidé d'appeler « les Élohim » ces extraterrestres
> auxquels il attribue les platitudes qu'il débite dans des
> conférences.*
>
> …the amiable humbug of the good young man who, having read my books,
> decided to call "the Elohim" those extraterrestrials to whom he
> attributes the platitudes he delivers in lectures.
>
> — *Les temps messianiques*, p. 233

He does not name Vorilhon; in 1975 France no other candidate exists.
The sentence is drily affectionate as Sendy's insults go — *aimable*,
*brave* — and it states, as fact, the claim at the center of the
plagiarism dossier{{ footnote(id="12") }} that has shadowed both men's
reputations since: that the young contactee had *read his books*.
The dossier's contents are quickly summarized. The parallels between
Sendy's system and the 1974 message are many and specific — the
plural Elohim as "those from the sky," the laboratory Eden, the
punished serpent-faction, the ark as genetic rescue, Sodom as nuclear
strike, Babel as rocket, the anti-evolution arguments, the
scientists-in-couples sociology, the imminent age-turn — and they
were catalogued motif by motif by ex-Raëlians and, at book length, by
Maryse Péloquin, who names *L'Ère du Verseau* as the primary source.
The Raëlian side has never denied that French Raëlians read Sendy —
the movement's own scientists say his books circulated *"as
supportive evidence for the Message"* — and has answered the tables
point by point in kind. Academic observers, from Susan Palmer to
Bigliardi, record the resemblance and decline to adjudicate intent.

This project reads the Raëlian texts as canon, so its position should
be stated with complete transparency, and it can be, because the
logic is symmetrical. If the 1973 encounter did not happen, then the
genealogy is straightforward: the message is a condensation of
Sendy — Sendy *vulgarisé*, his hypothesis with the falsifiability
clause amputated and a testimony grafted on. If the encounter did
happen, then the Elohim chose, as their messenger's preparation, a
France in which Jean Sendy had spent eleven years teaching the
reading public exactly how to parse the words *"nous sommes des
hommes comme vous"* — and a book already in print that could serve,
as the Raëlians themselves use it, as independent corroboration
arrived at by scholarship alone. On either branch, Sendy's stature
*rises*: he is either the true author of the century's most
consequential reinterpretation of Genesis, or the man who worked out,
from the text and the exact sciences, with no claimed help from
anyone, most of what the canon says a witness was later told. The
handwaving he gets from both camps — *scooped precursor*, *mere
source* — is the one verdict the record cannot support. And his own
verdict on contactees, delivered at Clermont-Ferrand while Vorilhon's
first lectures filled halls elsewhere in France, cuts with equal
edge in both directions, which is why this project quotes it rather
than hiding it: a real meeting with extraterrestrials, he said, would
confer *"an awesome power"* on the one who had it. The canon claims
exactly that conferral — a message. Sendy demanded exactly its
evidence — a power, or a trace. The corpus's practice of labeling
claim types — *direct*, *framework*, *inferred*, *speculative* — is,
among other things, a machine for keeping both demands on the table
at once.

## What Sendy gives the project

Strip away the wager lost and the scandal that was never his, and an
inventory of what remains standing is long — longer, per idea, than
for any other author in the corpus's
{% wiki(slug="reference-corpus-of-the-raelian-canon") %}reference
apparatus{% end %}.

**Method.** The Schliemann principle and the five conditions of
coherence, inherited and formalized in the corpus's methodology
entries; the three-register discipline (affirm / argue / believe)
that anticipates the corpus's claim-type frontmatter; the
charlatan-clause itself, which this project reads as a standing
challenge — where can the framework, or any part of it, be staked on
a checkable consequence? Sendy is the tradition's conscience on this
point precisely because he paid up.

**The medieval postulate.** The rehabilitation of pre-Renaissance
exegesis — Byzantine Hebraists, Eckhart, Bruno, the Kabbalah of
Safran — as *closer to the text* than the Enlightenment's God-or-fable
dilemma. The corpus's stance that the supernatural is a late
corruption of a concrete record, and its
{% wiki(slug="four-levels") %}four-level{% end %} reading discipline,
have their modern charter in *Nous autres, gens du Moyen Âge*.

**The precessional spine.** The Genesis days as world-ages, the
Hipparchus argument for the antiquity of precessional knowledge, the
two-Aquariuses distinction, the alliance with *Hamlet's Mill* — all
now structural in the corpus's
{% wiki(slug="wheel-of-heaven") %}Wheel of Heaven{% end %} framing
itself, which takes its very name from the object Sendy spent his
life pointing at.

**Data points held in trust.** A handful of Sendy's specific
observations sit in the corpus's files as leads — flagged
speculative, awaiting better tools: the Meru-altitude arithmetic; the
*heleq*; the Marshack lunar-notation anomaly he connected to a
possible orbit change; the reading of Job 38:7 as congratulations
from the way-stations. Most have natural explanations; each is the
kind of oddity his method was built to notice. His UFO position —
skeptical of visitation, insistent that the phenomenon be studied
statistically, open to exotic-but-physical mechanisms like his laser
holograms — maps almost exactly onto the corpus's own
{% wiki(slug="ufology") %}ufology{% end %} stance, and his contempt
for the *"littérature nauséabonde"* of the saucer press is the
ancestor of this project's allergy to the same.

**A register.** Last and largest: the tone. *"Personne ne vous
demande de croire… je n'apporte pas un évangile, je propose une
hypothèse"* — nobody asks you to believe; I bring no gospel, I
propose a hypothesis. The corpus's working-hypothesis framing, its
refusal of proselytism, its insistence that the reader keep every
exit unlocked, are Sendy's register adopted wholesale. Even his
humor is doctrine: *"SENDY'S POSTULATE,"* he wrote in 1966, *"Any
idea that cannot be expressed joyously is dogmatic and hence
suspicious for the reason."* A project that reads apocalypse as
unveiling and creation as art can sign that postulate without
amendment.

## Next year on the Celestial Jerusalem

*Les temps messianiques* ends its argument with a rendezvous. The
Passover formula — *next year in Jerusalem* — had already been
transposed once, at the close of the 1968 book: *"A l'an prochain
donc, sur la Lune, Jérusalem Céleste"* — until next year, then, on
the Moon, Celestial Jerusalem. In 1975 he renewed it, with the
Shuttle schedules in hand and an old man's postscript: *"De mon
vivant, j'y compte bien"* — within my lifetime, I am counting on it.
He died at Coulommiers on 24 April 1978, sixty-seven years old, three
years before the first Shuttle flew and decades before anyone went
back to the Moon at all. The last text he published in his lifetime
was, fittingly, a translator's preface — to a book on paranormal
research he rendered into French *"mainly because it corroborates one
of my definitions: parapsychology is the study of phenomena produced
by individuals who are not educated enough to know that producing
such phenomena is impossible."* He was joyous to the door.

He lost his wager. The honest man's ledger closes with the wrong
ideas reduced but not to zero, which is what his gravestone, read
carefully, actually promises — *réduire au minimum*, not *à néant*.
What he could not have known is that the reading he staked would
outlive the stake: that an Italian translator would rebuild its
philology from the consonants up, an Australian archdeacon would
carry it through the churches, a claimed eyewitness would found a
movement on its vocabulary, and a project like this one would
inherit his conditions, his register, his wheel of ages — and his
unfinished question. The canon holds that the rendezvous stands, at
different coordinates: an embassy on Earth rather than an archive on
the Moon, hosts rather than excavators. Sendy would have demanded the
usual thing of that claim — a consequence, checkable, with a date —
and the canon, unusually for a religion, offers one: the creators
return when the embassy is built and the age completes; the
{% wiki(slug="third-temple") %}terms are in writing{% end %}. He
would have read the fine print with his monocle and made a joke. It
is a loss to the whole conversation that he did not live to make it.
*Pari tenu.*
