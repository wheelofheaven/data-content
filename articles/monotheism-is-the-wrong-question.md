An {% wiki(slug="idolatry") %}idol{% end %} is easiest to defeat after it has
entered a museum.

Behind glass, cut off from incense, procession, music, food, clothing, priests,
and the architecture that once made it the still centre of a city, the statue
looks exactly as the prophets said it did. It has eyes and cannot see. It has a
mouth and cannot answer. A curator can lift it with cotton gloves. Isaiah's
carpenter can burn half a log for warmth, carve the other half, fall down before
it, and ask the timber to save him. The case appears closed before the placard
has even been read.

But the ancient argument was never quite that easy. The artisan who carved a
Mesopotamian cult image knew he had carved it. The priest who washed its mouth
knew it had recently been unable to receive food. The worshippers who carried
it through a city did not need a Hebrew prophet to explain that cedar came from
a tree. Their theology of the image was subtler: rites transferred the object
from the workshop into divine service; the image became a privileged body,
presence, or meeting place of a being who was not exhausted by the material in
front of them. Biblical polemic attacks that claim by collapsing every layer
back into lumber. It is brilliant rhetoric. It is not neutral ethnography.

That matters because the same polemic was later made to carry an even larger
argument. The history of {% wiki(slug="religion") %}Western religion{% end %}
is usually drawn as the victory of
{% wiki(slug="monotheism") %}monotheism{% end %}, **one** over **many**. Israel
escaped {% wiki(slug="plurality-of-gods") %}Canaanite polytheism{% end %} and
discovered one God; Christianity universalised him; Islam purified the result
into
{% wiki(slug="hanafiyya") %}*tawhid*{% end %}, the uncompromising unity of
Allah. On the other side of the line stand pagans and heathens, multiplying
gods because they have not yet reached the abstraction, or because they have
fallen away from it. The religious map becomes arithmetic.

The trouble is that the ancient texts keep refusing to count properly.
Deuteronomy can demand that Israel serve {% wiki(slug="yahweh") %}Yahweh{% end %}
alone while remembering nations allotted to other divine sons. Psalm 82 seats
Elohim in a council and has him
judge *among the elohim*. Paul can write that there is one God and one Lord,
then, in the same breath, acknowledge "many gods and many lords." The Qur'an
can condemn association (*shirk*) while repeatedly making its opponents admit
that Allah created heaven and earth. In each case, the live question is not
simply *how many beings exist?* It is: *Whom will you serve? Through whom may
you approach? What may represent the absent? Who has authority? Who deserves
the credit for creation?*{{ footnote(id="1") }}

This Explainer develops two claims, and they must be kept at different
evidentiary distances. The first is historical: **monotheism and polytheism are
often poor master categories for ancient anti-idol texts**. They can obscure
more than they reveal because they compress ontology, cult, allegiance,
representation, and creation into a single number. This claim now has a large
academic literature behind it.

The second is the Wheel of Heaven hypothesis: **the prophetic war against
idols may preserve a recurring restoration of memory, calling human beings away
from dead substitutes and back to their actual makers, the
{% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %}, usually through the authority of one
named Eloha or messenger**. On this reading, the later singular, immaterial,
omnipresent God is not what the oldest polemics were defending. He is what
centuries of theology made from a more concrete history. That claim is
`speculative`. It is not standard biblical scholarship, and it is not Islamic
theology. Its value depends on whether it explains the textual remainder better
than the categories it seeks to replace. The larger canonical architecture is
developed in [*The Religion of Religions*](/articles/the-religion-of-religions/).

## A seventeenth-century ruler laid across ancient worlds

The word *monotheism* is younger than the telescope. It was coined and
popularised by Christian philosophers in the second half of the seventeenth
century; Henry More is usually credited with its first English use. The word
names a real and important family of convictions, but it is not an ancient
self-description shared by Moses, Paul, and Muhammad. It is a ruler made in one
intellectual workshop and laid retrospectively across very different religious
worlds.

That ruler usually asks one question: **How many gods does this religion say
exist?** The answer sorts traditions into boxes. One is monotheism. More than
one is polytheism. Scholars created intermediate terms for evidence that would
not cooperate. *Monolatry* names the worship of one while others may exist;
*henotheism* names devotion to one supreme god within a plurality.{{ footnote(id="2") }}
Yet even these repairs keep number at the centre.

A better first pass is a matrix:

| Question | What it asks | An ancient answer might be |
|---|---|---|
| Ontology | What kinds of superhuman beings exist? | A high god, divine sons, messengers, demons, ancestors |
| Cult | Who receives sacrifice, prayer, vows, or pilgrimage? | One patron, several local powers, or one through intermediaries |
| Allegiance | Whose law and political authority bind this people? | Our covenant lord rather than the patrons of other nations |
| Representation | Can a being inhabit or work through an image, stone, name, throne, relic, or house? | Presence without identity; mediation without equivalence |
| Creation | Who made the world, humanity, or this nation? | One high creator, a council, a craftsman, or several agents |

The boxes can now fall in unexpected places. A community may recognise many
heavenly beings but sacrifice only to one. Another may call one supreme being
creator while filling its temples with subordinate gods. A third may deny that
an image is itself a god while treating it as the god's authorised body. A
fourth may confess one God while directing prayer through saints or angels.
These are not evasions. They are different religious systems answering
different questions.

Dan McClellan's recent survey of the category calls *monotheism* "unclear and
fraught" and notes that it has served narratives of civilisational progress and
decline. Mark S. Smith reached a more specific conclusion for the
{% wiki(slug="hebrew-bible") %}Hebrew Bible{% end %}:
there, monotheism is not simply a new stage after polytheism, but rhetoric that
reinforces Israel's exclusive relation with its deity. Paula Fredriksen goes
further for antiquity: statements of unique divinity often proclaim a god's
power and the worshipper's loyalty, not the solitary existence of that god.
Once that possibility is admitted, several famous verses change temperature.

## "No other Elohim before me"

The Decalogue does not open with a philosophical proof. It opens with a rescue
and a claim of jurisdiction, the same covenantal world traced through the
[Age of Aries](/timeline/age-of-aries/):

{% library(book="exodus", chapter=20, verse=2, verse_end=5) %}
I am Jehovah thy God, who have brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage.
Thou shalt have no other gods before me.
Thou shalt not make thyself any graven image, or any form of what is in the heavens above, or what is in the earth beneath, or what is in the waters under the earth:
thou shalt not bow down thyself to them, nor serve them.
{% end %}

The sequence matters. "I brought you out" establishes a relationship; "no
other *elohim* before me" makes it exclusive; the image ban polices its visible
boundary. The reason given is jealousy, the language of a covenant offended by
rival allegiance. The command does not say, "No other beings in the category
*elohim* exist." Indeed, "before me" would be a strange place to put
nonentities. It says that Israel must not install, serve, or bow to them in
Yahweh's presence.

Other early formulations are comparative rather than eliminative. After the
sea crossing, Exodus asks, "Who is like thee, Jehovah, among the gods?" Psalm
89 asks who among the sons of the mighty resembles Yahweh. In ordinary speech,
"Who among the kings is like this king?" exalts one ruler; it does not abolish
every other throne.

The Song of Moses preserves the most difficult evidence. In the older reading
of Deuteronomy 32:8-9, attested by a Dead Sea Scroll and Greek witnesses, the
Most High divides the nations "according to the number of the sons of God,"
and Yahweh receives Jacob as his allotted portion.{{ footnote(id="3") }} A few
lines later Israel sacrifices to *shedim*, powers "not Eloha," to "gods newly
come up." The poem condemns the transfer of loyalty. It does not inhabit an
empty sky. [*The Scrolls That Woke in Year One*](/articles/the-scrolls-that-woke-in-year-one/)
traces the manuscript discovery and the older "sons of Elohim" reading in
detail.

Psalm 82, a central witness in the corpus's
{% wiki(slug="plurality-of-gods") %}plurality reading{% end %}, is harder still
to flatten:

{% library(book="psalms", chapter=82, verse=1) %}
God standeth in the assembly of God, he judgeth among the gods.
{% end %}

The Hebrew repeats the same noun: *Elohim* stands in the assembly of El; in the
midst of the *elohim* he judges. The accused rulers are told, "Ye are gods, and
all of you are children of the Most High," then sentenced to "die like men."
Interpretations differ. They have been read as human judges, foreign gods,
members of a divine council, or a poem deliberately demoting old gods into
mortality. What the psalm cannot be made to resemble is the clean modern
proposition that the Hebrew Bible knows only one inhabitant of the divine
category.

Benjamin Sommer therefore defends a qualified use of *monotheism*: the Hebrew
Bible can mention other gods because they never rival Yahweh's ultimate control
as independent actors. That is a serious answer. It shifts the definition from
number to sovereignty. Yet that shift concedes the point at issue: biblical
oneness is not always numerical solitude. It can mean incomparable rule over a
populated heaven.

## Isaiah's axe, and what it actually cuts

The strongest case for ontological monotheism arrives in the exilic poetry of
the {% wiki(slug="prophet") %}prophet{% end %} Isaiah, chapters 40-55. Here
Yahweh says, "Before me there was no God formed, neither
shall there be after me"; "I am the first, and I am the last; and beside me
there is no God"; "I am Jehovah, and there is none else." These are not merely
commands to worship. They sound like denials that any comparable deity exists.

Then comes the Bible's great satire of the idol workshop. The smith tires at
his furnace. The carpenter stretches a line, chooses a tree, and makes an
attractive human form. Half the timber heats his meal. Before the remainder he
kneels:

{% library(book="isaiah", chapter=44, verse=16, verse_end=17) %}
He burneth part thereof in the fire; with part thereof he eateth flesh; he roasteth roast, and is satisfied: he warmeth himself also, and saith, Aha! I am warm, I have seen the fire.
And with the remainder thereof he maketh a god, his graven image: he falleth down unto it, and worshippeth it, and prayeth unto it, and saith, Deliver me; for thou art my god.
{% end %}

If any biblical text means "one being exists and every alleged god is inert
matter," this is the candidate. A responsible thesis must not explain it away.
The exilic writers do move toward a universal creator whose uniqueness is much
closer to later Jewish, Christian, and Islamic monotheism than the divine
allotment of Deuteronomy 32.

Yet the rhetoric still operates on more than one level. Saul Olyan argues that
Isaiah's incomparable-god formulas resemble praise used for deities elsewhere:
"none like you" magnifies rank and efficacy, and need not function as an
inventory of every superhuman being. Mark Smith situates the language in the
political catastrophe of exile. When states fell, their patron gods appeared
defeated. Isaiah answers not by conceding Yahweh's death but by expanding his
jurisdiction: the conqueror Cyrus is also his instrument; Israel's national god
is the only sovereign of history.

In other words, Isaiah's axe cuts in two directions. It cuts down the image as
an impotent human product. It also cuts away the old national map in which each
people had a patron of comparable scope. This is a real theological
development. It still does not make every earlier anti-idol passage a lecture
on the attributes of an immaterial Absolute.

## The idolater knew where the wood came from

The prophetic joke works because it suppresses the opponent's answer. Ancient
Mesopotamian religion did not normally claim that the wooden core, by itself,
had created heaven. A newly manufactured statue underwent the *mis pi* and
*pit pi*, the washing and opening of the mouth. It was led from the workshop to
riverbank and orchard, purified, separated from its artisans, clothed, fed, and
installed in a temple. Incantations assigned its birth to gods of craft and
declared it "born in heaven, made on earth."{{ footnote(id="4") }}

That last tension is the theology. Everyone could see that human hands had
worked on earth. The rite denied that those hands exhausted the image's origin.
It converted an artefact into a cultic body through which a deity could see,
hear, eat offerings, receive clothing, travel in procession, and hold court.
The statue was neither mere portrait nor the whole of a god. It was an
authorised locus of presence.

The distinction is familiar even in traditions famous for aniconism. The ark,
the cherubim throne, the temple, the divine Name, the Torah scroll, the Black
Stone, a relic, and a direction of prayer can all mediate presence without
being straightforwardly identical to the being revered. Religious communities
draw their permissible line in different places. Polemic wins by pretending
the rival has no line at all.

This does not rescue every cult image from the ancient critique. A material
object can become a substitute for the reality it was meant to mediate. Priests
can manipulate access. Empires can capture a statue and claim to have abducted
its god. Ritual economies can continue after their cosmology has been
forgotten. The prophets may have diagnosed a genuine collapse of symbol into
object. But the diagnosis is **misdirected relation**, not necessarily bad
arithmetic.

## One high god did not empty the pagan sky

The world into which Christianity and Islam emerged already contained strong
movements toward divine unity. Philosophers spoke of a first principle. Hymns
could raise one deity above all names. Inscriptions acclaimed "one god" or
*Theos Hypsistos*, God Most High. Local cults competed by making their patron
*megas*, great, and sometimes greatest.

Modern scholars have called this "pagan monotheism," though the phrase is
deliberately provocative. The worshipper might regard the many named gods as
powers, names, manifestations, servants, or subordinate members of a hierarchy
whose summit was one. The summit did not make the slopes disappear. Stephen
Mitchell and Peter Van Nuffelen's *One God* shows how easily ancient high-god
language crossed what the modern map treats as a wall between polytheism and
monotheism. Guy Stroumsa more recently argues that *henotheism* may often be
the more accurate word: worship of one without denying the others.

This late-antique setting changes the drama of conversion. The choice was not
always between believing that one divine being existed and believing that
twelve did. It could be a decision to abandon inherited sacrifices, withdraw
from the civic gods, refuse the emperor's cult, and give exclusive allegiance
to the God of Israel. Cult and politics moved before ontology was fully
systematised.

## Paul's crowded monotheism

Paul's argument over food sacrificed to idols catches the whole problem in six
verses. He agrees with the Corinthian slogan that "an idol is nothing in the
world" and that "there is no other God save one." A modern summary stops there.
Paul does not:

{% library(book="1-corinthians", chapter=8, verse=5, verse_end=6) %}
For and if indeed there are those called gods, whether in heaven or on earth, (as there are gods many, and lords many,)
yet to us there is one God, the Father, of whom all things, and we for him; and one Lord, Jesus Christ, by whom are all things, and we by him.
{% end %}

The two words doing the historical work are **"to us."** Paul contrasts the
many with the community's one God and one Lord. His formula is confessional and
allegiance-based. It also contains two figures inside the confession: the
Father "from whom" all things come and Jesus Christ "through whom" they come.
Counting persons is already less useful than mapping agency and worship.

Two chapters later Paul tightens the prohibition. Pagan sacrifices are offered
to demons, he says, and one cannot drink "the Lord's cup" and "the cup of
demons" or share both tables. Whatever *an idol is nothing* means, it does not
mean nothing is encountered through idol cult. The object is powerless; the
relationship is dangerous; the rival powers are sufficiently real to demand a
choice. Sean McDonough therefore reads the passage as a contest between forms
of mediation and allegiance, not merely a census of heaven.

Acts gives Paul a related speech among the images of Athens. The unknown God is
not confined to handmade temples and does not need human service. Yet Paul
begins from an altar already present and quotes Greek poets about humanity as
the divine offspring. The speech does not enter an atheistic city and add its
first god. It rearranges an overfull religious field around one creator and
judge.

## Islam as recovery, not invention

This is where {% wiki(slug="muhammad") %}early Islam{% end %} becomes more than
another example. The Qur'an does not present Muhammad as inventing a better
metaphysics. It presents him as
recalling humanity to an original covenant, confirming earlier messengers, and
following the *millat Ibrahim*, the religion of
{% wiki(slug="abraham") %}Abraham{% end %}.

The central verse is categorical:

> Abraham was neither a Jew nor a Christian, but he was a *hanif*, a
> submitter, and he was not among the associators. (Qur'an 3:67)

The chronology is the argument. Abraham precedes the institutions disputing
his inheritance. He therefore belongs to neither, while both can be judged
against him. Muhammad is commanded to "follow the religion of Abraham, a
*hanif*" (16:123). Jews and Christians are answered, "Rather, the religion of
Abraham" (2:135). Abraham and Ishmael raise the foundations of the House and
pray for a submitting community from their descendants (2:125-129).

The earliest biography develops the same restoration drama. Ibn Ishaq's four
seekers decide that the Quraysh have corrupted "the religion of their father
Abraham." They reject the stone around which their people circle because "it
could neither hear, nor see, nor hurt, nor help," and travel in search of the
{% wiki(slug="hanafiyya") %}Hanafiyya{% end %}. The episode was written in the
form we possess more than a century after Muhammad, and Mun'im Sirry has shown
that *hanif* had a more unstable prehistory than the later clean gloss
"primordial monotheist" suggests. Still, the Qur'an's own restoration claim
does not depend on the biography.

Nor does the Abrahamic-Ishmaelite association begin from nothing in the seventh
century. The fifth-century Christian historian Sozomen presents Saracens as
descendants of Ishmael, notes their circumcision and avoidance of pork, and
says their forebears had adopted the gods of neighbouring peoples.{{ footnote(id="5") }}
His account supports the pre-Islamic circulation of an Arab-Ishmaelite
genealogy. It does not support a straight biological line from Hagar through
every Arab group to the
{% wiki(slug="nabataeans") %}Nabataeans{% end %}, and this article does not
require one. A remembered ancestry can organise identity without functioning
as a modern pedigree.

Fred Donner's "Believers' movement" gives the restoration a social dimension.
On his reconstruction, the earliest community gathered righteous believers in
one God and the Last Day before a sharply bounded Islamic confession finished
forming. Jews and Christians could initially stand within its moral and
eschatological horizon. Later boundaries hardened. Whether every part of
Donner's model holds, it explains why the Qur'an can sound at once like a new
proclamation and an appeal to a community older than confessional names.

The important point for the present thesis is simple: **Islam's own grammar is
return**. The dispute concerns who has preserved Abraham's relation correctly,
not who can devise the most novel god. The [Age of Pisces](/timeline/age-of-pisces/)
places that restoration inside the longer prophetic chronology, while
[*Did the First Mosques Face Petra?*](/articles/the-first-mosques-faced-petra-not-mecca/)
tests the much more contested question of its earliest geography.

## The Qur'an's associators already know a creator

The common picture sets Muhammad before people who believe stones made the
universe. The Qur'an itself repeatedly supplies a different answer. Ask who
created the heavens and earth and subjected the sun and moon, it says, and
"they will surely say: Allah" (29:61). Ask who sends down rain and revives the
earth, and again they say Allah (29:63). The same confession recurs at 31:25,
39:38, and 43:87.

These are polemical questions, not transcripts of a religious census. G. R.
Hawting has warned that later Islamic accounts of a densely idolatrous Hijaz
may have converted Qur'anic polemic into a simple history of paganism. The
epigraphic reconstruction by Ahmad Al-Jallad and Hythem Sidky now adds material
depth: parts of Arabian religious practice had already moved toward a high
creator, while older goddesses and rites could survive as names, intercessors,
talismans, sacrificial customs, and inherited sanctuary practices.

The Qur'an names the actual dispute in the voice it gives its opponents:

> We worship them only that they may bring us nearer to Allah. (Qur'an 39:3)

And elsewhere:

> These are our intercessors with Allah. (Qur'an 10:18)

That is not disbelief in a high creator. It is a theory of access. The
associators say the lesser beings bring worshippers nearer to Allah and plead
at his court. The Qur'an attacks the association of partners, daughters,
intercessors, and unauthorised powers with the creator. Its Abraham asks the
idols why they cannot speak; its verdict on al-Lat, al-Uzza, and Manat is that
human naming has granted them an authority Allah never sent down.

The conflict therefore sits directly across the matrix. Ontologically, the
Qur'an contains angels, jinn, satans, and messengers. Creatively, it assigns
all things to Allah. Cultically, it reserves worship for Allah. In allegiance,
it demands submission to him and his messenger. Representationally, it refuses
the cult image. Mediationally, it permits only the intercession Allah
authorises. "One" is the compression of this total order, not merely the first
integer.

Later Muslim theologians made this order metaphysically formidable. Khalil
Andani's survey shows that *tawhid* became a field of debate over divine
simplicity, attributes, causation, embodiment, transcendence, and
intercession.{{ footnote(id="6") }} The Mu'tazila denied physical and temporal
qualities to God; Ash'aris affirmed eternal attributes without making them
separate gods; philosophers identified God as necessary existence; Isma'ili
thinkers pushed divine unity beyond ordinary predication; Sufis mapped
creation through manifestations of the divine names. There is no honest way to
call all this a disguised belief in finite biological creators.

Yet it arose from a proclamation whose immediate opponents often already said
that Allah created the world. Historically, the first pressure point was not
whether a high creator existed. It was whether worship, authority, and access
could be distributed among lesser beings and their objects.

## The Wheel of Heaven inversion

Within the [Age of Aquarius](/timeline/age-of-aquarius/), the Wheel of Heaven
canon begins by reversing the later settlement. It accepts the ancient
plurality and rejects the supernatural category. The Elohim are many, finite,
embodied makers. Yahweh is a named individual and institutional president, not
being itself. Gabriel or
Jibril is read as a messenger involved in prophetic contact, not an immaterial
species hovering between creation and creator.{{ footnote(id="7") }}

Its master claim about idolatry appears in precisely the period associated
with Abraham:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=3, verse=4) %}
But men, having fallen back into a very primitive state after the destruction of the most intelligent and of centers of progress like Sodom and Gomorrah, began stupidly to worship pieces of stone and idols, forgetting who had created them.
{% end %}

The final clause supplies the frame: **forgetting who had created them**. The
error is not belief that reality contains too many powerful beings. The canon's
own reality contains a civilisation of them. The error is the displacement of
memory from makers to objects.

The same source later binds three claims together: scripture placed "too much
of the supernatural into the truth"; translators replaced plural *Elohim* with
singular "God," transforming "the creators into a single incomprehensible
God"; and Christians were made to worship crossed wood, although "a cross is
not the Christ." [*The Translators' Wager*](/articles/the-translators-wager/)
examines that plural-to-singular transformation across modern literalist
readings.

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=21) %}
However, their errors are great, particularly that of having put too much of the supernatural into the truth, of having badly translated the biblical writings by replacing in the usual Bibles the term Elohim, which designates the creators, with God, a term in the singular, whereas Elohim, in Hebrew, is the plural of Eloha, thus transforming the creators into a single incomprehensible God. The other errors are to have made people worship a piece of wood put in a cross in memory of Jesus Christ. A cross is not the Christ. A crossed piece of wood signifies nothing.
{% end %}

This is not a marginal analogy. It is the canon's theory of religious history:

1. Real, multiple makers interact with humanity.
2. Particular representatives administer contacts, covenants, tests, and
   messages.
3. After catastrophe and cultural loss, memory contracts into names, rites,
   sanctuaries, and images.
4. The sign begins to receive what belonged to the reality behind it.
5. A prophet restores the relationship by destroying or relativising the sign
   and recalling allegiance to the represented source.
6. Later theology universalises the representative into the only
   supernatural God and reclassifies the other agents as angels, demons, false
   gods, or metaphors.

On this model, an ancient confession of "one" can be operationally true without
being ontologically singular. One authorised command, one mission, one
president, one covenant, one direction of loyalty, and one truth about creation
can represent a plurality of creators. A diplomat speaks for a nation without
being the nation's only citizen. A mission commander represents an institution
without being the only officer who exists. The singular is **representative**,
not **exhaustive**.

This is the proposed role of an Eloha in prophetic religion. Yahweh can speak
as Israel's creator because he represents and leads the Elohim project. Gabriel
can deliver a message with the authority behind it. The Qur'an can correctly
break the chain of dead images and inherited intercessors by restoring a single
line of allegiance, even if, in the Wheel of Heaven reading, later Muslim
theology misidentifies the authority at the end of that line as an immaterial
Absolute.

That last sentence marks the sharpest boundary in the article. Islam does not
teach this. The Qur'an repeatedly uses singular creation language for Allah,
denies partners, and rejects divine offspring. Classical *tawhid* excludes the
Elohim hypothesis at its foundation. Wheel of Heaven can therefore say that
Islam's intervention was **corrective in function** while differing from its
**theological self-explanation**. It restored attention from powerless cult
objects and unauthorised intermediaries to the source of creation; the canon
then offers a different account of what that source was.{{ footnote(id="8") }}

## What this reading explains

The hypothesis earns consideration only if it accounts for textual features
that the standard binary leaves awkward.

**It explains why exclusive commands coexist with plural heavens.** "No other
Elohim before me," the sons of God, the council of Psalm 82, Paul's gods and
lords, and the Qur'an's angels and jinn cease to look like embarrassing debris
inside monotheism. The beings can remain while cult and allegiance narrow.

**It explains why idol polemic attacks function rather than manufacture.** The
prophets repeat that an image cannot hear, speak, help, harm, or save. Those are
tests of agency. The issue is not principally the number of statues but their
failure to do what living representatives and remembered makers were said to
do.

**It explains the recurring grammar of restoration.** Abraham stands at the
edge of the [Age of Taurus](/timeline/age-of-taurus/); Moses returns Israel to
the covenant in the [Age of Aries](/timeline/age-of-aries/) after the calf;
prophets accuse the people of forgetting; Jesus calls hearers back to the law's
weightier matters; and the Qur'an, within the
[Age of Pisces](/timeline/age-of-pisces/), calls Abraham neither Jew nor
Christian and commands Muhammad to follow his religion. New messages present
themselves as recoveries because, on the hypothesis, the history being
recovered is one.

**It explains the oscillation between councils and single speakers.** Ancient
texts can show assemblies deciding, then let one name speak for the whole.
Institutional representation is ordinary in political and military records.
It becomes a contradiction only after the singular speaker is defined as the
sole possible divine being.

**It explains why "one" is so often ethical and political.** The Shema joins
Yahweh's oneness to total love. Paul joins one God and one Lord to a community
that cannot eat at rival tables. The Qur'an joins divine unity to worship,
justice, almsgiving, and rejection of inherited authorities. Oneness makes a
people coherent before it makes a metaphysics simple.

## Where the reading can fail

A hypothesis that explains everything is usually a vocabulary for refusing to
lose. This one needs hard edges.

**The first objection is historical development.** Mainstream scholarship can
explain the evidence without extraterrestrial memory. Ancient Israel emerged
from a West Semitic religious environment. Yahweh absorbed titles and
functions; royal and exilic crises drove exclusive worship; scribes preserved,
reworked, and sometimes demoted older divine-council language. Christianity
and Islam inherited that archive and intensified its universal claims. This
model has texts, inscriptions, archaeology, and ordinary mechanisms of cultural
change. The Wheel of Heaven model adds a hidden historical referent that no
accepted inscription identifies as a technological civilisation.

That is the strongest objection, and it stands. The present article does not
disprove it. It argues that the resulting categories still misdescribe the
surface of the texts and that a memory hypothesis remains possible, not that
possibility defeats the better-attested history.

**The second objection is that idol polemic is polemic.** If biblical and
Qur'anic authors caricatured rivals, their rhetoric cannot be used as a clear
window onto a prior memory crisis. The mute-statue argument may tell us more
about community boundary-making than about what image worship replaced. G. R.
Hawting's work makes the same warning for Islam: polemical "associators" cannot
simply be turned into a photographic account of pre-Islamic religion.

**The third objection is category substitution.** Replacing "god" with
"advanced being" does not automatically recover history. It may merely
translate ancient mythology into the prestige language of the technological
present. Susan Palmer and George Chryssides describe exactly this operation in
{% wiki(slug="raelism") %}Raëlism{% end %}: supernatural narratives become
scientific narratives while retaining
religious structure. Stefano Bigliardi presses the criticism further, arguing
that the movement borrows scientific authority more readily than scientific
method. A responsible Wheel of Heaven hermeneutic must therefore show why a
detail is better read operationally, not assume every cloud is a craft and
every messenger an astronaut. [*The Archdeacon and the Dragon*](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/)
models that distinction by separating a structural convergence from a failed
etymology.

**The fourth objection comes from the traditions themselves.** Jewish
monotheism cannot be reduced to a mistranslated plural. Christianity's
Trinitarian one is not a simple numerical one. Islam's Allah is not a collective
misheard as a person. Fourteen centuries of *tawhid* include subtle arguments
about unity, attributes, causation, and mediation that this hypothesis directly
contradicts. Intellectual honesty requires saying so before seeking points of
contact.

**The fifth objection concerns ancestry.** The Abraham-Hagar-Ishmael line is a
powerful scriptural and cultural genealogy. It is not a demonstrated genetic
route from a Bronze Age household through Nabataeans to early Muslims. The
restoration argument rests on remembered Abrahamic identity and Qur'anic
self-presentation, not on proving that every link in a family tree is
biological.

## What would change the verdict

Several discoveries would strengthen the hypothesis. An early text that
distinguished a living creator group from its cult images in unambiguously
non-supernatural language would be substantial. So would independent records
that identified the same named representative, institution, and technical
operation across cultures without literary dependence. Material evidence for
an advanced manufacturing or communication system in the relevant ancient
context would transform the argument from hermeneutics into history.

The reading would weaken if the plural and council texts proved uniformly late,
poetic, and dependent on one narrow scribal tradition; they do not, but their
distribution matters. It would weaken if anti-idol polemic consistently targeted
only the belief that statues themselves created the cosmos, since the memory
and mediation model would add nothing. It would weaken if "one" language could
be shown always and everywhere to deny every other divine being rather than to
order cult and allegiance. The sources already make that universal claim hard
to sustain.

Most decisively, the technological layer should be surrendered if it never
produces evidence unavailable to ordinary religious development. A lens that
only renames gods as visitors is decorative. A serious hypothesis must predict
where textual, archaeological, or material traces should differ.

## The statue and the empty sky

Return to the museum case. The statue is silent. The prophet was right about
that. But two errors can follow from its silence.

The first is the ancient error the polemic attacks: to give the object the
trust, fear, gifts, and obedience that belong to what it represents. The second
is the modern error: to imagine that once the object is exposed as wood or
stone, the only alternative is an empty universe ruled by one invisible,
immaterial mind.

The old texts leave more possibilities open. They remember councils, sons,
messengers, national patrons, demons, rival lords, and named agents. They also
demand one loyalty, one covenant, one source of law, one authorised path, and
one account of creation. Calling the whole first list "polytheism" and the
whole second list "monotheism" hides the machinery by which one became the
other.

The Wheel of Heaven restores a different axis. Not **one god or many gods**, but
**living makers or dead substitutes; remembered source or inherited sign;
authorised representative or accumulated intermediary**. On that axis, Islam's
*tawhid* can be recognised as one of history's great corrective acts without
being made to say what it does not say. It broke the authority of objects and
recalled Abraham as the figure before the divisions. The corpus agrees with the
correction and disputes the metaphysics that followed.

That is the hypothesis in its strongest defensible form. The prophets were not
necessarily emptying heaven. They were clearing the line of sight.
