On the evening of 7 October 1975, by the account the canon gives, a
French motor-racing journalist sat down to dinner on another world. The
host introduced the company with the unhurried courtesy of a man
presiding over his own table: directly opposite the guest, Jesus,
recreated from a cell preserved before the crucifixion; at his right
hand Moses; at his left Elijah; beside Jesus, the one remembered on
Earth as Buddha; a little further along, Muhammad. And then the
sentence on which the whole architecture of this corpus's comparative
work turns:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=64) %}
At his right is found Moses, at his left Elijah, at the left of Jesus
is seated the one whom, on Earth, one remembers under the name of
Buddha. A little further you can see Mohammed in whose writings I am
called Allah for he did not dare to name me out of respect. The forty
men and women present at this meal are all beings representative of the
religions created following our contacts on the Earth.
{% end %}

Five guests are named. The other thirty-five are a number. And even the
number is softer than the English suggests: the French original says
*la quarantaine d'hommes et de femmes* — the forty-*or-so* — a
roundness the published translation quietly hardened into a
count.{{ footnote(id="1") }} The canon never returns to the table with
a list. It never says who sat between Buddha and Muhammad, or whether
the woman across from Elijah founded a religion still practiced or one
extinct for three thousand years, or how many chairs held
representatives of traditions no historian has ever heard of because
their peoples left no writing.

This is, on its face, a strange way for a revelation to handle its own
prophetology. It is also — and this is the subject of the present
Explainer — a methodological gift. A canon that said *every* religion
descends from our contacts would license promiscuity: everything
authentic, nothing checkable, the word "prophet" inflated to
worthlessness. A canon that named its complete list would license a
sect: the saved traditions enumerated, the door shut, nothing left to
investigate. This canon does neither. It asserts that the class of
authentic contact-traditions is real, plural, and *bounded* — forty-ish
chairs, no more — and then leaves the seating chart almost entirely
blank. The blank is an assignment. Somebody has to work out, tradition
by tradition, with reasons stated in public, who was plausibly at that
table. And the canon raises the stakes of the assignment in its own
voice, because two years earlier it had already warned that the age
would be crowded with claimants:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=179) %}
It is said that when the times have come there will be many false
prophets: you have only to look around you to perceive that the times
have arrived. False prophets the makers of horoscopes, the newspapers
are full of them; false prophets those who want to hold, to the letter,
to the ancient writings, that is to say to the messages given by the
Elohim to the primitives of remote epochs, and who refuse the benefits
of science. They prefer to believe what primitive and narrow-minded men
recopied trembling with fear while listening to those they took for
gods because they came from the sky, rather than the message
transmitted to beings who no longer kneel stupidly before all that
comes from the sky and who try to understand the universe, to whom one
can address oneself as to adults. Look around you and you will see the
crowd of fanatical and obscurantist religious sects, which attract the
permeable young people, thirsty for truth.
{% end %}

Forty-or-so authentic lines; a crowd of false ones; and a messenger
described, in the canon's most quoted self-limitation, as
{% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=89) %}"the last prophet, but the first one to address Mankind asking them to understand and not to believe"{% end %}.
A tradition with that epistemology written into its founding documents
does not get to assign chairs by feel. It owes its readers an
instrument.

The corpus has, in fact, been building one. The wiki's
{% wiki(slug="list-of-prophets-and-religions") %}catalogue of prophets
and religions{% end %} classifies the world's principal traditions on a
five-tier authenticity scale — canonical, highly likely, likely,
unlikely, highly unlikely — and states five criteria by which the
assignments are made. What the catalogue states in the compressed
register of a reference entry, this article does at full length: where
the criteria come from, why these five and not others, how the
instrument is calibrated against known cases at both ends, what it says
about the traditions that have no prophet at all, and what it flags at
the frontier. The claim-typing is worth stating at the door. That the
Elohim made contact with human populations and that religions descend
from those contacts is a framework premise of this project — explicit
in the canon, not endorsed by mainstream scholarship. The *instrument*
built on that premise, though, is an inferred articulation of the
corpus's own method, and it is designed so that a reader who rejects
the premise entirely can still check every gear of it. That design
choice is not diplomacy. It is the whole point, and the final section
will return to it.

## Every canon builds a doorman

The problem is not new. It is close to the oldest problem organized
religion has, and the great traditions' solutions to it are the natural
history against which any new instrument should be designed.

Early Christianity's version arrived in the second century wearing the
face of a wealthy shipowner's son. Marcion of Sinope came to Rome
around 140 CE with a proposal of severe elegance: the real canon was
one Gospel (an edited Luke) and ten Pauline letters, and the Hebrew
scriptures were the record of a different, lower god. The church
excommunicated him and kept his idea — the idea that there should be a
*list*. The Muratorian fragment, usually dated to the decades around
200, is the earliest surviving attempt at one, and it already reasons
like a committee: this book is received, that one is disputed, this
forgery "cannot be received into the catholic church, for it is not
fitting that gall be mixed with honey." Not until 367, in the
thirty-ninth festal letter of Athanasius of Alexandria, does the
twenty-seven-book New Testament appear as an exact, closed enumeration
— three centuries of border control between the revelation and the
list of its authentic records. The rabbis ran a slower, quieter version
of the same process; the once-standard tale that a council at Yavneh
closed the Hebrew canon around 90 CE turns out to be a nineteenth-century
scholarly legend,{{ footnote(id="4") }} but the underlying
activity — books weighed, disputed, admitted, excluded, over
generations — is not legend at all.

Islam built the most formidable doorman of the three. The Qur'an's
transmission was comparatively controlled; the hadith — the hundreds of
thousands of circulating reports of what the Prophet said and did —
were not, and within two centuries the tradition had responded by
inventing what can only be called a forensic science of testimony. Every
report was required to carry its *isnād*, the named chain of its
transmitters; a companion discipline, *ʿilm al-rijāl*, "the science of
men," compiled critical biographies of the transmitters themselves —
their memories, their honesty, whether the dates of their lives
actually permitted the meetings the chain asserted; and every report
was filed on a graded scale from sound to fabricated.{{ footnote(id="3") }}
The medieval collectors rejected, by their own accounting, the
overwhelming majority of everything they examined. One can dispute any
particular verdict; one cannot dispute the achievement of the form. A
civilization decided that reverence for its Prophet *required* rather
than forbade the systematic grading of every sentence attributed to
him.

The pattern beneath all three cases is the one this corpus takes as
precedent. A tradition confident of its founding does not fear an
authentication instrument; it builds one, because the alternative —
admitting everything that flatters it — is how a revelation drowns in
its own apocrypha. The instrument is not the skeptic's tax on the
believer. It is the believer's own immune system. And there is a
corollary the three histories teach equally clearly: the instrument
always arrives *after* the canon, is always calibrated on cases the
community already regards as settled, and is always then extended to
the unsettled ones. Athanasius did not derive the Gospels from
criteria; he derived criteria from the Gospels and used them on the
Gospel of Thomas. The hadith graders did not doubt Muhammad; they
doubted transmitters. Anyone who objects that the present corpus's
instrument is calibrated on its own canon is describing how every such
instrument in recorded history has been built. The objection will get
its full hearing below; the natural history had to come first, because
it shows the objection proves too much.

## The disciplines of doubt

Religious civilizations built doormen for their own doors. The modern
disciplines built something more transferable: general methods for
grading testimony about extraordinary events, and the corpus's
instrument borrows from four of them by name.

The first is the oldest and bluntest. David Hume's essay on miracles,
published in 1748, reduces to a single maxim: no testimony suffices to
establish a marvel "unless the testimony be of such a kind, that its
falsehood would be more miraculous than the fact which it endeavours to
establish." Strip the polemical enjoyment from the sentence and what
remains is the sober core of every later method: testimony is evidence,
evidence competes with prior probability, and the assessor's first duty
is to ask which is more likely — that the event occurred, or that the
report is mistaken, embellished, or invented. The corpus's fifth test
will institutionalize exactly this question, with one deliberate and
openly declared difference from Hume: the prior. On the framework's
premises, contact between the Elohim and human populations is not a
violation of natural law; it is a recurring feature of terrestrial
history. The skeptic sets the prior of any given contact-claim near
zero and Hume's maxim shreds every report; the framework sets it
substantially higher and the maxim starts doing discriminating work
*between* reports. Same engine, different fuel — and the honest move,
which the instrument makes, is to say so on the label rather than
smuggle the prior in the axioms.{{ footnote(id="8") }}

The second is the twentieth century's most sustained exercise in
grading sacred testimony: the quest for the historical Jesus, which
codified its toolkit as the "criteria of authenticity" — embarrassment,
discontinuity, multiple attestation, coherence.{{ footnote(id="2") }}
The criteria are directly ancestral to two of the corpus's tests, and
so is their obituary. A 2012 collection edited by Chris Keith and
Anthony Le Donne assembled the field's own verdict that the criteria
had failed *as an algorithm* — sources are interpretation all the way
down, and no mechanical sieve extracts uninterpreted event from
interpreted memory. What survived the demise is what was always true:
the criteria are a discipline for arguments, a shared public grammar in
which a claim about authenticity can be stated, attacked, and defended.
The corpus's instrument inherits both the tools and the humility. Six
tests, and not one of them a machine.

The third discipline is forensic, and it contributes the instrument's
sharpest edge. In the 1870s an Italian physician writing as "Ivan
Lermolieff" scandalized the art world by reattributing old masters on
the evidence of earlobes and fingernails — the incidental details a
forger, concentrating on the famous features, executes on habit and
therefore in his own hand rather than his model's. Carlo Ginzburg's
celebrated essay on Giovanni Morelli's method named the underlying
logic the "evidential paradigm": authenticity betrays itself, and
fabrication betrays itself, in the unregarded margins.{{ footnote(id="2") }}
The paradigm's cleanest modern demonstration in religious materials is
the affair of the Gospel of Jesus's Wife — the business-card-sized
papyrus fragment unveiled at Harvard in 2012, in which Jesus says "my
wife." For four years the argument ran on content and ink chemistry and
stalled. What ended it was provenance: Ariel Sabar's investigation
walked the fragment's ownership story backward, document by document,
until the paper trail dissolved into a specific, findable man with a
Coptology dropout's training and a motive, and the fragment's champion
conceded the probable forgery. The lesson generalizes and the
instrument encodes it: when content analysis deadlocks, *pedigree*
decides. Where did the text physically come from, in whose hands, on
what dates, and does every link in that chain survive being pulled?

The fourth contribution is a frame rather than a tool: Aviezer Tucker's
account of historiography as Bayesian inference — competing hypotheses
evaluated by how well each predicts the surviving evidence, with priors
stated rather than hidden. The corpus adopts the logic and refuses the
arithmetic, for reasons the footnote states plainly.{{ footnote(id="8") }}
What the frame contributes is the instrument's overall shape: the
question is never "is this tradition provable?" but always "which
hypothesis — authentic contact, or the null — better predicts the
totality of what survives?"

## The instrument: six tests and a scale

The corpus's catalogue states five criteria. Formalized, ordered, and
joined by a sixth that the canon itself supplies, they read as follows.
Each is a test in the forensic sense: a question put to the evidence,
with stated failure modes, whose answers accumulate toward a verdict
without any single answer dictating one.

**Test 1 — Contact specificity.** Does the founding narrative describe
an *event*, or an atmosphere? The test asks for operational texture:
dates, places, appointments kept and repeated, physical apparatus,
dialogue with information content, the mundane logistics that
eyewitnesses report and mystics do not. The Raëlian founding narrative
is the type specimen within the framework — a named crater, a
six-morning schedule, a book brought along on instruction — but the
form is ancient: the burning bush is at a named mountain, Moroni's
visits fall on a dated September night with annual follow-ups, Muhammad's
revelations begin in an identified cave in a dated month. At the other
pole sit foundings that offer only weather — a feeling of presence, an
ineffable unity — which may be spiritually profound and are untestable
by design. The failure mode of Test 1 is romantic detail added late; a
narrative that grows more specific with each retelling decades after
the event scores against itself, which is why Test 1 never operates
without Test 4.

**Test 2 — Payload.** What did the contact *deliver* that the founder's
milieu could not have supplied? This is the criterion of discontinuity
transposed into the framework: the instrument searches the founding
deposit for content consistent with the Elohim program as the canon
describes it — plurality of inhabited worlds, creation as deliberate
biological engineering, the longevity of the visitors, a promised
return, an embassy or temple protocol — delivered *before* such content
was culturally available, or delivered with a specificity the ambient
culture cannot explain. Payload is the instrument's most
framework-internal test and the one that does the most sorting; the
cross-tradition inventory of what the payload actually looks like — the
recurring signature this article calls the watermark — gets a section
of its own below. It is
also the test most vulnerable to the sharpshooter fallacy — scanning a
vast corpus for anything that resembles the target and ignoring the
misses — so the instrument requires the payload to be *load-bearing* in
the tradition's own self-understanding, doctrine the tradition itself
treats as central, and not a verse fished from the margins. For the
founderless traditions of deep antiquity — Sumer, Egypt, the Maya, the
collective strata of the likely tier, where there is no contact
narrative to test — the payload test runs on *motifs*: the small set
of scenes that recur across unconnected mythologies with a stubbornness
that diffusion and shared psychology strain to cover — the assembled
council, the flood and the warned survivor, the serpent who gives
knowledge, the war in heaven, civilization received as a gift — and
that the framework reads as the damaged-archive residue of the same
events. The corpus maintains its own
{% wiki(slug="list-of-mythemes-and-mythological-motifs") %}catalogue of
mythemes and motifs{% end %} for exactly this work, and a
[companion Explainer](/articles/the-filing-cabinet-of-the-gods/) reads
the discipline's great instrument — Stith Thompson's *Motif-Index* —
at full length; a handful of motifs, present together and load-bearing,
are among the most telling authenticity markers an ancient tradition
can carry.

**Test 3 — External traceability.** Do the founder and the founding
event leave marks in records the tradition does not control? Hostile
witnesses are worth double: Tacitus on the Christians, the colonial
police file on the Cao Đài petition of 1926, the Quaker missionary's
journal at Cornplanter's town in the June of Handsome Lake's
vision.{{ footnote(id="9") }} A tradition whose founding exists only in
its own retrospective literature can still be authentic; it simply
cannot *score* here, and for remote antiquity the test relaxes into
archaeology by necessity. The failure mode is circular attestation —
"independent" sources that on inspection all drink from the tradition's
own well, the religious-history equivalent of citogenesis.

**Test 4 — Chain of custody.** Can today's text be walked backward to
the founding deposit, link by link, and how much interpretation
accreted at each link? The test rewards autograph proximity above
everything. By its light the catalogue's quiet stars are the modern
Japanese foundresses: Nakayama Miki's Ofudesaki survives in 1,711
verses of her own handwriting; Deguchi Nao's oracles were set down by
her own automatic hand — the hand of a woman who could not read what it
wrote, which incidentally armors the text against the suspicion of
literary calculation. The Gathas of Zarathustra show the opposite
profile: a founder-composition preserved, but through a transmission so
long and so damaged that the surrounding Avesta is rubble — which is
precisely why the catalogue holds Zoroastrianism at highly-likely while
suspecting its founding was as real as any. Test 4 is where the
corpus's own {% wiki(slug="reference-corpus-of-the-raelian-canon") %}reference-text program{% end %}
and translation apparatus live: a project that grades other traditions'
custody chains is obliged to fix and audit its own, and the obligation
runs even when the audit cuts against the canon — as it did when the
corpus's provenance work on the founding book's Kabbalah passage
[located its likeliest 1957 print source](/articles/the-book-closest-to-the-truth/)
and published the finding.

**Test 5 — The null hypothesis.** The instrument's governor. For every
tradition, the contact reading competes against the strongest available
alternative — documented literary sources, altered states of
consciousness, syncretic assembly, pious fraud, ordinary fraud — and
the contact reading carries the burden. The null is not an insult; it
is the default that makes a positive verdict mean something. Test 5 is
where Hume sits, and where the framework's declared prior does its
work: the question asked is never "could a human have invented this?"
(a human can invent anything) but "does the invention hypothesis
actually *predict* this evidence better?" When the founder's own prior
publications contain the cosmology, the null predicts the deposit
perfectly and the verdict follows. When an illiterate widow's automatic
hand produces decades of internally consistent oracles announcing the
reconstruction of the world, the null is still available — dissociative
graphomania is a documented phenomenon — but it predicts the *content*
less well, and the balance shifts.

**Test 6 — The diachronic test.** The canon's own contribution, from
the false-prophet passage quoted above, and the only test that is
explicitly temporal. The {% wiki(slug="prophet") %}prophets{% end %}
spoke in religious vocabulary because their audiences could process no
other; the {% wiki(slug="apocalypse") %}Apocalypse{% end %} — the age
of revelation, counted from 1945 — is defined precisely by the arrival
of an audience that can finally be addressed in the vocabulary of
science. A claimed revelation *after* that threshold that retreats
into mystification — horoscope logic, initiatory secrecy, vibratory
metaphysics — is moving against the direction of the whole program, and
the canon calls the mismatch by name. The test is structural rather
than personal: it does not require bad faith of anyone; it registers a
regression of register. Its bite is real: it bears on any post-1945
claimant, this canon's own movement included, which is one reason the
canon's insistence on "understand and not believe" reads as it does.

**The scale.** Six tests feed five tiers — canonical, highly likely,
likely, unlikely, highly unlikely — and the tiers are ordinal on
purpose. The corpus does not assign numerical scores, for the reason
the footnote on Bayes states: in historical argument, numbers this soft
do not add precision, they impersonate it.{{ footnote(id="8") }} Three
structural rules complete the instrument. First, *the canonical tier is
closed by the canon itself*: Judaism, Christianity, Islam, Buddhism,
Mormonism, and Raëlism are canonical because the founding books name
them —
{% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=54) %}"the Buddhist religion, the Islamic, the Mormons"{% end %},
the seating order of the meal, Joseph Smith listed among "all the other
great prophets" at LWTE 3:34 — and the instrument neither elects
members to that tier nor audits them into it. Its jurisdiction is the
open tiers, which is exactly the jurisdiction Athanasius's criteria had.
Second, *the classifications are two-ledger*: an authenticity tier is a
verdict about a founding narrative, never about the spiritual, ethical,
or cultural worth of a living tradition or its practitioners — the
catalogue says this in its own voice and the instrument repeats it,
because the distinction is the difference between comparative method
and sectarian scorekeeping. Third, *every assignment is revisable*: the
tiers are the corpus's current positions, held with reasons, movable by
evidence — and the frontier section below exists because the instrument
generates its own docket of cases to revisit.

One more boundary, because it prevents a whole family of confusions:
the instrument measures authenticity of origin, and origin only. It
does not measure success, size, longevity, or social benefit — the
Stark model measures those, and by Stark's criteria a tradition can
flourish magnificently on a fabricated founding or wither on an
authentic one. The two axes are independent, and most public argument
about "legitimate religion" goes wrong in its first sentence by
collapsing them.

## The watermark

Test 2 asks what a founding delivered. Run the question across the
catalogue's upper tiers and a second-order fact emerges: the deliveries
resemble one another. Beneath the wild variety of idiom — covenant at a
desert mountain, dharma under a fig tree, recitation in a Meccan cave,
plates from a New York hillside, six mornings in an Auvergne crater —
the founding deposits of the traditions the framework grades highest
carry a small, stable set of recurring features. The corpus calls the
set the watermark, on the analogy the word supplies: a mark worked into
the paper at manufacture, easy to miss in ordinary reading, disclosed
when the page is held to the light — and absent, however skilled the
printing, from paper made elsewhere.

Reading for the watermark requires a preliminary distinction, and it is
the distinction on which this whole section runs: a founding and the
institution that inherits it are different objects, and the instrument
grades only the first. Religions are tells. At the bottom lies the
founding stratum — the deposit laid down within the founder's
generation or as close to it as the custody chain reaches; above it,
century by century, accumulates the institutional overburden: the
professional priesthood, the property, the alliance with the throne,
the doctrine hardened into a boundary-marker, the founder's living
message reissued as the institution's charter. Max Weber gave the
process its permanent name — the routinization of charisma — and
observed that it is not a scandal, it is a fate: the founder's circle
must become an organization or the message dies with the generation
that heard it, and the organization, being human, thereafter behaves
like every other human organization with an endowment to protect. The
canon's own diagnosis of the process is harsher than Weber's and
targets its own family of traditions first — the churches that buried
the messages under the monuments:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=248) %}
Leave these Churches which are only monuments raised by primitives,
temples where they could adore things without value, pieces of wood and
pieces of metal. The Elohim do not need a temple in each city to feel
loved, it suffices them that men try to communicate telepathically with
them, rediscovering thus the original meaning of prayer.
{% end %}

*The original meaning* — the phrase concedes the stratigraphy. A
tradition can hold a canonical tier while its mature institution is, on
the canon's own account, a monument standing on top of the thing it was
built to transmit. The catalogue's open-questions register already
practices the distinction, holding Smith's founding contact apart from
the later LDS institution and the Jesus contact apart from the
council-era church; the watermark section states the underlying rule:
the tier belongs to the stratum, never to the overburden.

The rule would be pious hand-waving if the founding stratum were
unreachable, and the reason it is more than hand-waving is that the
stratum keeps proving *recoverable* — by measurement. The type case is
the one this corpus has already treated at
[full length](/articles/the-first-mosques-faced-petra-not-mecca/): the
earliest recoverable layer of Islam. Dan Gibson's surveys of the
first-century mosques found their prayer-walls oriented with striking
consistency toward Petra rather than Mecca, a claim that lives or dies
on protractors and foundation plans rather than on anyone's theology;
and on an independent line of evidence, Fred Donner's reconstruction of
the earliest community found an ecumenical "Believers" movement —
monotheist, pietist, apocalyptic, admitting Jews and Christians as
believers alongside the Prophet's Arabs — that only hardened into a
bounded confession generations later.{{ footnote(id="12") }} Two
unrelated methods, one result: the founding stratum of Islam differs
measurably from the institution that curates its memory, and the
difference is in exactly the direction the decay model predicts — a
wide, direct, disintermediated founding narrowed by its inheritors into
an apparatus.

The stratum even preserves its own name, and the name is a thesis.
Before "Islam" — *submission* — settled as the movement's label, the
earliest layer calls the religion it seeks and restores
*al-Ḥanīfiyyah*: the religion of Abraham, from *ḥanīf*, "one who
inclines away" — away from the idols, toward the upright original
worship. The Qur'an's central Abraham verse stakes the whole claim —
"Abraham was neither a Jew nor a Christian, but he was an upright one
(*ḥanīf*), a *muslim*, and he was not of the idolaters" (Q 3:67) — a
founder claimed *prior to* the traditions that quarrel over him, a
religion presented as recovered rather than founded. And Ibn Isḥāq's
biography opens the story a generation before Muhammad, with four named
men of the Quraysh who conclude that the stone their people circle
"could neither hear, nor see, nor hurt, nor help" and go "their several
ways in the lands, seeking the Hanifiyya, the religion of Abraham."
Read at the stratum, the founding of Islam is a restoration movement of
the Ishmael branch of the Abrahamic line — a fellowship of seekers
after a lost original, whose defining gesture is the turning-away from
vacant idols (the fourth watermark feature wearing its own etymology),
whose early community admitted Jews and Christians as fellow believers,
and whose ethical register is the Meccan suras' care for the orphan and
the traveler. Gibson's parallel is the right one: the earliest
Christians were followers of "the Way" before Antioch coined
"Christian," and on his reading Hanafiyya stood to "Islam" the same
way — the corpus's {% wiki(slug="hanafiyya") %}Hanafiyya{% end %} entry
carries the full file.

What the institution then did to that stratum is the decay model with
dates attached. Within a generation of the founder's death the
restoration movement was an empire of conquest; within a century, on
the revisionist reading, the name had changed, the prayer-direction had
changed, and the text had been re-pointed under an imperial governor;
and the connective narrative through which the world knows the
founding — the biography, the conquest story, the geography of the
origins — was composed and fixed by that empire's scholars roughly a
century and a half to two centuries after the events, the sīra itself
surviving only in a still later redaction.{{ footnote(id="14") }} The
hard evidence contemporary with the founding — the orientations in the
foundations, the coins and inscriptions, the Qur'an's own vocabulary of
believers and *ḥunafāʾ* — keeps disagreeing with that late narrative,
and the disagreement runs in one direction: the stratum is gentler,
wider, and more Abrahamic than the institution that narrates it. The
corpus's Petra file is argued, contested, and held with the tension the
sources force; what matters for the instrument is the demonstration of
*method*: the founding layer of a world religion is an empirical
object, and it can be drilled to.

Drill across the upper tiers, then, and the watermark resolves into
five features.

**Love as the operative ethic.** The founding strata legislate almost
nothing and command affection almost everywhere. The twin commandment
on which Jesus hangs "all the law and the prophets"; the mettā the
Buddha's earliest discourses radiate outward to all beings; the early
Meccan suras, whose fury is reserved for the neglect of orphans and the
hoarding of wealth; the Cao Đài amnesty announced amid "great
laughter"; the Bahá'í writings' relentless unity refrain. Institutions
inherit the ethic and reissue it as compliance; the founding versions
are strikingly pre-legal — a posture, an orientation of the person. The
canon's version makes love, precisely, the admission criterion for the
species:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=4, verse=53) %}
The peoples that do not manage to prove their intelligence will be
destroyed. Now men have almost proven that they are worthy of being
admitted by their creators as their equals; they lack… only a little
love. Love among themselves and above all toward their creators.
{% end %}

**Infinity, in time and space.** The founding strata open onto
enormousness: the Hebrew *olam*, the deep "forever" older than the
walls it was later confined to; the Buddhist world-ages through which
kalpas turn like slow wheels; the Jain cosmos with no first moment; the
canon's universe infinite in both directions, up through the scales and
down, [treated at length in its own
Explainer](/articles/the-infinite-in-both-directions/). The third book
goes as far as crediting the intuition wherever it appears — "Those who
think of 'God' as Infinity, as most oriental religions teach, are
right" (LWTE 1:72) — and the decay model's signature move runs the
other way: the institution domesticates infinity into a calendar, a
liturgy, a jurisdiction. A founding that shrinks the cosmos to the size
of its own parish was never carrying the watermark; a tradition whose
parish has shrunk a once-enormous cosmos has merely buried it.

**Respect toward the creators, without deification.** The strangest
feature, and the most diagnostic, because it cuts against the grain of
what institutions want. The founding strata consistently model the
right relation to the higher party as the respect of the younger for
the elder — gratitude, deference, aspiration to adulthood — while
resisting the slide into grovel. The Buddha deflects divinization for
four centuries of his tradition's memory; Muhammad's revelation
insists, against his followers' every instinct, that he is only a
messenger; the Gospel Jesus returns the compliment "good master" with
"why callest thou me good?" The canon states the norm from both ends of
the relation: the creators themselves, it reports, "could not bear to
see their creatures… on their hands and knees adoring them as if they
were idols" (LWTE 2:19), and the founding book's condition for the
{% wiki(slug="great-return") %}return{% end %} specifies the posture
exactly — {% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=25) %}"without religious mysticism, as responsible men but respecting their creators"{% end %}.
The third book compresses the whole trajectory into a sentence:

{% library(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=3, verse=37) %}
Until recently, Humanity was worshipping the creators who had created
each man with the ability to understand the creators, so now Man must
understand the creators, so as to be able to love them even more.
{% end %}

**The refusal of the vacant idol.** The commandment against images is
the watermark feature institutional religion has misread longest,
because the misreading flatters the institution. Read as a ban on
*other gods*, it becomes a charter for confessional monopoly — an odd
reading of a scripture whose own word for the divine party,
{% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %}, is grammatically plural. Read
in the founding stratum's own terms, the target is vacancy: the made
thing mistaken for the makers, representation eclipsing referent. The
canon's definition is exact — after the fall of the old centers, men
"began stupidly to worship pieces of stone and idols, *forgetting who
had created them*" (TBWTT 3:4) — idolatry as a failure of memory, the
statue standing where the engineers should be. The founding strata
police this boundary with a severity their institutions never
maintain. The Hebrew Bible's most instructive case is the bronze
serpent: a genuine artifact of the Mosaic contact, raised as a healing
standard in the wilderness — and centuries later, when the artifact
itself had become an incense-receiving cult object, a reforming king
{% libref(book="2-kings", chapter=18, verse=4) %}"brake in pieces the brazen serpent that Moses had made… and he called it Nehushtan"{% end %}
— smashed it, and demoted its name to "the bronze thing."{{ footnote(id="13") }}
The founding layer would rather destroy its own relic than let the
relic eat the reference. The same reflex marks early Buddhism, which
for centuries declined to portray its founder at all — the empty
throne, the footprints, the wheel, the riderless horse standing where
the man is *not*{{ footnote(id="11") }} — and early Islam, whose
aniconism began as the same discipline before hardening into the
institution's décor policy. And the *ḥunafāʾ* met above — the seekers
who judged that the stone their people circled "could neither hear,
nor see, nor hurt, nor help" — are the memory, preserved inside a
founding stratum, of exactly this refusal persisting *between*
foundings, carried by a lineage with no prophet of its own in the
field, waiting for one.

**A plural sky.** The founding strata assume, with the casualness of
people reporting rather than speculating, that the heavens are
inhabited and administered: the *bene elohim* of the Hebrew text, the
Qur'an's God of *the worlds* in its opening line, the "worlds without
number" of the Mormon deposit —
{% libref(book="book-of-moses", chapter=1, verse=33) %}"And worlds without number have I created"{% end %}
— the thousandfold and millionfold world-systems of early Buddhist
cosmology, each with its own earths and heavens, through which the
dharma is said to hold everywhere. Institutions, whose jurisdiction
ends at the congregation, flattened the plural sky into a metaphysical
singular and then spent the modern period embarrassed by its
re-population. On the framework's premises the pluralism of the
founding strata is the least surprising watermark feature of all: it
is what witnesses of an actual visitation would say, and what their
successors — who had met no one — would find least useful to preserve.

What does the watermark do for the instrument? Three things, stated as
corollaries. First, it gives Test 2 its positive template: payload is
this signature — love as admission criterion, infinity, respect
without grovel, the policed boundary against vacancy, the plural sky —
load-bearing in the tradition's own founding self-understanding.
Second, it forces the sampling rule: grade the earliest recoverable
stratum, because the overburden is evidence of nothing. Institutional
corruption is predicted equally by the framework (messages decay in
human custody; the canon says so in its own voice) and by the null
(human institutions drift; sociology says so in its) — and a datum
both hypotheses predict cannot discriminate between them. A corrupt
church neither convicts nor acquits its founding; only the founding
convicts or acquits the founding. Third, it sharpens the negative
verdicts that follow: the decisive finding against a fabricated
tradition is never that its institution decayed — whose has not? — but
that its founding stratum, drilled to directly, already lacks the
watermark. Which is the bench the next section sits down at.

## The negative control: Scientology on the bench

An instrument is calibrated at both ends. The canonical six anchor the
positive end by stipulation; the negative end needs a case rich enough
in documentation to be run through all six tests in public, and the
catalogue's strongest negative verdict — Scientology, highly unlikely —
is exactly that case. The verdict deserves better than a sneer, because
nothing discredits a method faster than treating its conclusions as
foregone. Here is the docket.

*Test 1 — contact specificity.* The founding document of the movement
is *Dianetics: The Modern Science of Mental Health*, published in May
1950 — trailed the month before in *Astounding Science Fiction* — and
it contains no contact narrative at all. It presents itself as
exactly what its subtitle says: a science, a therapeutic technology,
with an engineer's rhetoric and no revelation scene. There is no
crater, no cave, no angel with a schedule. The religious framing,
the "applied religious philosophy," the ministerial collars and the
cross arrive in 1953–54, in step with the incorporation of the first
churches — a sequence the movement's own archives document and Hugh
Urban's history reconstructs, down to the 1953 correspondence in which
Hubbard weighs what he calls "the religion angle" as a matter of
positioning and receipts.{{ footnote(id="5") }} A founding that
back-fills its revelation is the mirror image of what Test 1 looks for:
here the narrative does not grow from an event, the event grows from
the organization's needs, seventeen years late.

*Test 2 — payload.* The cosmological deposit, when it comes, is the
confidential OT III narrative of 1967: Xenu, the Galactic
Confederacy, the volcanoes, the hydrogen bombs, the thetans packaged
with implants, the craft that resemble DC-8s.{{ footnote(id="6") }}
Run the payload question against it: what does this deposit contain
that its author's milieu could not supply? The answer is nothing,
because the milieu in question is Hubbard's own bibliography. He had
been a professional space-opera writer for *Astounding* and its pulp
kin for over a decade before Dianetics; the whole-track material is
continuous in idiom, furniture, and narrative reflex with the fiction
he was paid by the word to produce. Where the Book of Mormon's
cosmological content exceeds what an upstate farmhand's shelf can
easily explain — the instrument will come to that — OT III's content is
*predicted* by its author's shelf, item for item. On Test 2 the
tradition does not score low; it scores negative, because the deposit
is affirmative evidence for the null.

*Test 3 — external traceability.* Abundant, and running the wrong way.
The founder's life is among the best-documented of any religious
founder in history, and the documentation — naval records, court
files, the biographical record assembled by Russell Miller and others
against ferocious litigation — falsifies the official hagiography on
point after point: the war wounds healed by the young science, the
crippled-and-blinded-veteran origin story, the nuclear-physicist
credential. A tradition is not damned because its founder embellished;
Test 3's registers are colder than that. What they record is that
everywhere the external evidence can check the tradition's account of
its own origin, the account fails — the exact inverse of the profile
the test rewards.

*Test 4 — chain of custody.* Impeccable, and that is the joke the
instrument was built to get. The OT materials are founder-autograph;
the church affirms Hubbard's authorship; the transmission is
notarized, litigated, and complete. Test 4, remember, does not ask
whether a deposit is *true*; it asks whether today's text can be
walked back to the founding hand. Here it can, perfectly — and what
waits at the end of the walk is a 1967 manuscript in the handwriting
of a science-fiction professional. A perfect chain of custody is a
perfect delivery system for whatever stands at its head. Scientology's
custody chain convicts its payload, which is the instrument working
exactly as designed: the tests are conjunctive in force, and no single
test — not even a perfect score on one — carries a verdict alone.

*Test 5 — the null hypothesis.* The null here is not merely available;
it is the best-evidenced explanation of any founding in the modern
record. Documented prior sources in the founder's own published
corpus; documented financial architecture, with the revelation
dispensed by priced subscription up a ladder of auditing fees;
documented consciousness of positioning in the founder's own 1953
letters. The invention hypothesis does not just accommodate this
evidence — it predicts it, in detail. On the framework's premises the
prior for authentic contact is generous; no prior survives a
likelihood ratio like this one.

*Test 6 — the diachronic test.* The claim arrives in 1950–67, deep
inside the age the canon defines by the audience's new capacity to be
addressed "as adults," in scientific plain speech. The tradition's
response to that audience is graded secrecy: the cosmology is
confidential, tiered, and priced, disclosed only at upper levels after
years of preparation — mystification as a business model, the precise
structural inversion of a message that asks to be understood rather
than believed.

Verdict: highly unlikely, on six tests out of six, several of them
returning affirmative evidence for fabrication rather than mere absence
of evidence for contact. And one clarification the two-ledger rule
requires: none of this is a finding about Scientologists, many of whom
report real benefit from practices whose founding narrative they may
never have been shown. The verdict is about a founding narrative,
reached by tests stated in advance — and the same tests, run on
Thelema, return the altered-state null (a founding "reception" fully
continuous with its receiver's documented ritual practice and literary
gifts), and on Theosophy the syncretic-assembly null (a cosmology whose
sources sit on nameable library shelves). The catalogue files all three
accordingly.

## Two Americans

The instrument's decisive demonstration, though, is not Scientology
alone. It is Scientology set beside the case that superficially
resembles it most — because any critic can build a sieve that catches
the tradition everyone expects it to catch. The test of the sieve is
the pair.

Joseph Smith and L. Ron Hubbard are the two most consequential new
scripture-producers in American history, and the surface symmetry is
almost complete. Two charismatic Americans of modest formal education
and large narrative gifts. Two new scriptures delivered to single named
men. Two founders accused of fraud in their lifetimes — Smith in court
in 1826 over treasure-seeing, Hubbard by half the world's press. Two
movements that professionalized young, fought their governments, buried
their founders as prophets, and built world institutions. The folk
verdict — *cults, both* — and the lazy ecumenical verdict — *religions,
both, who's to say* — agree in refusing to distinguish them. The
instrument distinguishes them on every test.

Where Hubbard's founding document announces a science and back-fills a
revelation, Smith's announces a revelation from the first sentence, and
its contact narrative is dense with Test 1 texture: a dated first
visitation on the night of 21–22 September 1823; a named visitor; a
specified hillside; an annual appointment kept four Septembers running
until the plates were released in 1827 — a *schedule*, the signature of
the genre the canon's own founding shares, down to the six consecutive
mornings above Clermont-Ferrand. Where Hubbard's payload is predicted
by his own bibliography, Smith's payload is the anomaly the framework's
catalogue flags as its reason for the canonical reading: a tangible,
embodied God; exalted humans who become gods in their turn; a Christ
who has visited "worlds without number"; a named star-region, Kolob,
associated with the divine administration — plurality-of-worlds
theology, load-bearing and doctrinally central, from a farmhand in the
1830s, decades before such cosmology had any respectable ambient
currency, and pointing with disconcerting directness at the cosmic
architecture the Raëlian books would describe in 1973. The corpus's
{% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %} reading of the LDS deposit is
developed in the catalogue and its companion entries; what matters here
is the *shape* of the scoring — content the founder's milieu strains to
supply, in the slot where the tradition itself carries its greatest
weight.

Test 3 splits the pair just as cleanly. Around Smith's plates stand
eleven signed witnesses in the book's own front matter — men who
handled something, whose statements were contemporaneous, several of
whom later broke with Smith in bitterness and had every worldly reason
to recant, and did not.{{ footnote(id="7") }} The witness file is
complicated — visionary sight, financial entanglement, a shared
treasure-culture — and the instrument weighs the complications; Richard
Bushman's biography, which the corpus leans on precisely because it is
not apologetics, lays them out without flinching. But *complicated
attestation* and *falsified attestation* are different findings, and
Hubbard's file contains the second: where the external record touches
Smith's story it muddies it, where it touches Hubbard's it breaks it.
On Test 5, the null hypothesis strains against the Mormon deposit — the
fraud reading must explain the witnesses' non-recantation, the
composition speed, and above all the payload's specific gravity — and
relaxes comfortably around the Scientological one. And on Test 6 the
two are not even in the same era's jurisdiction: Smith spoke before
1945, in the religious vocabulary his audience could process, exactly
as the framework expects of a genuine pre-Apocalypse
{% wiki(slug="prophet") %}prophet{% end %}; Hubbard spoke after, and
chose the sealed room.

Jan Shipps spent a career arguing that Mormonism must be studied as a
genuine *new religious tradition* — the way one studies early
Christianity's emergence from Judaism, not the way one studies a
scheme. The instrument arrives at Shipps's respect by forensic means,
and it arrives at the opposite verdict next door by the same means.
That double result — the same six tests, run in public, sorting the
American pair that folk taxonomy cannot split — is the calibration that
entitles the instrument to an opinion about harder cases.

## The custodians: legitimacy without a prophet

The user's-eye view of the catalogue suggests every entry is a founder
plus a verdict, and that would miss a category the canon itself forces
on the instrument. The founding book's fifth chapter, in the same
breath that names the confirmed traditions, awards its single highest
compliment to something that is not a prophet-founded religion at all:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=54) %}
The Kabbalah is the book closest to the truth, but almost all the
religious books allude to us more or less clearly, especially in the
countries where the creators had bases: in the cordillera of the Andes,
in the Himalayas, in Greece where Mythology also contains great
testimonies, the Buddhist religion, the Islamic, the Mormons — it would
take pages to cite all the religions and sects that testify in a more
or less obscure way to our work.
{% end %}

The {% wiki(slug="kabbalah") %}Kabbalah{% end %} has no founding
contact. It has no Sinai, no crater, no dated visitation; it is a
transmission lineage — centuries of anonymous and semi-anonymous
custodians compiling, glossing, and encrypting older material. The
canon calls it the book closest to the truth anyway. Which means the
instrument, as so far stated, is incomplete: it grades *originative*
legitimacy — was there a contact at the root? — and the canon plainly
recognizes a second kind, call it *custodial* legitimacy: the fidelity
of a tradition that originated nothing but preserved much, a tradition
whose virtue is archival.

The distinction earns its keep immediately across the catalogue's
middle tiers. Hermeticism and Gnosticism sit at "likely" with no
credible founding contact of their own — Hermes Trismegistus is
pseudepigraphy all the way down — because what the corpus reads in them
is custody: fragments of contact-derived cosmology (demiurgic creation,
archonic administration, descent-and-return anthropology) carried
inside late-antique literary vehicles. The mystery schools are graded
the same way. So, at the far end of the scale, is the negative case:
Theosophy's problem is not that Blavatsky founded nothing — custodians
found nothing by definition — it is that what she curated is
*traceable to the wrong shelves*, a documented assembly of published
Orientalia rather than a lineage carrying anything the null cannot
supply. Custodial legitimacy has its own version of every test:
specificity of what is preserved rather than of an event; payload in
the preserved content; traceability and custody of the lineage itself;
the null of syncretic assembly always waiting.

This is also the category that answers a question the movement's own
readers raise: what of the "sects" the canon mentions without
condemning — the small lineages, the schools, the orders? The canon's
sentence is genuinely permissive: *it would take pages to cite all the
religions and sects that testify in a more or less obscure way.* A sect
with no prophet can be a legitimate witness in the only sense the
framework cares about, the evidentiary sense: it can be holding a
piece of the record. The instrument's job in the middle tiers is
mostly this kind of assay work on archives; the crowning of prophets is
rare business.

## The frontier

Everything to this point has been calibration and machinery. A method
that never generates new business is an ornament, so this section runs
the instrument forward — and the claim-typing shifts accordingly. What
follows is speculative in the corpus's technical sense: interpretive
synthesis beyond what any source states, offered as a docket for
review, emphatically not as settled positions of the catalogue.

**The Mandaeans.** The instrument's most conspicuous flagged omission.
The last living Gnostic community — Aramaic-speaking, practicing
repeated baptism in living water, revering John the Baptist and
rejecting Jesus, carrying in the Ginza Rabba a cosmology of light-worlds
and descending messenger beings{{ footnote(id="10") }} — appears
nowhere in the corpus's current catalogue. Run the tests loosely:
custody is strong for so old a minority (a continuous priestly
scribal line); payload is suggestive in exactly the framework's
registers (a plurality of worlds of light, emissaries who descend,
a demiurge who engineers); external traceability reaches at least to
early Islam, which met them as the "Sabians" of the Qur'an — a
tradition the catalogue already grades highly likely under its
Harranian entry. Whether Mandaean tradition is originative (a genuine
Jordan-valley contact lineage through the Baptist movement) or
custodial (an archive of someone else's contact) is precisely the kind
of question the instrument exists to pose. The docket recommends an
entry.

**Handsome Lake.** On 15 June 1799, at Cornplanter's town on the
Allegheny, a Seneca man ruined by alcohol collapsed, was laid out for
dead, revived, and reported three messengers — "four beings" in the
mature tradition — who delivered, across this and subsequent
visitations, a structured code: temperance, land retention, household
peace, ritual reform. The Gaiwiio, the Good Message, is recited in
longhouses still. What flags the case is Test 3: a Quaker missionary
was resident in the town that June, recorded the collapse, the
revival, and the reported content in his journal within days, and the
delegation's papers preserve it — hostile-neutral eyewitness
proximity that almost nothing else in the catalogue's modern wing can
match.{{ footnote(id="9") }} Payload is thin in the framework's
registers — the message is ethical and social rather than
cosmological — and the null (a near-death crisis vision in a culture
rich in vision practice) is fully available. But the instrument was
built to be run, not to be feared: a "likely" entry for the Longhouse
religion, argued on the record, would honor the case better than the
current silence.

**Tenrikyō, and the promotion question.** Nakayama Miki's 1838
possession-contact, her forty-nine years of documented activity, and
above all the Ofudesaki — the founding deposit in the foundress's own
hand, 1,711 verses, the cleanest Test 4 profile in the entire
catalogue — already earn the tradition its highly-likely tier. The
speculative question is sharper: what *would* it take to move a
tradition upward — and the instrument's honest answer exposes the
architecture: nothing could move it to canonical, because that tier is
closed by canon naming, and no accumulation of forensic virtue
substitutes for a sentence in the founding books. The tiers measure two
different things at their top and middle, canonical membership by
stipulation, everything else by evidence — and saying so out loud is
the price of honesty the next section pays in full.

**The pre-announced successor.** One frontier case is generated by the
canon rather than by history's back-catalogue. Two traditions in the
catalogue's upper tiers maintain a *named, scheduled* future figure —
Buddhism's Maitreya, Vaishnava Hinduism's Kalki — and the canon's
second book closes by claiming the Buddhist expectation explicitly:
{% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=228) %}"your writings indicate that the new Buddha must be born in the West; recognize the foreseen signs!"{% end %}
A prophetology with forty-or-so chairs and a
{% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=257) %}"last of the line of the prophets"{% end %}
has a structural interest in expectation-figures: they are the chairs
the traditions themselves left visibly empty, the reservation cards on
the table. The instrument can say nothing about a figure who has not
arrived; what it can do — and this is the docket item — is treat a
tradition's *maintained expectation* as itself a payload feature, part
of the deposit to be graded like any other.

## The objections

Four objections have been standing in the back of the room since the
first section, and each deserves its answer in full view.

**Circularity.** *The instrument is calibrated on the canon it serves;
the six canonical traditions are canonical because the canon names
them; the payload test defines "signal" as resemblance to the canon's
own cosmology. The ruler is calibrated on the thing it measures.* All
true, and the natural-history section was written to put the truth in
proportion: Athanasius's criteria were calibrated on the books his
churches already read; the hadith graders' science presupposed the
Prophet it protected; Meier's criteria presupposed that there was a
historical Jesus to find. Every authentication instrument in the
history of religion is canon-relative, because authentication is
always authentication *against* something. Since every instrument has
a standard, what distinguishes them is whether they declare it. This
one declares it three times over — in the closed
canonical tier, in the framework prior of Test 5, and in the
claim-type stamped on this very article. The reader who swaps the
prior gets a different catalogue and is invited to compute it; the
disagreement will then be located exactly where it belongs, in one
stated premise, rather than diffused through a hundred unstated
verdicts. A method that localizes its own bias to a single declared
variable is not escaping circularity — nothing does — it is making
circularity *inspectable*, which is the most any comparative framework
of any confession has ever achieved.

**Unfalsifiability.** *A framework that can classify any tradition
somewhere has explained nothing.* But the tiers are not a rainbow of
accommodation; they are a scale with a bottom, and the bottom is
occupied. The instrument returns negative verdicts, states the
evidence, and specifies what would overturn them — an OT III
manuscript materially predating Hubbard's pulp career would blow up
the Scientology docket, and the corpus would owe the revision. It has
paid such debts against its own interest before: the provenance audit
that traced the canon's most technical passage to a likely 1957 print
source was performed by this project, on its own scripture, and
published. Frameworks allergic to falsification do not fund forensic
work on their own founding books.

**The Mormon eyebrow.** *Mormonism at canonical while Bahá'í — older
claims to the same progressive-revelation structure, vastly better
documented persecution record, no eyebrow-raising witness
entanglements — sits a tier below: is the catalogue not just
transcribing the canon's table of contents?* Yes. That is what the
closed tier *is*, and the honest architecture is to say so rather
than to retrofit forensic justifications for a membership fixed by
stipulation. The instrument's integrity lives in the open tiers, and
the Bahá'í entry is its showcase, in both directions: the tests score
the Bahá'í deposit generously — Test 1 texture in the Síyáh-Chál and
Riḍván narratives, a payload of serialized revelation and world-unity
program, custody through an unusually well-preserved authorial corpus,
traceability through the diplomatic archives of its persecutors — and
nothing but the closed door holds it out of the top tier. A critic who
finds that door arbitrary has understood the framework correctly: it
is a religion's framework, with a revealed element no instrument
generates. The framework's wager is that owning the stipulation openly
is more scientific — in the only sense "scientific" can bear in
historiography, explicit method and inspectable reasoning — than the
alternative practiced almost universally, which is to hold the same
stipulations and call them neutrality.

**Bad taste.** *Grading other people's religions is odious.* The
two-ledger rule answers half of this: no tier says anything about the
worth, wisdom, or dignity of a living tradition or its practitioners,
and the catalogue repeats the disclaimer at every opportunity because
it is load-bearing. The other half of the answer is that the
alternative to explicit grading is not abstention — it is implicit
grading. Every comparative-religion syllabus, every "world religions"
survey that includes the Bahá'í Faith and omits Scientology, every
government registry of recognized confessions, every eyeroll at one
new religion and reflexive respect for an old one, is a tier system
with the reasons hidden. The corpus's position is that the reasons
should be printed.

## The forty-or-so

Return, last, to the table. Five names, thirty-five silhouettes, and a
number that in the original language is an approximation — *la
quarantaine*, the forty-or-so.{{ footnote(id="1") }} It would have
cost the canon nothing to print a list; revelations have enumerated
far duller things. It printed a *quantity* and a *criterion* — beings
"representative of the religions created following our contacts on the
Earth" — and left the enumeration as an exercise, in a book whose
stated ambition is an audience that understands rather than believes.
This corpus's reading of that editorial choice is the article's closing
inference: a list would have made disciples; a criterion makes
investigators. The blank seating chart is the canon's own invitation to
build exactly the instrument this Explainer has now assembled in
public — six tests, five tiers, two ledgers, one declared prior, one
watermark held to the light, a docket of open cases, and a standing
obligation to revise.

The list will never be finished. Traditions died scriptless in the
Andes and the steppe whose chairs no method can identify; the
instrument's honest output for them is silence, not closure. What can
be finished — what is, with this article, substantially on the record —
is the method: every verdict argued from stated tests, every test
traceable to a discipline with a literature, every premise labeled,
every negative finding falsifiable, every audit run even when it cuts
the auditor. Fifty years of scholarship on new religious movements has
asked, with genuine puzzlement, what kind of religion insists it is
also a science. Here is one operational answer: the kind that, asked
*which religions are real*, declines to answer with either a dogma or a
shrug — and publishes its instrument instead.
