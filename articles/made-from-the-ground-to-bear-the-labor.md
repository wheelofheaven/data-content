Sometime before 1800 BCE, a Babylonian scribe wrote down the reason the
world contains people. It was not a happy reason. The lesser gods, the
Igigi, had been set to dig the irrigation canals of Mesopotamia — "for
2,500 years and more," the poem says — until they broke their tools,
burned their baskets, and marched on the house of the chief god at night.
The strike was settled not by breaking the rebels but by replacing them.
A new worker would be built to carry the baskets, and the gods would
never dig again. That worker was us.

The text is *Atraḫasīs*, and it is not alone. Three other creation
accounts older than most of the Bible — two Sumerian, one the opening of
Genesis — tell a version of the same story, and they agree on two
details that ought to be arbitrary and are not. Humanity is made from the
ground: clay, dust, the silt of the riverbank. And humanity is made *for*
something: to take over labor the gods no longer wish to do. Across three
languages and more than a thousand years, the material is terrestrial and
the purpose is work.

This Explainer reads those four witnesses at the level of the line, using
the project's own translations of the cuneiform. It then sets them
against the wider cross-cultural record, where the first half of the
motif — man from clay — turns out to be nearly universal while the second
half is not. The gap between the two is the whole argument. Mainstream
scholarship has a strong account of how the texts are related, and that
account is given its due. The {% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Wheel of
Heaven{% end %} reading is then offered as one further layer, marked as
interpretation: that the recurrence is compressed memory of a creation
performed from local materials, for a function, by the makers the
project calls the {% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %} — a small and
advanced civilization who had labor of their own.

## Four witnesses, read closely

### The hoe that made mankind

The first witness is the one the project's library translated most
recently, and the oldest in spirit: the Sumerian **Song of the Hoe**, a
hymn built on a pun that runs the length of the poem. Enlil, "who brings
forth what is fitting," resolves to make the seed of the land come up
from the ground. He separates heaven from earth, fixes their bond at a
place named *Uzu-e-a* — "Where Flesh Came Forth" — and drives in the hoe.
What comes up is not a crop but a people. Humanity does not arrive; it
germinates:

{% library(book="song-of-the-hoe-woh", chapter=1, verse=20) %}
Before Enlil, his Land began to break up through the soil.
{% end %}

A few lines on, the hymn states the equation without ornament — "the hoe
is the brick-mould; the hoe is what made mankind exist"
({% libref(book="song-of-the-hoe-woh", chapter=1, verse=96) %}line 96{% end %}).
Two images of manufacture are folded together in one tool. The hoe breaks
the soil, and humanity sprouts from it like barley; the hoe is also the
mould that shapes a mud brick, and humanity is pressed out of it like a
casting. Either way the substance is the same, and it is the ground. The
place-name says as much on its own: flesh came forth *here*, from *this
dirt*. The Sumerian does not reach for a heavenly substance or a divine
emanation. It reaches for the field.

### A substitute, so the gods may rest

The second witness, also Sumerian, is blunter about the reason. In
**Enki and Ninmah**, the lesser gods are breaking their backs dredging
the watercourses; they weep and blame Enki, the wise god asleep in the
freshwater deep. His mother, the primordial Nammu, carries the complaint
to him, and the instruction he gives is a job order:

{% library(book="enki-and-ninmah-woh", chapter=1, verse=23) %}
Fashion a substitute for the gods, so that they may be released from
their toil!
{% end %}

The word is *substitute*. Humanity is defined, in its founding text, as a
labor-saving replacement — the thing you build so that you need not do
the work yourself. Ninmah pinches the clay from above the *abzu*, the
subterranean freshwater, and shapes the first humans on Enki's design.

The poem then does something a naive creation story would not. It stages
a drinking contest. Enki and Ninmah, deep in beer, wager over the
creature they have made: Ninmah forms humans with deliberate defects —
the barren woman, the one who cannot hold back urine, the blind, the
lame — and dares Enki to find a place in society for each. He does. The
episode is usually read for its aetiology of disability, and it is that.
But notice the structure underneath: the makers iterate. They produce
variant forms, test them against a function, and keep the ones that work.
The first humans of *Enki and Ninmah* are not a single perfect act of
creation. They are a run of prototypes, some sound and some flawed,
argued over by their designers — a structure that surfaces again, later
in this article, on the other side of the world.

### God and man, mixed together in the clay

The third witness is the fullest statement the ancient world produced,
and until recently this project could only summarize it from the standard
editions. The Wheel of Heaven library now hosts a translation of the Old
Babylonian **Atraḫasīs**, so the lines can be read directly. They repay
it.

After the Igigi rebellion, Enki proposes the solution in open council.
The language is administrative — a proposal, a role, a term of
employment:

{% library(book="atrahasis-woh", chapter=1, verse=192) %}
Let the womb-goddess create a lullû, and let him be the bearer of the
yoke. Let him carry the yoke, the task imposed by Enlil; let man bear the
labor of the gods.
{% end %}

*Lullû* is the primitive worker, the human-to-be. The purpose is stated
in the same breath as the design: this creature exists to bear a yoke
that presently sits on divine shoulders. But *Atraḫasīs* is not content
to make man out of dirt alone. The birth-goddess Nintu will not proceed
without the right material, and the material Enki specifies is not only
clay. A god must die into it:

{% library(book="atrahasis-woh", chapter=1, verse=210) %}
Let one god be slaughtered, that the gods may be cleansed by his
slaughter. With the flesh of the god and his blood let Nintu mix the
clay, so that god and man may be mixed together in the clay.
{% end %}

The god they choose is named Wê-ila, and the poem is precise about what
he contributes. He is the god "who had *ṭēmu*" — intelligence, reason,
the capacity for planning. He is slaughtered; from his flesh and blood
Nintu mixes the clay; and from the flesh of the god arises an *eṭemmu*, a
ghost or animating spirit that "makes the living know its sign, so that
he not be forgotten." The human being that results is a compound of three
things held in exact balance: terrestrial clay for the body, the flesh
and blood of a god for the substance, and the divine intelligence for the
spark. Then the labor is loaded on, in the goddess's own words of
completion:

{% library(book="atrahasis-woh", chapter=1, verse=239) %}
I have removed your heavy labor, I have imposed your labor-basket upon
man.
{% end %}

What follows is the part that reads least like myth and most like a
protocol. Nintu does not breathe a race into being with a word. She works
in units. She nips off fourteen pieces of clay, sets seven on the right
and seven on the left, places a brick between them, and the womb-goddesses
"seven and seven they fashioned: seven male, seven female." The first
humans come off the line two by two, in a designed batch, from measured
portions of a prepared mixture. The account is not embarrassed by the
industrial character of it. It is proud of it.

Set the four theological layers of *Atraḫasīs* side by side and the
Mesopotamian answer to "why is there humanity" is complete: a terrestrial
body, built from the makers' own biological material, animated by their
intelligence, batch-produced to a specification, for the express purpose
of taking over their labor. Every element the later witnesses carry
singly, *Atraḫasīs* carries at once.

### The human from the humus

The fourth witness is the one most readers know, and it has been read for
so long as theology that its mechanics go unnoticed. **Genesis 2** opens
on a world that is finished but idle — "no bush of the field was yet on
the land… and there was no human to work the ground"
({% libref(book="genesis-woh", chapter=2, verse=5) %}Genesis 2:5{% end %}).
The problem is stated before the solution, and the problem is labor
undone. Then:

{% library(book="genesis-woh", chapter=2, verse=7) %}
YHWH-Elohim formed the human, dust from the ground, and breathed into his
nostrils the breath of life; and the human became a living being.
{% end %}

The Hebrew is doing three things the English can only gesture at. The
verb *yāṣar*, "formed," is the potter's word — the same verb used of a
craftsman at his wheel, which is why the older translations reach for
"fashioned." The human is *ʿāfār min hā-ʾădāmāh*, "dust from the ground,"
and the noun for the human, *ʾādām*, is a pun on *ʾădāmāh*, the soil
itself: the earth-creature from the earth, a wordplay the Hebrew makes
unavoidable and no translation fully saves. And the creation is a
two-step — first the body of dust is shaped, then the breath of life is
added to a form already complete. That is the same two-step as
*Atraḫasīs*: assemble the terrestrial body, then introduce the animating
principle.

Genesis will spend the next several thousand years elevating what it
begins here — the labor becomes a garden entrusted to a keeper, "to work
it and to guard it"
({% libref(book="genesis-woh", chapter=2, verse=15) %}Genesis 2:15{% end %}),
and the keeper becomes a covenant partner. That elevation is real and it
matters, and a later section takes it seriously. But the elevation is
built on the older skeleton, not in place of it. Strip the theology back
to the mechanism and Genesis 2 says what the Sumerian and the Babylonian
say: a maker, a material taken from the ground, a deliberate act of
shaping, an animating breath, and a task.

## Beyond Mesopotamia: the wider clay

If the story stopped at four Near Eastern witnesses, the simplest
explanation would be the right one — these texts are relatives, and
relatives share stories. But the first half of the motif, the making of
humans from earth, does not stay in the Near East. It is one of the most
widely attested ideas in the world's mythologies. The folklorist Stith
Thompson gave it a call number, **A1241, "Man made from clay,"** and the
project's own [motif catalogue](/wiki/list-of-mythemes-and-mythological-motifs/)
files a single row under it that already names half the planet. The
[companion Explainer on Thompson's index](/articles/the-filing-cabinet-of-the-gods/)
argues the general case; here is the specific one.

In **Egypt**, the ram-headed god Khnum sits at a potter's wheel and turns
human beings out of Nile clay — and not only the body. He shapes the *ka*,
the life-force or double, on the same wheel beside it, so that what leaves
Khnum's workshop is a manufactured being complete with its animating
principle. He is styled the divine potter, the modeller of gods and
people and even the fetus in the womb. The fullest hymns to Khnum as
mankind's potter are late — the Greco-Roman temple texts of Esna — though
the potter's-wheel iconography runs back to the New Kingdom divine-birth
scenes. What Egypt does *not* attach to the image is a labor motive:
Khnum makes life as a craftsman, not to relieve the gods of toil. The
material half of the motif is vivid; the purpose half is absent. That
distinction will matter shortly.

In **Greece**, the picture has to be drawn carefully, because the popular
version is later than people assume. The archaic poet Hesiod knows
Prometheus as a fire-thief and knows the first *woman*, Pandora, as a
figure moulded from earth and water by Hephaestus on Zeus's order — but
Hesiod does not make Prometheus the sculptor of humankind. The image of
Prometheus moulding the whole race from clay is Hellenistic and Roman:
Ovid's *Metamorphoses* leaves the human's maker pointedly open; the
handbook of Pseudo-Apollodorus states flatly that "Prometheus moulded men
out of water and earth"; and the traveller Pausanias reports that at
Panopeus in Phocis two cart-sized stones "have the colour of clay… and
smell very like the skin of a man," which the locals said were the clay
left over when Prometheus fashioned the human race. It is a relic-cult of
the motif — the leftover material from the making of man, shown to
tourists. Here too the labor-and-purpose clause is missing. The Greek
clay is craftsmanship, not conscription.

In **China**, the goddess Nüwa moulds the first people by hand from yellow
earth. The earliest text to give the vivid detail, the Eastern Han
*Fengsu Tongyi*, adds a second stage that no other tradition matches for
candor about class: tiring of shaping each figure by hand, Nüwa dips a
cord into the mud and drags it, and the drops that fall become people —
so that those she moulded by hand from yellow clay became the noble and
the rich, and those flung from the cord became the poor and the common.
The manufacturing method *is* the social hierarchy. It is the purest
surviving statement of a made-for-station reading: humanity sorted at the
moment of creation by how carefully each unit was produced.

And in the **Popol Vuh** of the K'iche' Maya, the makers try humanity
three times, and the trial is explicitly a search for a working design.
Their first people are of mud and earth, and they fail — they cannot hold
their shape, they dissolve in water, they cannot speak or think. Their
second, of wood, populate the earth but have "nothing in their hearts,"
no memory of their makers, and are wiped out in a black rain. Only the
third, the people ground from yellow and white **maize**, succeed — and
they succeed against a stated purpose: the makers wanted beings who would
speak their names, keep their days, and nourish them. The Popol Vuh is not
a clay-creation that worked; it is a clay-creation that was *rejected* in
favor of a better material after field testing. But the structure it
preserves is the one *Enki and Ninmah* half-showed through its drinking
contest: iteration toward a specification, failed prototypes discarded,
a purpose the product is measured against. Thompson files this pattern
one shelf over, at **A1280, "Multiple creations of humanity"** — and the
project's catalogue puts the Popol Vuh in exactly that row.

The examples could be multiplied — the Yoruba god Obatala moulding bodies
from earth for Olodumare to breathe into, with the imperfect ones traced
to Obatala's drinking; the Vedic *Puruṣa-sūkta*, which is not clay at all
but the dismemberment of a cosmic being into the human classes, and which
therefore preserves the *station* half of the motif without the *earth*
half. But the cases already assembled make the necessary point, and it is
a dividing one.

## The two halves, and where they travel

Lay the whole record out and it separates cleanly into two claims that
the Mesopotamian and Hebrew texts hold together and the rest of the world
mostly holds apart.

**The material is terrestrial, and this half is nearly universal.** Clay,
dust, mud, yellow earth, the silt of the riverbank — from Elephantine to
the Yellow River to the Guatemalan highlands, when a tradition says where
humanity came from, it points at the ground under its feet. This half of
the motif is cheap, in the sense that it is easy to arrive at
independently: a species that makes pots and bricks from wet earth, and
that returns to the earth when it dies, has every reason to imagine
itself shaped from the same stuff. A skeptic can grant the universality
of *man from clay* and explain it entirely without shared history. The
material half proves little on its own.

**The purpose is labor, and this half does not travel nearly so well.**
The specific clause — humanity was made *in order to* take over work the
gods were tired of doing — is concentrated, in its full and explicit
form, in Mesopotamia and its Hebrew neighbor. Khnum's Egypt has the clay
and not the clause. Prometheus's Greece has the clay and not the clause.
Nüwa's China has the clay and a *class* dimension but not a labor
mandate. The Popol Vuh has the purpose and the prototypes but reaches its
purpose through maize, not clay. Only in the Mesopotamian family, and in
the Genesis account that opens on a world with "no human to work the
ground," do both halves lock together into a single sentence: made from
the earth, *and therefore, and in order to*, labor.

That asymmetry is the fact worth an explanation. It is easy to see why
*man from clay* recurs everywhere. It is much harder to see why the
particular, unflattering pairing — *manufactured from local dirt to serve
as someone else's workforce* — should be the stable core of the one
tradition that stands at the head of Western scripture.

## Why is the pairing so stable?

The mainstream answer, and a strong one as far as it reaches, is
**literary descent**. The Mesopotamian accounts are demonstrably related:
*Enki and Ninmah*, *Atraḫasīs*, and the human-creation sections of the
later *Enūma Eliš* share vocabulary, scenes, and the clay-and-blood
mechanism, and W. G. Lambert's *Babylonian Creation Myths* traces the
lineage tablet by tablet. Genesis, in turn, was composed in a world
saturated with this material — the same world that gave the Hebrew Bible
a flood narrative sitting beside the Mesopotamian deluge closely enough
that the dependence is rarely doubted. Tikva Frymer-Kensky's study of
*Atraḫasīs* and Genesis 1–9 shows the biblical primeval history working
through the very sequence — creation, human proliferation, divine
displeasure, flood — that the Babylonian epic lays down first. On this
reading the *ʾādām*/*ʾădāmāh* pun is a Hebrew localization: a native
re-tooling of an imported pattern, clay rendered into the local idiom.

Diffusion explains the *transmission* well. What it explains less is the
*selection*. Of all the elements a creation story could conserve as it
passes down a line of scribes and across into a neighboring language, why
this one? Cultures revise inherited myths constantly, and the revision
usually runs toward dignity. The Mesopotamian scribes had every incentive
to make humanity something nobler than a "substitute" built to empty the
canal-baskets; the Hebrew authors had a theology capacious enough to
begin the human story anywhere it liked. Both kept the manufacture, and
both kept the labor. Genesis softens the tone — the basket becomes a
garden, the yoke becomes a vocation — but it does not drop the structure:
the human is still formed, still from the ground, still for a task, still
in two steps. Bernard Batto's account of biblical *mythopoeic
speculation* is useful here precisely because it shows the biblical
writers as conscious craftsmen extending old myths for new ends — which
makes their retention of the made-for-labor core a choice, not an
accident of inheritance. A motif that survives every incentive to
discard it is at least worth asking whether it is conserving *information*
and not only *text*.

Two further explanations deserve their place, and both are partly right.
Convergent invention accounts for the bare material half — the clay is
the obvious material, as argued above, and needs no shared source. And
the cognitive study of religion would add that a *manufactured* human,
made by an agent for a reason, is a more memorable and more transmissible
idea than an abstract or self-existent one, so folklore drifts toward it.
Grant both. They explain why so many traditions say *man from clay*. They
do not obviously explain why one family of traditions, alone, welds that
clay to a specific and undignified purpose and keeps the weld intact
through a thousand years and a change of religion. The bare motif is
convergent. The pairing looks conserved.

## Reading it through the frame

Everything to this point has been comparison, and stands on its own. What
follows is interpretation — a construal offered under the project's
`inferred` label, consistent with the texts but not forced by them, and
to be weighed as such.

The Wheel of Heaven canon offers a reading on which the stability is not a
puzzle at all. On the {% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %} hypothesis,
humanity was in fact made — deliberately, from the materials of this
planet, by a small and advanced civilization of makers who had their own
reasons. The Raëlian source states it without metaphor: the Elohim
synthesized human beings in laboratories, from terrestrial matter, in
their own biological image, using the techniques the project files under
{% wiki(slug="genetic-engineering") %}genetic engineering{% end %} and
{% wiki(slug="life-engineering") %}life engineering{% end %}. On that
reading the four texts are not independent inventions and not only a chain
of copied tales. They are compressed, culturally refracted memory of the
same event, which is exactly why the load-bearing details — a terrestrial
material, a deliberate act, an animating step, a purpose — survive while
the names of the gods and the shape of the story change from telling to
telling.

Read this way, the specific oddities of *Atraḫasīs* stop being poetic
excess and start being oddly exact. "God and man mixed together in the
clay" is what a genetic-engineering account would say in the only
vocabulary a Bronze-Age scribe possessed: the makers' own biological
material combined with terrestrial substrate. The slaughtered god's
*ṭēmu*, the intelligence folded into the mixture, is the design step that
turns an animal body into a reasoning one — what the project's reading of
the human-synthesis programme calls the consciousness-integration phase.
The two-step of Genesis, body of dust then breath of life, is the same
sequence seen from Israel. And the labor-clause that every witness keeps
is simply the answer to the question the myths are actually asking, which
is not "what are we made of" but "what are we *for*."

There is also a contemporary detail that is hard to set aside. For the
whole history of reading these texts, the one element that could be
dismissed as obviously mythological was the workshop itself — the idea
that somebody manufactured life from earth in a laboratory was exactly
the sort of thing pre-scientific people would imagine. That dismissal is
no longer available on the old terms. In 2010 a team at the J. Craig
Venter Institute booted a living, self-replicating bacterial cell from a
genome assembled from off-the-shelf chemicals; the discipline now reads,
writes, and builds genomes as ordinary work. Within a single human
lifetime we have acquired, in prototype, the precise capability the oldest
texts assign to the makers. This does not show that the texts are memory.
It removes the easiest reason for assuming they cannot be.

## Counterarguments

**"It is just aetiology."** The strongest objection is that the motif is
social projection rather than memory. Agrarian societies ran on brutal
collective labor — the corvée, the canal-gangs, the brick-quotas — and a
story that says *the gods built you to do exactly this* is the most
natural ideological backfill imaginable. There is real force here, and it
is partly right: these texts plainly do sanctify the labor order, and the
Nüwa cord-and-clay episode all but confesses it. But aetiology accounts
for the *purpose* half and not the *material* half. "We toil because the
gods decreed it" needs no clay, no *Uzu-e-a*, no two-step of body then
breath, no god slaughtered into the mixture. The persistence of the
terrestrial-fabrication detail, in the same internal structure across
traditions that had every freedom to drop it, is the part projection does
not predict.

**"This is Sitchin in a coat."** Ancient-astronaut readings of
Mesopotamian creation carry a deservedly bad name. Zecharia Sitchin's
translations are rejected across Assyriology; Kenneth Feder's standard
survey of archaeological pseudoscience treats the whole genre as a
cautionary tale, and on the philology he is right. The reading offered
here is deliberately not that. It rests on no contested rendering of a
single sign and no re-translated tablet. It rests on the consensus
translations — the ones in the project's own library, produced through a
documented pipeline and checked against named scholarship — and offers an
*interpretation* laid on top of accepted text. A reader can reject the
interpretation and lose nothing of the translation. That is the test the
discredited versions fail.

**"The clay is just the obvious material."** This is the sharpest
objection, and the cross-cultural section was written partly to concede
it. Yes: earth is the cheap, convergent, everywhere-available answer to
"what were we shaped from," and the near-universality of *man from clay*
is no evidence of anything by itself. But the framework reading does not
rest on the bare clay. It rests on the *pairing* — clay welded to a
labor-and-purpose clause — and on the two-step structure, body then
animating spark, that the bare motif does not require and the Mesopotamian
and Hebrew texts nonetheless share. "Obvious material" explains the dirt.
It does not explain why one tradition alone treats the dirt as the raw
input to a manufacturing process with a stated output.

**"Genesis is theology, not engineering."** Genesis 2 does something the
Mesopotamian texts do not: it turns the labor into vocation. The human is
not a slave-substitute but a gardener entrusted with the place, and the
whole later arc of the Hebrew Bible builds dignity and covenant on that
foundation. This is a genuine, irreducible difference, and the project's
editorial standards forbid flattening it into sameness. But the reframing
is built *on* the older skeleton, not instead of it. The human is still
formed, still from the ground, still by a potter's verb, still for a task.
What Genesis changes is the human's *relationship* to the work, from
conscription to stewardship. It does not change the account of what the
human is or how it was made.

## Conclusion

The motif is real, and it is stable in its Mesopotamian and Hebrew home:
made from the ground, made to bear the labor, conserved across three
languages and a millennium. Set against the wider record, the two halves
of it come apart in an instructive way. The clay is everywhere, and
proves little; the pairing of clay with a manufactured purpose is
concentrated where the texts are demonstrably related, and survives every
pressure to soften it. Mainstream scholarship explains how the text
travelled. It explains less easily why this particular, unflattering
content was the part that refused to drift.

The Wheel of Heaven reads it as memory — refracted through four cultures
and rendered in each one's vocabulary, but memory — of a deliberate
creation from terrestrial materials, for a purpose, by makers with work
of their own. That reading is offered as interpretation and not as proof,
and it is falsifiable in principle. If the earliest and most independent
witnesses turned out not to share the material-and-purpose pairing — if
the clay were a late harmonization layered on by scribes who already knew
the other versions — the memory reading would weaken to ordinary
diffusion. If the science of human origins continues to show a fully
gradual emergence with no seam where a design step would sit, the literal
half loses its contemporary mirror and the motif reverts to a very good
story about a very hard life. Those are the terms on which a careful
reader should give it up. Until then, the oldest texts keep saying the
same thing in the same order — a body from the ground, a spark from
above, a task to perform — and the youngest laboratories are learning the
vocabulary in which to hear it as something other than a myth.
