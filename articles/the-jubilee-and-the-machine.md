In the summer of 1932, at the bottom of the Great Depression, the town
of Wörgl in the Austrian Tyrol began printing money designed to rot. A
third of the town was out of work. The mayor, Michael Unterguggenberger,
a former railway machinist, issued labor certificates that lost one
percent of their face value every month unless the holder bought a stamp
and glued it on — currency with a built-in cost of hoarding, so that the
only sensible thing to do with it was spend it. The certificates moved
through Wörgl many times faster than the schilling moved through
Austria. The town repaved its streets, rebuilt its water system, put its
unemployed to work on a bridge, and paid its taxes to itself in advance,
while unemployment in the surrounding country went on climbing. Visitors
came from across Europe and America to look at the miracle{{ footnote(id="1") }};
some two hundred Austrian mayors prepared to copy it. Then the Austrian
National Bank asserted its monopoly over the issue of money, the courts
agreed, and in September 1933 the experiment was closed by law — a
working answer shut down, as its partisans never tired of repeating, at
the height of the crisis it was answering.

The idea Wörgl had briefly incarnated belonged to a man who had died two
years earlier, in a garden colony outside Berlin named Eden: Silvio
Gesell, a self-taught German merchant who had made his money importing
surgical instruments into Buenos Aires and lost his orthodoxy watching
Argentina's currency destroy and remake fortunes around him
{{ footnote(id="2") }}. Three years after Wörgl was shut, the most famous
economist alive would devote a section of the most influential economics
book of the century to him — "the strange, unduly neglected prophet
Silvio Gesell," Keynes wrote, "whose work contains flashes of deep
insight," before delivering a sentence his profession has still not
digested: "I believe that the future will learn more from the spirit of
Gesell than from that of Marx."

Gesell is one entry in a file this article proposes to read whole. The
file's members rarely knew one another. It contains a Viennese financial
journalist who set a utopia in the Kenyan highlands, an American printer
whose land tax became the best-selling economics book of the nineteenth
century, a school of Anglophone philosophers with a taste for
seventeenth-century natural law, a self-taught industrial designer in
the Florida scrub, the programmers who gave away the most valuable
software on Earth, and a venture capitalist with a manifesto. What they
share is a conviction: that scarcity, past a certain technological
threshold, is an artifact of institutional design; that the
socio-economic order is a made thing which can be remade; and that the
correct objective function for the remaking is human flourishing — the
blossoming of the species' potential — rather than accumulation. And the
file has a natural comparison point, because the corpus this project
reads contains, delivered across six mornings in December 1973 and
extended in 1975, a compact and strangely complete member of the family:
the doctrine the {% wiki(slug="new-commandments") %}New
Commandments{% end %} name **Humanitarianism**, and its longer horizon,
{% wiki(slug="paradism") %}Paradism{% end %}. The canon's own claims are
labeled `framework` throughout this project — explicit in the source,
unendorsed by mainstream scholarship. The comparative reading that
follows is labeled what it is: `inferred`.

## The message in the crater

The scene, within the frame, is the crater of an extinct volcano near
Clermont-Ferrand, where a twenty-seven-year-old motor-sport journalist
named Claude Vorilhon — {% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %}, from the
second morning on — reported six days of conversation with a visitor he
would come to call {% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Yahweh{% end %}. The sixth
chapter of the book he published the following year is, whatever else it
is, a political economy. It opens with
{% wiki(slug="geniocracy") %}geniocracy{% end %} — government screened
for intelligence — and proceeds immediately to property:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=7) %}
Your world is paralyzed by profit, and communism does not manage to give
men a carrot sufficient for them to want to make efforts and to
progress. You are born equal; that too is in the biblical writings. The
power must make you be born approximately equal in fortune. … To avoid
that, property must be abolished without, however, instituting
communism. This world is not yours; that too is written in the Bible.
You are only its tenants. Thus, all goods must be leased for forty-nine
years. That abolishes the injustice of inheritances. Your inheritance,
the inheritance of your children, is the whole world if you know how to
organize yourselves to make it pleasant. This political orientation of
humanity is not communism; it concerns itself with the future of
humanity; call it Humanitarianism if you want to give it a name.
{% end %}

The mechanics are worked through with the patience of a mortgage
adviser. A man finishes his studies at twenty-one, leases a house for
forty-nine years, and owns his tenure of it free and clear at seventy;
at his death the house reverts to the community, except the family home,
which his children may occupy in perpetuity. Merit is pointedly
preserved — the text runs a two-sons example, one industrious and one
idle, and lets the industrious one grow rich:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=9) %}
The hardworking one will very quickly earn more money than the lazy one.
He will then be able to lease a house costing twice as much in place of
the first. … But at his death everything will revert to the community,
except the family house, which will revert to his children. In a way, a
man can make a fortune for himself, according to his merit, but not for
his children. To each his merit.
{% end %}

Everything of value — land, enterprises, stocks, gold, buildings —
belongs to the community and is leased back, for a maximum of
forty-nine years, to those who have earned the means
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=10) %}TBWTT 6:10{% end %}).
And then the text does something none of its 1970s contactee peers
thought to do: it cites its sources. The forty-nine-year term, it says,
"is already prescribed in the Bible," and it quotes Leviticus 25 at
length — the sabbath of years counted seven times seven, and the
land-tenure principle beneath it: *"the land shall not be sold in
perpetuity, for the land is mine, while you are guests and residents
with me"*
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=13) %}TBWTT 6:11–13{% end %}).
Humanitarianism presents itself as recovery — the Jubilee re-issued at
planetary scale. Its stated
prerequisites are the rest of the sixth chapter: geniuses in power, who
"will understand the usefulness of these reforms," and a single world
government with a new world currency and a common second language
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=16) %}TBWTT 6:14–16{% end %}).

The 1975 message adds the second axis. Where the first book reformed
tenure, the second attacks work itself:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=37) %}
Science and technique can totally liberate men not only from the worry
of hunger in the world, but also permit them to live without obligation
to work, the machines being well able to charge themselves with the
daily tasks all alone thanks to automation. … These fantastic machines
which do the work of five hundred persons must permit these five hundred
persons to live instead of serving to fatten a single person: the boss.
{% end %}

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=38) %}
No man must be at the service of another nor work for another against a
salary. The machines can well do the chores and charge themselves with
all the work, permitting man to consecrate himself to the only thing for
which he is made: to think, to create, to blossom. … Work has nothing
sacred when it is motivated by nothing other than the necessity of
earning the wherewithal to live painfully; it is even terribly debasing
to sell oneself, thus to sell one's life, in order to be able to eat by
effecting work which simple machines can do.
{% end %}

The Keys spell out the sequence. Everyone has the right to live
decently, working or idle
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=98) %}ETTMTTP 3:98{% end %});
those who contribute more may have more, as "a provisional rule
obviously, while waiting for man to be capable of having all the chores
accomplished by robots in order to devote himself solely to his
blossoming, after having totally suppressed money"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=96) %}ETTMTTP 3:96{% end %});
in the interim, a single world currency replaces the national ones
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=162) %}ETTMTTP 3:162{% end %});
and the engineering priority is stated as a commandment: "Put everything
to work to create the biological robot that will free you from the lowly
chores and will allow you to blossom"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=100) %}ETTMTTP 3:100{% end %}).
The third book closes the loop, listing among the mission's own
objectives a research center whose work on "the creation of biological
robots will allow the elimination of work and consequently money"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=23) %}LWTE 1:23{% end %}).

And the canon claims an exemplar. Asked about life on the creators' own
world, Yahweh describes a civilization that has finished the program:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=45) %}
All the people «work», but because they feel like it, given that there
is no money among us. Thus, those who «work» do things that are always
very well done, since they are done by vocation. Only the eternals have
very precise missions, such as for example the surveillance of the
electronic brains and of the computers dealing with the vital problems,
like energy, food, organization, etc.
{% end %}

Ninety thousand biological robots serve nine thousand residents; cities
are single kilometer-scale structures designed by one architect apiece,
"kinds of cubes so as not to «eat» the countryside"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=47) %}TBWTT 7:39–47{% end %});
a {% wiki(slug="council-of-eternals") %}council of seven hundred{% end %}
administers the automated substrate for seven billion people who are
under no obligation to do anything at all. Within the frame, this is
what the doctrine is *for*: the terrestrial program is a copy of a
working original. Even the adversary in the canon's dramatis personae
prices the question in these terms —
{% wiki(slug="satan") %}Satan{% end %}, the skeptic of the
{% wiki(slug="council-of-eternals") %}Council{% end %}, "will not be
convinced that something good can be expected of men until they are
united, and money and all weapons are destroyed"
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=72) %}LWTE 2:72{% end %}).
In the canon's accounting, the abolition of money sits beside
disarmament as one of the two proofs the species owes.

So the doctrine has two axes. The first is the **Jubilee axis**: tenure
in place of ownership, equality at birth, merit rewarded within a life,
nothing compounding across generations. The second is the **machine
axis**: automation absorbing labor, work ceasing to be the price of
existence, money expiring when the scarcity it rations does. The rest of
this article walks the human genealogy of each — the thinkers who, with
no access to the frame and mostly no knowledge of each other, kept
arriving at the same two reforms.

## The land that cannot be sold

The Jubilee axis is the older one, and its taproot is the text the canon
itself quotes. Leviticus 25 orders the years counted in sabbaths — seven
times seven — and in the year of release the land returns: sales of land
are, in effect, leases priced by the number of harvests remaining, "for
the land is mine; for ye are strangers and sojourners with me." Whether
the instruction was ever executed is disputed{{ footnote(id="4") }};
what is not disputed is what it *is* — a periodic reset of accumulated
holdings, built on the premise that the earth itself is not a
commodity but a trust.

The modern rediscovery began with the physiocrats of eighteenth-century
France, who argued that all net wealth traces back to land and proposed
the *impôt unique*, a single tax on rent{{ footnote(id="3") }}. Their
heir, though he found the argument on his own, was Henry George, a
San Francisco printer and journalist who watched California's land
values explode ahead of its wages and asked, in *Progress and Poverty*
(1879), why want increases with wealth. His answer — that rent, the
unearned increment of location, absorbs the gains of progress — came
with a remedy of surgical narrowness: tax land values to their full
rental worth, abolish every other tax, and leave improvements, wages,
and enterprise untouched. Merit keeps everything merit makes; the
community takes only what no one made. The book's reach is hard to
overstate; Gesell, no disciple, recorded it with a competitor's envy:
"Georges Buch erlebte die größte Auflage, die ein Buch je erlebt hat" —
George's book achieved the largest circulation any book had ever
achieved. The scholarly anthology this article leans on renders the
verdict soberly: George "undoubtedly became the most popular exponent of
'left-libertarianism' both in his time and to later generations."

Set George's cut beside the canon's and the geometry is the same with a
different radius. Both leave the fruits of work with the worker; both
return the unearned base to the community. George draws the line at
land. Humanitarianism draws it around everything — "stocks, gold,
enterprises, ready money, buildings, everything that can have value"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=10) %}TBWTT 6:10{% end %})
— and enforces it with a clock rather than a tax: the forty-nine-year
reversion. Where George socializes a flow, the canon sunsets the stock.

## A garden in the highlands

Eleven years after *Progress and Poverty*, a Viennese economist and
financial journalist named Theodor Hertzka published a novel that
functioned as a prospectus. *Freiland: Ein sociales Zukunftsbild*
(1890) opens with an association founded at a public meeting in The
Hague to make "a practical attempt at the solution of the social
problem" — a commonwealth promising "perfect freedom and economic
justice at once," guaranteeing "every worker the whole and undiminished
enjoyment of the fruits of his own labour" (our translation, here and
below). The settlers sail for the highlands of equatorial East Africa,
between Mount Kenya and Kilimanjaro, where the land is *herrenlos* —
ownerless — and there they build a society on four rules: no property in
land, "individual or collective," the soil "free like the air";
interest-free working capital advanced to anyone from a central bank;
producer associations that anyone may join or leave at will; and a
maintenance right for women, children, the old, and the incapacitated,
justified as every person's share in the inheritance of "the mental
labor of countless preceding generations." Rent, interest, and profit
are never banned. They simply die of exposure — "we forbid interest as
little as we forbid the employer's profit or ground rent," the bank's
director explains to a skeptical English visitor; "these three branches
of income do not exist here for the simple reason that no one is ever in
the position of having to pay them." Hertzka's speaker at the world
congress states the objective in a line that could head this whole
file: earlier attempts failed "because they wanted to establish the
equality of poverty; ours will succeed because it means the equality of
wealth. … Art and science presuppose abundance and leisure."

Two details of the novel matter to this project beyond its economics.
The first is a name. When the expedition reaches its valley below the
Kenya glaciers — "the land, as far as the eye could reach, was like a
garden, but man had not yet taken possession of this paradise" — a
settler declares the archangel's flaming sword dispersed in the
waterfall's mist and christens the place **Eden**. The second is what
the book set in motion. Freiland societies sprang up across Europe
within months; the one expedition they mounted was turned back on the
African coast in 1894 and the movement dissolved{{ footnote(id="5") }}.
But among the book's close readers was another Viennese journalist.
Theodor Herzl opens *Der Judenstaat* (1896) by measuring his own plan
against Hertzka's — *Freiland*, he wrote, is an ingenious but
"complicated piece of machinery" with many cogs and no proof it could be
set in motion — and the settlement movement Herzl launched produced, at
Degania on the Sea of Galilee in 1909, the thing Hertzka had only
imagined: a real commonwealth of common land, common production, and no
wages. The kibbutz became the longest-running voluntary
common-ownership experiment in the modern record — Martin Buber's
"experiment that did not fail" — before the debt crisis of the 1980s
pushed most of the movement into privatization{{ footnote(id="6") }}.
A century of operation, and a re-admission of the market at the end of
it: the kibbutz file contains, in miniature, both the promise the
Jubilee axis makes and the incentive problem the canon's merit-leases
are engineered to answer
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=95) %}ETTMTTP 3:95{% end %}:
"There must be equality of men among themselves at the start, at birth,
but not afterward").

## The money that spoils

Gesell read George and pronounced the diagnosis right and the
instrument wrong — a land tax was "a sheep in wolf's clothing," easy for
landowners to praise because impossible to enforce, and in any case
land was only half the problem. The other half was money itself. His
*Die natürliche Wirtschaftsordnung* (1916) locates the flaw in an
asymmetry so ordinary it had escaped notice: every commodity spoils, and
money does not. The farmer's potatoes rot and the newspaper ages, so
their owners must sell; the holder of gold can wait, and charges for not
waiting. Interest, on Gesell's analysis, is a toll levied by the party
who can withhold:

> Der König steht am Schlagbaum; er sperrt die Grenze und sagt: der
> Zehnte ist mein! Der Geldgeber steht vor dem Geldschrank; er sperrt
> den Austausch der Waren … und sagt, wie der König: der Urzins ist
> mein!
>
> *The king stands at the toll-bar; he closes the border and says: the
> tithe is mine! The money-lender stands before the money-chest; he
> closes off the exchange of the goods that depend on its contents as
> their medium — and says, like the king: the basic interest is mine!*
>
> — *Die natürliche Wirtschaftsordnung*, Part 5

The remedy follows from the diagnosis with a logician's insolence: make
money mortal.

> Geld, das wie eine Zeitung veraltet, wie Kartoffeln fault, wie Eisen
> rostet, wie Äther sich verflüchtigt, kann allein sich als Tauschmittel
> von Kartoffeln, Zeitungen, Eisen und Äther bewähren. … Wir müssen also
> das Geld als Ware verschlechtern, wenn wir es als Tauschmittel
> verbessern wollen.
>
> *Only money that goes out of date like a newspaper, rots like
> potatoes, rusts like iron, and evaporates like ether can prove itself
> as the medium of exchange for potatoes, newspapers, iron, and ether.
> … We must therefore make money worse as a commodity if we wish to make
> it better as a medium of exchange.*
>
> — *Die natürliche Wirtschaftsordnung*, Part 4

This is *Freigeld* — free money — and Wörgl was its field trial. Gesell
paired it with a *Freiland* of his own (all land publicly leased at
auction, the rents distributed monthly to mothers by the number of
their children — a child-keyed basic income, in 1916) and with an
International Valuta Association, a proposed world currency union to
carry the system across borders. His Robinson Crusoe parable makes the
whole theory fit in a sentence — a castaway's stores rot while a
newcomer's loan request compounds, and the castaway finally sees it:
"Wieviel Beweiskraft doch die Ratten, Motten und der Rost haben!" —
*what evidential force the rats, the moths, and the rust have!* Keynes,
weighing all of it in chapter 23 of the *General Theory*, granted the
mechanism ("the idea behind stamped money is sound"), filed the
objection that Gesell had "constructed only half a theory of the rate of
interest," and described the project in terms that belong in this file's
epigraph: "the establishment of an anti-Marxian Socialism … based on an
unfettering of competition instead of its abolition." Irving Fisher,
across the Atlantic, was blunter: "I consider myself an humble student
of Silvio Gesell."

The canon's monetary program shares Gesell's staging and then leaves
him behind. Both prescribe a single world currency as the transitional
instrument — the canon in so many words: "While waiting to be able to
suppress money, create a new world currency replacing the national
currencies. That is the solution to the monetary crisis"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=162) %}ETTMTTP 3:162{% end %}).
Both treat money as a tool that has escaped its function and must be
brought to heel. But Gesell wanted money perfected — markets running
faster and cleaner than gold ever allowed — where the canon treats even
perfected money as scaffolding, to be struck when the robots arrive.
Gesell drowns interest in a sea of capital; the canon drains the sea.

## Self-owners on a common earth

For most of the twentieth century the Jubilee axis survived in
economics as an eccentricity and in philosophy hardly at all. Its
academic rehabilitation arrived in 2000, when Peter Vallentyne and
Hillel Steiner published two anthologies whose shared preface reads
like a mission statement for this entire file: "The current search for
a new and cogent 'third way' between socialist economic systems and
laissez-faire capitalist ones parallels the quest in political
philosophy for a coherent 'third way' between unconstrained egalitarian
theories and right-libertarianism. Left-libertarianism, we believe, is
the needed theory." The definition is a two-clause machine:

> Left-libertarian theories of justice hold that agents are full
> self-owners and that natural resources are owned in some egalitarian
> manner. … natural resources — resources which are not the results of
> anyone's choices and which are necessary for any form of activity —
> may be privately appropriated only with the permission of, or with a
> significant payment to, the members of society.
>
> — Vallentyne, "Left-Libertarianism: A Primer" (2000)

You own yourself and everything you make; the earth you did not make is
everyone's. The anthologies trace the position through a
two-century relay — Grotius and Pufendorf on original community, Locke
on self-ownership, the agrarian radicals Ogilvie and Spence, Thomas
Paine's *Agrarian Justice* (1796) with its ground-rent-funded universal
endowment at twenty-one and pension at fifty (the first UBI-shaped
instrument in the record), the Continental "liberal socialists" Colins
and Huet, Herbert Spencer's law of equal freedom, George, Walras,
Rignano — and the editors' historiographic point is the one this
article keeps striking: "the issues and the contending positions have
remained surprisingly constant."

Two of the school's moves land directly on the canon's doctrine.
François Huet held that a person owns outright only what they created
or earned; what they merely *inherited* returns at death to the
"general patrimony" — which is Humanitarianism's inheritance rule,
stated in 1853. And Steiner grounds the abolition of bequest in the
metaphysics of rights: "dead people do not have rights, and hence
left-libertarianism need not, and arguably should not, recognize …
the validity of bequests. Any assets owned by a person at death are
deemed abandoned." The forty-nine-year lease operationalizes exactly
that intuition, with the family-home exception as its one concession to
sentiment. The school's third member in the user-facing sense, Philippe
Van Parijs, converts the common share into a permanent cash flow — a
basic income funded by the rents on everything "given" rather than
made, employment rents included — and thereby supplies the
transitional instrument the canon's interim rule
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=98) %}ETTMTTP 3:98{% end %})
gestures at: a decent living as of right, contribution rewarded on
top. The chief difference is scope and clock. The philosophers common
the *unmade*; the canon commons everything and lets merit hold any of
it for at most a working lifetime.

## The city as organism

The machine axis has its own prophet, and he worked from a
twenty-five-acre compound in the Florida scrub. Jacque Fresco — a
self-taught industrial designer whose *The Venus Project: The Redesign
of a Culture* (1995) is fifty-five illustrated pages of total system —
begins where the canon's second message begins, with automation
("cybernation," in his coinage) irreversibly absorbing labor, and draws
the same conclusion at the same altitude: the obstacle is the monetary
order itself. "A monetary system evolved many years ago as a device to
control human behavior in an environment with limited resources," he
writes; "it is only when resources are scarce that money can be used to
control their distribution — one could not, for example, sell sand or
salt water on the beach." His alternative he named the resource-based
economy: "a resource-based economy utilizes existing resources rather
than money, and provides an equitable method of distributing these
resources in the most efficient and humane manner for the entire
population." The thought experiment he reaches for is the one Gesell
would have recognized inverted — destroy the tool and see what remains:

> If all the money in the world were destroyed, as long as topsoil,
> factories, and other resources were left intact, we could build
> anything we choose to build and fulfill any human need. It is not
> money that people need, but rather it is freedom of access to most
> necessities without ever having to appeal to a government bureaucracy
> or any other agency.
>
> — *The Venus Project*, p. 5

Delivery comes as architecture: circular cities laid out in concentric
belts, run by a "cybernated system" he calls the city's "electrical
autonomic nervous system," ownership replaced by library-style access
centers, decisions "arrived at through statistical mechanics" by
technicians rather than politicians, "the entire city function[ing] as
a single, complex organism." A reader of the canon's seventh chapter
will experience the specification as familiar. The Elohim's cities are
single structures a kilometer on a side, each "conceived by the same
architect," cubic "so as not to «eat» the countryside," their vital
systems — energy, food, organization — supervised by computers under
the eyes of the eternals
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=47) %}TBWTT 7:45–47{% end %});
the terrestrial program prescribes communal houses for fifty thousand
in open country for the same stated reason, that a planet of detached
houses with gardens has no countryside left
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=101) %}ETTMTTP 3:101{% end %}).
Two moneyless, cybernated, nature-sparing city-organisms, specified
within twenty years of each other, by authors with no plausible chain
of transmission between them. Fresco even brushes the ceiling of this
project's subject matter, deadpan: "While some of the world's leading
scientists are trying to probe the outer reaches of the galaxy in
search of intelligent extra-terrestrials, at present The Venus Project
is more concerned with the application of human intelligence to
resolving the problems here on Earth." His back cover, less guarded,
calls the Florida compound "a second Eden."

## The commons of the mind

For every member of the file so far, abundance was a projection. In one
domain it arrived, and the institutions that formed around it are the
file's only entries that are neither plan nor novel but operating
infrastructure. When the marginal cost of copying information fell to
zero, a production system assembled itself that no economics textbook
had predicted: Linus Torvalds's 1991 bulletin-board posting became the
software substrate of the world's servers and phones, licensed so that
no one could ever enclose it; Wikipedia, written by no one in
particular, retired the shelf of paid encyclopedias; and Yochai
Benkler, the Yale scholar who named the phenomenon "peer production,"
supplied its arithmetic — the world's connected population carries
"between two billion and six billion spare hours among them, every
day," a standing reservoir of unpriced capacity. Tapscott and Williams's
*Wikinomics* (2008) documented how far the logic reaches beyond code:
their set piece is Goldcorp, a failing Toronto miner whose chief
executive, electrified by the Linux story, posted fifty years of
proprietary geological data on the open web — "we'll ask the world to
tell us where we're going to find the next six million ounces of gold"
— and watched contestants from fifty countries identify a hundred and
ten targets, half unknown to the company, four-fifths of them
productive. The commons out-prospected the firm.

The movement produced its martyr, and his manifesto belongs in this
file's primary sources. "Information is power," Aaron Swartz wrote in
the *Guerilla Open Access Manifesto* (2008). "But like all power, there
are those who want to keep it for themselves."{{ footnote(id="7") }}
His argument — that the scientific record, largely publicly funded,
belongs to everyone and its enclosure behind paywalls is a private tax
on the species' inheritance — is Paine's ground-rent argument
transposed into the knowledge economy: the unmade commons, appropriated,
owing compensation. The canon's version of the same instruction is
addressed to the whole research enterprise: liberation from material
constraint would take only a few years, it claims, if "all the
technical and scientific capacities … set about working flat out, no
longer for particular interests but for the whole community"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=99) %}ETTMTTP 3:99{% end %})
— peer production, proposed in 1975 as the fast path to the golden age.
The contemporary aesthetic movement this corpus tracks as
{% wiki(slug="cyberparadism") %}Cyberparadism{% end %} counts open
source collaboration among its six Signs of the approaching abundance,
alongside fusion energy and full automation; on the file's evidence the
ordering is right, because open source is the one Sign already at
industrial scale.

## The techno-capital machine

The file's newest entry arrived on October 16, 2023, from the least
utopian address imaginable: the website of a venture-capital firm. Marc
Andreessen's *Techno-Optimist Manifesto* is five thousand words of
anaphora — "We believe" — and its abundance passages could be spliced
into Fresco or the canon's second message without a visible seam. "We
believe the measure of abundance is falling prices." "We believe we
should push to drop prices across the economy through the application
of technology until as many prices are effectively zero as possible."
"We believe if we make both intelligence and energy 'too cheap to
meter', the ultimate result will be that all physical goods become as
cheap as pencils."{{ footnote(id="8") }} The engine has a name borrowed,
with attribution, from the philosopher Nick Land: "the techno-capital
machine, the engine of perpetual material creation, growth, and
abundance." The enemies are ideas — the manifesto's own list runs
through "existential risk," "sustainability," "ESG," the "Precautionary
Principle," "de-growth," "the limits of growth" — and the document
closes with a roll of Patron Saints in place of citations
{{ footnote(id="9") }}.

The convergence with the canon is real and worth stating precisely.
Both hold that the material problem is soluble and that the solvent is
science; both treat energy abundance as the hinge (fusion is the
manifesto's "second silver bullet" and the first of Cyberparadism's six
Signs); both regard the counsel of limits as the counsel of decline —
the canon's own imperative is to "profit from life on this earth which
science can transform into paradise"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=39) %}ETTMTTP 2:39{% end %}).
Then the paths cross and part with a clang. Andreessen: "We believe a
Universal Basic Income would turn people into zoo animals to be farmed
by the state. Man was not meant to be farmed; man was meant to be
useful, to be productive, to be proud." The canon: "Every being has the
right to have enough to live even if he does not work"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=98) %}ETTMTTP 3:98{% end %})
— and work done under the whip of subsistence is "terribly debasing," a
sale of one's life to do "work which simple machines can do"
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=38) %}ETTMTTP 2:38{% end %}).
The manifesto insists technological change "increases" the need for
human work and sanctifies the machine that runs on markets forever. The
canon reads the same machine as a chrysalis: markets and money are the
larval instruments, and the adult form — "to think, to create, to
blossom" — has no price system in it.

The finding deserves italics, because nothing in the file predicts it:
*the venture capitalist is the conservative here, and the 1973 contactee
text is the radical.* Andreessen offers abundance with wage labor,
prices, and accumulation preserved in perpetuity — capitalism at escape
velocity. The canon offers abundance as the solvent in which all three
dissolve. The manifesto's critics, for their part, read its politics as
older than its technology — Ezra Klein filed it under "reactionary
futurism," a document less about tools than about the conviction "that
we were once brave and strong and we have become soft and weak";
Jacobin traced its lineage to Nietzschean aristocratic radicalism. The
critics' quarrel is with the manifesto's *who benefits*; the canon's
quarrel is with its *what for*. Neither disputes the machine.

## One doctrine, two axes

Laid side by side, the file resolves into a table. Every member holds a
piece; the canon's doctrine, dictated whole in 1973–75, holds all of
them at once.

| Question | The human genealogy | The canon |
|---|---|---|
| Who owns the earth? | Leviticus's Jubilee; George's socialized rent; Hertzka's ownerless soil; left-libertarian common ownership of the unmade | "You are only its tenants" — all assets community-owned, leased 49 years (TBWTT 6:7, 6:10) |
| What survives death? | Huet's reversion to the general patrimony; Rignano's escalating transfer tax; Steiner's abandoned assets; Piketty's r > g alarm | Inheritance abolished except the family home (TBWTT 6:8) |
| What is money for? | Gesell's demurrage and world currency; Fresco's abolition; Fisher's stamp scrip | World currency as bridge, then total suppression (ETTMTTP 3:162, 3:96) |
| Who does the work? | Fresco's cybernation; Benkler's peer production; the manifesto's AI and robotics | Machines and biological robots; work by vocation only (ETTMTTP 2:38, 3:100; TBWTT 7:39) |
| What is a person owed? | Hertzka's maintenance right; Paine's endowment; Van Parijs's basic income | A decent living as of right, working or not (ETTMTTP 3:98) |
| What is a person for? | "Abundance and leisure" as the precondition of art and science (Hertzka) | "To think, to create, to blossom" (ETTMTTP 2:38) |
| Who decides? | Fresco's technicians; the manifesto's founders; the philosophers' just institutions | Geniocracy, then the automated substrate under minimal supervision (TBWTT 6:5, 7:45) |

Three features distinguish the canon's entry within its own family.
First, completeness: it is the only member that runs both axes —
Jubilee and machine — as one program, with the property reform
explicitly staged as the transitional regime and the automation
explicitly staged as its retirement. Second, earliness: the automation
passages predate the personal computer; the interim rules anticipate,
sometimes verbatim, positions (basic income, inheritance reversion,
world currency) whose academic and political waves crested decades
later. Third, and unique in kind, the exemplar claim: every human
member of the file argues from theory or extrapolation, while the canon
claims description — a civilization that has run the program to
completion and is offering the terrestrial copy as its "political and
humanitarian baggage," transferable, it says, without the danger that
would attend the scientific kind
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=65) %}TBWTT 5:65{% end %}).
That last claim is exactly what this project's labels exist for: it is
`framework` — explicit in the source, unverifiable by any archive, and
the reason the comparison is interesting rather than merely tidy.

## Where the readings part ways

A file this convergent invites the suspicion of curation, so the
divergences and failures should be stated at full strength.

**The record is a record of defeats.** Wörgl was closed by court order
after fourteen months. The Freiland movement died on the coast of Kenya
without ever unpacking. Gesell's Eden endured as
a garden suburb, not an economic order; his week as finance delegate of
a doomed council republic ended in a treason trial. The kibbutzim built
a century of working communism at village scale and then, under debt
and generational drift, mostly re-admitted salaries and markets. The Venus Project never grew past its
twenty-five acres. Open source conquered its domain — and its domain is
the one where goods copy for free; its maintainers are still paid, when
they are paid at all, by the money economy it routes around. Every
experiment in the file either stayed small, was stopped by the state,
or re-admitted the market. The canon would answer that this is the
predicted signature of sub-civilizational-scale implementation — its
own doctrine names world government and geniocratic administration as
prerequisites
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=6, verse=14) %}TBWTT 6:14{% end %})
— but a critic is entitled to hear that as the utopian's oldest escape
clause: the design works, given a precondition history has never once
supplied.

**The calculation problem has not been dissolved, only re-platformed.**
Mises and Hayek's objection to planning — that prices carry dispersed
knowledge no administrator can gather — cut down every twentieth-century
attempt to run an economy by intention, and it applies with full force
to Fresco's statistical mechanics and to the canon's computers
supervising "energy, food, organization"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=45) %}TBWTT 7:45{% end %}).
The rejoinder now current — that machine intelligence at sufficient
scale *is* the missing calculator — is precisely the live wager of the
2020s, and it is an open one. There is an irony in the seating chart:
Hayek is among Andreessen's Patron Saints, which places the file's most
committed abundance movement under the patronage of the thinker whose
argument forbids the moneyless endpoint most of the file aims at.

**Geniocracy is the doctrine's steepest cliff.** An intelligence-gated
franchise strikes at the moral core of democratic equality, and
mainstream political philosophy rejects rule-by-the-measured in all its
variants; the objections — who builds the tests, who audits the
builders, what "raw intelligence" even denominates — are the standard
ones and they are strong. The corpus treats the commandment as
directional rather than operational, and its own texts assign the
program's realization to non-Raëlian political movements at arm's
length
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=1, verse=95) %}LWTE 1:91–95{% end %});
none of that discharges the objection. It is recorded here in its own
voice.

**The canon hedges its own abundance.** The strangest sentence in this
file's canonical stratum is a limit. Discussing whether humanity will
inherit the creators' role, Yahweh remarks: "we are not sure that man
can ever stabilize himself in abundance"
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=59) %}TBWTT 5:59{% end %})
— and elsewhere the odds of the species surviving its own test are put
at one in a hundred
({% libref(book="lets-welcome-the-extraterrestrials", chapter=2, verse=103) %}LWTE 2:103{% end %}).
The doctrine is not a promise that abundance arrives; it is a
specification of what to build if aggression is mastered first. The
sunny reading of the file — abundance as destiny — belongs to
Andreessen, and to no one else in it.

**And the file's members were men of their times.** George's movement
splintered into single-tax colonies and municipal campaigns; Hertzka's
Africa is the empty Africa of the exploration literature, ownerless
only in the surveyor's imagination; Gesell's masterwork carries period
rhetoric his modern students excise{{ footnote(id="10") }}. The
genealogy is offered as evidence of a recurring structure of thought,
and not as a communion of saints.

## The gardens they kept planting

One pattern in the file was not planned by anyone in it. Hertzka's
settlers, reaching their valley under the glaciers, named it Eden.
Gesell wrote his economics in a reform colony called Eden, and died
there. Fresco's compound in the Florida scrub was advertised as "a
second Eden." Three independent builders of abundance, reaching for a
name, reached for the same garden — the place where provision precedes
labor, where the fruit is on the trees before anyone has earned it.
Mainstream reading takes the name as the obvious cultural shorthand for
paradise, which it is. The corpus's reading runs one layer deeper: in
the canon, {% wiki(slug="cyberparadism") %}Eden was a real
installation{% end %} — the laboratory-garden of the creators, an
engineered abundance in which the species began — and the creators'
own world still runs on its economics
({% libref(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=2, verse=52) %}ETTMTTP 2:51–52{% end %}).
Within the frame, the reformers' recurring garden is a memory working
itself out as a plan: the file this article has read is a species
reverse-engineering, from inside scarcity, the operating conditions it
was created in. That is a `framework` reading resting on an `inferred`
pattern, and both labels apply. The pattern itself, though, is in the
primary sources, verbatim, three times.

What the frame adds last is stakes. The canon's
{% wiki(slug="golden-age") %}Golden Age{% end %} is one tine of a fork
whose other tine — the self-destruction this project's
{% wiki(slug="apocalypse") %}Apocalypse{% end %} file documents, and
which a companion article reads through the nuclear literature at full
length in [*The Reporter at the Razor's
Edge*](/articles/the-reporter-at-the-razors-edge/) — is reached by
default. The machine axis is racing: the automation the 1975 text
promised is, in the decade of large models and humanoid robotics, a
capital-expenditure line item. The Jubilee axis is not: wealth
concentrates across generations at rates Piketty's *r > g* made
canonical, and no polity on Earth is debating a forty-nine-year lease.
The file's diagnosis, from Leviticus to the manifesto, is that the
second axis is a choice. Hertzka said it with the confidence of 1890:
nothing now compels us to endure the misery of an obsolete world
order, nothing prevents us from the happiness and abundance the
existing means of civilization could furnish — "nichts, als unsere
eigene Thorheit." *Nothing, except our own folly.* The canon said it
shorter, in 1975, in a sentence addressed to a species that had just
built the machines and not yet decided what they were for:

{% library(book="extraterrestrials-took-me-to-their-planet", chapter=3, verse=99) %}
You can, in a few years, make a completely different world. You have
arrived at the golden age.
{% end %}
