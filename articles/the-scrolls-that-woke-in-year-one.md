Some time in the winter that straddled 1946 and 1947, a young Ta'amireh
Bedouin known as Muhammad edh-Dhib — "the wolf" — was in the cliffs
above the northwest shore of the Dead Sea, about a mile from a ruin the
Arabs called Khirbet Qumran. In the story as it is usually retold, a
stray animal from his herd led him up the scree, and a stone he threw
into a dark opening answered with the sound of breaking
pottery.{{ footnote(id="2") }} Inside the cave stood rows of tall clay
jars, most empty, one holding bundles wrapped in linen gone black with
age. The bundles were leather, and the leather was covered in writing.

John J. Collins — the Yale scholar of Second Temple Judaism whose
Princeton "biography" of the scrolls is the most level-headed short
account of what followed, and whose readings this article leans on
throughout — is careful with this scene: the versions edh-Dhib told later disagree with each other, and
the sober residue is only that Ta'amireh tribesmen took three scrolls
from a cave south of Jericho "some time in late 1946 or early 1947."
Even the sober residue is remarkable enough. The three bundles were a
complete book of Isaiah roughly a thousand years older than any Hebrew
Bible manuscript then known; a rule book for a vanished religious
community; and a commentary on the prophet Habakkuk that read every
verse as coded news about "the end of days." Fragments of some nine
hundred manuscripts would eventually come out of eleven caves in those
cliffs. Two millennia of silence, and then jar after jar of voices.

Eighteen months before the stone went into the cave, a different flash
had gone up over a different sea. The {% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raëlian{% end %}
canon — the founding source material of this project — dates the
present age from it: the atomic destruction of Hiroshima on August 6,
1945 marks the opening of the {% wiki(slug="apocalypse") %}Apocalypse{% end %},
the age of *unveiling* in the word's plain Greek sense, and the
movement counts its years from that hinge, so that 1946 is year
one.{{ footnote(id="1") }} The chapter of the first message that
explains the count is titled exactly that —
{% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=1) %}"1946, year 1 of the new era"{% end %} —
and the messenger's own selection is pinned to the same event:

{% library(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=1, verse=47) %}
Lastly, we decided to choose someone after the first atomic explosion,
which took place in 1945, and you were born in 1946. We have been
watching you since your birth—and even before. That is why we have
chosen you.
{% end %}

Set the two clocks side by side and the chronology invites comparison:
the oldest library of the Hebrew Bible woke up in year one. This
article is about that coincidence — what exactly surfaced, through
whose hands it passed, why it took forty years and a scandal to reach
the public, and what it did to our picture of scripture. The claim
that the timing *means* something is the canon's frame extended by
this project, and it is labeled at the top of this page for what it
is: interpretive synthesis, `speculative` in the corpus's own
taxonomy. The manuscripts, the dates, and the quotations underneath it
are checkable, and the checking is the pleasure of the thing.

## A calendar that starts with a flash

The Greek *apokalypsis* means an uncovering — the removal of a veil,
the disclosure of what was hidden. The catastrophic sense is a
medieval accretion; the {% wiki(slug="apocalypse") %}corpus's reading{% end %},
following the canon, takes the word at its etymology: the age that
opened in 1945 is the age in which concealed things become legible,
because humanity has finally built the instruments — scientific,
archaeological, philological — to read them. On that frame, one would
expect the opening years of such an age to be busy. They were.

In December 1945, at the base of the Jabal al-Tarif cliffs in Upper
Egypt, a farmer named Muhammad Ali al-Samman dug up a sealed jar and
found thirteen leather-bound Coptic codices — the Nag Hammadi library,
fifty-two mostly Gnostic treatises including the Gospel of Thomas,
buried since the fourth century.{{ footnote(id="7") }} Within about a
year, the Qumran jars gave up the first scrolls. On September 30,
1946, Claude Vorilhon, later {% wiki(slug="rael") %}Raël{% end %}, was
born — "if you were born in 1946, it is not by chance," the first
message says of the timing
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=5, verse=7) %}TBWTT 5:7{% end %}).
In June 1947 a private pilot named Kenneth Arnold described nine
objects over Mount Rainier moving "like a saucer would if you skipped
it across the water," and the press coined the term that named the
{% wiki(slug="ufology") %}modern contact era{% end %}; the Roswell
incident followed within a fortnight. On November 29, 1947 the United
Nations voted to partition Palestine. On December 16, 1947 the first
transistor worked at Bell Labs — the device on which every subsequent
instrument of the information age, including the one rendering this
sentence, depends.{{ footnote(id="8") }} On May 14, 1948 the state of
Israel was declared; the scrolls had been announced to the world in
the papers five weeks earlier.

Coincidence-clusters are cheap: choose a sufficiently eventful period,
select its contents afterward, and a pattern will appear. Nothing here
overcomes that problem or assigns a probability to the cluster. Its
interest is interpretive rather than predictive. The period contains two
recovered scriptural libraries, the re-establishment of a Jewish state,
the transistor, the beginning of the modern sighting era, and the birth
of the man to whom the messages were later attributed. Those events fit
the canon's idea of an age of unveiling unusually well, but the fit is
visible in retrospect and should be weighed accordingly.

## The wolf, the cobbler, and the archbishop

What happened to the three bundles requires little embellishment.
In March 1947 the Bedouin carried them to Bethlehem and showed them to
antiquities dealers, and the scrolls found their way — "apparently
because the scrolls were written on leather," Collins notes drily — to
a Syrian Orthodox merchant and cobbler named Khalil Eskander Shahin,
known to everyone as Kando. Kando alerted Mar Athanasius Yeshue
Samuel, the Syrian Orthodox Metropolitan at St. Mark's Monastery in
the Old City of Jerusalem, and the archbishop had reasons of his own
to listen. Church memory preserved two ancient reports of manuscripts
from caves near Jericho: Origen of Alexandria had used a Greek psalter
found "at Jericho in a jar" around 200 CE, and the Nestorian patriarch
Timotheus I, writing about 800 CE, described an Arab huntsman whose
dog went into a cave and led him to books of the Old Testament "and
others." The caves had been found before, and forgotten before. In
July 1947 Mar Samuel bought the first batch from Kando for a sum
usually reported as twenty-four Palestinian pounds — on the order of a
hundred dollars for the oldest Bible manuscripts on Earth.

That autumn the other half of the find — a second Isaiah scroll, a
scroll of hymns, and a manual for an apocalyptic war — reached Eleazar
Sukenik, professor of archaeology at the Hebrew University, through an
Armenian dealer. Jerusalem was already dividing into armed zones, and
the first authentication of the Dead Sea Scrolls was performed across
a military barrier: "Initially Sukenik had to peer at a fragment
through a barbed wire fence," Collins records. Days later, with a pass
into the dealer's zone, Sukenik examined the scrolls properly, judged
them genuine, and bought them — "in November of that year," as Collins
puts it, "just before the United Nations passed its resolution
authorizing the creation of the state of Israel." Family memory makes
the interleaving tighter still: Sukenik traveled to Bethlehem for the
scrolls against his son's security advice in the very days of the
vote, and his son — of whom more shortly — later drew the moral in
print. "It is as if these manuscripts had been waiting in caves for
two thousand years," Yigael Yadin wrote, "ever since the destruction
of Israel's independence, until the people of Israel had returned to
their home and regained their freedom." One may decline the theology
and keep the datum: the scrolls and the state re-entered history in
the same week, and the men holding the scrolls noticed at the time.

The American act of the drama began in February 1948, when the Syrians
brought four scrolls to the American School of Oriental Research —
carried there, in one of history's better sentences, by an emissary
who "returned by taxi, carrying in his briefcase the great Isaiah
scroll, the Manual of Discipline, the Commentary on Habakkuk and the
Genesis Apocryphon." The director was away; a recent PhD named John
Trever, whose hobby was photography, talked the Syrians into letting
him photograph the scrolls, matched the script to the Nash
Papyrus{{ footnote(id="3") }}, and airmailed prints to William Foxwell
Albright, the reigning authority on Hebrew palaeography. Albright's
reply dated the script to the second century BCE and pronounced the
find "the greatest manuscript discovery of modern times." The Yale
press release of April 10, 1948 that introduced the scrolls to the
world also introduced their first cover story, asserting that they had
been "preserved for many centuries in the library of the Syrian
Orthodox Monastery of St. Mark" — a provenance Collins calls flatly
inaccurate, noting that "the Syrian archbishop on more than one
occasion alleged that the scrolls were found in a monastery." The
scrolls arrived in public trailing intrigue the way comets trail dust,
and they never stopped.

## Miscellaneous for sale

Mar Samuel took his four scrolls to America in January 1949 and could
not sell them. The legal title was clouded — Jordan considered him a
smuggler — and, in the poisoned air after partition, Collins writes,
"he did not want to sell them to a Jew." After five years he resorted
to the classified section. In June 1954, under the heading
"Miscellaneous for Sale," the *Wall Street Journal* carried this:

> **"The Four Dead Sea Scrolls."**
> Biblical Manuscripts, dating back to at least 200 BC, are for sale.
> This would be an ideal gift to an educational or religious
> institution by an individual or group.
> Box F 206, The Wall Street Journal.
>
> — *Wall Street Journal*, June 1954, as reproduced in Collins, ch. 1

The buyer was a New York banker named Sidney Esteridge, who paid
$250,000. Unknown to the archbishop, Esteridge was a front: the money
and the instructions came from Yigael Yadin — Sukenik's son, lately
chief of staff of the Israeli army, then lecturing in the United
States. Sukenik had died the year before, still convinced the
scattered scrolls belonged together. Through a classified ad and a
concealed principal, his son reunited them, and in 1965 the Israel
Museum built a white dome in Jerusalem to house them: the Shrine of
the Book, to which this article will return on foot.

Yadin was not finished. Since the early 1960s he had been negotiating
with Kando for a rumored further scroll whose contents no one outside
Bethlehem had seen. In June 1967 the war put Bethlehem under Israeli
control, and Yadin — by then military adviser to the prime minister —
sent a small group of intelligence officers to Kando's house. Collins
gives the episode a single devastating clause: they located Kando,
"and after an interrogation that has been described as 'unpleasant,'
they took possession of the scroll," which had been hidden under floor
tiles in a shoebox and damaged by damp. It was the Temple Scroll, the
longest of all the Qumran manuscripts, a rewriting of the Torah in the
first-person voice of God. Kando was eventually paid $105,000 in a
settlement funded largely by an English industrialist. Between the
barbed-wire fence of 1947 and the shoebox of 1967, the acquisition
history of the scrolls is a precise miniature of the region's — every
transfer of parchment shadowing a transfer of territory.

## The scandal of the century

The caves were not done. Bedouin prospectors — always a step ahead of
the archaeologists, as Collins concedes — found Cave 2 in 1952, and in
the late summer of that year Cave 4, a stone's throw from the Qumran
ruin, containing the shredded remains of hundreds of manuscripts.
Cave 3 yielded the strangest object of all: two oxidized rolls of
beaten copper, engraved with a list of sixty-four treasure caches —
some two hundred tons of gold and silver, with directions. The
editorial team split over whether the Copper Scroll was inventory or
fantasy; the excavator of Qumran, Roland de Vaux, allegedly dismissed
it as the "whimsical product of a deranged mind," a verdict later
scholarship has quietly reversed, since folklore is rarely engraved on
copper in a dry documentary style.

Cave 4 was the trap. Frank Moore Cross, who spent years at the sorting
tables, described the material: "Many fragments are so brittle or
friable that they can scarcely even be touched with a camel's-hair
brush. Most are warped, crinkled, or shrunken, crusted with soil
chemicals, blackened by moisture and age." An international team of
eight was assembled in 1953–54 under de Vaux to piece the jigsaw
together — brilliant, tiny, and skewed: several Catholic priests,
no Jews ("at the insistence of the Jordanian government," Collins
notes, since the fragments sat in Jordanian East Jerusalem), and no
plan for what would happen when the Rockefeller money ran out in 1960.
The money ran out. The editors dispersed to professorships, kept
exclusive rights over their assigned fragments, and published at a
pace that became first embarrassing, then notorious. By the thirtieth
anniversary Geza Vermes issued the sentence that stuck: "unless
drastic measures are taken at once, the greatest and most valuable of
all Hebrew and Aramaic manuscript discoveries is likely to become the
academic scandal par excellence of the twentieth century."

The scandal had casualties with names. John Allegro, the team's one
agnostic, went on the BBC in January 1956 to claim the texts showed a
sect whose crucified Teacher was expected to rise again — "the
terrible Jannaeus… dragged forth the Teacher, and as now seems
probable, gave him into the hands of his Gentile troops to be
crucified" — and drew a public letter from five of his own colleagues:
"We are unable to see in the texts the 'findings' of Mr. Allegro…
either he has misread the texts or he has built up a chain of
conjectures which the materials do not support." Allegro, convinced he
was being suppressed by a Catholic cabal (two of his opponents were
Presbyterians), finished his own career in 1970 with a book deriving
Christianity from a hallucinogenic mushroom cult. John Strugnell, the
prodigy who had joined the team at twenty-four, became editor-in-chief
in 1985, told an ABC newscast that critics demanding access were "a
bunch of fleas who are in the business of annoying us," and was
destroyed in 1990 by a Ha'aretz interview, given in the trough of
manic depression and alcoholism, in which he called Judaism "a
horrible religion." One colleague said he had "drenched the Scrolls in
the blood of the Holocaust." Collins, who studied under him, writes
the fairest epitaph: "Strugnell was a flawed character, to be sure,
but he was never malicious. That is more than could be said for some
of his most vocal detractors."

The forty-year embargo bred the inevitable theory: that the Vatican
was sitting on the scrolls because they disproved Christianity. Michael
Baigent and Richard Leigh's 1991 bestseller *The Dead Sea Scrolls
Deception* gave it mass circulation. On this the scholarship is not
divided, and this project — which has its own quarrels with how
religious institutions have handled inconvenient texts — reports the
verdict without hedging: "No serious scholars take such claims
seriously," Collins writes of the Vatican rumor, and of its promoters,
"scarcely any scholar has found Eisenman's reading of the Scrolls
persuasive at all." The delay needed no conspiracy. It was
perfectionism, mortality, alcohol, underfunding, and the oldest
academic sin, the hoarding of sources — and Collins's aphorism about
the profiteers deserves its fame: "No one sells books by showing that
what we believed all along turns out to be true."

The liberation, when it came, came from the age's own signature
instruments. In 1988 a privately compiled concordance of the
unpublished texts was distributed to a few libraries; a doctoral
student named Martin Abegg reverse-engineered the scrolls from it
with a computer, and in September 1991 the reconstructed texts were
published over the editors' fury. Days later the Huntington Library in
California announced that a forgotten security set of photographs in
its vault was open to all comers; the Israel Antiquities Authority
protested, William Safire called its officials "insular jerks" in the
*New York Times*, and on October 27, 1991 the monopoly collapsed. Under
Emanuel Tov, thirty-three volumes of the official edition appeared in
under twenty years. Collins's summary is the one to keep: "the release
of the Scrolls was unequivocally a good thing. Despite the dire
warnings of the official editors, chaos did not result." An unveiling
age got its unveiling — forty-four years late, by concordance,
computer, and one librarian with a spine.

## The canon that wasn't closed yet

So much for the container. The cargo is stranger than the smuggling.

Every book of the {% wiki(slug="hebrew-bible") %}Hebrew Bible{% end %}
except Esther turned up in the caves — and so, in bulk, did books the
later canon expelled. Fragments of the
[Book of Enoch](/library/book-of-enoch/) surfaced in
their original Aramaic, roughly eleven manuscripts' worth from Cave 4
alone; before Qumran, the book had survived only in Ethiopic, and its
Jewish origin could still be doubted. The Book of Jubilees — a
retelling of {% wiki(slug="genesis") %}Genesis{% end %} on a 364-day
solar calendar — appears in around fifteen copies and is *cited as an
authority* in the sect's own Damascus Document. Collins draws the
uncomfortable arithmetic plainly: "If we judge by the number of copies
preserved, such books as 1 Enoch and Jubilees were more important to
the sectarians than Proverbs or Qoheleth." The Ethiopian church, which
kept Enoch canonical while everyone else called it apocrypha, turns
out to have been the better archivist. For a corpus like this one,
which treats the Watchers material of Enoch — the descent of the
*benei ha-Elohim* of Genesis 6, their teaching, their hybrid children
— as compressed memory rather than fantasy, Qumran is the receipt: in
the last centuries BCE, in Judea, in Aramaic, Enoch was scripture.

The text of the canonical books was equally unsettled. The caves
yielded Hebrew manuscripts agreeing with the Masoretic text, others
agreeing with the Samaritan Pentateuch, others agreeing with the Greek
Septuagint where it diverges — including a short Hebrew Jeremiah
one-eighth leaner than the received book — side by side in the same
collection, with no sign the sect cared. Collins spells out the
consequence: "For Christians brought up to believe in verbal
inspiration, this may come as something of a shock. The actual words
of the Bible, even the words of the Pentateuch or Torah, were not
definitively fixed in the time of Christ."

Two readings recovered from the caves matter more to this project
than all the rest, and both concern the word
{% wiki(slug="elohim") %}Elohim{% end %}.

The first is a single line of Deuteronomy. In the received text, Moses'
song says the Most High fixed the boundaries of the nations "according
to the number of the sons of *Israel*" — a phrase that has puzzled
readers forever, since Israel does not yet exist in the scene. The
Qumran fragment 4QDeut(j) preserves what the verse said before a
scribe corrected it: the nations were divided "according to the number
of the sons of *Elohim*," each people allotted to one of the divine
plurality, with {% wiki(slug="yahweh") %}Yahweh{% end %} receiving
Jacob as his portion.{{ footnote(id="5") }} Mainstream textual
criticism, no friend of this corpus's conclusions, judges the Qumran
reading original. Here is the {% wiki(slug="plurality-of-gods") %}plurality{% end %}
this project argues from, written on leather for once, with the edit
that removed it caught in the act. It is the
single most consequential variant in the scrolls, and the reason the
Deuteronomy fragment appears in the footnotes of every serious
divine-council study and of this project's own
[Wallis essay](/articles/the-archdeacon-and-the-dragon/) alike.

The second is a text from Cave 11 about a figure named Melchizedek —
the priest-king who blesses Abraham in Genesis and then vanishes from
the narrative. The Qumran commentary assembles Leviticus, Isaiah, and
the Psalms around a final Day of Atonement at which Melchizedek, a
*heavenly* officer, executes the judgment; and to secure the claim it
quotes {% libref(book="psalms", chapter=82, verse=1) %}Psalm 82:1{% end %}
— "Elohim stands in the assembly of El, in the midst of elohim he
judges" — and names the standing *elohim*:
Melchizedek.{{ footnote(id="6") }} A community of Torah-observant
Judeans, two centuries before the rabbis, read *elohim* as a word that
could designate a member of a class of powerful beings and applied it
to an individual who was neither the Most High nor a metaphor. The
corpus's {% wiki(slug="list-of-etymological-readings") %}etymological
case{% end %} has never had a better ancient witness.

The messianic file from the caves runs in the same direction. The
Community Rule expects two anointed ones, "the messiahs of Aaron and
Israel," plus a prophet. An Aramaic apocalypse, 4Q246, says of a
coming figure: "Son of God he shall be called, and they will name him
'Son of the Most High'… His kingdom is an everlasting kingdom" —
phrasing so close to the Annunciation in Luke that when the text was
finally published in 1992, Collins records, "newspapers from London to
Los Angeles trumpeted: 'Son of God among the Dead Sea Scrolls!'"
Another fragment, 4Q521, promises a messiah at whose coming the Lord
"will heal the wounded, give life to the dead and preach good news to
the poor" — the same un-Isaianic triplet {% wiki(slug="jesus") %}Jesus{% end %}
recites to the Baptist's messengers in Matthew 11. None of this makes
Christianity a Qumran franchise, and Collins spends a patient chapter
dismantling the writers who claimed it does. What it shows is narrower
and, for this project, more useful: the titles, the expectations, and
the plural Elohim-grammar that the churches later treated as unique
revelations or as grammatical embarrassments were the common stock of
Judea in the last century before the era turned.

## Children of light, children of darkness

Who hid the library? The majority answer since 1948 has been the
Essenes — the celibate, property-sharing, ferociously pure Jewish
order described by Philo and Josephus, and located by Pliny the Elder,
in the one classical sentence that started it all, on the west shore
of the Dead Sea, "without women and renouncing love entirely, without
money, and having for company only the palm trees," a community that
somehow renewed itself "for thousands of centuries." The Community
Rule from Cave 1 matches Josephus's Essenes down to the staged
initiation and the common purse, and Cross's famous formulation of the
alternative still carries the argument: a skeptic "must suppose that
one [sect], carefully described by classical authors, disappeared
without leaving building remains or even potsherds behind; the other,
systematically ignored by the classical sources, left extensive ruins,
and indeed a great library. I prefer to be reckless and flatly
identify the men of Qumran with their perennial houseguests, the
Essenes."

Whoever they were sociologically, theologically they were a community
living inside a countdown. Their rule book divides humanity at
creation into two camps under two spirits — "those born of truth
spring from a fountain of light, but those born of injustice spring
from a source of darkness… all the children of righteousness are ruled
by the Prince of Light… all the children of injustice are ruled by the
Angel of Darkness" — and their War Scroll opens with a title that
needs no commentary: "The rule of the war on the unleashing of the
attack of the sons of light against the company of the sons of
darkness, the army of Belial." Seven battles, three falling to each
side, the seventh decided by the hand of God. Their
pesher{{ footnote(id="4") }} commentaries read every old prophecy as
sealed dispatches addressed to themselves, "the final generation,"
decodable only by their founder: "God told Habakkuk to write down that
which would happen to the final generation, but He did not make known
to him when time would come to an end… the Teacher of Righteousness,
to whom God made known all the mysteries of the words of His servants
the Prophets."

They were wrong about the schedule. The end that came in 68 CE was a
Roman legion burning the settlement, and the movement left, in
Collins's blunt audit, no discernible heirs: "There is a reason why
this movement did not survive, and why its tenets were not taken up by
mainline Judaism. They were simply too extreme to have enduring
appeal." Here the comparison this article has been circling can be
stated. The Qumran covenanters and the canon behind this project are
both, in the technical sense, apocalyptic communities: both hold that
history has a hinge, that the hinge is near or arrived, and that the
faithful should reorganize their lives around it. But they answer the
hinge in opposite postures. Qumran read apocalypse as war — purity
behind a rampart, humanity pre-sorted into light and darkness, the
end as the destruction of the sons of the wrong lot. The canon reads
apocalypse as *unveiling* — the age in which the old texts become
readable as records, and whose required architecture is not a desert
redoubt but an {% wiki(slug="embassy") %}embassy{% end %} for a
{% wiki(slug="great-return") %}return{% end %}
({% libref(book="the-book-which-tells-the-truth", chapter=7, verse=5) %}the bombs, TBWTT 7:5–6{% end %},
remain the age's stated danger, not its instrument). One movement
sealed its library in jars against the end of its world. The other
proposes to build a guest house. Between those two responses to the
same conviction — that the age has turned — runs most of the moral
distance this corpus cares about.

## The desert kept other letters

Norman Golb of the University of Chicago
spent four decades arguing that the scrolls never belonged to a desert
sect at all: that Khirbet Qumran was a Hasmonean fortress, that no
scroll was ever found in the ruins, that the roughly five hundred
scribal hands are far too many for one community, and that the caves —
together with the manuscript finds at Masada — preserve "remnants of
an extensive Hebraic literature hidden by inhabitants of Jerusalem,
making use of the underground tunnels leading eastward… before and
during the Roman siege of 70 A.D." On Golb's map the scrolls are the
salvaged libraries of a capital under death sentence, carried down the
wadis by refugees. Collins, who gives Golb's observations more credit
than most defenders of the consensus, still finds the core
unbelievable — "It is incomprehensible that the Jerusalem temple would
have contained such an archive of sectarian writings, critical of the
temple" — and proposes the sensible middle: the scrolls are sectarian,
but they are *many* communities' libraries, carried to the wilderness
when the war came. Either way, the human picture is the same and worth
sitting with: the scrolls exist because people fleeing an annihilation
buried what they could not carry, and no one came back.

The cliffs kept the next annihilation's papers too, and here the story
bends toward ground this project has walked before. When the second
Jewish revolt collapsed in 135 CE, refugees carried their documents
into caves further south — Wadi Murabba'at, Naḥal Ḥever — and among
them was a woman named Babatha, whose leather satchel of property
deeds, recovered by Yadin's expedition in the Cave of Letters,
included contracts drawn up in *Nabataean* Aramaic, under the law of
the caravan kingdom of {% wiki(slug="petra") %}Petra{% end %}.{{ footnote(id="9") }}
The connection is older than her: the Hasmoneans built Machaerus,
across the water from Qumran, precisely "to guard against the
Nabateans," and the one philologist recruited to the original Cave 4
team for his command of Nabataean, Jean Starcky, spent his career
between the two corpora. The {% wiki(slug="nabataeans") %}Nabataeans{% end %}
matter to this corpus because of where their story goes next: Dan
Gibson's qibla survey argues that early Islam's sacred geography
points at Petra, an argument this project examined in
[Did the First Mosques Face Petra?](/articles/the-first-mosques-faced-petra-not-mecca/).
No causal thread runs from Qumran to the qibla, and none is claimed
here. What the Dead Sea rift supplies is something quieter: a single
desert corridor, from the Qumran cliffs down past En-Gedi and Masada
toward Petra, that functioned for a thousand years as the region's
involuntary archive — the place where, whenever an age ended violently
for somebody, the papers went into the rock and waited for the age
with instruments fine enough to read them. The scrolls waited two
thousand years. The qiblas waited fourteen hundred. The reading age,
on the canon's calendar, is this one.

## Ten minutes alone in the jar

In 1965 the reunited scrolls were installed on the ridge in West
Jerusalem, in a building that is itself a thesis. The Shrine of the
Book, by Frederick Kiesler and Armand Bartos, is two-thirds
underground; its white dome reproduces at architectural scale the lid
of the Cave 1 jars, and it stands deliberately opposite a
free-standing wall of black basalt — the sons of light and the sons of
darkness, poured in concrete and stone.{{ footnote(id="10") }} You
enter through a low passage like a cave mouth. Collins, watching the
crowds, permitted himself one novelistic sentence about what the
scrolls became: "Hundreds of thousands of people have waited patiently
to catch a glimpse of selected illegible fragments in dimly lighted
display cases and come away feeling that they have touched the past."

I can report on that feeling, because I have tested it under unusually
good conditions. I visited the Shrine in the early autumn of 2022, on
a viciously hot Jerusalem day, and by queuing first at opening I had
the building to myself for about ten minutes. What I was not prepared
for was the air. The interior is engineered for parchment — cool, dim,
and faintly wet — and stepping into it out of the desert glare is a
change of world: a mineral dampness, cave-conditions manufactured
inside a white dome, so unfamiliar in that climate that my memory
filed it as somewhere off-planet; the production designers of *Alien*
worked in the same register, though the Shrine wears it serenely. The
architecture is doing exactly what the jars did. It is a climate in
the shape of a building, keeping the scrolls persuaded that they are
still in the cliff. Alone in that corridor, with the facsimile of the
Isaiah scroll wrapped around its drum like a Torah opened to every
page at once, the two clocks of this article were both audible — the
one that ran out in 68 CE, and the one, on the canon's count, in year
77 and running.

I owe the reader one more disclosure, since this corpus practices
showing its work. That trip is where this project began. The same
journey took me over the border at Petra, through the siq to the
carved facades of the city the qibla evidence points to; and on the
evening of the autumn equinox of 2022, on a hotel rooftop, I decided
to start building Wheel of Heaven. I mention it neither as evidence
nor as omen but as provenance: this article's fascination with buried
libraries, hinge-years, and desert archives is not disinterested, and
you should weigh its arguments knowing that. The scrolls teach the
same hermeneutic — every pesher tells you more about the commentator's
generation than about Habakkuk's.

## Two clocks

Collins reaches for a folktale to describe his subject: the biography
of the scrolls "is somewhat like that of Rip van Winkle. While other
texts from antiquity influenced the Renaissance or the Reformation,
the Scrolls just slept. What we have witnessed in the last sixty-five
years or so is not so much a biography as a post-resurrection
afterlife." His closing line runs the same direction: "All the Scrolls
have now finally been delivered to the light of day. The biography of
the corpus is still in its adolescence."

An adolescence that began in year one. That is the whole of this
article's speculative claim, and it can be put in two sentences. A
community that believed it was living at the end of an age sealed its
library — its Enoch, its two-spirit cosmology, its "sons of Elohim,"
its heavenly *elohim* named Melchizedek — into jars in a cliff, and
the jars stayed shut through the entire run of the era whose scripture
was edited, translated, and closed without them. They opened in the
first months of the calendar that counts from Hiroshima, into the one
generation equipped with the philology to read them, the photography
to fix them, the computer to reconstruct them, and — on the canon's
account — the explanation to place them. The skeptic's reading is
available and respectable: caves erode, shepherds wander, some year
had to be the year. The corpus's reading is the one its name commits
it to: that an age of unveiling would begin by unveiling something,
and it did — jar after jar of voices, awake in year one, still being
read in year 81.
